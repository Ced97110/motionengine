# Shot Charting

## Summary
Shot charting is the practice of recording the number of shots made and attempted from each specific location during a shooting workout. It transforms subjective practice into objective measurement, allowing players and coaches to see real improvement over time, identify weak zones, and set meaningful goals. the shooting specialist calls it an essential habit for any player committed to becoming a better shooter: "If you go to the gym and shoot for a couple of hours, you won't know the facts and figures, such as how many shots you took or how many shots you made, unless you chart them." [S5, p.11]

The confidence cycle is central to the value of charting: seeing concrete improvement in the numbers builds confidence, confidence leads to better performance, better performance generates more data to chart — a self-reinforcing loop. "Confidence leads to success and success leads to confidence." [S5, p.11]

## When to Use
- Every individual shooting workout, regardless of duration
- When diagnosing why a player is shooting a low percentage in games (identify which zones are weak)
- When setting progressive shooting goals over a season or offseason
- When comparing a player's current performance to their baseline from earlier in the year
- As a data source for deciding which spots to prioritize in the next workout

## Key Principles
1. **Chart every session.** Keep a dedicated notebook and pen in the gym bag. Record makes and attempts every time you shoot, without exception. [S5, p.11]
2. **Record by specific location.** Note the exact spot for each set (e.g., "right corner three," "left wing midrange," "free throw"). Use abbreviations consistently (RC = right corner, LC = left corner, etc.). [S5, p.11]
3. **Use a makes-out-of-attempts format.** Example: "threes: RC, 25 for 30; threes: LC, 25 for 35." This gives both volume and efficiency data at a glance. [S5, p.11]
4. **Set spot-specific goals.** Once charting reveals a weak zone, set a goal number — e.g., "make 25 in a row from this spot before moving on." Goals provide accountability and a clear end-point for each practice block. [S5, pp.11-12]
5. **Raise the bar over time.** Early goals might be "5 in a row from the three-point line." Intermediate goals grow to "25 in a row from the college line" and "10 in a row from the pro line." Elite aspiration: "page upon page of 25 for 25." [S5, p.12]
6. **Practice all zones, not just strengths.** Shot charts will reveal if a player only practices comfortable spots. "Work on shots that you have trouble making consistently. Pretty soon, your weaknesses will become your strengths." [S5, p.16]
7. **Use misses as diagnostic data.** Chart not just volume and percentage but direction of misses (short, long, left, right). This guides mechanical correction. [S5, p.14]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Shot charting is relevant for any position that takes field goal attempts. Post players should chart paint shots and mid-post jumpers; guards should chart all perimeter zones.

## Variations
### Makes-in-a-Row Format
Instead of (or in addition to) makes-out-of-attempts, track the longest consecutive make streak from each spot. This format sharpens focus and penalizes inconsistency more severely than a simple percentage. [S5, p.12]

### Timed Format
Add a time element: "How many makes from this spot in 2 minutes?" The next session, try to beat the previous score. This introduces game-speed urgency and keeps charting engaging. [S5, p.17]

### Game-Simulation Format
Chart shots from the exact locations and shot types a player takes in games (identified from film or scouting), so practice data directly predicts game performance.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Practicing without recording** → Without data, improvement is invisible and problems go undiagnosed. Always chart. [S5, p.11]
2. **Only practicing from strong spots** → Charts will reveal this bias. Force equal time (or more) in weaker zones. [S5, p.16]
3. **Setting goals that are too easy** → Goals should require focused effort to achieve. If you always hit your goal on the first try, raise the standard. [S5, p.12]
4. **Discarding chart notebooks** → Longitudinal data (weeks and months of charts) is far more valuable than any single session's numbers. Keep all records.
