# ACL Injury Management in Basketball

## Summary
Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) tears account for up to 64% of all knee injuries in basketball. The ACL is the primary restraint against anterior tibial translation (~86% of stability) and provides rotatory stability. For basketball athletes who wish to return to their prior level of play, surgical reconstruction is the standard of care, though a thorough preoperative rehabilitation (pre-hab) program is essential before surgery. Rehabilitation after reconstruction progresses through three phases — initial, intermediate, and final — before objective return-to-sport criteria are met [S10, Ch.30].

## When to Use
- Any basketball player who sustains a cutting or landing mechanism injury with immediate swelling, pop, and inability to continue
- Medical staff making graft selection and surgical timing decisions
- S&C coaches and athletic trainers designing post-ACL reconstruction return-to-play progressions

## Key Principles
1. ACL incidence: female basketball players 0.29/1000 AE; male 0.08/1000 AE [S10, p.351]
2. Non-contact mechanism is most common — pivoting while accelerating/decelerating or landing on heel with slight knee flexion [S10, p.352]
3. Surgical reconstruction is the mainstay for basketball athletes due to high translational forces and cutting/pivoting demands [S10, p.353]
4. Preoperative range of motion normalization is essential — preoperative stiffness closely correlates with postoperative ROM loss [S10, p.355-356]
5. Quadriceps strength deficit >20% preoperatively predicts persistent weakness after surgery — strengthen before operating [S10, p.356]
6. RTS is rarely before 6 months; 15× greater second ACL injury risk in the first 12 months post-reconstruction [S10, p.339]
7. Psychological factors significantly affect return-to-sport rate despite adequate functional recovery [S10, p.357]

## ACL Anatomy
- Composed of type I collagen (90%) and type III collagen (10%)
- Originates: posterior aspect of lateral femoral condyle
- Inserts: anterior to medial tibial eminence; average 31 ± 3 mm length
- Two bundles: anteromedial (taut in flexion), posterolateral (taut in extension)
- Ultimate strength: 1730–2500 Newtons
- Blood supply: middle geniculate artery; innervation: tibial nerve [S10, p.352]

## Diagnosis

### History
- Instability event during landing or cutting (non-contact most common)
- Audible/palpable pop with immediate swelling
- Unable to continue sports activity [S10, p.352]

### Physical Examination
- **Lachman test** (key test): knee at 30° flexion; anterior tibial translation >3 mm + soft end-point = positive; high sensitivity and specificity [S10, p.352]
- **Pivot shift test**: leg in extension with ankle internally rotated, apply valgus force and flex knee; subluxation then reduction of lateral tibia = positive; not very sensitive (24%) but extremely specific (98%); 74% sensitive under general anesthesia [S10, p.352]
- **Anterior drawer test**: knee at 90°, anterior force on tibia; less sensitive than Lachman; more useful for chronic tears [S10, p.352]
- Assess effusion (intra-articular pathology marker)

### Imaging
- X-ray: identifies bony abnormalities (Segond fracture = anterolateral ligament avulsion), tibial spine avulsion; does not show ligamentous injury
- MRI: confirms ligament injury, identifies associated pathology (MCL, lateral meniscus root tear, bone bruising on central lateral femoral condyle and posterior lateral tibial plateau); not required for diagnosis [S10, p.352-353]
- With chronic ACL tears: posterior horn medial meniscus becomes primary anterior translation restraint and may subsequently tear [S10, p.353]

## Management

### Conservative Management
- Limited role in basketball athletes due to high translational forces and cutting demands
- Pre-hab focus: normalize ROM, eliminate effusion, strengthen secondary stabilizers [S10, p.353]

### Surgical Management: Graft Selection

**Timing**: After preoperative rehabilitation and ROM normalization (usually 3–4 weeks) to decrease arthrofibrosis risk [S10, p.353]

**Autograft options** (preferred in basketball athletes):
- **Bone–patellar tendon–bone (BTB)**: bony incorporation faster, lower re-rupture rates; donor site morbidity (anterior knee pain); most commonly used in high-level basketball players [S10, p.354]
- **Hamstring**: higher tensile strength when quadrupled, lower donor site morbidity, slower bony incorporation [S10, p.354]
- **Quadriceps**: thick soft tissue option; risk of patella fracture/quad rupture as complications [S10, p.354]

**Allograft**: less commonly used in basketball athletes — higher re-rupture rate in young, active populations [S10, p.354]

### Surgical Technique
- Options: trans-tibial, anterior medial portal, two-incision, all-inside methods
- Literature has not demonstrated clinical differences between techniques [S10, p.355]
- Single-bundle or double-bundle reconstruction available; double-bundle shows no superior clinical results despite biomechanical advantages [S10, p.355]

### Fixation
- Graft fixation must withstand closed-chain exercises for at least 12 weeks [S10, p.355]
- Patellar bone blocks with interference screws: highest stiffness and fixation strength
- Screw placement must be parallel to bone block; >30° divergence increases pullout risk [S10, p.355]
- Soft tissue grafts: interference screws, screw/washer, suture posts, staples, cross-pins, buttons

## Rehabilitation Phases

### Pre-Hab (Preoperative)
- Goals: normalize ROM, eliminate effusion, strengthen secondary stabilizers
- Achieve near-normal ROM before surgery to prevent postoperative motion loss [S10, p.355]
- Address quadriceps weakness/atrophy preoperatively [S10, p.356]

### Phase 1 (Initial — Weeks 0–6 Post-Surgery)
- Quadriceps activation and full ROM restoration
- Knee extension to 0° by weeks 2–4
- Knee flexion to 120–130° by weeks 4–6
- Passive patellar mobilization by weeks 4–6
- Progress from isometric → concentric quadriceps exercises
- Closed kinetic chain exercises (leg press, squats, step-ups) at 4–6 weeks; then add open kinetic chain ~4–6 weeks post-surgery
- Balance boards for neuromuscular control [S10, p.356]

### Phase 2 (Intermediate — Weeks 6–12)
- Prerequisites: wound healed, effusion resolved, voluntary quad control, normal gait, extension ≥0°, flexion ≥120–130°
- Advance neuromuscular training: static → dynamic, add perturbation training
- Full ROM strength training (closed and open kinetic chain)
- Resume jogging at weeks 10–12 with correct form
- Initiate sport-specific training under close supervision [S10, p.356]

### Phase 3 (Final — Week 12+)
- Prerequisites: all CKC/OKC exercises pain-free; LSI >80% for quad and hamstring strength
- Intensify sport-specific and neuromuscular training
- Increase agility training
- Basketball-specific drills: ball chase drills, sprint-backpedal drills with/without dribbling, low-intensity cutting drills, single-leg squatting on tilt boards while passing [S10, p.357]
- Jump-landing training and plyometric exercises targeting basketball-specific ACL mechanisms

## Basketball-Specific Return-to-Play Criteria
Before a basketball player can safely return to sport (RTS), at minimum the following must be met [S10, p.357]:
1. No knee pain with basketball-related movements (running, jumping, pivoting, decelerating, cutting)
2. No knee buckling or apprehension during basketball-related movements
3. Normal and symmetric gait and running pattern
4. Limb Symmetry Index (LSI) >90% for both hamstring and quadriceps strength
5. LSI >90% for a hop test battery (vertical jump, hopping for distance, single-leg hop-and-hold, side hopping)
6. Drop jump testing: no valgus movement, symmetric knee flexion, upright and lateral truncal posture

## Common Mistakes
1. Operating before preoperative stiffness resolves → increased arthrofibrosis risk; wait for near-normal ROM [S10, p.355]
2. Using allograft in young, active basketball athlete → higher re-rupture rate [S10, p.354]
3. Advancing rehab by time points only without functional criteria → psychological readiness and strength symmetry must also be evaluated [S10, p.357]
4. Screw divergence >30° from bone block → increased pullout and fixation failure risk [S10, p.355]
5. Ignoring psychological readiness — psychological factors significantly decrease RTS rate despite adequate objective scores [S10, p.357]
