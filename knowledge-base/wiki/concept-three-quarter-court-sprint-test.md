# Three-Quarter Court Sprint Test

## Summary
The three-quarter court sprint test is a field sprint assessment that measures linear court speed for basketball athletes. The player sprints from one baseline to the three-quarter court mark (roughly three-quarter the length of the floor). It is a simple, basketball-specific measure of acceleration and top-end speed that is used in high school, NCAA, and NBA Combine settings.

Coaches use this test as part of a holistic assessment battery to establish baseline fitness, monitor progress, support roster decisions, and guide conditioning program design [S11].

## When to Use
- Pre-season baseline fitness screening
- Post off-season program evaluation
- Roster decisions / talent scouting
- Injury rehabilitation return-to-play benchmarking
- Comparing athletes to population norms by level

## Key Principles
1. Player starts at the baseline (start line); timer is positioned at the three-quarter-court finish line
2. Timing begins on first movement and stops when the player crosses the finish line
3. Perform multiple trials and record the best (fastest) time
4. Use a stopwatch or timing gates for accuracy; timing gates are preferred
5. Results are interpreted against level-specific normative data

## Setup
- Player (P) starts at the baseline
- Timer (T) stationed at the three-quarter-court finish line
- Measure and mark the finish line clearly with a cone
- Equipment: stopwatch or timing gates

## Coaching Application
- Guards consistently score faster than forwards and centers at every level — use position-specific norms when evaluating athletes
- NBA-level averages (3.29 sec) are faster than NCAA DI men's average (3.36 sec), confirming the test is sensitive to elite performance differences
- A high school male athlete under 3.06 sec is performing at the elite end of the high school spectrum
- Use this test alongside the [[concept-t-test-basketball|T-test]] and [[concept-vo2max-test-basketball|VO2max test]] for a complete physical profile

## Common Mistakes
1. Starting before the signal → ensure a consistent, standardized start command
2. Stopping before fully crossing the finish line → emphasize running through the line
3. Using a single trial → always take the best of at least 2-3 trials
4. Comparing across levels without position adjustment → use position-specific and level-specific norms
