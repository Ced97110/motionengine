# Lower Body Exercise Technique Fundamentals

## Summary
Basketball is a multiplanar sport requiring sprinting, jumping, shuffling, cutting, and crossover stepping. Lower body strength derived from resistance training helps athletes maintain proper mechanics, generate high power output, reduce injury risk, and sustain performance. Power may even be the primary quality for running and jumping [S11, Ch.6]. A well-balanced program must include multiplanar bilateral and unilateral lower body exercises.

All lower body exercises share common technical requirements: correct hand grip, stable body positioning, controlled movement range and speed, and proper breathing. These fundamentals apply across every squat, hinge, and lunge variation.

## When to Use
- Applies to every lower body resistance training session throughout the season
- Especially critical when introducing new athletes to barbell/dumbbell exercises
- Review fundamentals at the start of each training block or after a break in training

## Key Principles

### Hand Grips and Width
1. **Pronated (overhand) grip** — palms down, knuckles up
2. **Supinated (underhand) grip** — palms up, knuckles down
3. **Neutral grip** — knuckles pointing laterally, as in a handshake
4. **Alternating grip** — one hand pronated, one supinated (common in deadlifts for heavy loads)
5. **Hook grip** — pronated with thumb under the index and middle fingers (Olympic lifts)
- Common grip widths: shoulder-width and hip-width; wide = farther than shoulder/hip width; narrow = closer [S11, Ch.6]

### Body Positioning
- Stable position = **feet flat on the floor, neutral spine, chest up, shoulders back** [S11, Ch.6]
- Proximal stability (spine) and distal stability (floor contact) are both critical for basketball athletes
- Proper alignment places appropriate stress on muscles and joints while protecting the spine

### Movement Range and Speed
- All exercises should be performed through **full range of motion**
- Slow, controlled movements allow full ROM to be reached
- For power exercises: accelerate the bar to **maximal speed** while maintaining form
- Non-power lower body exercises improve muscle function and joint control through full ROM — with direct carryover to basketball performance [S11, Ch.6]

### Breathing Guidelines
- **Exhale through the sticking point** (most strenuous portion — typically the transition from eccentric to concentric)
- **Inhale during the less stressful phase** (lowering/eccentric)
- Use the **diaphragm**, not chest breathing — diaphragmatic breathing fills lungs fully
- **Valsalva maneuver** (expire against closed glottis): creates rigid fluid/air compartments in torso to support vertebral alignment under heavy loads — use with experienced athletes only; risks include dizziness, high blood pressure, blackouts [S11, Ch.6]
- Benefits of proper breathing: safer exercise, hernia prevention, reduced blood pressure spikes, less back pain, better blood flow, increased relaxation and focus

### Spotting Guidelines
- Spotter's sole responsibility = **athlete safety**
- For barbell exercises on the back/front: spot inside the rack with safety bars at appropriate height
- Spotter should be **at least as strong** as the athlete lifting
- Communication is critical: spotter must know when bar will be handled, how many reps, when athlete is ready
- Number of spotters depends on load, athlete experience, and spotter strength [S11, Ch.6]
- Spotting needs vary from athlete to athlete

## Common Mistakes
1. **Chest breathing instead of diaphragmatic** → teach belly breathing before adding load
2. **Using Valsalva with inexperienced athletes** → restrict to advanced, resistance-trained players only
3. **Partial range of motion** → use box/pad as depth guide; cue athlete to achieve parallel thighs
4. **Rounding the back under load** → reduce weight; cue "chest up, shoulders back"
5. **Poor spotter communication** → establish pre-set rep targets and a verbal ready signal
