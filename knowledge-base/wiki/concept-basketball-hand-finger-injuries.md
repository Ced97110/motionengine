# Basketball Hand and Finger Injuries

## Summary
The fingers and thumb represent the most likely site of acute orthopedic fractures in basketball players. A 2017 NBA study (Morse et al.) identified 137 hand injuries, with 71.5% occurring to the fingers and 28.5% to the hand. There was no relationship found between injury type and player position. Dominant and non-dominant hand function and dexterity are both critical to basketball performance, making these injuries particularly impactful [S10, pp.210-211].

## When to Use (Relevance)
- Pre-season screening for prior hand/finger injury history
- Sideline management when a player jams a finger, falls on an outstretched hand, or suffers a blow to the hand
- Return-to-play decision-making after hand surgery or fracture

## Key Injury Patterns

### 1. Proximal Interphalangeal (PIP) Joint Injuries
The PIP joint is the most frequently injured site in basketball. Injuries can include:
- Disruption of ligaments and tendons
- Intra-articular fractures
- Volar dislocation or forced flexion → acute rupture or chronic attenuation of the triangular ligament at the distal end of the central slip
- **Boutonniere deformity**: lateral bands migrate volarly → PIP flexion + DIP hyperextension [S10, p.210]

### 2. Distal Interphalangeal (DIP) Joint Injuries
- Terminal extensor tendon and FDP (flexor digitorum profundus) tendon injuries
- Fracture/dislocations
- **Mallet finger**: disruption of the terminal extensor tendon from the distal phalanx — commonly caused by the ball jamming the fingertip; more common in baseball but occurs in basketball [S10, p.210]
- **FDP avulsion**: forceful DIP hyperextension (e.g., finger caught on net) [S10, p.210]
- **Dunk lacerations**: hand impact with sharp rim edges or flange connecting rim to backboard [S10, p.210]

### 3. Metacarpophalangeal (MCP) Joint — Thumb UCL Injury
- Most common MCP injury in basketball
- **Mechanism**: abduction moment at the thumb MCP joint — falls onto outstretched hands with thumb abducted
- Results in ulnar collateral ligament (UCL) tear [S10, p.210]

### 4. Metacarpal and Phalangeal Fractures
- Second and third most common NBA hand injury pattern
- No position predilection [S10, p.210]

### 5. Carpometacarpal (CMC) Dislocations
- Dorsal dislocations more common on the ulnar side of the hand [S10, p.210]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Both dominant and non-dominant hand function are critical — dribbling, shooting, passing, and rebounding all depend on hand integrity

## Common Mistakes (Clinical)
1. **Ignoring PIP swelling** → Missing boutonniere deformity diagnosis — assess for central slip integrity
2. **Attempting to 'buddy tape' a mallet finger** and return immediately → risk of chronic deformity without proper splinting
3. **Missing FDP avulsion** — occurs when finger is caught on the net; treat as serious until imaging confirms
4. **Underestimating thumb UCL tears** — partial tears may allow play with taping; complete tears ("gamekeeper's thumb") require surgical consultation

## Return to Play Considerations
- Mallet finger: requires continuous splinting × 6–8 weeks; return with protective splint
- Boutonniere deformity: splinting of PIP in extension; surgical referral if acute rupture confirmed
- Thumb UCL: partial tears may be managed conservatively; complete tears require surgical repair before return
- Phalangeal/metacarpal fractures: individualized based on fracture stability, displacement, and position
- Dunk lacerations: repair and return once wound sealed and dexterity confirmed [S10, pp.210-211]
