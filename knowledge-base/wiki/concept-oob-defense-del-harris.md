# Defending Out-of-Bounds Plays (Del Harris)

## Summary
Del Harris presents a comprehensive system for defending all three out-of-bounds situations — sideline (SLOB), baseline (BLOB), and full-court. The emphasis is on denying two specific high-percentage passes in each situation, and then using the defense's numerical advantage (5 defenders vs. 4 inbounds receivers) to implement trick coverage when advantageous. Preparation and pre-planning are essential; the defense must have a diagrammed playbook of key opponent OOB plays before the game. [S7, pp.260-262]

## When to Use
- On every opponent inbound situation throughout the game
- Especially after time-outs when the opponent has likely called a specific play
- Late-game situations (last 30 seconds) require adjusted coverage — see Late-Game section

## Key Principles
1. Have an assistant coach diagram the opponent's key OOB plays before the game — especially late-game situations
2. Deny two specific passes in each OOB situation (see below)
3. The defense has a numerical advantage (5 vs. 4) — exploit it with trick coverages after time-outs
4. Any trick coverage must be pre-planned with a signal — never improvised

## Sideline Out-of-Bounds (SLOB) Defense

### Two Passes to Deny
1. **Direct pass into the low post** — X5 fronts/denies the low post receiver
2. **Pass down to the strong-side corner** — X3 (needs active hands) plays in the passing lane to the corner, preventing either a drop pass to the post or a catch-and-shoot three-pointer

### Trick Coverage Options After Time-Out
- **Shift to zone** on the first pass reception
- **Trap on the first pass below the foul line**
- **Trap when ball is passed to the weak side**
- **Switch certain or all players** on the initial action

### Inbounds Passer Techniques
- **Standard approach**: Pressure passer toward baseline for 2-3 counts, then jump off to face the 4 inbounds players to deny the first open man; defender must recover to own man when the ball comes in
- **Scouting approach**: Check the ball 2 counts, then quickly find the one player you most want to deny (e.g., the best shooter/scorer)
- **Lob situation (1-2 seconds left)**: Keep a taller player with active hands on the ball out of bounds the entire 5 seconds; prioritize covering the lob receiver

### Risk Warning
- Trapping the first pass is dangerous if the inbounds passer is a good shooter — after releasing the ball, he becomes dangerous as either a step-in shooter or a slice cutter

## Baseline Out-of-Bounds (BLOB) Defense

### Two Passes to Deny
1. **Direct pass under the goal** — inbounds passer's defender moves at an angle off the ball toward the basket; uses "see ball and action behind" position with active hands; also prevents easy weak-side corner pass
2. **Easy catch-and-shoot in the strong-side corner** — where most inbounds passes go

### Coverage Options for Strong-Side Corner
- **Option A**: Man-to-man, body up, switch in emergency only
- **Option B**: Put a smaller defender outside a stack or pick, another in the lane — they switch; the outside defender passes off the first cutter and retreats to cover the corner area (Figure 16.13)
- **Option C**: Defender on ball checks the middle of the lane, switches out to the corner; the lane defender (X3) switches into X1's original position — pre-determined or communicated between X1 and X3 (Figure 16.14)

### Zone vs. Man Under the Basket
- Zone can work at high school/college level but becomes risky at higher skill levels — overload or draw-and-cut can produce quick layups

## Full-Court Out-of-Bounds Defense

### Standard Coverage
- Team's existing press/zone/half-court philosophy generally dictates this

### Short Seconds Remaining: "Centerfield" Method
- Inbounds passer's defender backs up to **half-court area** to play "centerfield"
- Watches ball and follows the LONG pass to help the defender contain the receiver and prevent a shot
- If a short pass is made first, ignores it and waits for the LONG pass — double-teams on air time
- If receiver drives upcourt for a quick shot, gives immediate help

### Alternative: Pressure the Passer
- Some teams pressure the passer with 1 or 2 men the entire 5 seconds
- Most effective when only 1-2 seconds remain and the lob is the primary concern
- Requires the tallest available player with active hands on the ball

## Common Mistakes
1. **No scouting of OOB plays** → The two most likely late-game plays should be diagrammed; don't let them catch you unprepared
2. **Inbounds defender ignoring the passer** → After the ball comes in, the passer becomes a dangerous scorer — get back to him immediately
3. **Tricking without a signal** → Any trick coverage (zone shift, first-pass trap) must have a pre-designated call; chaos results otherwise
4. **Zone under the basket at high levels** → A smart offense will overload or cut through the zone for an easy layup; use man-to-man for skilled opponents
