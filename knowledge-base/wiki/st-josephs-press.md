# St. Joseph's Press (3-1-1 Half-Court Zone Trap)

## Summary
The St. Joseph's Press is a deceptive full-to-half-court press that fakes a three-quarter-court man-to-man press before quickly dropping back into the front court and attacking from a 3-1-1 half-court zone trap alignment once the ball crosses mid-court. Developed by Jack Ramsey at St. Joseph's University and adapted by Ed Krinsky and Herb Brown, it was used when **either guard scored** — field goal or free throw — as the personnel-keyed defensive trigger. [S1, pp.183-184]

## When to Use
- After either guard scores (field goal or free throw) — personnel-keyed trigger
- When you want to create a half-court trapping situation while disguising it as a full-court press
- To make the offense commit to a ball-handler's penetration before the trap springs
- When channeling the ball to the corner is a viable tactic given the opponent's personnel

## Formation (Half-Court 3-1-1 Zone Trap)
- **3 (High)**: Three perimeter defenders high, keying on the ball and wings
- **1 (Middle)**: Defender at the free-throw line fronts the low post; protects the high post entry
- **1 (Baseline)**: Baseline defender covers weak-side block and weak-side wing; also the corner trapper

## Key Principles
1. **Fake the three-quarter-court man-to-man press.** The initial appearance is full-court man pressure — the offense believes they are in a full-court press. [S1, p.183]
2. **Drop quickly into the front court.** Before the ball crosses half-court, all five defenders retreat to their 3-1-1 positions. [S1, p.183]
3. **Jump the ball at half-court.** Once the ball crosses the mid-court line, attack from the 3-1-1 alignment. [S1, p.183]
4. **Channel to the corner.** The primary scheme is to force the ball to the corner, then trap the corner with the baseline defender and the nearest wing. [S1, pp.183-184]
5. **Middle defender (free-throw line) fronts the low post.** Prevents the easy entry to the post. [S1, p.184]
6. **Two high defenders protect the nail and elbow.** After the trap springs, the remaining high defenders cover the free-throw line area and the weak-side block to cut off escape passes. [S1, p.184]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG/SF (3 high)**: Initially mimic man-to-man press, then retreat and fill the three high positions; one wing covers the near sideline and helps initiate the corner trap
- **PF (middle/free-throw line)**: Front the low post; protect the high-post entry; defend nail and elbow after trap springs
- **C (baseline)**: Cover weak-side baseline away from the ball; trap the corner with the near wing once ball is channeled there

## Variations
### Without Inbounds Pressure (Soft Entry)
Do not pressure the inbounds pass at all — let the ball enter freely, then jump it at mid-court as a surprise. [S1, p.184]

### Double-Team the Inbounds Passer (Baseline OOB)
When defending a baseline out-of-bounds, double-team the inbounds passer with both the C and PF. The inbounds passer cannot move his feet laterally without traveling. Guards drop all the way under the basket to intercept high passes or deflect them down court. [S1, pp.184-185]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Defenders not getting back into 3-1-1 positions fast enough** → the deception fails; offense gets a clear read on the zone before mid-court
2. **Middle defender abandoning the low-post front** → allows easy post entry that defeats the entire trap scheme
3. **Corner trap timing off** → both the wing and the baseline defender must arrive simultaneously; late trappers leave the corner shooter open
4. **High defenders sinking on the trap** → they must hold at nail/elbow to cut off the skip pass out of the trap
