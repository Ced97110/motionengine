# Harness Pull

## Objective
Increase acceleration speed by being towed faster than normal running conditions using a partner harness-and-handle system.

## Setup
- 2 players connected by a harness-and-handle system (commercially available speed harness)
- The pulling partner runs at approximately half the towed athlete's speed
- Open flat surface — field, track, or gym

## Execution
1. Both athletes attach to the harness system — towed athlete in back, pulling partner in front holding the handles.
2. At signal, the pulling partner begins running forward, towing the other athlete.
3. The towed athlete runs at a speed **faster than normal unassisted conditions**, using the pull to experience supramaximal velocity.
4. Run for the desired distance (typically 15–30 yards).
5. Rest fully; switch roles.

## Coaching Points
- The goal is to expose the nervous system to speeds above what the athlete can produce alone — this is "overspeed" training.
- Towed athlete must maintain proper sprint mechanics even at elevated speed — do not just "get dragged."
- Arm action remains quick and controlled; leg turnover matches the assisted speed.
- The pulling partner controls the level of assistance — move faster to increase tow force.
- This drill is best used after an athlete has developed a strength base so technique holds at high speeds.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Short tow (10–15 yards), slow-moderate partner speed
2. **Intermediate**: 20-yard tow, partner at near-full speed
3. **Advanced**: 25–30 yard tow, partner at full sprint — maximum overspeed exposure
