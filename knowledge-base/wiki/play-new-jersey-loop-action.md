# NBA Loop Action

## Overview
The NBA "Loop" is a classic early-offense action typically used after made free throws. It brings the best shooter off staggered screens set by the 4 and 5. The New Jersey Nets (John Calipari) and Milwaukee Bucks (George Karl) each adapted the basic loop with variations, including a post-up continuation. The shooters — Kendall Gill/Kerry Kittles for New Jersey, Ray Allen for Milwaukee — are masters at reading the defense off the screens to choose between a jump shot, catch-and-drive, or pull-up. [S14, pp.41-43]

## Formation
- **1** dribbles to initiate (left side, penetrating toward the wing)
- **2** (shooter) is on the right block or baseline, working off staggered screens
- **4** at the low baseline (first screener)
- **5** on the mid-block or lane (second screener)
- **3** clears to the ball-side corner

## Phases

### Phase 1: Staggered Screen Cut (Basic Loop)
- **1** dribbles to the left side, setting up a passing angle to the right high-wing area
- **2** cuts off the **staggered screens set by 5 and 4** (or 4 and 5, depending on alignment)
- **2** cuts to the **top of the key**
- **3** clears to the ball-side corner (giving **2** space to drive if jump shot is denied)
- **1** passes to **2** for the shot or drive

### Phase 2: Two-Read for Shooter (Calipari Variation)
- **If the defense chases 2 hard to the top**: **2** catches in a cleared side for a quick **catch-and-drive**
- **If the defender takes the shortcut** (cuts off 2 through the weak side of the screens): **2** raises up for the **jump shot** off the stagger

<!-- DIAGRAM: Fig. 4.2 — Calipari variation: 2 cuts to ball side of screens (not weak side). Shooters (Gill/Kittles) read defender to choose shot or drive. 3 in ball-side corner. -->

### Phase 3: Milwaukee Post-Up Continuity (Karl Variation)

#### Option 1: Post-Up for 5
- After **1** passes to **2** at the top:
  - **4** (who set the second screen for 2) continues into the lane and **sets a pick for 5**
  - **5** curls back across the lane to the **right block**
  - **2** looks to make the **low post-feed to 5**

<!-- DIAGRAM: Fig. 4.3 — 2 at top after loop catch. 4 sets lane pick for 5. 5 curls to right block. 2 feeds 5 in post. -->

#### Option 2: Reversal Post-Up for 4 (When 5 Is Denied)
- If **5** is denied in the post:
  - **2** swings to **1**
  - **1** reverses to **3**
  - **3** passes inside to **4**, who has established a **post-up position on the left block**

<!-- DIAGRAM: Fig. 4.4 — Reversal chain: 2→1→3→4 (left block). 5 and 4 positions from the loop continuity. -->

## Key Coaching Points
- The **loop timing** is critical — the screeners must be set when the ball handler begins his dribble
- **Calipari's key adjustment**: having shooters cut to the **ball side** of the screens (rather than weak side) lets them read the defense and either shoot immediately or attack the cleared side [S14, p.43]
- The **double-read** off the stagger (shoot vs. drive) is what makes great shooters dangerous in this action
- **Karl's post continuation** adds a third phase that makes the defense work on both the perimeter and the interior
- If the defender chases hard over the top, the ball side is cleared for an uncontested drive

## Counters
- Hard chase → catch-and-drive on cleared side
- Shortcut/cheat → shooter raises up for jump shot
- 5 denied in post → reverse to 4 on left block
