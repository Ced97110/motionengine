# Front Foot to Free Foot

## Objective
Teach the simplest of the three on-ball defensive stances — the front-foot-to-free-foot overplay — and develop reactive footwork against all four attacker options: jab step, jab-step pull-back, jab-step crossover, and reverse pivot.

## Setup
- Half court, free-throw line area (same setup as Drill #129)
- Groups of 3: player 1 (offense), X1 (defense), end of line
- Rotation: offense → defense → end of line
- Coach designates pivot foot
- Duration: 3 minutes

## Execution
1. Player 1 breaks to the free-throw line; coach designates pivot foot.
2. X1 rolls ball to 1. X1 places front foot near 1's **free foot**, overplayed **half a body** to that side (the overplay).
3. Player 1 performs the following in order:
   - Jab step
   - Jab-step pull-back
   - Jab-step crossover
   - Reverse pivot
4. Observe X1's footwork:
   - **Jab step**: X1 does not react unless 1's free foot gets outside the overplay; if it does, X1 may use a slight swing step
   - **Jab-step pull back**: X1 does not react — no uncontested jump shot available under tight coverage
   - **Jab-step crossover**: X1 uses a slight slide step + slight retreat step; X1's first step matches the attacker's first step; X1 moves right foot slightly right, then slides left foot for balance
   - **Reverse pivot**: same foot movement as the jab-step crossover — slight slide + retreat; attacker's back is to the basket (no shot available); X1 only needs a half-step slide to prevent the drive
5. Repeat until all footwork is perfect.

## Coaching Points
1. **This is the simplest stance** — "even beginners have no trouble understanding and using this stance" [S15, p.270] — because the attacker is forced to put the ball on the floor to attack
2. Overplay must be **at least half a body** to the free-foot side — this eliminates the drive to the free-foot side AND (with tight coverage) the jump shot
3. The **only achievable advantage** for the attacker is a move to their jab-step crossover or reverse-pivot direction — but the defender's foot is already back, so neither gains advantage
4. **Biggest disadvantage**: defender must balance between too tight (attacker's free foot can clear the overplay for an unstoppable drive) and too loose (easy jump shot); "the only logical stance is the overplay on the side of the free foot" [S15, p.269]
5. Usually overplay **right-handed players to their right** and left-handed players to their left
6. Defender's head must stay level throughout all footwork adjustments
7. Body position identical to #129: deep crouch, palms near floor, arms wide, torso slightly forward

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Walk through each move type slowly; correct foot position after each
2. **Intermediate**: Game-speed sequence with coach calling moves
3. **Advanced**: Live 1-on-1 from triple-threat position with overplay established
