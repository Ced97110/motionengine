# Elbow Complex

## Summary
The elbow complex is the middle link of the shooting chain. Its job in the jump shot is to hinge cleanly under the ball, deliver the hand to the release point, and decelerate after the snap. Unlike the shoulder (a ball-and-socket) or the wrist (a small multi-joint complex), the elbow is mechanically simple — it is a hinge that flexes and extends in one plane.

Because the elbow is simple, "elbow flair" — the visible drift of the elbow laterally away from the ball-and-rim line — is almost never an elbow problem. It is a downstream symptom of either restricted shoulder external rotation ([[concept-anatomy-shoulder-girdle]]) or undertrained scapular stabilizers. Fix proximal; the elbow falls into line.

## When to Use
This anatomy matters to coaching decisions involving:
- Jump-shot release alignment (the "elbow under the ball" coaching cue)
- Lateral alignment variance shot-to-shot (elbow drift is the visible signal)
- Deceleration after release (the elbow is the brake on the follow-through)
- Pushing actions: closeout contests, post hooks, jump-pass mechanics
- Recovery from the rare overuse injury (medial / lateral epicondylitis)

## Key Principles
1. **The elbow is a downstream signal, not a source.** When you see flair, look up the chain to the shoulder. Cuing "tuck the elbow" without addressing shoulder mobility produces a shot that feels strained and fatigues earlier.
2. **The triceps decelerate the follow-through.** Strong triceps = clean stop after release. Weak triceps = "whippy" release that varies arc shot-to-shot. Train triceps for ENDURANCE more than max strength.
3. **Train alignment under fatigue, not in fresh sets.** Most elbow alignment failures show up after 200+ shots in a session. Endurance work — extended sets at moderate load — builds the alignment that holds late.
4. **The elbow is the third hinge.** Force flows: legs → core → shoulder → elbow → wrist. A strong elbow can't compensate for weakness above. See [[concept-anatomy-glute-max]] for the source of the shooting motion's force.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG / SG**: highest shot volume; alignment endurance is the limiter
- **SF**: catch-and-shoot windows demand instant elbow alignment from a moving start
- **PF / C**: post-hook mechanics + putback finishes load the elbow asymmetrically; close attention to alignment under contact

## Variations
### Humeroulnar Joint
The primary hinge — between the trochlea of the humerus and the trochlear notch of the ulna. Pure flexion and extension. The alignment-control joint.

### Humeroradial Joint
Between the capitulum of the humerus and the head of the radius. Allows the radius to glide and rotate, contributing to forearm pronation / supination.

### Proximal Radioulnar Joint
The pivot at the upper end of the forearm, where the radial head rotates within the radial notch. Together with the distal radioulnar (in the wrist complex), this controls forearm rotation — the rotation that puts spin on the ball.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Cuing "tuck the elbow" as the only fix for flair** → masks the real problem (shoulder mobility); shooter feels strained and fatigues fast. Correction: shoulder external-rotation mobility + scapular stability work upstream.
2. **Locking the elbow at release** → no deceleration; arm "snaps" and high recoil. Correction: hold the follow-through; train the triceps for controlled decelerator endurance.
3. **Asymmetric strength between flexors and extensors** → bicep dominance pulls the elbow into excessive flexion at release; triceps weakness lets it swing through. Correction: 1:1 training ratio between rows / curls and dips / pushups for shooters.
