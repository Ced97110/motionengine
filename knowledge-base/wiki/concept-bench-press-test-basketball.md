# Bench Press Test for Basketball — Normative Data

## Summary
The bench press maximum-repetition test is a standard upper-body strength assessment for basketball athletes. The test measures how many repetitions an athlete can complete at a fixed load (typically 185 lb / 84 kg for men). Normative data are available from the National Basketball Conditioning Coaches Association (NBCCA) for high school and NCAA Division I athletes, and from the NBA Combine for professional prospects (2000–2020 seasons, n=1,081) [S11].

Upper-body pushing strength is important in basketball for post play, absorbing contact, securing rebounds, and defending dribble penetration. The bench press test provides a reliable, standardized measurement for tracking development over time.

## When to Use
- Pre-season and post-season strength assessments
- Comparing an athlete's upper-body strength to position peers
- Identifying athletes who need prioritized upper-body strength work
- Monitoring a strength development program's effectiveness over time

## Key Principles
1. **Standardize load and grip width** — use 185 lb (84 kg) for men; women typically use a percentage of bodyweight; grip should be consistent
2. **Full range of motion required** — bar touches chest on every rep; lock out at the top
3. **No bouncing the bar** — controlled eccentric, no elastic rebound from the chest
4. **Spot every attempt** — safety is non-negotiable, especially near maximum effort
5. **Do not confuse strength and power** — the bench press test measures strength endurance; explosive pushing power requires different testing

## Common Mistakes
1. Allowing partial reps → enforce full ROM; misrepresents true strength
2. Testing after a heavy upper-body session → test in a rested state to get valid numbers
3. Ignoring female norms — female basketball athletes have different absolute strength profiles; use sex-specific normative tables
