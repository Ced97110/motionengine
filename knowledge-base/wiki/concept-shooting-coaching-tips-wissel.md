# Coaching Tips for Shooting (Wissel)

## Summary
Hal Wissel closes Chapter 1 with specific coaching tips for shooting instructors — a player-first, relationship-based approach that prioritizes individual needs over generic instruction. The central insight: coaches should teach players to coach themselves, not just absorb what the coach knows. Brief, inspiring instruction beats long detailed lectures because players improve by shooting, not by listening [S7, p.16].

## When to Use
- Before beginning any individual shooting instruction session
- When building a shooting program for a team or camp
- When a player is resistant to coaching or in a confidence crisis
- When designing individual player development plans

## Key Principles
1. **Get to know the player first** — listen more than talk; build trust and comfort before correcting mechanics; start with what the player is doing well [S7, p.16]
2. **Coach individually** — don't teach a player everything you know; teach what that specific player needs to improve their shot [S7, p.16]
3. **Encourage two-way communication** — ask players what they like and don't like; listen to their answers to understand their confidence level and self-awareness [S7, p.16]
4. **Ask the right questions**: "When you're shooting well, what are you doing?" — answers reveal confidence level and self-knowledge. "I just shoot!" = automatic/confident or unaware. "My shoulders go toward the basket" = good self-understanding [S7, p.16]
5. **Keep it simple** — brief, straightforward, inspiring instruction; long detailed lectures cause players to lose interest; players improve by shooting, not listening [S7, p.16]
6. **Maintain high confidence levels** — tell players "You are a shooter!"; never let them believe they'll achieve less than success [S7, p.16]
7. **Teach self-coaching** — "I'm here to coach and help you, but you're going to learn to coach yourself" [S7, p.16]
8. **Don't take resistance personally** — some players don't want help; success with one player attracts others [S7, p.16]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Overloading with information** → Keep instruction to one or two mechanics; players can't absorb everything at once
2. **Talking more than shooting** → Get players shooting as quickly as possible; instruction time comes at the expense of repetitions
3. **Ignoring the player's perspective** → Ask questions first; understand what the player believes before imposing external corrections
4. **Starting with weakness** → Begin with strengths to build trust and confidence before addressing corrections
