# STAR Shoulder Rehabilitation Framework for Basketball

## Summary
Shoulder rehabilitation in basketball is challenging due to the extreme ranges of motion and angular velocities demanded of the glenohumeral joint during sport. The glenohumeral joint has minimal bony congruency and relies heavily on the rotator cuff and surrounding musculature for dynamic stabilization. This chapter applies the **Staged Approach for Rehabilitation of Shoulder Disorders (STAR)** — a criterion-based, irritability-stratified rehabilitation framework — to basketball athletes returning from shoulder injury or surgery [S10, Ch.54].

The STAR framework classifies athletes into three irritability stages (**High, Moderate, Low**) based on pain intensity, pain timing during motion, and disability level. All interventions are matched to irritability stage and progression is criterion-based, not time-based [S10, p.688].

## When to Use
- Acute shoulder injuries during basketball (e.g., rebounding contact, landing on outstretched arm)
- Post-surgical shoulder rehabilitation
- Any shoulder condition causing pain, limited ROM, or reduced function
- Screening for readiness to return to basketball activities

## Key Principles
1. **Irritability drives all decisions** — Classify the athlete's irritability first; match every intervention to the current stage [S10, p.687]
2. **Criterion-based progression** — Progress when the athlete meets objective criteria, not on a fixed timeline [S10, p.687]
3. **Pain monitoring model** — Use a 0–10 pain scale and RPE scale to guide acceptable pain allowance during exercise [S10, p.689]
4. **Continual reassessment** — Reassess irritability continuously to detect changes and maximize rehabilitation efficiency [S10, p.689]
5. **Maintain conditioning** — Avoid deconditioning by using cross-training (cycling, pool, elliptical, running) even in high irritability stages [S10, p.693]
6. **Compensations are signals** — Do not progress into overhead or sport-specific motions if neuromuscular compensations are present [S10, p.691]
7. **Shared decision-making** — Collaboration between clinicians, athletes, and coaches is required for safe integration into sport [S10, p.687]

## Irritability Classification System

| | High Irritability | Moderate Irritability | Low Irritability |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pain** | ≥7/10 | 4–6/10 | ≤3/10 |
| **Night/Rest Pain** | Consistent | Intermittent | Absent |
| **ROM** | Pain before end range; AROM < PROM | AROM ≅ PROM | AROM = PROM |
| **Disability** | High | Moderate | Low |
| **Intervention Focus** | Minimize stress; activity modification; monitor impairments | Mild-moderate stress; address impairments; basic functional restoration | Moderate-high stress; address impairments; high-demand functional restoration |

[S10, p.688 — Table 54.1, reprinted from McClure et al.]

## Phases of Rehabilitation

### Early Phase (High → Moderate Irritability)

**Goals**: Reduce pain, restore sleep, restore basic functional arm use (ADL)

**Interventions**:
- Activity modification, relative rest from provoking activities
- Immobilization only if indicated by surgical restriction or severity
- Contralateral arm exercise to minimize disuse and improve ROM if direct arm use is contraindicated
- **Electrical stimulation** for pain management:
  - Sensory TENS (200 µs pulse, 10–100 pps, 30 s on, 0 s off, 15 min): general pain management
  - Motor TENS (400 µs pulse, 2 pps, 45 s on, 15 s off, 15 min): soft tissue/muscle source pain
  - Noxious (400 µs pulse, 50 pps, 12 s on, 8 s off, 10 min): ligament/tendon pain [S10, p.688 — Table 54.2]
- Gentle ROM exercises with short hold times (≤5 s passive/active assisted)
- Low-grade Kalteborn or Maitland Grade I–II joint mobilizations [S10, p.689 — Table 54.3]
- Closed chain, axial loading, or supported arm movements when open chain is painful:
  - Stair railing slides
  - Quadruped weight-shifting
  - Wall slides
- Graded stress in non-painful planes/ranges

**Aerobic Maintenance**: Ergonomic cycling intervals immediately; progress to jogging/shuttle intervals as irritability reduces [S10, p.693]

**Criteria to Progress from Early to Middle Phase**:
- Resisted isometric testing: Low to moderate irritability classification
- Joint mobility: Low irritability or stiff/painful with Grade III–IV mobilizations
- ROM: Low irritability at end ranges; capable of AROM with low irritability
- Soft tissue irritability to palpation: Low to absent [S10, p.693]

### Middle Phase (Moderate → Low Irritability)

**Goals**: Resolve acute impairments, restore full ROM, build isotonic strength

**Interventions**:
- **Isometrics** at moderate irritability — use RPE scale to track progress
  - Criterion to progress isometrics to isotonics: ≤3/10 pain with resisted isometric testing (low irritability) [S10, p.689]
- Isotonic abduction and external rotation at lower angles
- PNF/manual resistance techniques for restricted ranges
- Rhythmic stabilization / alternating isometrics for internal rotation/flexion limitations [S10, p.689 — Fig. 54.1]
- Active ROM in various planes around the painful arc [S10, p.689 — Fig. 54.2]
- Progression options for painful shoulder abduction [S10, p.690 — Table 54.4]:
  - A: ABD above or below painful arc → progress loads toward arc
  - B: ABD isometrics at painful arc → progress intensity or add perturbation
  - C: Eccentrics through painful arc → progress loads
  - D: Progress loads in scapular plane → reduce weight and progress toward ABD plane
  - E: Perform horizontal ABD plane at angle of pain
- Soft tissue treatment (Motor TENS, manual techniques) for specific tender structures limiting progress [S10, p.690]
- Support-assisted open kinetic chain exercises (wall, dowels, tables, bands, therapist assist):
  - Active assistive flexion with dowel [S10, p.691 — Fig. 54.3]
  - Active assistive flexion with ball [S10, p.691 — Fig. 54.4]
  - Eccentric lowering (wall-assisted) [S10, p.692 — Fig. 54.5]
  - Band-assisted shoulder abduction [S10, p.692 — Fig. 54.6]
- External rotation bias flexion (bands around wrists) to reduce pain during flexion ROM [S10, p.693 — Fig. 54.7]

### Late Phase (Low Irritability → Return to Sport)

**Goals**: Restore high-demand functional activities, sport-specific shoulder performance

**Interventions**:
- Full range isotonic strengthening
- Plyometric shoulder progressions (medicine ball throws, etc.)
- Sport-specific overhead mechanics
- Progressive return to basketball activities (passing, shooting, rebounding)

## Joint Mobilization Guidelines by Irritability Stage

| | High | Moderate | Low |
|---|---|---|---|
| Joint Mobilizations | Grade I–II / empty feels | Grade II–III / stiff & painful | Grade III–IV / stiff & limited mobility |
| Passive/AAROM | ≤5 s hold | 5–15 s hold | >15 s if ROM limited |
| Active ROM | Performed in low-to-mod irritability arc | Performed in low-to-mod irritability arc | Performed with increased loads and/or volume |

[S10, p.689 — Table 54.3]

## Conditioning Maintenance During Rehabilitation
Shooting technique changes occur with physical fatigue; maintaining aerobic capacity is critical [S10, p.693]:
- **High irritability**: Ergonomic cycling intervals
- **Moderate irritability**: Progress to jogging, shuttle intervals
- **Low irritability**: Return to basketball-specific conditioning

Options: cycling, pool conditioning, elliptical machine, stair master, running (as tolerated)

## Common Mistakes
1. **Time-based progression** → Use criterion-based progression only; the athlete must meet objective markers [S10, p.687]
2. **Skipping to overhead work too soon** → Compensations or moderate irritability contraindicate overhead and sport-specific motion [S10, p.691]
3. **Failing to maintain conditioning** → Deconditioning during shoulder rehab sets back the full return-to-performance timeline [S10, p.693]
4. **Not addressing soft tissue restrictions** → Tender structures (e.g., infraspinatus) must be directly treated if limiting exercise progression [S10, p.690]
5. **Inadequate communication with coaching staff** → Medical-coaching communication directly impacts training availability and injury outcomes [S10, p.682]
