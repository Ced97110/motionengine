# PRP Classification Systems

## Summary
Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) is not a single product — it is a family of preparations with dramatically different biological compositions depending on preparation technique, centrifugation protocol, and commercial system used. The lack of standardization is the primary reason for conflicting results in PRP research. Understanding the classification systems is essential for interpreting orthobiologics literature and making evidence-based clinical decisions in basketball sports medicine [S10, pp.607-608].

## Key Principles

### What Is PRP?
1. PRP is harvested autologous blood concentrated via centrifuge to contain 1.5–9× the platelet concentration of baseline serum, or >1 million platelets/mL [S10, p.606].
2. Platelet activation releases growth factors: VEGF, PDGF, FGF, ILGF-1, interleukin-1β, interleukin-10, TNF-β [S10, p.606].
3. These mediators provide anti-inflammatory effects, stimulate matrix synthesis, collagen production, angiogenesis, cell proliferation, and cell differentiation [S10, pp.606-607].

### Classification System 1: Dohan Ehrenfest (most comprehensive)
4. Based on **cell content (leukocytes)** and **fibrin architecture** — four main families:
   - **P-PRP (LP-PRP)**: No leukocytes, low-density fibrin network — *preferred for cartilage and most soft tissue applications*
   - **L-PRP (LR-PRP)**: With leukocytes, low-density fibrin network — *shown to be harmful for chondral repair*
   - **P-PRF**: No leukocytes, high-density fibrin network
   - **L-PRF**: With leukocytes, high-density fibrin network [S10, p.608]

### Classification System 2: Mishra Classification
5. Based on leukocyte presence, platelet activation, and platelet concentration — four types:
   - **Type 1 PRP**: L-PRP solution (leukocyte-rich, not activated)
   - **Type 2 PRP**: L-PRP gel (leukocyte-rich, activated)
   - **Type 3 PRP**: P-PRP solution (leukocyte-poor, not activated)
   - **Type 4 PRP**: P-PRP gel (leukocyte-poor, activated)
   - Each subtype A (≥5× blood platelet concentration) or B (<5× blood platelet concentration) [S10, p.608]

### Classification System 3: PAW Classification
6. Based on **P**latelet absolute count, **A**ctivation method, and **W**hite cell (leukocyte) content — most commonly used in sports medicine research [S10, p.608].

### Critical Clinical Distinction: LP-PRP vs. LR-PRP
7. **LR-PRP** (leukocyte-rich) creates a LESS ideal environment for chondral repair — expresses pro-inflammatory markers and catabolic cytokines [S10, p.607].
8. **LP-PRP** (leukocyte-poor) is preferred for cartilage injuries and for high ankle sprains based on current evidence [S10, pp.594, 607].
9. For muscle injuries, **platelet-poor plasma (PPP)** or LP-PRP with platelets REMOVED (second spin) induces myoblast differentiation (regeneration) — standard PRP induces proliferation only [S10, p.596].

### Why Results Conflict in the Literature
10. Different commercial PRP systems produce dramatically different platelet, leukocyte, and growth factor concentrations in the final product [S10, p.608].
11. Many studies lack transparency in reporting preparation techniques — makes study-to-study comparison difficult.
12. Further variables: number of injections, timing of doses, injection volume — all affect outcomes and are not yet standardized [S10, p.608].

## Practical Application for Basketball Medical Staff

| Injury Type | Preferred Formulation | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| ACL reconstruction (graft maturation) | LP-PRP | Moderate (MRI improvement) |
| ACL partial tear (healing) | PRGF/LP-PRP | Low-moderate |
| High ankle sprain (AITFL) | LP-PRP | Moderate (2 RCTs) |
| Lateral ankle sprain (acute) | NOT recommended | Negative RCT |
| MCL injury | LP-PRP (caution on dose) | Low |
| Muscle injury (significant) | PPP/LP-PRP (platelet-removed) | Low-preclinical |
| UCL injury (partial, conservative-refractory) | LP-PRP | Moderate |
| Cartilage injury | LP-PRP | Moderate (younger patients) |

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating all PRP as equivalent** → the type of PRP (LP vs LR, activated vs non-activated, concentration) dramatically affects outcomes [S10, pp.607-608].
2. **Using LR-PRP for cartilage injuries** → pro-inflammatory cytokines in LR-PRP are catabolic to chondrocytes [S10, p.607].
3. **Ignoring the second-spin distinction for muscle injuries** → standard PRP (unseparated) promotes proliferation, not differentiation; platelet-removed PPP promotes regeneration [S10, p.596].
