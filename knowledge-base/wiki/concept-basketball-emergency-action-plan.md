# Basketball Emergency Action Plan (EAP)

## Summary
Every location used for basketball practice or competition must have a venue-specific Emergency Action Plan (EAP) in place before any sporting activity begins. The EAP is a comprehensive, pre-established protocol for every foreseeable emergency scenario — from cardiac arrest to spinal cord injury. It specifies AED locations, EMS entry routes, transportation methods, destination hospitals, and the role of every personnel member. The athletic trainer or athletic director oversees EAP coordination in conjunction with the team physician and local EMS [S10, p.89].

## When to Use
- Established before the first practice of every season at every venue
- Reviewed and rehearsed with all medical personnel before competition begins
- Activated immediately when any emergency occurs on the court, in the locker room, or in transit

## Key Principles
1. **Every venue needs its own EAP**: A plan at the home arena does not cover away venues — each location requires a specific plan
2. **Sudden cardiac arrest = SCD until proven otherwise**: If a player collapses and is unresponsive, assume SCD and activate AED + CPR immediately [S10, p.88]
3. **Early defibrillation is the most important factor**: The AED must be readily accessible — not locked away — at all practices and games
4. **Rehearse before the season**: Skill retention for rarely used emergency protocols deteriorates within months; annual refreshers are mandatory [S10, p.88]
5. **Spinal cord injury readiness**: All venues must have spinal stabilization/transport equipment available and all personnel must be trained on its use
6. **EAP covers non-life-threatening events too**: Minor injuries, illness, and transfers are also part of the EAP framework

## EAP Components

### Mandatory EAP Elements
- **AED location**: Exact position of defibrillator relative to the court
- **EMS entry point**: Specific gate/door/route for ambulance access to the playing court
- **Transportation method**: Ambulance specifications, backup options
- **Destination hospital**: Pre-designated facility with relevant capabilities (e.g., cardiac catheterization lab)
- **Personnel roles**: Who calls EMS, who initiates CPR/AED, who guides EMS to patient, who accompanies athlete
- **Facility maps and diagrams**: Routes from court to medical room, from medical room to EMS access
- **Emergency contact list**: All key personnel phone numbers
- **Spinal injury protocol**: Equipment location, stabilization technique, transport procedure

### Scenario-Specific Protocols Required
- Sudden cardiac arrest (SCA/SCD)
- Traumatic spinal cord injury
- Severe laceration / hemorrhage
- Concussion / head trauma
- Fracture / dislocation
- Severe allergic reaction / anaphylaxis
- Heat illness / hyponatremia
- Eye injury
- Dental avulsion

## Personnel Responsibilities
- **Athletic Trainer / Athletic Director**: Oversees EAP coordination, ensures all personnel are oriented, leads rehearsals
- **Team Physician**: Co-develops EAP with ATC and local EMS; provides medical direction for all protocols
- **All Medical Personnel**: Must be oriented to and rehearse every foreseeable EAP scenario; complete annual CPR/AED refresher training
- **Local EMS**: Must be included in EAP development and familiar with venue-specific access routes

## Equipment Required at Every Venue
- AED (automated external defibrillator) — accessible within 90 seconds from anywhere on the court
- Spinal stabilization and transport equipment
- Medical sideline bag (ATC) with basic injury management supplies
- Advanced medical care equipment (team physician)
- All supplies maintained and accessible during transit (not in checked luggage) [S10, p.88]

## Common Mistakes
1. **EAP not rehearsed before the season** → AED and CPR skill retention degrades within months; annual refresher training is not optional
2. **EAP is generic, not venue-specific** → Every location used for practice or competition requires its own detailed plan with venue-specific maps and entry routes
3. **Medical equipment stored in team luggage during travel** → Emergencies can occur in transit; essential supplies must be in the cabin or immediately accessible
4. **EMS not included in EAP development** → Local EMS must know the venue layout and their designated entry point before an emergency occurs
