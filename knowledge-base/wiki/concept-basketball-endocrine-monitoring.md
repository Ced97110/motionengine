# Basketball Endocrine Monitoring of Training Load

## Summary
The endocrine system plays a critical role in physiological adaptation to basketball training by modulating anabolic and catabolic processes. Key hormones and cytokines — including testosterone, cortisol, growth hormone (GH), insulin-like growth factor-I (IGF-I), and interleukin-6 (IL-6) — respond to exercise in measurable, interpretable ways that can be used to gauge training load, assess recovery quality, and identify overtraining risk [S10, p.25].

The testosterone-to-cortisol ratio and IGF-I levels are the most practically useful markers. An inability to increase circulating IGF-I levels and/or the testosterone/cortisol ratio before a target competition should be treated as an alarming signal that the athlete's preparedness is suboptimal [S10, p.30]. This chapter (Eliakim & Nemet) synthesizes evidence primarily from basketball and related team sports.

## When to Use
- Monitoring training load balance throughout the competitive season
- Identifying early signs of overtraining before performance declines appear
- Tailoring recovery modalities (sleep, cryotherapy, tapering) to individual hormonal responses
- Tracking fitness adaptations across preseason, in-season, and postseason phases

## Key Principles
1. **Anabolic-catabolic balance determines adaptation** — Dominance of the anabolic response (GH, IGF-I, testosterone) builds muscle and fitness; prolonged catabolic dominance (cortisol) combined with inadequate nutrition leads to overtraining and injury [S10, p.25].
2. **Testosterone/cortisol ratio is the primary training load indicator** — High cortisol with low testosterone/cortisol ratio indicates a catabolic state; monitoring this ratio tracks the athlete's readiness [S10, pp.25, 31].
3. **IGF-I follows a systemic-local divergence pattern** — Circulating IGF-I initially decreases with training load (systemic catabolic response), while local muscle IGF-I increases from early training. A rebound above baseline in circulating IGF-I indicates successful adaptation [S10, p.26].
4. **Tapering is associated with IGF-I recovery** — Reducing training intensity before competition triggers a parallel increase in circulating IGF-I, serving as a hormonal readiness marker [S10, p.29].
5. **High-volume resistance training elevates cortisol most** — Among training types, strength-hypertrophy sessions produce the greatest cortisol response in female basketball players; cortisol changes track training strain [S10, p.26].
6. **As training progresses, practices become more anabolic** — With proper training adaptation, each session produces greater GH response and reduced IL-6 and cortisol — a signal of improved resilience [S10, p.30].
7. **Decreasing-distance interval protocols have greater metabolic/anabolic effect** — Despite similar total volume, decreasing-distance intervals (400→300→200→100 m) produce higher lactate and GH than increasing-distance protocols, but are perceived as less intense [S10, p.27].

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Monitor individual hormonal baselines; players who deviate from their own historical norms may need recovery interventions even when subjective wellness appears acceptable.
- **SF and PF playing >25 min/game**: Highest catabolic risk in March–April (last third of season) — require specific monitoring and possible training modifications [S10, p.31].
- **Players with <13 min/game**: May have insufficient training stimulus to maintain anabolic adaptation — may need supplemental training to compensate [S10, p.31].

## Variations
### Playing Venue Effects
Pre-match salivary testosterone levels are higher when playing at home, and pre-match hormone levels correlate with somatic anxiety — competition venue significantly affects the hormonal response and potentially match outcome [S10, p.30].

### Opponent Difficulty Effects
Higher cortisol is found prior to playing difficult opponents versus easy opponents or regular training, reflecting perceived status threat — regardless of competition stage (regular season vs. playoff) [S10, p.31].

### Mindfulness Training
8 weeks of mindfulness training attenuated the competition-related cortisol increase in wheelchair basketball players — relaxation methods may be a useful adjunct to endocrine management [S10, p.32].

### Red-Light Photobiomodulation (Recovery)
30 min of red-light whole-body irradiation for 14 nights was associated with higher melatonin levels, improved sleep quality, and improved endurance performance in elite female basketball players [S10, p.28].

### Cryotherapy Caution
Local cold-pack application following sprint interval training significantly decreased anabolic markers (IGF-I, IGFBP-3) during recovery, suggesting cold packs may have negative effects on anabolic adaptation. Reserve cryotherapy for traumatic injuries or combine with active recovery, not complete rest [S10, p.28].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating circulating IGF-I as a direct performance predictor** → Circulating IGF-I reflects overall well-being and energy balance, not necessarily performance capacity; local muscle IGF-I is more directly performance-relevant [S10, p.29].
2. **Ignoring seasonal timing** → The most catabolic hormonal state occurs in March–April (last third of regular season) — coaches who maintain the same training load throughout the season risk overtraining their key forwards [S10, p.31].
3. **Using player self-report as a substitute for objective markers** → While self-assessment physical conditioning scores correlate with IGF-I changes, hormonal measurement provides objective confirmation — use both [S10, p.29].
4. **Applying the same recovery modality universally** → Cold packs decrease anabolic factors; sleep and active recovery support them. Tailor recovery methods to the type and phase of training [S10, p.28].
