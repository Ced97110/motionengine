# Lower-Body Plyometrics for Basketball

## Summary
Plyometric training uses the stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) — the rapid eccentric loading of a muscle immediately followed by explosive concentric contraction — to develop reactive strength and explosive power. In basketball, lower-body plyometrics directly translate to higher jumps for rebounds and shots, quicker first steps, faster direction changes, and improved layup push-off speed.

The key training variable is **ground contact time** — the shorter the contact between jumps or hops, the more reactive (and game-specific) the training effect. All lower-body plyometrics in *Basketball Anatomy* are organized around this principle and mapped to specific basketball game situations. [S2, pp.111-119]

## When to Use
- In-season: 1–2 sessions/week for maintenance, lower volume
- Off-season/preseason: 2–3 sessions/week for development, higher volume
- After a solid strength-training base is established (players should be able to squat 1.5× body weight before high-intensity box work)
- Not appropriate for players actively rehabilitating lower-extremity injuries

## Key Principles
1. **Minimize ground contact time** — the faster the SSC cycle, the greater the reactive strength gain
2. **Land softly** — absorb through ankle, knee, and hip flexion simultaneously; "quiet landings" protect joints and signal proper deceleration mechanics
3. **Arm drive** — synchronized upward arm throw on every takeoff adds 5–10% to jump height
4. **Bilateral before unilateral** — master two-leg plyometrics before progressing to single-leg variants
5. **Progressive overload** — increase height, distance, or number of consecutive reps before increasing speed
6. **Sport-specific simulation** — the most valuable plyometrics mimic actual game movements (e.g., depth jump into jump shot, split jump into run)

## Basketball Game Connections
| Plyometric Exercise | Basketball Situation |
|---|---|
| Single-leg hurdle jump | Layup push-off, fast-break first step |
| Depth jump → jump shot | Shooting over a defender |
| Multiple-box jump | Offensive rebounding, second jump |
| Skater hop | Jab step, defensive slide, cutting |
| Split jump → run | Rebound to fast break |

[S2, pp.111-119]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Jumping off the box (depth jump)** instead of stepping off → the reactive loading phase is lost; correction: step, do not jump, off the box
2. **Slow ground contacts** — standing between reps → defeats the SSC purpose; correction: "touch and go" mindset
3. **Valgus knee collapse on landing** → high injury risk; correction: cue "knees over toes" on every landing
4. **No arm drive** → reduced jump height; correction: "throw your hands to the ceiling"
5. **Too much volume too soon** → fatigue-induced form breakdown and injury; correction: start with 2 boxes/2 sets and build

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions:** Lower-body plyometrics are universal in basketball — every position benefits from higher jump, faster first step, and quicker direction change
- **PF/C:** Emphasize multiple-box jump and depth jump for rebounding and post power
- **PG/SG:** Emphasize skater hop and split jump for lateral quickness and first-step acceleration
