# Low-Post Fakes and Moves — Youth/Motion Offense System

## Summary
In a motion offense, the 3-second rule keeps the low-post area open and accessible — no player may stay in the post area longer than 3 seconds to receive a pass. But once a player catches the ball in the big block area, they are free to take more time to execute their move (still subject to the 3-second lane violation rule). This creates a structured post play environment where **all five players** must learn low-post moves — not just traditional post players.

The progression of moves taught in this system builds systematically: drop step baseline → drop step middle → spin and half-spin → up-and-under → pump fake series. Each move is a counter to another, making the full system difficult to defend.

## When to Use
- Any time a player cuts to the low-post block and receives a pass in the low post
- When a motion offense cutter ends up in the post from any direction (baseline cut, V-cut, flash cut)
- In the 3-second window before the ball is received; after receiving the ball, execute the move

## Key Principles

### 1. The 3-Second Post Rule (Motion Offense Context)
- Players have **3 seconds to receive the pass** in the low post
- If the pass is not received in 3 seconds, the player must **clear the low post** for teammates
- Once the ball is caught in the block area, additional time may be used for the move (still within the lane 3-second rule)

### 2. Always Check Baseline First
- In every low-post situation, the attacker turns the head slightly to check the defender's shoulder and (peripherally) their foot placement
- **If you can clearly see the defender's shoulder** → baseline drive is blocked → use a fake or drop-step middle
- **If baseline is open** → drop-step baseline for the power layup
- Checking baseline first is the consistent read that makes the entire system simple [S15, p.232]

### 3. Jump Stop on the Catch
- Player cuts to the low-post block and executes a **jump stop** approximately one step above the big block
- The jump stop preserves the choice of pivot feet — the most important footwork decision in post play
- From the jump stop: drop-step baseline uses one foot; drop-step middle uses the other

### 4. The Full Move Hierarchy
| Level | Move | Shot | Counter |
|-------|------|------|--------|
| Foundation | Drop-step baseline | Power layup | Drop-step middle |
| Foundation | Drop-step middle | Baby hook | Drop-step baseline |
| Intermediate | Spin (off drop-step middle) | Jump shot/layup | Half-spin |
| Intermediate | Half-spin (counter to spin) | Jump shot/layup | Spin |
| Intermediate | Up-and-under | Layup | Pump fake |
| Advanced | Pump fake series | Power layup/up-and-under | Any |

### 5. The Slide-Step Dribble
- When the drop-step lands too far from the basket, use the **slide-step dribble** to get closer without turning into the defender
- Also used to prevent a perimeter defender from deflecting the dribble on the drop-step middle

### 6. Universal for All Players
- "In the motion offense, **all players must learn to cut to the low-post block** and use low-post moves. Don't teach these moves only to your post players." [S15, p.232]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PF/C**: Primary low-post receivers; must master the full move hierarchy
- **SF**: Must learn drop step and baby hook as they cut to the block in motion offense
- **PG/SG**: Must learn the basics (drop step baseline and middle) as all motion offense players cycle through the post

## Variations
### Drop-Step Baseline (Full Technique)
1. Jump-stop; establish pivot foot
2. Drop-step as far as possible while maintaining balance; place drop-step foot at least level with (ideally beyond) the defender's foot; square up parallel to the baseline
3. Dive toward basket with power layup; keep both hands on ball until laying it on the backboard
4. May throw buttocks toward the middle to create slight contact and prevent shot blocking
5. If too far from basket: slide-step dribble closer
6. If defender is taller and on your back: pump fake series to get them off balance

### Drop-Step Middle (Full Technique)
1. Jump-stop; check baseline; see defender's shoulder → choose drop-step middle
2. Drop-step with the middle-side foot as far as possible while maintaining balance
3. Use slide-step dribble if needed to get closer to the basket
4. Shoot baby hook with finesse; spring into the air over the front of the rim
5. If defender is not off-balance enough: pump fake instead of baby hook; execute up-and-under if defender bites

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping the jump stop** → losing pivot foot choice removes all flexibility; correct immediately
2. **Not checking baseline first** → taking the harder drop-step middle when baseline was open
3. **Circuitous drop step** → the drop step must be a direct drive, not a curved path; give up the layup
4. **Hooking the non-dribbling arm around the defender** → automatic turnover; arm must be straight and strong
5. **Teaching post moves only to post players** → all five players must learn them in the motion offense
