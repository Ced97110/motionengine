# Basketball Accelerometry-Based Training Load

## Summary
Wearable accelerometers provide a superior method for quantifying exercise intensity and dose during basketball training compared to traditional time-motion analysis or heart rate monitoring. The latter two methods underestimate basketball's external demands (jumping, shuffling, direction changes) or suffer from cardiorespiratory lag that misses brief supramaximal efforts [S12, p.28].

The key metrics are:
- **Average Net Force (AvFNet)** — accelerometry-derived measure of relative exercise intensity
- **Accumulated Impulse** — product of AvFNet × duration, measuring total exercise dose in Newton-seconds (N·s)

This framework allows coaches to objectively quantify training loads across a competitive season, compare phases of different match schedule difficulty, and identify positional differences in training demands [S12, pp.27–31].

## When to Use
- Across the competitive season to track week-to-week load fluctuations
- To compare training intensity across different phases (pre-season, easy/medium/hard schedule periods)
- When designing recovery sessions vs. high-intensity sessions
- To identify positional differences that require individualized load prescription
- As an objective complement to subjective wellness questionnaires

## Key Principles
1. **AvFNet measures what HR and time-motion analysis cannot** — High-frequency (100 Hz) tri-axial accelerometry captures supramaximal bursts, jumping, and rapid direction changes that HR lag and time-motion analysis miss [S12, p.28]
2. **Calibrate to the individual** — Individual AvFNet-to-VO2 relationships must be established through a Yo-Yo IR1 test; r² = 0.93–0.97 confirms strong individual predictive power [S12, p.30]
3. **Seven intensity zones** — Relative intensity can be categorized into sedentary through supramaximal zones based on VO2 reserve (%VO2R) [S12, p.29]
4. **Most training time is low-intensity** — 64.3 ± 6.1% of training is spent in sedentary behavior or very light intensity — a critical finding for coaches designing workouts [S12, p.30]
5. **Hard schedule = most consistent load** — During the hard match schedule, exercise dose was similar across all three sessions per week (no tapering was observed), suggesting coaches do not adequately adjust for schedule difficulty [S12, p.31]
6. **Back-court players are more supramaximal** — Back-court players (PG, SG) perform significantly more supramaximal intensity activity than front-court players [S12, p.30]

## The Seven Basketball Training Intensity Zones

| Zone | Intensity | %VO2R Range | Coaching Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sedentary | < 20% | Standing, minimal movement — dead time in practice |
| 2 | Very Light | 20–<30% | Walking, slow transitions — warm-up/recovery |
| 3 | Light | 30–<40% | Jogging, skill drills — technical work |
| 4 | Moderate | 40–<60% | Half-speed work, tactical drills — team concepts |
| 5 | Vigorous | 60–<90% | High-intensity drills, competitive situations |
| 6 | Maximal | 90–<100% | Near-maximum efforts — sprint/conditioning drills |
| 7 | Supramaximal | ≥ 100% | Short explosive bursts exceeding aerobic maximum |

*Distribution during training (combined front-court and back-court):*
- Sedentary + Very Light: **64.3%** of total training time
- Session 3 each week consistently had the highest vigorous intensity proportion (mean difference vs. Session 1: 1.5%, p=0.026) [S12, p.31]

## Positional Differences in Training Load

### Front-Court Players (SF, PF, C)
- Spend significantly **more time in very light intensity** (21.6% vs. 14.8% for back-court)
- Less supramaximal activity
- Larger body mass means the same court movements generate higher absolute forces despite lower relative intensity percentages
- **Implication**: Front-court players may need additional high-intensity supplemental conditioning [S12, p.30]

### Back-Court Players (PG, SG)
- Perform significantly **more supramaximal intensity** activity (5.6% vs. 1.7% for front-court)
- More first-step acceleration, defensive slides, and transition sprints create more explosive efforts
- Greater ACWR risk during hard match schedules due to higher intensity demands
- **Implication**: Back-court players' recovery needs are different from front-court players; blanket prescription underserves both groups [S12, pp.30–31]

## Effect of Match Schedule on Training Load

| Schedule Phase | Session Pattern | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Easy (vs. lowest-ranked teams) | Load peaks in Session 2 | Normal periodization: high load mid-week, taper pre-match |
| Medium (double-header + away) | Load peaks in Session 2, Session 1 & 3 significantly lower | Recovery and pre-match tapering appropriately reduced |
| Hard (away double-header + grand finalist) | No significant differences between sessions | **Dose did NOT vary** — coaches failed to taper adequately during highest-demand schedule phase |

*Implication for coaches: During hard match schedule phases, deliberately plan lower-dose sessions before and after games. The data shows this typically does NOT happen naturally* [S12, p.31].

## Player Responsibilities
- **All players**: Wear accelerometer/microtechnology device in every court session, including warm-ups
- **S&C Coach**: Calculate AvFNet and Impulse values for each session; compare to individual baseline; flag sessions significantly above individual norm
- **Head Coach**: Review position-group load data weekly; adjust practice design to reduce sedentary/very light time and increase meaningful intensity windows

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using heart rate alone** — HR underestimates supramaximal bouts and lags behind rapid intensity changes; AvFNet captures what HR misses [S12, p.28]
2. **Applying group session prescriptions** — Front-court and back-court players experience the same practice at different relative intensities; individualized supplemental work addresses this [S12, pp.30–31]
3. **Not reducing load during hard schedules** — Research shows coaches typically maintain the same session structure regardless of fixture difficulty [S12, p.31]
4. **Ignoring sedentary time in practice** — If 64% of practice time is sedentary/very light, there is substantial room to redesign drills to increase meaningful intensity [S12, p.30]
