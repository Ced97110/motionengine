# Patellofemoral Pain (PFP) in Basketball Players

## Summary
Patellofemoral pain (PFP) accounts for 25% of knee problems seen in sports medicine clinics. An observational study of 810 adolescent basketball players showed a 25% prevalence — 26% in females and 18% in males. Basketball players are particularly susceptible because running produces ground reaction forces 2-3× body weight, while jump landings produce up to 9-10× body weight; professional basketball players jump an average of 70 times per match [S10, p.411].

PFP has a notoriously poor long-term outcome if untreated — more than half of patients report unfavorable recovery 5-8 years after initial diagnosis and conservative management. The anatomical causes are multifactorial: bone morphology (trochlear dysplasia, patellar alta/morphology), bony alignment (Q-angle, TT-TG distance, coronal/torsional deformities), soft tissue restraints (MPFL, VMO, lateral retinaculum/ITB), hip and core stability, and cartilage integrity [S10, pp.411-419].

## When to Use
- Basketball player with anterior knee pain worsened by jumping, squatting, descending stairs, or prolonged knee flexion
- Screening for anatomical risk factors in players with recurrent anterior knee pain
- Designing rehabilitation programs targeting specific PFP contributors

## Key Principles
1. **Envelope of Function**: PFP occurs when the patellofemoral joint is pushed into the zone of supraphysiologic overload — beyond the tissues' capacity to maintain homeostasis (Dye's theory) [S10, p.413]
2. **Multifactorial etiology**: No single cause; bone morphology, alignment, soft tissue, hip/core stability, and cartilage all contribute
3. **Trochlear dysplasia is the predominant instability factor**: A flat trochlea is more likely to cause lateral patellar translation than even an injured MPFL [S10, p.413]
4. **MPFL provides 60% of medial restraint** at 20° knee flexion; damage or congenital abnormality causes instability [S10, p.418]
5. **VMO is the primary active medial stabilizer**: VMO hypoplasia or altered recruitment between VMO and vastus lateralis is a major cause of dynamic instability [S10, p.418]
6. **Hip weakness drives dynamic valgus**: Patients with PFP display greater hip abduction and external rotation weakness — leading to increased hip adduction/internal rotation during functional movements [S10, p.418]
7. **Patella alta increases risk**: High-riding patella creates decreased contact in early flexion, higher contact force in deep flexion, and greater lateral displacement/tilt [S10, p.415]

## Differential Diagnosis of Anterior Knee Pain (Table 34.1)
- Patellar instability (objective, potential, painful syndrome)
- Synovial impingement (pathologic plica, Hoffa syndrome)
- Osteochondrosis (Osgood-Schlatter, Sinding-Larsen-Johansson)
- Bursitis (pre-patellar, infrapatellar, MCL, pes anserine)
- Tendinopathies (patellar tendon, pes anserine, semimembranosus)
- Primary/metastatic tumors
- Infection
- Localized pigmented villonodular synovitis [S10, p.412]

## Anatomical Factors

### 1. Trochlear Dysplasia
The trochlear groove deepens from proximal to distal; a shallow/flat proximal groove allows lateral patellar subluxation especially in early flexion. At 20° flexion, 74 N is needed to produce 10mm lateral displacement; at 90°, 125 N is required [S10, p.413].

**Dejour's Classification of Trochlear Dysplasia:**
| Type | Lateral Radiograph | Axial Imaging |
|------|--------------------|---------------|
| A | Crossing sign | Shallow trochlea, morphology preserved |
| B | Crossing sign + Supratrochlear spur | Flat trochlea |
| C | Crossing sign + Double contour | Convex lateral facet, hypoplastic medial facet |
| D | Crossing sign + Spur + Double contour | Facet asymmetry + cliff pattern (vertical transition) |

**Three radiographic signs on lateral X-ray**: Crossing sign, supratrochlear spur, double contour [S10, p.414]

### 2. Patellar Morphology

**Wiberg Classification (axial plane)**:
- Type I (10%): Concave medial and lateral facets, approximately equal size
- Type II (65%): Flat/slightly convex medial facet, smaller than lateral
- Type III (25%): Considerably smaller and convex medial facet — highest cartilage damage incidence; associated with most severe trochlear dysplasia

**Grelsamer Classification (sagittal plane)** based on patella length / articular surface length ratio:
- Type I (most common): Ratio 1.2-1.5
- Type II (long nose): Ratio >1.5
- Type III (short nose): Ratio <1.2

**Patella Alta**: High-riding patella linked to PFP and instability; multiple measurement indices (Insall-Salvati, Caton-Deschamps, Blackburne-Peel). New method: Sagittal Patellofemoral Engagement (SPE) index — SPE <0.45 is pathological (uses MRI cartilaginous measurements rather than bony landmarks) [S10, p.415-416].

**Patellar tendinopathy prevalence**: Up to 32% in professional basketball players [S10, p.415]

### 3. Bony Alignment

**Q-Angle**: Classic measure of lateral vector force — larger Q-angle = greater lateral forces on patella. Clinical validity debated but biomechanical importance unquestioned [S10, p.416]

**TT-TG Distance** (Tibial Tuberosity–Trochlear Groove): Quantitative measure of tibial tuberosity lateralization. Normal: 12mm; Pathological: >20mm. Can be measured on CT or MRI using two axial cuts [S10, p.416-417]

### 4. Coronal (Varus-Valgus) Deformities
- **Valgus malalignment**: Increases Q-angle and TT-TG; significantly lateralizes patella; increased lateral PF pressure
- **Varus deformity**: Associated with proximal tibial rotation causing lateral patellar displacement [S10, p.417]

### 5. Torsional Deformities
- **Increased femoral anteversion**: Internal rotation of entire femur → increased Q-angle → patellofemoral malalignment and instability. Normal femoral anteversion ≈15° (range 7-24°); females have slightly more than males [S10, p.417]
- **External tibial rotation**: Lateral displacement of tibial tuberosity → abnormal Q-angle and patellar tracking → PFP and instability
- At 30° femoral rotational deformity: significant nonlinear increase in PF contact pressures [S10, p.417]

### 6. Foot Disorders
High incidence of abnormal foot eversion and/or pronation in PFP patients — speculated to contribute through altered lower limb kinematics [S10, p.417]

## Soft Tissue Restraints

### Medial Stabilizers (0-30° flexion — primary role)
1. **MPFL** (Medial Patellofemoral Ligament): Thin band from adductor tubercle to medial patella border — provides 60% of total medial restraint at 20° flexion [S10, p.418]
2. Patellomeniscal ligament
3. Vastus medialis obliquus (VMO)
4. Medial retinaculum

*As flexion increases, patella engages the trochlear groove and stability relies more on bone contact*

### Lateral Stabilizers
- Lateral patellofemoral ligament
- Joint capsule
- **Iliotibial Band (ITB)**: Tightness increases lateral patellar tracking and tilt; greatest influence at 20° flexion
- Lateral retinaculum (3 layers: superficial/deep fascia, intermediate/ITB-quadriceps derivatives, deep/joint capsule)

### VMO Specifics
- VMO oblique portion: oriented at 47° ± 5° to femoral axis
- Primary active medial stabilizing force
- VMO hypoplasia/dysplasia = major cause of dynamic patellar instability
- Altered VMO-vastus lateralis recruitment order can cause instability even with normal muscle tissue [S10, p.418]

## Hip and Core Stability
Poor core stability → lack of frontal plane dynamic control → contralateral pelvic drop → homolateral excessive hip adduction + internal rotation (dynamic valgus) → altered PF kinematics.

- PFP patients show significantly greater hip abduction and external rotation weakness
- Dynamic valgus increases tension in lateral patellar retinaculum via ITB
- Correlation between PFP, lumbar lordosis, and sacral inclination ("knee-spine syndrome"): low back lordosis → compensatory knee flexion → increased PF joint load [S10, pp.418-419]

## Cartilage
- Average patellar cartilage thickness: 4.1mm (thickest in body) — due to high joint reaction forces (up to 20× body weight during sporting activities)
- Thinner cartilage = higher stress for same load
- Patellar cartilage thinning significant in male PFP patients but NOT in females — suggesting cartilage is a non-dominant factor in isolation
- Medial/central patellar areas have richer intraosseous innervation than lateral — these nerves deliver nociceptive signals via intraosseous pressure increases (edema) or subchondral bone pressure increases (cartilage failure/reduced contact area) [S10, p.419]

## Clinical Evaluation Framework

### History
- Onset (sudden vs. progressive), training changes, specific activity triggers

### Physical Examination
- Walking pattern and global posture
- Knee inflammation signs (swelling)
- ROM, lower limb alignment and symmetry
- Pain location
- Patellar instability signs
- Muscle status and flexibility (hamstring/quadriceps tightness, anterior pelvic tilt)
- Tenderness at inferior patellar pole (SLJ), tibial tuberosity (Osgood-Schlatter)

### Imaging (Minimum)
- Weight-bearing AP X-rays of knee (preferably full lower limb for coronal alignment)
- Skyline/merchant view (patellar alignment and arthrosis)
- Lateral view with perfect femoral condyle superimposition (trochlear dysplasia, patellar height)
- Additional: MRI/CT for cartilage/soft tissue/torsional deformities

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating PFP as a single diagnosis** → Multiple anatomical contributors must be individually identified
2. **Ignoring hip and core** → Hip abductor/rotator weakness is a primary driver of dynamic valgus in basketball players
3. **Missing trochlear dysplasia** → Requires perfect lateral X-ray with complete femoral condyle superimposition
4. **Using only bony patellar height measures** → SPE index using cartilaginous MRI landmarks provides superior functional assessment
5. **Focusing only on local treatment without addressing foot pronation** → Foot orthoses may be part of comprehensive management
