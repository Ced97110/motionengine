# MLI Rehabilitation and Return-to-Basketball Protocol

## Summary
Postoperative rehabilitation after multi-ligament knee injury (MLI) reconstruction is as important as the surgery itself. Delayed or poorly structured rehabilitation leads to arthrofibrosis — the most common and debilitating complication. The protocol follows a periodization model (endurance → strength → power) with strict, measurable criteria for phase advancement, and culminates in basketball-specific agility and jump testing before return to play.

## When to Use
- Following any multi-ligament knee reconstruction in a basketball player
- As a framework for athletic trainers and physical therapists managing complex knee injuries
- For setting realistic, evidence-based return-to-play timelines with athletes and coaches

## Key Principles

### Four Core Rehabilitation Principles (Mueller et al.) [S10, p.370]
1. **Protect the surgical reconstruction** and restore joint range of motion
2. **Manage the scarring process** to prevent arthrofibrosis
3. **Minimize muscular atrophy** and restore preinjury levels of muscular strength
4. **Utilize return-to-sport testing** to guide objective progression

### Phases of Periodization [S10, p.371]
- **Phase 1 (Tissue Protection)**: Weeks 0–8 — ROM, protection, edema control
- **Phase 2 (Endurance)**: Weeks 8–15 — light loading, walk-run, quad activation
- **Phase 3 (Strength)**: Weeks 15–21 — progressive loading, single-plane agility
- **Phase 4 (Power)**: Weeks 21+ — multiplane agility, plyometrics, basketball movements
- **Return to Sport**: 9–12 months — multidisciplinary clearance

## Phase-Specific Criteria

### Phase 1 (Weeks 0–2): Tissue Protection
- **ROM**: 0–90° knee flexion + patellofemoral ROM
- **Gait**: Non-weight-bearing
- **Exercises**: Patellofemoral mobilization (initiated immediately), four-way SLR, ankle pumps, gluteal sets [S10, p.370]
- Avoid aggressive flexion for 4 weeks; limit extension to neutral (no hyperextension) for 6 weeks [S10, p.370]

### Phase 1 (Weeks 2–8): Continued Protection
- **ROM**: 0–10° of contralateral side
- **Gait**: Protected weight-bearing
- **Physical Exam**: Swelling within 1 cm of opposite knee
- Advanced weight-bearing exercises start when criteria for crutch discontinuation met (≥6 weeks postop) [S10, p.371]

### Phase 3 (Weeks 8–15): Endurance Phase
- **ROM**: Full active ROM equal to contralateral side
- **Gait**: Full weight-bearing unassisted ×20 minutes
- **Physical Exam**: Quad index >80%
- **Flexibility**: Anterior reach within 8 cm of contralateral leg
- **Lifting**: Leg press 2.5× body weight (10 reps); single-leg squat from 10" height for 15 reps
- **Running**: Walk-run program
- **Jumping**: DorsaVi — pass single-leg squat [S10, p.372]

### Phase 3 (Weeks 15–21): Strength Phase
- **Physical Exam**: Quad index 90%; quad girth within 1 cm; hamstring/quad ratio >60%
- **Flexibility**: Anterior reach within 4 cm of contralateral leg
- **Lifting**: Leg press 2.8× body weight (10 reps)
- **Running/Agility**: Single-plane speed and agility
- **Jumping**: DorsaVi — box drop [S10, p.372]

### Phase 4 (Weeks 21+): Power Phase
- **Physical Exam**: Quad within 1 cm of contralateral side
- **Flexibility**: Anterior reach within 2 cm of contralateral leg
- **Lifting**: Leg press 3.1× body weight (10 reps)
- **Running/Agility**: Multiplane speed and agility; T-test within 90% of contralateral side
- **Jumping**: DorsaVi single-leg vertical jump within 90% of contralateral side [S10, p.372]

### Return to Sport (9–12 months)
- Multidisciplinary decision: physician + athletic trainers + coaches
- Objective tests: **Vail Sports Test, SANE, IKDC** — <5% difference from contralateral side [S10, p.372]

## Basketball-Specific Return-to-Play Progressions [S10, p.372]
1. Incorporate **lateral movement, jumping, and pivoting** gradually in the final phase
2. Progression: **single-plane agility → multiplane agility**
3. Sport-specific drills (cutting, pivoting, jumping) begin after cycling/treadmill independence without brace
4. **PCL/PLC patients**: avoid lunging exercises with knee flexion >45° — disproportionately loads PCL [S10, p.371]

## Measurement Tools
- **Single hop test**: assesses neuromuscular control and eccentric quad loading [S10, p.372]
- **Single-leg squat** (spring-loaded technology reduces impact): quantifies weight distribution, assesses eccentric quad strength [S10, p.373]
- **Quad index**: bilateral quad strength comparison
- **DorsaVi system**: motion analysis for squat quality and vertical jump
- **T-test**: multiplane agility assessment
- **Vail Sports Test / SANE / IKDC**: comprehensive return-to-sport scoring

## Common Mistakes
1. **Immobilizing the knee postoperatively** → arthrofibrosis is the most common MLI complication; immediate ROM must begin [S10, p.370]
2. **Skipping patellofemoral mobilization** → proximal patellar hypomobility causes extensor mechanism disruption [S10, p.370]
3. **Advancing phases without meeting criteria** → increases re-injury, muscle imbalance, and knee inflammation risk [S10, p.371]
4. **Returning to sport based on time alone** → objective testing is mandatory; time alone is insufficient [S10, p.372]
5. **Ignoring PCL-specific loading limits** → deep knee flexion in lunges overloads PCL graft in PCL/PLC injuries [S10, p.371]
6. **Delaying agility and basketball-specific movements** → pivoting, cutting, and jumping must be progressively reintroduced; cannot begin only in week 21+ without prior groundwork [S10, p.372]
