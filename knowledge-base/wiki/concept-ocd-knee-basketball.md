# Osteochondritis Dissecans (OCD) of the Knee in Basketball

## Summary
Osteochondritis dissecans (OCD) is defined as a "focal idiopathic alteration of subchondral bone with risk for instability and disruption of adjacent cartilage that may result in premature osteoarthritis." It most commonly occurs between ages 10-15 and is particularly relevant for basketball players due to high jump volumes (averaging 70 jumps per match in professionals). The posterolateral aspect of the medial femoral condyle is the most common location within the knee [S10, p.401].

Prognosis is better for younger patients with open distal femoral physes. Key risk factors for poor healing include sclerosis on radiographs, synovial fluid behind the lesion on MRI (indicating instability), and lesions in the lateral femoral condyle or patella [S10, p.402].

## When to Use
- Adolescent basketball player with vague, poorly localized activity-related knee pain and recurrent effusions
- Advanced cases: mechanical symptoms (catching, locking)
- Positive Wilson's test: pain with internal tibial rotation during extension 90°→30°, relief with external rotation

## Key Principles
1. **Staging drives treatment**: Stable lesions in skeletally immature patients → non-operative; unstable lesions or older patients → surgery [S10, p.403]
2. **Radiographs for monitoring**: Weight-bearing AP, lateral, and tunnel/notch views (notch view catches posterior lesions missed on AP)
3. **MRI for initial characterization**: Size, subchondral bone status, cartilage integrity, stability, loose bodies
4. **Six radiographic healing features**: Articular surface shape, boundary appearance, sclerosis, ossification, lesion size, overall healing — used for monitoring
5. **Do not rush**: Rehabilitation phases should not be accelerated; guided by radiographs and MD
6. **Return to sport**: 4-6 months, only after radiographic confirmation of healing [S10, p.403]

## OCD Staging (Cascade of Injury)
1. Softening of overlying articular cartilage with intact surface
2. Early articular cartilage separation
3. Partial detachment of articular cartilage lesion
4. Osteochondral separation with loose body formation

## Treatment Algorithm

### Non-Operative (Stable lesions, open physes)
- Restricted weight-bearing, bracing, close radiographic follow-up
- 50-75% healing rate without fragmentation [S10, p.403]

### Operative Indications
- Unstable lesions OR lesions in older/skeletally mature patients

### Surgical Options
1. **Arthroscopic subchondral drilling**: Stable lesions → promotes healing
2. **ORIF (Open Reduction Internal Fixation)**: Unstable but fixable lesions → 85% healing rate with open physes
3. **Cartilage restoration/transplant**: Large (>2cm × 2cm) unfixable lesions

## Rehabilitation Protocol (Drilling/Fixation — Table 33.4)

### 0-6 Weeks
- **Weight-bearing**: Non-weight-bearing (NWB)
- **ROM**: Full as tolerated (avoid WB ROM exercises)
- **Exercises**: Isometric quad and hamstring strengthening; stretching of quads, hamstrings, and calves

### 6-12 Weeks
- **Weight-bearing**: Progress to full WB with MD consent after radiographic evaluation
- **ROM**: Full symmetrical ROM to contralateral side
- **Exercises**: Progress strength training guided by pain and symptoms; NO high-impact activities

### 12 Weeks: Return to Sport Phase
- **Note**: This phase should not be rushed; guided by radiographs and MD
- **Weight-bearing**: Full
- **Exercises**: Begin running program; begin impact strengthening; begin functional return-to-sport activities
- **Monitor**: Pain and symptoms; slow rehab if they occur
- **Return to full sport**: 4-6 months, after radiographic healing confirmation and physician clearance

## Prognosis Factors

### Better Prognosis
- Younger patient
- Open distal femoral physes
- Stable lesion (no synovial fluid behind lesion on MRI)
- Medial femoral condyle (most common location)

### Worse Prognosis
- Near skeletal maturity
- Sclerosis on radiographs
- Synovial fluid behind lesion on MRI (unstable)
- Lateral femoral condyle or patella location

## Common Mistakes
1. **Missing tunnel/notch view on radiograph** → Posterior lesions invisible on standard AP view
2. **Rushing return to impact** → Must confirm radiographic healing first
3. **High-impact activities before 12 weeks** → Risks lesion progression
4. **Ignoring mechanical symptoms** → Catching/locking suggests loose body requiring urgent evaluation
