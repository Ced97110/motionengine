# GPP Athletic Performance Pyramid

## Summary
The four-component athletic performance pyramid organizes basketball physical development into a sequential hierarchy: **General Physical Preparation (GPP) → Strength → Power → Speed**. Each layer depends on the one below it — you cannot sustainably develop power without first developing strength, and you cannot develop strength without the foundational work capacity that GPP provides [S11, p.274].

This model, adapted from the NSCA Basics of Strength and Conditioning Manual, provides the structural rationale for postseason and off-season programming in basketball. GPP forms the base of the pyramid, and it is addressed primarily in the postseason training phase before transitioning to strength-dominant, then power-dominant, then speed-dominant mesocycles.

## When to Use
- When designing a complete annual periodization plan for basketball athletes
- As the conceptual framework explaining why postseason work is NOT optional even for elite athletes
- When communicating training priorities to athletes, coaches, and parents
- When selecting exercises and loading schemes for each phase of the off-season

## Key Principles
1. **GPP is the base** — work capacity, basic strength, and corrective strengthening are the foundation on which all other qualities are built. Without GPP, strength gains plateau quickly, power training becomes injury-prone, and speed gains are limited.
2. **Strength before power** — the body cannot express power it doesn't have the strength base to support. Attempting power training on an inadequate strength foundation increases injury risk and reduces adaptation.
3. **Power before speed** — on-court speed is an expression of power. Acceleration, first-step quickness, and change-of-direction ability all require the rapid force expression that power training develops.
4. **Each phase carries the previous** — GPP qualities are maintained throughout the off-season and preseason. Each ascending phase adds new emphasis without abandoning the base.
5. **GPP addresses deficiencies** — the postseason GPP phase is the critical window to correct movement imbalances, strength asymmetries, and stability deficits before higher-intensity training amplifies them.

## The Four Layers

### Layer 1: General Physical Preparation (GPP)
- **Components**: Work capacity, aerobic base, basic strength, corrective exercises, postural and stability improvement
- **Training tools**: Circuit training, antagonist-paired sets, moderate-load/high-rep resistance training, isometric holds, slow-tempo repetitions
- **Duration**: Primarily postseason (4–6 weeks), continuing into early off-season
- **Load**: 60–75% 1RM, 12–15 reps, 30–60 sec rest

### Layer 2: Strength
- **Components**: Maximal force production, neuromuscular efficiency, structural development (hypertrophy if needed)
- **Training tools**: Compound multi-joint barbell/dumbbell exercises, progressive overload, higher loads
- **Duration**: Primary focus in off-season Phase 1
- **Load**: 75–85% 1RM, 6–10 reps

### Layer 3: Power
- **Components**: Rate of force development (RFD), explosive strength, stretch-shortening cycle efficiency
- **Training tools**: Olympic lifts, plyometrics, complex training, contrast methods
- **Duration**: Primary focus in off-season Phase 2
- **Load**: 70–85% 1RM for strength component; ballistic/explosive for power component

### Layer 4: Speed (Apex)
- **Components**: First-step quickness, acceleration, court agility, maximal sprint speed
- **Training tools**: Sport-specific SAQ drills, court conditioning, game-speed movements
- **Duration**: Primary focus in preseason
- **Integration**: Court work + maintained strength/power base

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping GPP and going straight to heavy strength training** → Leads to injury and movement compensations
2. **Treating GPP as "just conditioning" (running)** → Running does not activate as many motor units or require as much force production as resistance training [S11, p.277]
3. **Jumping to power work before strength is established** → Plyometrics and Olympic lifts on a weak base produce poor results and increase injury risk
4. **Ignoring aerobic base in basketball athletes** → The aerobic system is critical for recovery between repeated high-intensity efforts on the court [S11, p.275]
