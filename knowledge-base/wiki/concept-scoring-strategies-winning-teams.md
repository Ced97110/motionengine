# Scoring Strategies That Differentiate Winning Teams

## Summary
A 2018 study (Conte & Lukonaitiene, *Sports* 2018, 6:50) analyzed all 40 games of FIBA EuroBasket Women 2017 to identify which scoring strategies separated winning from losing teams across three game-score contexts: close games (1–9 pts), balanced games (10–19 pts), and unbalanced games (20–33 pts). Five strategies were examined: Fast Break Points (FBP), Points in the Paint (PP), Points from Turnover (PT), Second Chance Points (SCP), and Points from the Bench (PB).

The central finding: **fast break points did not differentiate winners from losers in any game context** at this level. All other strategies differentiated winners from losers in at least one game-score context, with inside game (PP) and forcing turnovers (PT) being the most consistent discriminators. These findings provide a data-driven framework for coaching priorities in game preparation and practice design.

## When to Use
- When establishing offensive priorities in the game plan
- When evaluating whether your team's practice emphasis aligns with what wins games
- When designing in-game adjustments based on score margin
- When evaluating scout reports and opponent tendencies

## Key Principles
1. **Points in the paint (PP) are the most reliable winning indicator.** Winning teams scored substantially more PP in both close and unbalanced games. The inside game — post entries, drives, and offensive rebounds — creates the highest-efficiency possessions. "The interaction between outside and inside players has been suggested to be a crucial element in European basketball and in NBA."
2. **Points from turnovers (PT) discriminate in close and unbalanced games.** Turnovers give opponents fast-break and early-offense opportunities. Winning teams capitalize; losing teams give the ball away more. Protecting the ball is as important as scoring.
3. **Second chance points (SCP) matter — except in close games.** Winning teams scored more SCP in balanced and unbalanced games, but the difference was unclear in close games. Offensive rebounding is a discriminant factor when games are not at the wire.
4. **Fast break points (FBP) do not differentiate winners from losers** at the elite women's tournament level. Tournament teams use more set offense and fewer fast breaks — partly due to congested schedules causing fatigue, and partly due to higher-level defenses denying transition opportunities. Fast break efficiency is 63–73%, but if both teams produce similar volumes, it cancels out.
5. **Bench scoring shows opposite patterns by game context.** In close games, winning teams scored *fewer* bench points (starters played more decisive minutes). In unbalanced games, winning teams scored *more* bench points (best players rested during garbage time). Bench quality matters differently depending on game situation.
6. **Inside pass effectiveness increases possession efficiency by ~14%.** Research shows ball possessions including an inside pass rose from 49.8% to 63.3% effectiveness in professional play — the strongest single tactical move a team can make.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Protect the ball; minimize turnovers; attack the paint off PnR and isolation; facilitate inside-out offense.
- **SG/SF**: Attack the paint on drives and catch-and-attack actions; pursue offensive rebounds aggressively on guard-side misses.
- **PF**: Establish deep post position; catch and score in the paint; offensive rebound on every shot.
- **C**: Anchor the inside game as a post scorer and offensive rebounder; screen for penetration; convert on lobs and putbacks.

## Variations
### Close Game Strategy (1–9 pt margin)
- Prioritize paint attacks and drawing fouls (PP most important)
- Force turnovers through active defense and ball pressure (PT discriminates)
- Keep starters in — bench scoring hurts in close games
- Offensive rebounding is not a clear discriminator; don't sacrifice transition defense for it

### Balanced Game Strategy (10–19 pt margin)
- Second chance points become the key differentiator — commit to offensive rebounding
- Other strategies produce unclear differences; maintain ball security

### Unbalanced Game Strategy (20+ pt margin)
- All four strategies strongly discriminate — inside scoring, turnover conversion, second chances, and bench scoring all matter
- Use bench players to score — it's a strong signal of team depth and allows starters to rest

## Common Mistakes
1. **Over-prioritizing fast break offense** → At high levels in tournament play, fast breaks don't differentiate winners. Practice set-offense efficiency as primary, fast break as secondary.
2. **Neglecting offensive rebounding in favor of quick transition** → Second chance points win balanced and unbalanced games. Balanced teams must crash the glass.
3. **Keeping bench players scoreless in lopsided wins** → Use garbage time to get bench players game-ready and in scoring rhythm — it builds team depth and rests starters.
4. **Playing not to turn the ball over (passive ball movement)** → Active pressure on the other team to force their turnovers is more important than passive caution. Play aggressively to generate PT.
