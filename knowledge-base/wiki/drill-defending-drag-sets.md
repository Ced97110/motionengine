# Defending Drag Sets Drill

## Objective
Teach defenders to identify, communicate, and defend pick-and-roll screens set spontaneously during transition — the most difficult PnR situation to defend.

## Setup
- 10 players, 5 vs 5 on the full court
- Begins following a **made free throw** (simulates the most common drag trigger)
- O5 inbounds; O1 is the primary ball handler attacking up a sideline
- Defense retreats and must locate opponents while covering 94 feet

## Execution
1. O5 inbounds the ball and gets it to O1, who begins a dribble attack up either sideline as the defensive team retreats.
2. As O1 — under constant pressure from X1 — approaches midcourt or the high frontcourt sideline, O5 (trailing down the middle) changes direction and sets a screen on X1, enabling O1 to get to the middle.
3. **This is difficult** because the defense is retreating and locating their assigned opponents simultaneously.
4. **Best coverage**:
   - Call out the screen early ("Screen! Screen!")
   - If X1 gets screened, **X5 must switch**, slow down, and "string out" O1 (delay O1's middle penetration while running alongside him).
   - This gives X1 time to recover and get back to O1.
   - X5 must **not** let O1 penetrate the middle off the dribble.
5. If O1 beats X1 despite the switch, **X5 must be prepared to help** at the rim.

## Coaching Points
- **"Find your man first, then find the screen"** — defenders in transition must locate opponents before help calls can be made.
- **"Call it early"** — the only way to survive a drag screen is advance communication; late calls are useless.
- **"String it out"** — X5's job on the switch is not to attack O1, but to run alongside him (string out) and prevent him from reaching the middle, giving X1 recovery time.
- **"Middle is off-limits"** — X5 must never allow O1 to penetrate the middle off the dribble under any circumstances [S13, pp.214-215].
- Drag PnRs are becoming increasingly prevalent at all levels and must be **constantly practiced**.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Walk through the backcourt coverage assignment only — who pressures O1, who trails, who calls the screen.
2. **Intermediate**: Live 5v5 from a made free throw; O5 sets the drag screen at a predetermined spot so defenders can see the action at speed.
3. **Advanced**: O5 sets the drag screen at a random spot (sideline, midcourt, or frontcourt); defense must recognize, communicate, and execute the switch-and-string-out without knowing where it's coming.
