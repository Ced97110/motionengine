# Basketball Offensive Terminology (Herb Brown Glossary)

## Summary
Herb Brown's glossary defines the offensive concepts, play signals, cuts, sets, and tactical actions his teams use across all phases of play. These terms represent the shared language of high-level basketball coaching. Multiple synonyms are provided wherever Brown notes them, since opposing scouts and coaches use different terminology for the same actions.

## Key Principles
1. Consistent vocabulary between coaches and players eliminates hesitation and accelerates execution.
2. Many maneuvers have multiple names — knowing both enables better scouting reports.
3. Offensive terminology organizes court geometry (where to be) and player action (what to do) into single, coachable words.

## Glossary of Offensive Terms

### Ahead
Passing the ball **from the wing to the corner up the court** in early offense or in transition from offense to defense. [S1, p.233]

### ATO
*After a time-out* — acronym scouts use to diagram an opponent's set play following a timeout. [S1, p.233]

### ATW (All the Way)
Coaching term for running **all options of a set play from start to finish** before attempting a shot. Familiarizes players with every scoring possibility. [S1, p.233]

### Backpick / Rip / Step-Up / Blind Screen
A player without the ball **comes up behind a player (with or without the ball) and sets a screen from behind**. The screen can be vertical or diagonal. [S1, p.233]

### Blast
A **two-one-two high-post offensive set**. Also describes the first diagonal cut from high to low post on the strong side. Frequently used as a signal. [S1, p.234]

### Blind Pig
A **flash post from the weak-side baseline to the elbow or pinch post** when the offensive wing is being overplayed, setting up the wing for a backdoor cut once the pass goes to the flashing post. Classic pressure-release action usable anywhere on the court. [S1, p.234]

### BOB
Acronym: **Baseline Out-of-Bounds play** (under-the-basket OOB). [S1, p.234]

### Box Set
Offensive alignment where **four players (without the ball) form a box/square**, each approximately 15–17 feet apart, usually on or above the elbows and blocks. [S1, p.234]

### Brush Screen
A **cutter without the ball cuts inside and in front of the ball-handler** and goes to the corner, enabling the ball-handler to drive and slash to the basket. [S1, p.234]

### Bump / Bump Back
After setting a screen, the screener **flares, pops, or comes back to the ball** when his defensive player attempts to help. Frees the screener. [S1, p.235]

### Bury
A guard passing to a wing **cuts in front of the wing** to the strong-side corner. [S1, p.235]

### Buttonhook
(Football term) Player fakes a cross screen, **quickly stops, comes back to the ball, and seals his defender on his back**. Usually occurs in the lane. [S1, p.235]

### Centerfield
Area **above the circle/three-point line in the middle of the court** (offensive end) where a player positions to receive an outlet pass when teammates can't reverse the ball. Also provides defensive balance. Defensively: the area above the free-throw line nail for weak-side help. [S1, p.235]

### Chase
1. Low-post offensive player goes across the lane **in the same direction as the dribbler** in a pick and roll.
2. Player passes the ball across court or out of a double-team and **follows the pass** to get free for a return. [S1, p.235]

### Cherry Pick / Tighten / Leak Out
Defender contesting a jump shot **takes off for the offensive end** hoping for an easy basket following a defensive rebound before the shooter can recover. Also used to distract the shooter and break concentration. [S1, p.235]

### Circle
Post man without the ball **circles up or down the lane toward the ball** to create a passing lane for a doubled post man by circling the big man's defender. Also describes the second big man's action in a pick and roll. [S1, p.236]

### Corner
Area where the baseline and sideline meet; also describes **spacing in an overloaded offensive set** (e.g., triangle offense). [S1, p.236]

### Counter
Offensive player comes up from the baseline or over a screener to the weak side, using a **back screen to free himself** from his defender. Also called flaring off a back or jam screen. [S1, p.236]

### Coward's Press
*(See defensive glossary)* — Full-court press without trapping intent; mainly slows the offense. [S1, p.236]

### Crossing
Two offensive players run from one side to the other **with or without screening**, forcing defenders to switch or chase. [S1, p.237]

### Curl
Offensive player **runs tightly off a post screen** just outside the free-throw lane, practically rubbing shoulders with the screener, to run his defender off the screen and free himself or the screener for a catch-and-shoot. [S1, p.237]

### DHO
Acronym: **Dribble Hand-Off** between two offensive players. [S1, p.237]

### Diagonal Cut
Offensive cut from the weak side to the strong side **diagonally, across the defender's face**, to receive the ball. Considered one of the hardest cuts to defend. [S1, p.237]

### Diagonal Down Screen
Angled down screen usually set **from the high post for a player on the opposite low block**. [S1, p.237]

### Direct / Drop
Pass from the PG to a player **on or above the elbow**, who then makes a straight pass to a player posting up on the same side. [S1, p.238]

### Dive
Offensive player with the ball **drives directly at a teammate's defender** who is overplaying, forcing the defender to turn his head, enabling a backdoor cut to the basket. [S1, p.238]

### Double-Doubles
**Successive double screens** for players trying to receive in scoring position. Often staggered and difficult to defend; many come from the blind side. [S1, p.238]

### Double Screen
**Two-man shoulder-to-shoulder** baseline or other two-man screens. [S1, p.238]

### Drag
**High or wing PnR in offensive transition or early offense** — catches the defense by surprise in an awkward position. [S1, p.238]

### Dribble Used
Defensive pressure approach on a big man with the ball on the perimeter: **play him as if he has already used his dribble** to limit his ability to feed the post or reverse the ball. [S1, p.238]

### Duck In
Weak-side low post flashes or steps **into and across the near leg of his defender** toward the ball to receive a pass in scoring position inside the paint, sealing the defender on his back. [S1, p.238]

### Early Offense
**Secondary break situation**: fast break not available but the defense is not yet set. [S1, p.239]

### Elbow Area / Pinch Post
Spot **parallel to or slightly above/below the free-throw lane** where an offensive player posts with his back to the basket. Defenders try to pressure and deny all passes here. [S1, p.239]

### Empty / Clear Out
Term signaling a teammate that **the lane and side are clear** and the opponent is isolated — he can go one-on-one. [S1, p.239]

### EOG
Acronym: **End-of-Game play**. [S1, p.239]

### Fist
**Hand signal** on offense or defense designating a specific play or tactic. Example: signal for PnR, post-up, double-stack action, or trap. Teams add directional modifiers (up, down, out, right, left) to specify the exact play. [S1, p.239]

### Fist to Palm / Punch
**Direct post-up signal on the low block**. [S1, p.239]

### Flash / Weak-Side Cut
Quick, hard cut **from the weak side to the strong side**, mainly through the paint. [S1, p.239]

### Flat
Offensive set where **four players spread across the baseline** on either side of the free-throw lane. [S1, p.239]

### Flex Cut
**Horizontal baseline cut** by an offensive player over or under a post screener, usually away from the ball, to the strong side through the free-throw lane. Also a name for the entire offensive set. [S1, pp.239-240]

### Floppy
Offensive set with **wings crossing on the baseline off down screens** set by big men, creating a scissors action. [S1, p.240]

### Get
Player passes to a wing/perimeter player and **follows the pass to get the ball back**. Some pro teams use this to describe a side PnR. [S1, p.240]

### Go
Dribble drive **from one side of the court to the other** on a high PnR maneuver. [S1, p.240]

### G to F / G to G
- **G to F**: Guard-to-forward penetrating pass — moves the ball closer to the basket; most offenses begin here. [S1, p.240]
- **G to G**: Guard-to-guard horizontal pass — swings the ball across court; initiates two-guard front sets. [S1, p.240]

### Hawk Cut
Diagonal cut **from the high-post area perimeter on the ball or weak side to the strong-side post** or baseline-sideline area. Named for pro team usage in the 1970s–80s. [S1, p.241]

### Head Hunt
Having the **screener specifically screen the offensive player's defender** to free a particular offensive player to receive the ball. [S1, p.241]

### High Post
**Area from the free-throw line to above the top of the key or three-point line**. [S1, p.241]

### Hold
Offensive player makes his cut and **instead of clearing or screening across, maintains position** and posts his defender up on the low block. [S1, p.241]

### Horns
Offensive formation/signal for **a specific play** — each team defines it differently. Often describes a set with two big men at the elbows and the PG at the top. [S1, p.241]

### Ice / ISO
Abbreviation for **one-on-one isolation play** — all other offensive players clear away so the ball-handler goes one-on-one with his defender. [S1, p.242]

### Improve Position to Gain Possession
Principle: the **receiver of a pass from a pressured teammate must step out** and create a passing lane. Don't force the pass if angles haven't been established. [S1, p.242]

### Inside Out
Offensive tactic: **go inside first** to make the defense shrink/collapse/trap, then kick the ball out to a shooter. [S1, p.242]

### Jam Screen
**Back pick set for a player on his defender** after he has passed the ball and cut away — to free him for a flare. [S1, p.242]

### Kickback
Dribbler drives toward the middle **drawing the defense**, then passes back to an open player who can shoot or post to an inside player who has sealed in the lane. [S1, p.242]

### Lift
When posting up in the drop/direct action, the **2–3 uninvolved offensive players step out to the weak-side perimeter above the three-point line** to occupy their defenders and prevent easy help on the post feed. [S1, pp.242-243]

### Lob
Pass to a **fronted or overplayed post/perimeter player** who turns, seals/locks his defender, or fakes high and goes backdoor for a pass aimed at the front of the rim. [S1, p.243]

### Lock and Lob
**High-low action**: when the low post is being fronted, the post man seals/locks his defender on his back while the wing passes to the rim or to the high post for a lob to the low-post player. [S1, p.243]

### Loop / Split
Pressure-relief maneuver: **an offensive player without the ball splits two defenders laterally** to open a clear side for the dribbler or provide a throwback option when the dribbler is trapped. [S1, p.243]

### LSG / LSQ
- **LSG**: Last shot in a game.
- **LSQ**: Last shot in a quarter. [S1, p.244]

### Midpost
Area **outside the lane halfway between the foul line and the low block**. [S1, p.244]

### Misdirection Screen
Usually a **back screen set by the player farthest from the ball for the player nearest the ball** — often a surprise and difficult to defend. Can be a cross or flare screen. [S1, p.244]

### Mismatch
Defensive situation where a **taller player is guarded by a smaller or weaker player** (or one in foul trouble); offense directs the ball there to exploit the advantage. [S1, p.244]

### Motion
**Structured freelance offense**: five players passing, cutting, and screening, reversing the ball, reading the defense, and creating easy baskets off ball movement. All cuts are dictated by defensive reads. [S1, p.244]

### Multiple Screens
**Successive screens** set to free an offensive player trying to receive in scoring position, or to occupy the defense. [S1, p.245]

### Multiply
Team that just scored a **breakaway/steal basket immediately regroups and pressures full court** to create another scoring opportunity before the offense can recover. [S1, p.245]

### Need / Crunch
**Late-game must-have-a-basket play**. Scouting reports should emphasize the opponent's need/crunch play. [S1, p.245]

### Open
**Passing game without a post man** — similar to motion; emphasizes passing and cutting by all players. [S1, p.245]

### Penetrate and Pitch / Draw and Kick
Dribbler drives toward the goal and, **if stopped, pitches/kicks to a teammate for an open shot**. Effective vs. zone and man-to-man. [S1, p.245]

### Pick and Roll
**On-the-ball screen by a big man on the dribbler's defender** — designed to force a switch or help, creating a scoring opportunity before the defense can recover. Considered one of the most difficult offensive plays to defend. [S1, p.246]

### Pick the Picker / Screen the Screener
Offensive action where **the screener receives a screen from another teammate** after setting his screen, freeing him for a catch-and-shoot opportunity. [S1, p.246]

### Pin Down
**Screen from high to low**: the high man screens the low man's defender. Can be diagonal or vertical. [S1, p.246]

### Pistol
Signal for a **flare or jam screen by an inside player for a perimeter player** who has just passed the ball. Difficult to defend because defenders must quickly choose between a big and small switch; an offensive slip can follow the back screen. [S1, p.246]

### Pop
Offensive player **fakes a cross screen or inside cut, then pops back toward the ball** for a pass. [S1, p.246]

### Power / Punch
Term/signal for **passing the ball inside to a player on the low post**. [S1, p.246]

## Common Mistakes
1. Misusing the "lift" action → perimeter players fail to step out, allowing easy help on the post feed. [S1, p.242-243]
2. Forcing passes into a fronted post without executing the lock-and-lob — defense intercepts the entry. [S1, p.243]
3. Calling ATW but stopping at the first option — players never learn the full play. [S1, p.233]
4. Ignoring improve-position principle under pressure → passer forces bad pass; turnover results. [S1, p.242]
