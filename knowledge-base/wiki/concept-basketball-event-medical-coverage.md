# Basketball Event Medical Coverage Framework

## Summary
From local competitions to World Championships, basketball events require a systematic medical coverage plan that accounts for every zone of the arena — court, seating areas, press areas, VIP sections, exterior spaces, and the locker rooms. The team physician and medical coordinator must pre-plan evacuation routes, ambulance positioning, designated hospital networks, doping control room setup, and crisis protocols well before the first whistle.

The complexity scales dramatically with event size. At a local game, a single accreditation may cover all zones; at a Euroleague or FIBA World Championship, the arena is divided into restricted zones requiring specific multi-area accreditations for medical personnel who need to move freely throughout the facility [S10, pp.105-110].

## When to Use
- Planning medical coverage for any organized basketball competition
- Pre-event preparation meetings with security, police, and ambulance services
- Establishing reference hospital agreements for a tournament
- Setting up doping control procedures
- Creating crisis communication plans for major events

## Key Principles

### Court and Arena Logistics
1. **Pre-walk the evacuation route**: Before competition, physically walk the fastest path from court → locker room → ambulance. Account for obstacles: LED panels, court seats, camera cables, TV structures, and any raised overlay court surfaces [S10, p.105].
2. **Media and VIP zones require separate evacuation plans**: Media desks with equipment are difficult to evacuate; VIP sections often have single access points and heavy security. Plan multiple alternative evacuation paths for both [S10, p.105].
3. **Clean the court after entertainment**: Cheerleading, trampoline shows, and slam dunk contests leave slippery materials on the court. Medical staff must monitor game breaks and ensure full court cleanup before players return [S10, p.109].
4. **Alcohol is the most frequent external medical call**: Event security must enforce alcohol consumption limits; the medical team should be prepared for alcohol-related incidents in exterior zones [S10, p.106].

### Ambulance System
1. **Always have a replacement unit on standby**: Any time one ambulance transports a patient, a second ALS (Advanced Life Support) unit with physician and nurse must be on standby at the arena. Absence of ALS coverage may be grounds for game suspension [S10, p.106].
2. **Scale ambulance count to spectator numbers and risk level**: Use prior security/police meetings to estimate crowd size and fan behavior to determine the right number of vehicles [S10, p.106].
3. **Log arrival/departure times and verify parking**: Every session, confirm ambulances are in pre-assigned spots with clear emergency exit pathways [S10, p.106].
4. **ALS required for all phases**: Setup, competition, teardown — every phase of a major event requires ALS presence [S10, p.106].

### Designated Hospitals
1. **Analyze hospital capabilities before the event**: Verify 24-hour emergency service, radiology (XR, MRI, CT, ultrasound), lab capacity, ICU, and minor surgery teams. Note distance and ambulance travel time [S10, p.107].
2. **Establish a reference hospital for teams and VIPs**: Negotiate priority assistance agreements to reduce waiting time, ensure imaging access, and provide confidential care. Players without ID may use competition accreditation as identification [S10, p.107].
3. **Plan for helicopter transport if needed**: If the best trauma center is far, pre-designate a heliport space and establish transport protocols [S10, p.107].
4. **Account for local health factors**: Country health infrastructure, seasonal illness surges, endemic diseases, and holiday population growth all affect hospital availability [S10, p.107].
5. **Ensure medical insurance coverage**: Teams are typically responsible for their own medical expenses. Verify insurance covers the host country. Pay upfront and seek reimbursement if necessary [S10, p.107].

### Doping Control Room
1. **Separate from the medical room**: The doping control room must be in perfect hygienic condition, well-lit and ventilated, with a waiting area. It must comply with the Internal Regulations of Anti-Doping for basketball [S10, p.109].
2. **Locked access**: Only accredited personnel or those authorized by doping agents may enter. Game clock display inside is recommended.
3. **Adequate fluid supply**: Sufficient sealed drinks must be available for athletes providing samples.
4. **Drug-testing volunteers (chaperons)** need specific accreditation for court, locker rooms, pressroom, and doping control area [S10, p.108].

### Crisis Protocol
1. **Define a crisis management team**: Requires Crisis Director/Spokesperson, Security Coordinator, Press Officer, Services Coordinator, and Medical Officer [S10, p.109].
2. **Triggers for crisis activation**: Team member seriously injured/killed, spectators injured/killed, fights with multiple injured [S10, p.109].
3. **Medical officer role in a crisis**: Supervise medical response, mobilize extended ambulance support if needed, establish all medical facts, travel to hospital, and relay condition updates to the crisis team [S10, p.109].

### Out-of-Competition Assistance
1. **24/7 contact for multi-day events**: Provide all teams with the reference hospital address and a medical coordinator phone number available around the clock [S10, p.110].
2. **Shared hotel nursing station**: When teams share a hotel, a nursing station and physiotherapy room should be available. Elite teams often have their own private medical resources [S10, p.110].

### Accreditations
1. **Medical staff need multi-zone accreditation**: Medical personnel must move freely between court, locker rooms, press area, VIP sections, and exterior zones [S10, p.108].
2. **Use corporate accreditation + upgrade system**: For staff without individual accreditations, a bracelet or sticker upgrade grants access to restricted zones per shift [S10, p.108].
3. **Referees also need coverage**: Plan medical access to referee locker rooms during and after games [S10, p.108].

## Player Responsibilities
- **All players**: Must follow the designated evacuation path when injured. If transported to hospital, may use competition accreditation as ID.
- **Players regarding doping**: Must not take any medication, supplement, or substance without prior approval from the team physician [S10, p.117].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not pre-walking the evacuation route** → physically rehearse the path from court to ambulance before the event starts, accounting for all physical obstacles.
2. **Forgetting entertainment cleanup** → build cleanup time into the halftime/timeout entertainment schedule so the court is clear before play resumes.
3. **Single ambulance only** → always contract a replacement unit; ALS absence can cause game suspension.
4. **Assuming hospital quality** → verify ICU, imaging, and surgical availability at every designated hospital before the event.
5. **Generic accreditations for medical staff** → ensure medical team has verified multi-zone access so they can reach injured parties anywhere in the arena.
