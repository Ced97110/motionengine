# Basketball Training Load Monitoring (Accelerometry)

## Summary
A 2018 study (Staunton et al., *Sports* 2018, 6:69) was the first to assess relative exercise intensity in basketball training using accelerometry-derived Average Net Force (AvF_Net), a method suitable for sports with rapid changes in movement patterns. The study found that 64% of training time was spent in sedentary or very-light intensity activity, that few position-specific differences existed in training load, and that exercise dose varied by session within easy and moderate match-schedule weeks but not during hard match-schedule weeks. These findings have direct implications for how coaches structure practice, monitor athletes, and periodize training relative to competition schedules.

Unlike heart rate or VO₂ — which lag behind actual movement intensity due to cardiorespiratory inertia — accelerometry-derived force metrics can accurately quantify short-duration intermittent bursts, making them far more appropriate for basketball's stop-start nature.

## When to Use
- When designing practice schedules relative to an upcoming match (easy, medium, or hard competition week)
- When evaluating whether training is match-specific in its intensity profile
- When identifying periods of elevated injury risk across a season (higher exercise dose correlates with greater injury incidence)
- When deciding how much instruction time to include in practice vs. active drill time

## Key Principles
1. **Instruction time reduces match-specificity.** Because 64% of basketball training is sedentary/very-light (vs. ~59% of match-play), excessive coaching instruction standing periods pull training away from game-like demands. "Technical/tactical drills can be combined with conditioning goals in order to more closely replicate match demands."
2. **Heart rate and VO₂ are poor proxies for intermittent exercise intensity.** Cardiorespiratory lag means physiological measures do not reflect actual activity intensity moment-to-moment during basketball. Accelerometry fills this gap.
3. **Training load does not vary by position.** Front-court and back-court players receive similar exercise dose and intensity zone distribution in training — even though match-play shows clear positional differences. Coaches struggle to individualize positional load in team settings.
4. **Periodize within the competition week, not just across the season.** During easy and moderate match schedules, Session 2 (mid-week) had significantly higher exercise dose than Session 1 (recovery) and Session 3 (pre-match taper). During hard match schedules, no variation existed between sessions — suggesting coaches may flatten periodization when schedule pressure is highest.
5. **Higher exercise dose = higher injury risk.** Prior research links elevated cumulative training load during a season to greater injury incidence. Monitoring AvF_Net can flag high-risk periods before injuries occur.
6. **Objective feedback changes prescription.** Because coaches in this study received no data on player exercise dose, the training reflected only their intuitive prescription. Providing objective load data could allow coaches to better calibrate practice to match demands.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Receive essentially equivalent training exercise dose and intensity distribution in team settings. Coaches who want to individualize load must design position-specific drills or separate sessions.

## Variations
### Session Periodization by Match Schedule Difficulty
- **Easy/Medium schedule**: Use a 3-session microcycle with Session 1 as recovery (low load), Session 2 as the primary conditioning session (high load), and Session 3 as a pre-match taper (low load).
- **Hard schedule (2+ games/week)**: Load variation between sessions is difficult or impossible to maintain — all sessions tend to converge at a moderate level.

### Combining Skill and Conditioning Goals
Design technical/tactical drills with built-in conditioning demands (e.g., continuous ball-movement drills, transition-based 5-on-5) to reduce standing/instruction time while maintaining tactical teaching objectives.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Excessive standing instruction time** → Replace with brief cue + active repetition model. Demonstrate once, then let athletes move.
2. **Using heart rate alone to monitor practice intensity** → Heart rate lags 30-60 seconds behind actual effort in intermittent exercise. Supplement with time-in-motion or accelerometry data where available.
3. **No periodization during hard match schedules** → Even when games are frequent, plan at least a rough recovery/load/taper structure across the week.
4. **Assuming all positions need the same practice stimulus** → Positional match demands differ substantially (guards: more lateral movement, speed; forwards/centers: more contact, paint work). Team-wide drills obscure these differences.
