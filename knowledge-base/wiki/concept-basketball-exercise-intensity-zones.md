# Basketball Exercise Intensity Zones

## Summary
Relative exercise intensity during basketball training can be categorized into seven zones based on percentage of VO2 reserve (%VO2R), adapted from American College of Sports Medicine guidelines [S12, p.29]. Using accelerometry-derived AvFNet calibrated to individual Yo-Yo IR1 test data, each second of training can be assigned to one of seven zones from sedentary through supramaximal.

This system allows coaches to see exactly how much of each training session is spent in meaningful intensity ranges, and to compare intensity profiles across sessions, positions, and phases of the season. The key finding: **64.3% of basketball practice time is sedentary or very light intensity** — suggesting most practices are far less physically demanding than games [S12, p.30].

## When to Use
- Evaluating practice design: is enough time being spent at game-relevant intensities?
- Comparing training intensity across different practice types (skill day vs. live scrimmage)
- Identifying positional intensity profiles to customize individual conditioning
- Benchmarking training intensity against competition intensity data

## Key Principles
1. **VO2 Reserve is the correct relative measure** — VO2R (peak VO2 minus resting VO2) represents the usable aerobic range above rest, making it a true relative intensity measure regardless of fitness level [S12, p.29]
2. **Individual calibration is required** — AvFNet-to-VO2R relationships must be established individually via a Yo-Yo IR1 test (r² = 0.93–0.97) [S12, p.30]
3. **Supramaximal extrapolation is possible** — Because individual linear relationships extend beyond VO2max, supramaximal intensity (≥100% VO2R) can be estimated via extrapolation [S12, p.28]
4. **More vigorous intensity in later sessions** — Session 3 (last session before the next game) consistently had the highest vigorous intensity proportion, likely because match-simulation drills are scheduled last [S12, p.31]

## The Seven Zones

### Zone 1 — Sedentary (<20% VO2R)
Standing, minimal movement. In basketball practice this includes: waiting for instruction, watching demonstrations, substituting out, free throw shooting from a stationary position, early tactical walkthrough phases.

*Average in professional training: 40–52% of time (front-court vs. back-court combined)*

### Zone 2 — Very Light (20–<30% VO2R)
Slow walking, casual movement between drills, light stretching. Overlaps with active warm-up beginning phases and drill transitions.

*Front-court: ~21.6% of time; Back-court: ~14.8% of time* — significant positional difference [S12, p.30]

### Zone 3 — Light (30–<40% VO2R)
Jogging, slow footwork drills, skill repetition at below-game speed. Early-season technical drills, film walk-throughs on the court.

*Combined average: ~7.8–11.7% of time*

### Zone 4 — Moderate (40–<60% VO2R)
Medium-paced drills, half-speed scrimmage, tactical work. Represents the majority of organized team practice content.

*Combined average: ~7.8–10.1% of time*

### Zone 5 — Vigorous (60–<90% VO2R)
High-intensity competitive drills, 3-on-2 fast breaks, contested rebounding, press defense drills, conditioning-integrated skill work. The intensity level most representative of average game pace.

*Combined average: ~10.3–11.7% of time; higher in Session 3* [S12, p.31]

### Zone 6 — Maximal (90–<100% VO2R)
Near-maximum efforts: full-speed transition drills, top-speed sprint sequences, conditioning runs. Brief in duration but physiologically significant.

*Combined average: ~2.4–3.5% of time*

### Zone 7 — Supramaximal (≥100% VO2R)
Explosive bursts exceeding aerobic capacity: jump-stop sequences, explosive first steps, defensive sprints, collision rebounds. Back-court players spend significantly more time here than front-court players.

*Front-court: 1.3–2.2% of time; Back-court: 3.6–7.0% of time* — large positional difference, especially during the medium match schedule [S12, p.30]

## Coaching Application
- **Problem**: 64.3% of practice time in sedentary/very light zones is far below game demands
- **Solution**: Redesign drills to minimize standing time; reduce explanation time; use hustle-back rules; add conditioning components to skill drills
- **Positional prescription**: Back-court players already approach game intensity more closely; front-court players need additional high-intensity supplemental work to match game demands
- **Session 3 design**: The naturally higher vigorous intensity in Session 3 (last session before game) aligns with standard taper-to-game periodization — use this to intentionally schedule match-simulation work in Session 3

## Common Mistakes
1. **Assuming practice intensity mirrors game intensity** → Practice averages 293 N AvFNet but games are higher; the gap must be monitored and bridged [S12, p.30]
2. **Using the same prescription for front-court and back-court** → They experience the same practice at different relative intensities; position-specific supplemental conditioning is required [S12, pp.30–31]
3. **Not measuring supramaximal time** → Traditional HR monitoring cannot detect supramaximal efforts; these explosive bursts are disproportionately important for basketball performance [S12, p.28]
