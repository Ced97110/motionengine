# Triangle Offense

## Summary
The Triangle Offense (also called the Sideline Triangle or Triple-Post Offense) is a continuity half-court system developed by Tex Winter and popularized by Phil Jackson with the pro team (the iconic scorer/the top defensive wing era) and team (Bryant/O'Neal era). It has proven effective for nearly 60 years, from college basketball in the 1960s through multiple pro championships.

The system creates a three-player triangle on the strong side — wing, corner, and post — while placing the weak-side guard at the top of the circle and the weak-side wing on the far side. The offense reads the defense at every pass and responds with the best available action, making it extremely difficult to scout or scout and stop. "We are always in our offense and ready to operate." [S7, p.99]

## When to Use
- Half-court offensive sets against man-to-man defense
- When defense applies heavy ball pressure (pressure releases and lag principle)
- When trying to exploit a post mismatch (split cuts to the post)
- To wear down the defense over a full game with constant motion and cutting
- When building an egalitarian offense that doesn't over-rely on one star

## Key Principles
1. **Form the sideline triangle first** — PG (1) passes to wing (3) and fills the corner; weak-side guard (2) gets to the middle of half-court at the top of the circle; this creates the triangle of 3-5-1 on the strong side.
2. **Four second-pass reads** — After receiving the wing entry pass, 3 reads four looks in order: (1) center/post (5), (2) weak-side guard (2) at top of circle, (3) weak-side wing (4) on the backdoor step, (4) strong-side guard (1) in the corner. [S7, p.97]
3. **Passer is the first cutter** — After passing to the post, the wing becomes the first cutter, cutting to the side of the post to free that player. [S7, p.97]
4. **Continuity** — If no option develops, the ball swings and the triangle re-forms on the other side. "We are always in our offense and ready to operate!" [S7, p.99]
5. **Execution counts** — The system demands detail and fundamentals over athleticism. [S7, p.109]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (1)**: Initiates with entry pass to wing or corner; cuts after the pass (corner, speed cut, or solo cut); manages pressure releases (lag, blind pig, wing reverse)
- **SG (2)**: Spots up at garden/fan spot (top of circle, weak side) as second look; back cuts when overplayed; executes dribble weave; maintains defensive balance
- **SF (3)**: Receives wing entry pass; reads four second-pass options; makes split cuts, solo cuts, rebound screen cuts; can screen for 4 (screen-the-screener) when continuing
- **PF (4)**: Occupies weak-side wing or freeze spot; flashes high post when called; sets screens; reads whether to pop or cut to basket off screen-the-screener actions
- **C (5)**: Anchors the post; receives split-cut passes; kicks to garden spot (2) when doubled; passes to cutters; flashes to elbow on third/fourth look actions; screens and rolls

## Variations

### Split Cuts (Post Cuts) — First Look
After 3 passes to 5: 3 fakes inside then cuts baseline side of 5; 1 follows as close as possible. Multiple options: 3 screens for 1, 3 screens for 2, 3 screens for 4, or 5 fans to 2 at the garden spot. 5 can drop-pass to 1 speed-cutting to baseline. [S7, pp.97-98]

### Screen-the-Screener Action — First Look Continuation
If no one is open off split cuts: 3 cuts into lane and screens for 4 (screen-the-screener); 2 replaces 3; 4 pops to free-throw area for pass from 5 or rolls to basket. [S7, p.99]

### Rebound Screen Cut — First Look Option 3
After pass to 5, 3 starts as if going to the rebound but screens instead (rebound screen cut); 1 step-fakes on baseline and cuts off 3's screen in front of 5. [S7, p.99]

### Second Look — Pass to Weak-Side Guard
If 5 is not open, 3 passes to 2 at the top of the circle. 2 can shoot, pass to 5 ducking in, pass/dribble weave to 1, or drive with 4 reversing to rebound position. [S7, pp.100-101]

### Pinch Post Action — Second Look Option 2
4 pops out but is overplayed, so he reverses backdoor; 2 passes to 4 over-the-top. If 4 can't receive backdoor, he pops to elbow; 2 speed-cuts off 4 for a flip pass (two-man play); 2 drives and reads — if doubled, 4 reverse-pivots for return pass; 5 freezes at block. [S7, p.101]

### Third Look — Backdoor to Weak-Side Wing
Option 1: 3 passes to 4 at ball-side elbow; 2 makes backdoor speed cut (drop pass if head and shoulders past defender). Option 2: If 2 misses backdoor, he corners; 3 cuts for pass from 4; 3 drives or kicks to 5 or 2. If no option: 4 dribble weaves to 1 in the corner; triangle re-forms. [S7, pp.102-103]

### Fourth Look — Strong-Side Guard in Corner
3 passes to 1 in corner; 3 makes banana cut; 5 flashes to ball-side elbow; 5 screens and rolls on 1 driving to top of key; 3 screened by 4 on weak side; 2 fans away; 1 can shoot, drive, or pass to 5 on roll, 2, or 4. [S7, p.103]

### Solo Cut Series
Instead of corner fill, 1 makes a speed cut to free-throw line area or basket after passing to 3. 3 passes to 5 and makes a solo cut to either side of 5. Various screens follow depending on cuts. [S7, p.104]

## Pressure Releases

### Moment of Truth
The "moment of truth" is when 1 picks up his dribble 3 feet in front of his defender. 3 must time his pop-out to receive the ball exactly at this moment. The "line of truth" is the imaginary line across the floor 3 feet in front of the defender guarding the ball handler. [S7, p.105]

### Lag Principle
If 3 is not open, 1 passes to 2 who "lags" behind the line of truth (3+ feet) as a safety valve; 2 then passes to 4 popping out. Requires precise timing and considerable dribbling to execute. [S7, p.105]

### Blind Pig Action
If 2 is overplayed: 4 flashes to top of lane; 1 quickly passes to 4; 2 speed-cuts backdoor for a drop pass. If 2 not open: 2 freezes weak side; 1 cuts over top of 4 for a pass and drives or dribbles to operating spot. Further options: 4 can pass to 3 and screen down for 1; 4 can also pass to 1 and set a screen for him. [S7, pp.106-108]

### Wing Entry on the Blind Pig
1 can't pass to 4, so passes to 3; 4 then back screens for 1; 3 looks for a high over-the-top pass to 1 cutting (right side cleared). Wing screen-and-roll follows: 4 sets side screen for 3 driving to lane, then screens for 2 in corner. [S7, p.108]

### Blur Screen
If 2 is overplayed or violated the lag principle: 2 cuts first, 4 cuts right off 2's tail ("blur screen"); 4 receives from 1; 1 speed-cuts backside; 3 screens for 2 coming up for defensive balance. [S7, p.109]

### Wing Reverse
If 1 can't pass to 2 or 3, he passes to 5 at ball-side elbow; 3 cuts backdoor ("wing reverse"); 5 drop-passes if 3 is head and shoulders past defender. If 3 not open: 1 screens 2 ("guard squeeze action"); 2 cuts to 3 feet in front of 5; 5 passes to 2 or 1 rolling to free-throw area. [S7, p.109]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stopping a cut mid-route** → Always complete the cut so the passer can anticipate your position; a half-finished cut leads to turnovers.
2. **Forming the triangle without spacing** → 1 must fill the corner, 2 must be at the top of circle — if spacing collapses, all four reads are compromised.
3. **Rushing the second pass** → 3 must read the defense before choosing among the four looks; the offense's strength is in reading, not in scripted action.
4. **Ignoring the garden/fan spot** → 2 at the top/weak side is the critical safety valve when the post is doubled — failure to spot up there kills the spacing.
5. **Cutting without awareness** → If all four off-ball players cut simultaneously, a traffic jam results and no one is open. [S7, p.111 — Continuity chapter cross-reference]
