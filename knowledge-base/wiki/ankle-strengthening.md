# Ankle Strengthening

## Summary
Ankle strength is the foundation of stability during running, jumping, cutting, and changing direction. The most common injury in basketball is the ankle sprain, which typically occurs when a player lands on an opponent's foot while rebounding. Despite this, ankle strengthening is frequently overlooked in basketball training programs [S2, p.137].

Plantar flexion strength (gastrocnemius/soleus) is especially important for plyometric activities — it is the primary driver of push-off power for running and jumping. Strong peroneals provide lateral ankle stability that prevents inversion sprains. All ankle musculature must be trained for complete protection and explosive performance [S2, pp.137-138].

## When to Use
- Pre-season and in-season as ongoing maintenance
- Following any ankle sprain as part of rehabilitation progression
- When a player shows recurrent ankle instability or fatigue-related sprains
- As a complement to proprioception training

## Key Principles
1. **Train all planes of motion**: Dorsiflexion, plantar flexion, inversion, and eversion must all be addressed [S2, p.137]
2. **Develop elastic ability**: Strength alone is insufficient — develop the muscle's elasticity for explosive push-off [S2, p.138]
3. **Add lateral work**: Lateral band walks develop both ankle and hip strength essential for defensive slides and cuts [S2, p.139]
4. **Progress load gradually**: Begin with body weight, add resistance band, then progress to added weight (dumbbells)
5. **Maintain throughout the season**: Ankle strength declines if not trained consistently; don't treat it as pre-season only work [S2, p.137]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Develop plantar flexion and peroneal strength for push-off and lateral stability. Integrate ankle exercises into weekly training to maintain readiness for the forces of cutting, driving, and landing.

## Variations
### Resistive Ankle Motion (Rehab/Beginner)
Seated with resistance band around foot — perform full range of motion: dorsiflexion, plantar flexion, inversion, eversion. Start with light band, increase resistance [S2, p.137].

### Standing Heel Raise (Beginner → Intermediate)
Stand with knees extended; lift heels off floor and lower slowly. Progress by holding dumbbells. Builds gastrocnemius/soleus plantar flexion strength essential for jumping and sprinting [S2, p.138].

### Band Lateral Walk (Intermediate → Advanced)
Resistance band around ankles; maintain minisquat position; walk sideways. Develops lateral ankle stabilization and hip strength simultaneously. Critical for defensive positioning and explosive lateral cuts [S2, p.139].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Ignoring ankle work entirely** → Integrate ankle strengthening into every week of training, year-round
2. **Only doing heel raises** → Must train all four planes of ankle motion (dorsiflexion, plantar flexion, inversion, eversion)
3. **Not maintaining neutral foot position during lateral walk** → Feet must stay neutral to properly target the targeted muscles; avoid toeing in or out
4. **Stopping at body weight** → Progress to added resistance once body weight is mastered [S2, pp.137-139]
