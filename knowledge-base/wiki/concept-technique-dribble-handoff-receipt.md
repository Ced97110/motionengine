# Dribble Handoff — Receipt

## Summary
A dribble handoff (DHO) is a live-ball exchange where the ball-handler dribbles toward a stationary or moving teammate and hands the ball off on contact, while that teammate uses the handoff like a ball-screen to attack downhill. This page covers the receipt side: the cutter's footwork, the timing of the hand-meet, and the read tree immediately after catch. The DHO is a screen and a pass in one action; receipt discipline makes or breaks it.

## When to Use
- As a primary action for a shooting guard or wing off a high-post exchange
- In flow offense where an initial pass is declined in favor of a DHO
- As a counter when the defense denies the standard wing entry pass

## Key Principles
1. Receiver approaches at an angle — a straight-line cut to the ball is easy to deny. Curl the cut to the passer's outside shoulder.
2. Contact the passer's near hip on the receipt — a legal body shield against the trailing defender.
3. Two-foot catch is preferred when the receiver intends to shoot; one-foot catch when attacking.
4. First dribble after receipt goes away from the defender's anticipated path — use the handoff as a legal screen.
5. Eye discipline: read the big's drop or switch decision on the first dribble, not before.

## Player Responsibilities
- **Receiver (2 or 3)**: angled cut, contact the passer's hip, read on first dribble
- **Passer (4 or 5)**: live-dribble delivery to the receiver's outside hand, hold after the handoff to act as the screen
- **Weak-side spacer**: maintain corner spacing — a collapsing spacer ruins the drive

## Common Mistakes
- Receiver approaches straight-line — defender jumps the exchange
- Receiver catches with body already turned downhill — loses shooting option
- Passer releases the ball before contact — DHO becomes a regular handoff, screen value lost
