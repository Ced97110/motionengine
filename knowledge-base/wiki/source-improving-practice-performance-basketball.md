# Improving Practice Performance Basketball — Summary

## Overview
This source (S12) is a collection of peer-reviewed sports science research articles published in *Sports* (MDPI, 2018) that are directly applicable to basketball coaching, conditioning, and game strategy. Unlike the coaching manuals in this wiki's primary sources, S12 brings an evidence-based, research-grade perspective to basketball practice and performance questions.

The ingested section (pp.32–51) covers three distinct research articles addressing: (1) how to accurately measure and manage exercise intensity and training load in basketball practice, (2) which scoring strategies empirically separate winning from losing teams in elite women's basketball, and (3) how body composition varies by position and changes across seasons and college years in NCAA Division I basketball athletes.

The common thread across all three studies is **objective monitoring** — of training load, game performance, and athlete physiology — as the foundation for evidence-based coaching decisions.

## Key Themes
1. **Training load and match-specificity**: Practice sessions contain significantly more sedentary/low-intensity time than matches. Coaches who embed conditioning within technical drills close this gap.
2. **Evidence-based game strategy**: Paint points, forced turnovers, second chance points, and bench scoring differentiate winners from losers — not fast break points at elite women's tournament level.
3. **Body composition by position and time**: Guards are leaner than forwards; women basketball players lose muscle mass during the season; structured resistance training builds FFM across college years.
4. **Athlete monitoring as a coaching tool**: Whether measuring accelerometry-derived exercise dose, game-scoring statistics, or body fat %, the recurring message is that objective data enables more precise and effective coaching interventions.

## Unique Contributions
S12 is the only source in this wiki that brings peer-reviewed experimental/observational research to coaching questions. It provides:
- **Quantitative benchmarks** (body composition norms, exercise intensity zone distributions)
- **Causal evidence** (which scoring strategies actually win games, not just correlate with winning)
- **Monitoring methodology** (why accelerometry > heart rate for intermittent exercise; how to use BOD POD for BC tracking)
- **Women's basketball specific data** — most coaching literature focuses on men; S12 provides evidence specifically from elite and collegiate women's programs

## Notable Quotes

> "An even greater focus on force production should be utilized when training women for power." — Fields et al., p.50
