# General Adaptation Syndrome and Basketball Training

## Summary
The **General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)**, originally outlined by Hans Selye (1936, refined 1956) and also called the **supercompensation cycle**, is the physiological model that explains how the body responds to training stress. Understanding GAS is essential for coaches designing basketball training programs — it dictates when to load players, when to back off, and why consistent progressive overload is required for physical improvement [S2, pp.2-3].

In short: training applies stress → the body is disrupted → the body repairs and rebuilds *above* the previous baseline → if the stress is appropriately re-applied at the right time, the player improves. Apply stress too early, too often, or too intensely → exhaustion and injury. Apply too little stress → no adaptation and wasted training time.

## When to Use
- When planning weekly, monthly, or seasonal training loads
- When deciding when to increase weights, jump heights, or running intensity
- When a player is showing signs of overtraining (stuck at detraining phase)
- When returning a player from injury — restart the stress/adaptation cycle at appropriate loads

## Key Principles
1. **Alarm phase** — A training stimulus disrupts the body's homeostasis. Performance temporarily dips as the body is stressed [S2, p.2].
2. **Resistance phase** — The body repairs and recovers, returning toward homeostasis. This is the active recovery window after a training session [S2, p.2].
3. **Supercompensation phase** — The body adapts *above* the previous baseline, becoming better equipped for the stress it encountered. This is the ideal window to apply the next training stimulus [S2, p.2].
4. **Exhaustion/detraining phase** — If the stimulus is applied too frequently or with too great an intensity before recovery is complete, performance drops below baseline. If training ceases entirely, the supercompensation gains are lost [S2, pp.2-3].
5. **Progressive overload is required** — The body adapts to a given stress level. To continue improving, stress must be progressively increased (heavier loads, greater jump heights, faster running speeds) [S2, p.3].
6. **Assess before prescribing** — Players should be assessed for physical deficits, needs, and goals so training stress is appropriately calibrated. A stress level that triggers adaptation in one player may be exhausting for another [S2, p.3].

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Players are responsible for honest communication about fatigue, soreness, and performance dips so coaches can adjust loads appropriately.
- **Coaches**: Monitor training logs, performance metrics, and player feedback to time loading and recovery windows correctly.

## Variations
### In-Season Training
Loads are reduced but intensity is maintained. The goal is to stay in the supercompensation window without generating alarm-phase fatigue before games.

### Off-Season Development Block
Full alarm-resistance-supercompensation cycles can be run with 1-2 week loading blocks followed by a deload week before the next loading block.

### Return-to-Play
Post-injury athletes restart GAS cycles at very low stress levels (alarm phase with minimal load) and progress through the cycle slowly, confirming adaptation before advancing.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Training at the same intensity indefinitely** → Body adapts to the stress level and no further gains occur. Must progressively overload.
2. **Loading before full recovery** → Compressing the supercompensation window pushes players into exhaustion/detraining, increasing injury risk and decreasing performance.
3. **Detraining during the season** → Reducing training load to zero during the season eliminates supercompensation gains earned in the off-season. Maintain minimum effective dose.
4. **One-size-fits-all loading** → Players at different fitness levels have different homeostasis baselines. A load that creates healthy stress for one player may exhaust another.
