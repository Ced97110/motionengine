# Concussion Management in Basketball

## Summary
Sport-related concussion (SRC) is a significant health concern in basketball at all levels. Basketball consistently ranks in the top 5 sports for concussion incidence. Unlike football or ice hockey, basketball players wear no helmets, making biomechanical study and prevention more challenging. SRC is defined as a traumatic brain injury induced by biomechanical force, producing a physiological disturbance without structural change on imaging. Diagnosis is clinical, based on history and physical exam. The current standard leans toward multimodal tools such as the SCAT5. Early sub-symptom exercise (after 24–48 h rest) accelerates recovery [S10, pp.225-230].

## When to Use (Relevance)
- Sideline evaluation after any head impact, collision, or fall on the court
- Pre-season baseline testing and education
- Managing any player with post-concussion symptoms
- Return-to-play decision-making after suspected or confirmed SRC

## Epidemiology
- ~300,000 Americans affected by SRC annually; majority under age 20 [S10, p.227]
- High school basketball (2005–2014):
  - Girls: practice 0.11/1000 AEs, competition 0.74/1000 AEs
  - Boys: practice 0.08/1000 AEs, competition 0.33/1000 AEs
  - 4th–5th leading cause of injury in practice; 2nd in competition (behind ligament sprain) [S10, p.227]
- Collegiate basketball (2005–2014):
  - Women: practice 0.35, competition 1.00/1000 AEs
  - Men: practice 0.45, competition 1.26/1000 AEs
  - Top 5 injury cause in practice; 2nd in competition [S10, p.227]
- NBA (2006–2014): average of 15 concussions per season [S10, p.227]
- WNBA combine (2000–2008): 7.1% of 500 professional women had suffered a concussion [S10, p.227]

## Definition and Common Features
SRC placed on the traumatic brain injury spectrum per the 5th International Conference on Concussion in Sport:
- Force may be direct (head) or indirect (force transmitted from elsewhere on the body)
- **No structural change** on imaging — physiological disturbance
- Symptoms can be immediate or evolve over minutes to hours
- Wide range of symptoms (physical, cognitive, emotional); LOC not required
- Symptoms not explained by drugs, alcohol, or other conditions
- Resolution is typically sequential [S10, p.226]

## Pathophysiology
- Impact → acceleration/deceleration forces on neuronal structures
- Complex neurochemical cascade: axonal membrane stretching, unregulated ion channel outflow, glutamate release, increased Na/K ATP-pump activity → **depleted energy stores (glucose)**
- Injury-related decrease in resting cerebral blood flow adds to energy mismatch
- Cerebral blood flow returns to normal as symptoms resolve
- Repetitive injury → more prolonged deficits
- Most common basketball mechanism: **elbow-to-head contact during rebounding** [S10, p.228]

## Diagnosis Framework

### Pre-Season Preparation
1. **Preparticipation physical evaluation** including concussion history
2. Screen for: learning disorders, ADHD, mood disorders, headache disorders, current medications
3. **Emergency Action Plan (EAP)** for courtside emergencies
4. Baseline testing (NCAA recommended): symptom checklist, cognitive evaluation, balance testing
5. **Educate athletes** on concussion risks — delayed reporting = longer recovery time [S10, pp.228-229]

### Sideline Evaluation Sequence
1. **Screen for emergent transfer needs**:
   - Loss of consciousness
   - Tonic posturing
   - Balance disturbance
   - Cervical spine trauma
   - Neurological deficit, recurring emesis, worsening headache/mental status → **immediate ED transfer**
2. If no emergent concerns: brief sideline screening for SRC (not a final diagnosis)
3. If screening positive → full evaluation in quiet, distraction-free environment (training room/locker room)
4. **Serial examination**: SRC is an evolving injury; symptoms change over minutes to hours [S10, pp.229]

### Key Assessment Domains
- **History**: mechanism, current symptoms (headache, "fog," emotional changes)
- **Balance and gait**: unsteadiness, slowed reaction
- **Neurocognition**: information processing, speech
- **Neurological exam**: gross sensation, muscular strength, cranial nerve function
- **Vestibular-ocular function** (office setting)
- **Cervical spine** (office setting)

### Critical Rules
- "When in doubt, sit them out"
- **An athlete diagnosed with SRC must never return to activity on the same day** — critical for basketball with multiple games or practice sessions in one day [S10, p.230]
- No licensed medical professional present → athlete removed, not returned until evaluated

## Assessment Tools

### Sport Concussion Assessment Tool (SCAT5)
- Combines: immediate/acute assessment, rapid neurological screen, graded symptom checklist, SAC cognitive testing (immediate/delayed word recall, digits backwards), Maddocks questions, mBESS balance assessment
- For use by medical professionals only; minimum 10 minutes to administer
- Ages 13+; Child SCAT5 for ages 5–12
- Lay person version: **Concussion Recognition Tool 5 (CRT5)** [S10, p.230]

### Balance Error Scoring System (BESS)
- 3 × 20-second stance trials: double leg, single leg (non-dominant), tandem stance
- Performed on both firm and foam surfaces; eyes closed
- Errors counted: opening eyes, hands off hips, falling, >30° hip turn, unable to return in >5 sec [S10, p.230]

## Recovery and Return to Play

### Key Evidence-Based Principles
- **New guidance**: Sub-symptom aerobic exercise should begin after only 24–48 h of rest (not full rest until asymptomatic)
- **Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test (BCTT)**: athlete walks on treadmill with increasing speed and incline until concussive symptoms return → identifies target heart rate for exercise prescription
- Prescribing sub-symptom threshold aerobic exercise within 1 week of SRC → **reduced incidence of delayed recovery beyond 30 days** [S10, p.225]

### Persistent Post-Concussive Symptoms
- Symptoms continuing beyond expected recovery window:
  - Adults: > 2 weeks
  - Children: > 4 weeks [S10, p.226]

### Repetitive SRC Concerns
- Increased recovery length
- Increased risk of concussion from less forceful impact
- Increased severity of symptoms
- **CTE** (Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy): progressive neurodegenerative disease with mood, behavior, and cognitive symptoms; **no current direct causal link** to concussions confirmed [S10, p.226]

## Key Principles
1. SRC diagnosis is clinical — no imaging or blood test confirms it
2. Early sub-symptom exercise accelerates recovery; complete rest is no longer recommended beyond 24–48 h
3. Basketball's lack of helmets makes biomechanical data collection and prevention harder than in football
4. Elbow-to-head during rebounding is the most common concussion mechanism in basketball
5. Delayed reporting = longer return-to-play; pre-season education is essential
6. Never same-day return after SRC diagnosis

## Common Mistakes
1. **Completing the game or practice after a suspected concussion** → longer recovery, increased re-injury risk
2. **Complete rest until symptom-free** → outdated; early sub-symptom exercise shown to be beneficial
3. **Using SCAT5 as a standalone diagnostic tool** → must be used in conjunction with clinical judgment by trained medical professional
4. **Missing gradual symptom evolution** → SRC symptoms may not appear until minutes-to-hours post-impact; serial exam required
5. **Not establishing an EAP before the season** → delays response to emergent situations
