# Pin Screen

## Summary
The pin screen is an off-ball screen set on a defender guarding a player who is cutting toward the ball and away from the basket on the perimeter. The screen literally "pins" the defender inside, allowing the cutter an open catch-and-shoot opportunity on the perimeter. After setting the screen, the screener immediately reverse pivots to seal his defender and post up, creating a dangerous two-option attack: the perimeter shooter and the low-post scorer. [S6, pp.261-264]

## When to Use
- When a perimeter player (wing or corner) needs to cut toward the ball to receive a pass
- When the ball is at the top of the key or opposite wing and the screener's defender is near the low block
- When the defense is trapping or aggressively helping on cuts — the screener's post-up becomes the primary option
- When you want to create a simultaneous inside-out threat

## Key Principles
1. The cutter (e.g., 03) must first use a V-cut to set the defender up before using the pin screen — a poorly set-up defender defeats the screen's purpose.
2. The screener (e.g., 04) sets the screen on the **defender** (X3) guarding the cutter, pinning that defender in.
3. As the cutter scrapes off the screener's **lowest shoulder**, the cutter should already have hands ready, shoulders turning inward toward the passer, and footwork controlled for an immediate front pivot.
4. The cutter pivots off the **inside heel** with a 180-degree front pivot to square up to the basket in triple-threat position upon receiving the pass.
5. Immediately after screening, the screener **reverse pivots** off the contact foot (closest to defender), swinging the free foot around to seal the defender and post up.
6. The reverse pivot gives the screener immediate vision of the ball and passer plus a position advantage over the defender — regardless of whether the defender goes low, high, or switches.
7. The passer (01) must read both the cutter and the screener: if the defender rushes the cutter, pass inside to the posting screener; if a closeout comes, the cutter can shot-fake and feed the post.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (01)**: Kill the dribble and read both options — pass to the perimeter cutter for the open shot, or pass directly to the posting screener (04) at the mid-post block. Must use proper passer pivoting to deliver the ball to interior teammates.
- **SG/SF (02/03)**: Execute a V-cut to set the defender up. Scrape off the screener's lowest shoulder. Pre-set hands and shoulders toward the passer. Pivot off inside heel (front pivot, 180°) to square up. Read: shoot off the catch, drive, or pass back inside to 04.
- **PF (04)**: Set the pin screen on the cutter's defender. Immediately reverse pivot off the contact foot to seal the defender and post up at the mid-post/low-block area. Maintain position advantage with proper footwork to receive the interior pass.
- **C (05)**: May be a secondary perimeter passer; must also be prepared to execute passer pivoting footwork to deliver a ball inside if 01's vision is denied.

## Variations
### Slip the Pin Screen (04's Counter)
If the defensive helper (X4) commits to the low side of the screen to stop the screener's post-up, 04 slips the screen early and cuts directly to the mid-post block before X4 can recover. This is especially effective when the defense over-helps. [S6, p.263]

### Switch Defense Counter
If the defense switches X3 and X4 on the pin screen, 04 now has a size or speed advantage on the new (smaller) defender X3 and can post up even more aggressively. The switch is the easiest counter to exploit. [S6, p.263]

### High-Side Help Counter
If X4 hedges on the high side of 04's pin screen, 04 uses the **upper (top) foot as the pivot foot** and swings the lower foot through, creating more room in the low post and better drop-step/shooting angles to the basket. [S6, pp.263-264]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Cutter fails to set up the defender first** → The pin screen is ineffective if the defender is already in good position. Always execute a hard V-cut before using the screen.
2. **Cutter's footwork is uncontrolled coming off the screen** → Forces a jump stop or extra dribble instead of an immediate scoring opportunity. Practice the inside-heel front pivot repeatedly.
3. **Screener watches the cutter instead of reverse pivoting immediately** → Loses the position advantage. The reverse pivot must happen at first contact, not after seeing where the ball goes.
4. **Passer ignores the screener posting up** → The two-option attack is the play's strength. Reading only the cutter lets the defense recover.
5. **Screener uses the wrong pivot foot** → If X4 is on the high side, use the top (upper) foot as pivot; if X4 is on the low side, use the lower foot. Choosing incorrectly reduces scoring angles.
