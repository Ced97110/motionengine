# The Rule of Three and the Rule of Six

## Summary
The Rule of Three and the Rule of Six are Paul Wade's two foundational programming rules for explosive power training in *Explosive Calisthenics*. Together they define both the rep range within each set and the total volume of work per exercise per session. These rules are deliberately simple — designed to eliminate the complexity of traditional plyometric programming while ensuring the athlete always trains at maximum speed-strength output [S9, pp.4-9].

For basketball coaches and athletes, these two rules provide a practical, equipment-free framework for programming jumps, power push-ups, and other explosive movements without confusion or overtraining.

## When to Use
- Any time an athlete is training for speed, explosiveness, and quickness (not endurance or muscle mass)
- When programming jumps, power push-ups, and other explosive power movements from the [[concept-explosive-six-hierarchy]]
- Both rules apply ONLY to power moves — skill moves (kip-ups, flips) use time surfing and consolidation training instead

## Key Principles

### The Rule of Three
**Never exceed 3 reps per set when training for speed-power.** [S9, p.4]

Rationale:
- In a set of 10 squat jumps, the *first* rep (after proper warm-up) is the fastest. By rep 10, fatigue toxins have built up, energy is depleted, and the jump is the *slowest*. You are training your body to move slowly [S9, p.5]
- Stopping after 1-3 reps ensures every rep is trained at maximum speed
- Safety: on high-velocity explosive movements, form breakdown on rep 10 is far more likely to cause injury than on reps 1-3 [S9, p.5]
- Low reps are sufficient for power adaptation: "Some of the elite powerlifters and weightlifters become the strongest human beings on the planet on a diet of very low-rep sets" [S9, p.5]

Valid set structures under the Rule of Three:
- 1-rep sets (singles)
- 2-rep sets (doubles)
- 3-rep sets (triples)
- Hybrids (e.g., 1×3, 1×2, 1×1 in sequence)

### The Rule of Six
**Total working reps for each exercise must equal a multiple of six.** [S9, p.6]

| Level | Total Working Reps |
|---|---|
| Beginner | 6 reps |
| Intermediate | 12 reps |
| Advanced | 18 reps |

"Working reps" = all reps performed after warming up. Warm-up reps do not count.

### Combined Example — Beginner Squat Jumps (6 total reps)
Possible set/rep combinations:
- **6×1** — Six singles. Maximum focus per rep; no rebound effect; takes longest
- **3×2** — Three doubles. Exploits some myotatic rebound; good focus
- **2×3** — Two triples. "Bread-and-butter" of explosive training; efficient
- **3×1, 1×3** — Hybrid. Three singles followed by a triple
- **1×3, 1×2, 1×1** — Descending hybrid

"There are plenty of roads to Mecca, my friend. There are lots of different options... as long as you follow the Rule of Three and the Rule of Six." [S9, p.8]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Choose a set/rep combination that suits your psychology (some prefer singles, some triples — both work equally well). Mix approaches session-to-session [S9, p.9]

## Variations
### Beginner Level (6 total reps)
Best entry point: new to explosive training, returning from layoff or injury, or the current step is extremely difficult to perform even once.

### Intermediate Level (12 total reps)
Default for athletes who have established a training base and are making regular progress.

### Advanced Level (18 total reps)
For athletes who can easily handle at least step 7 in their current exercise chain.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Exceeding 3 reps per set for power work** → You begin training slower movement patterns. Switch to endurance training if that's your goal; stick to ≤3 reps if you want speed [S9, p.6]
2. **Doing too much total volume** → "Explosives take their toll on the joints, soft tissues and even the bones — an excessive amount will actually increase your likelihood for injury." Stick to low-to-moderate work performed well [S9, p.9]
3. **Sitting or lying down between sets** → Tells the nervous system to downregulate. Stay on your feet; rest 10-30 seconds, maximum ~3 minutes [S9, p.10]
4. **Racing to the Master Step** → That's fairly easy to do. The goal is *linear improvement* at each step, not reaching the top as fast as possible [S9, p.3]
5. **Applying Rule of Three/Six to skill movements** → These rules are for power moves only. Kip-ups, flips, and other skill exercises use time surfing and consolidation training

## Rest Between Sets
- Rest as long as needed to recover your breath: typically **10-30 seconds**
- Maximum rest: **~3 minutes** — beyond this, neurological benefits of the previous set begin to dissipate
- Never sit down or lie down between sets
- "You are not fatiguing yourself during your explosives work the way you would during a muscle-building set" [S9, p.10]

## Level Classification
- **Beginner**: New to explosive training; returning from layoff/injury; current exercise is extremely difficult even for one rep
- **Intermediate**: Does not fit beginner or advanced criteria
- **Advanced**: Can easily handle step 7 of the chain being worked [S9, p.9]
