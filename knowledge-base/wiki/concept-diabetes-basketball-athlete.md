# Diabetes Mellitus Management for Basketball Athletes

## Summary
Diabetes mellitus (DM) — both type 1 and type 2 — does not preclude competitive basketball participation. NBA players including Adam Morrison, Chris Dudley, and Gary Forbes have all competed with type 1 DM at the highest professional level. However, diabetes requires structured management protocols specific to the demands of basketball, which is classified as a high-intensity exercise using both aerobic and anaerobic energy systems. Coaches, athletic trainers, and team physicians need to understand pre-exercise glucose protocols, hypoglycemia recognition, and the unique risk of late-onset post-exercise hypoglycemia (LOPEH).

The global burden of diabetes is massive (415 million adults in 2015, projected 642 million by 2040), and basketball programs at all levels should be prepared to manage this condition safely.

## When to Use
- Pre-participation physical evaluation (PPE) screening
- Pre-practice and pre-game management for any athlete with diagnosed DM
- When an athlete shows acute symptoms during practice or competition
- Post-game/practice recovery monitoring for athletes with type 1 DM
- When designing conditioning programs and nutrition plans for athletes with DM

## Key Principles

### Pre-Exercise Glucose Testing Algorithm
1. **Blood glucose < 100 mg/dL**: Defer exercise; athlete must ingest minimum 30 grams of carbohydrate and retest in 15 minutes
2. **Blood glucose 100–250 mg/dL**: Proceed with exercise; recheck glucose every 30 minutes or if any symptoms of hypoglycemia appear
3. **Blood glucose > 250 mg/dL**: Check urine for ketones first
   - **Ketones negative**: Proceed with exercise; check glucose every 15–30 minutes (even above 300 mg/dL, with extra caution)
   - **Ketones positive**: Defer exercise; hydrate; consider insulin; recheck ketones in 15–30 minutes

### Hypoglycemia Recognition
**Early/mild signs** (may mimic normal exercise response — be vigilant):
- Hunger, anxiety, sweating, tremor, palpitations, feeling of impending doom

**Advanced signs** (may be first recognized by teammates or staff):
- Weakness or fatigue, speech changes, lack of coordination, vision changes, odd behavior or confusion

**Emergency response** (unresponsive athlete): Use parenteral glucagon or intravenous dextrose — these must be available at all times when caring for an athlete who uses exogenous insulin.

### Insulin Management Considerations
- **Type 1 DM**: Main treatment is exogenous insulin
- **Injection site**: Avoid injecting into exercising muscles or overlying subcutaneous tissue (increased absorption → hypoglycemia risk). In basketball athletes: **avoid legs and arms; use abdomen or low back**
- **Pre-competition dose adjustment**: A 30–50% reduction in short-acting insulin with the meal preceding competition may be needed; must be individualized
- **Insulin pumps**: Athletes may disconnect pumps and use as-needed injections during practice and competition; no specific sport rules govern pump use

### Late-Onset Post-Exercise Hypoglycemia (LOPEH)
- Can occur **6–12 hours (up to 28 hours)** after exercise completion
- Mechanism: Increased glucose utilization for glycogen replenishment + increased insulin sensitivity
- **High danger**: Can occur during sleep; associated with seizures, arrhythmias, and death
- **Prevention**: Post-exercise calorie intake combining carbohydrates with fat and protein
- **Team physician/athletic trainer must have emergency glucagon and IV dextrose accessible**

### Type 2 DM Considerations
- Exercise (including basketball) is the **most important treatment** for type 2 DM
- Skeletal muscle is the main site of glucose utilization
- Athletes on oral or injectable non-insulin medications may need dose reduction on intense training days
- Some athletes may manage type 2 DM with diet and exercise alone

## Diagnosis Criteria (Table 71.1)
Any ONE of the following is sufficient:
- Symptoms of hyperglycemia + random plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL
- Fasting plasma glucose (8+ hr fast) ≥126 mg/dL
- 2-hour plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL during OGTT
- HbA1c ≥6.5%

## Common Mistakes
1. **Missing early hypoglycemia signs during high-intensity play** → symptoms like sweating and anxiety mimic normal exercise response; establish a monitoring plan with the athlete pre-game.
2. **Injecting insulin into the exercising limb** → dramatically increases absorption rate and hypoglycemia risk; always use abdomen or low back in basketball athletes.
3. **Not planning for LOPEH** → athletes and families must understand the 6–28 hour post-exercise risk window; have a nighttime snack and monitoring plan in place.
4. **Not stocking emergency glucagon** → any athletic trainer/team physician caring for a type 1 DM athlete must have parenteral glucagon and IV dextrose accessible.
5. **Failing to individualize dose adjustments** → insulin needs change as fitness improves across the season; regular medical reassessment is required.
