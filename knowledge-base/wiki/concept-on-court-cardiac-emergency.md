# On-Court Cardiac Emergency Management

## Summary
Sudden cardiac death (SCD) is rare but catastrophic. The highest mortality rates in NCAA Division I athletes were found among basketball players, with a rate of 1:3,126 for Division I male basketball players [S10, p.160]. The primary determinant of survival from sudden cardiac arrest (SCA) is time to defibrillation — survival declines 7-10% for every minute defibrillation is delayed. CPR and prompt AED application are the most critical on-court interventions available to any sports medicine team [S10, p.161].

## When to Use
- Any athlete who collapses and is unresponsive
- Any non-traumatic collapse
- Brief seizure activity following collapse (may be cardiac, not neurological)
- Agonal/gasping respirations following collapse

## Key Principles

### Screening and Prevention
1. Minimum standard: targeted personal history, family history, and careful physical examination [S10, p.160]
2. Power 5 conferences: 62% institute routine ECG screening; 78% for male basketball players
3. FIBA recommends all international-level basketball players receive a 12-lead ECG in addition to basic screening
4. The "Seattle Criteria" improves ECG interpretation accuracy in athletic populations [S10, p.160]
5. Even a normal ECG does not preclude further assessment if cardiac symptoms, family history of inherited cardiovascular disease, or premature SCD is present
6. Identify at-risk players in advance, especially for away events

### AED Preparedness
1. AED location and emergency protocols must be rehearsed at least annually by the medical team AND reviewed prior to any participation [S10, p.160]
2. Goal: less than 3-5 minutes from collapse to shock
3. AED batteries must be checked before every game and training session [S10, p.166]
4. Defibrillation is the greatest single factor affecting survival from cardiac arrest [S10, p.161]

### Recognizing Sudden Cardiac Arrest
- Athlete collapses and is unresponsive
- Non-traumatic collapse (no external mechanism)
- Brief seizure activity following collapse
- Agonal or gasping respirations [S10, p.161]

### On-Court Response Sequence
1. Recognize collapse → suspect SCA
2. Initiate emergency protocol immediately
3. Assess airway, breathing, and circulation
4. **Start CPR immediately** — chest compressions without waiting for pulse check if SCA is suspected. CPR can double or triple survival when administered properly [S10, p.161]
5. **Apply AED as soon as possible** — attach, analyze rhythm, shock if indicated
6. Continue CPR between AED cycles
7. Arrange urgent transport to emergency facility as soon as deemed safe [S10, p.161]

### Key Statistics
- Division 1 basketball player cardiac death rate: **1:3,126** [S10, p.160]
- CPR doubles or triples survival chance when administered properly
- Survival declines **7-10% per minute** without defibrillation [S10, p.161]

## Common Mistakes
1. Waiting to assess the pulse before starting CPR → chest compressions should begin immediately on any unresponsive athlete with suspected cardiac arrest; CPR rarely causes harm if no arrest exists [S10, p.165]
2. Delaying AED application to continue manual CPR → AED is the greatest survival factor; apply as soon as available
3. Missing SCA in a patient with seizure-like activity → brief seizure and agonal breathing after collapse are signs of cardiac arrest, not necessarily neurological emergency
4. Not rehearsing protocols annually → AED location and emergency protocols must be practiced, not just documented
