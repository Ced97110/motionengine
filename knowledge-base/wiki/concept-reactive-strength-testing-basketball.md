# Reactive Strength Testing for Basketball

## Summary
Reactive strength is an athlete's ability to quickly transition from an eccentric muscle action to a concentric muscle action — the stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) in practice. In basketball, reactive strength underlies rapid direction changes, rebound jumps, and second-jump ability. Two tests measure reactive strength: the **Drop Jump Test** (total-body RSI) and the **Repeated Jump Test** (ankle-dominant RSI). Both use the Reactive Strength Index (RSI) as their primary metric.

**RSI formula:** RSI = airtime (or jump height) ÷ floor contact time

A higher RSI indicates a better ability to quickly absorb and redistribute ground reaction forces. Both tests are valid and reliable, but no normative or descriptive data are currently available for basketball athletes — making pre-/post-testing comparisons the primary application [S11].

## When to Use
- Assessing an athlete's SSC capabilities relative to their own baseline
- Identifying athletes whose reactive strength lags behind their maximal strength (strength deficit analysis)
- Monitoring plyometric training response across a training block
- Return-to-play testing after lower-extremity injuries (ankle, knee)
- Differentiating training approaches: athletes with high RSI may need less plyometric volume

## Key Principles
1. **Discard trials with floor contact times above 0.25 seconds** — longer contact times indicate the athlete is absorbing instead of reacting; not a true SSC expression
2. **Use a 30 cm (12 in.) box as the baseline drop jump height** — adjust based on athlete experience, maturity, and size
3. **Position the box close to the testing mat** — prevent deviation from the target landing strategy
4. **Allow ≥2 minutes of rest between trials** — ensures maximal neuromuscular output
5. **Use the 5 lowest contact-time jumps out of 10** for the repeated jump RSI average — captures the athlete's best reactive performance, not their average
6. **Calibrate all instruments before testing** — timing mats and photocell mats must be properly zeroed

## Drop Jump Test Protocol

### Equipment
- Timing mat or photocell mat (to collect airtime and floor contact time)
- Optional: force plate + motion capture for biomechanical variables
- Plyometric box (30 cm / 12 in. baseline)

### Steps
1. Calibrate all instruments
2. Athlete starts in a shoulder-width stance on the box, steps off with one leg
3. Land with both feet simultaneously, immediately rebound into maximum-effort vertical jump
4. Redo any trial with contact time >0.25 seconds
5. Allow ≥2 min rest between trials

*Primary metric: RSI = airtime ÷ floor contact time* [S11]

## Repeated Jump Test Protocol

### Equipment
- Timing mat or photocell mat
- Optional: force plate + motion capture

### Steps
1. Calibrate all instruments
2. Athlete starts in shoulder-width stance on the floor, performs maximum-effort countermovement jump
3. Land with both feet simultaneously, immediately rebound into another maximum-effort jump
4. Perform 10 consecutive jumps; identify the 5 with lowest floor contact times; average their RSI
5. Allow ≥2 min rest between trials

*Primary purpose: assess ankle-dominant lower-body reactive strength — directly relevant to rebounding and shot-challenging* [S11]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Reactive strength is position-agnostic — every player benefits from rebounding and second-jump ability training
- **PF, C**: Second-jump ability is most critical for contested rebounding; target this test in program assessment
- **PG, SG**: Quick ground contacts in transition; drop jump RSI most relevant to fast-break finishing footwork

## Common Mistakes
1. Accepting contacts >0.25 sec → the athlete is not expressing reactive strength; redo the trial
2. Not calibrating mats → timing errors invalidate RSI calculations
3. Comparing to nonexistent normative data → use individual baselines only; no published norms exist for basketball
4. Testing the repeated jump without a countermovement → must start with a CMJ, not a static jump
5. Rushing rest periods → under-rested athletes produce artificially low RSI scores; minimum 2 min between trials
