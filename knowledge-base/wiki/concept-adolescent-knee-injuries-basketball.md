# Adolescent Knee Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Adolescent basketball players face a unique spectrum of knee injuries distinct from adult athletes due to open growth plates, developing ossification centers, and sport-specific biomechanical demands. The major categories are: tibial tubercle avulsion fractures, meniscal injuries (including discoid meniscus), osteochondritis dissecans (OCD), and overuse apophyseal injuries (Osgood-Schlatter, Sinding-Larsen-Johansson, patellar tendonitis). Return to play requires full, painless range of motion, appropriate strength, and neuromuscular control — never just time-based clearance.

The fundamental take-home message is that prevention through preseason examination, proper coaching/training, and adequate recovery time is more important than treatment. Physicians, coaches, and parents must all be educated [S10, pp.399-409].

## When to Use
- Any adolescent basketball player presenting with knee pain, swelling, or mechanical symptoms
- Pre-participation screening to identify at-risk athletes
- Return-to-play decision-making after knee injury or surgery
- Designing training loads to avoid overuse injury in skeletally immature players

## Key Principles
1. **Growth plate awareness**: The tibial tubercle is the last physis to fuse; adolescent jumpers/sprinters are at high risk for apophyseal injury at this site during the 3-4 years before growth cessation [S10, p.399]
2. **Meniscal repair over meniscectomy**: In pediatric/adolescent athletes, meniscal repair should always be attempted when possible — 80-87% success rate; meniscectomy carries high long-term morbidity and chondral injury risk [S10, p.400]
3. **OCD prognosis factors**: Better prognosis in younger patients with open physes; worse with sclerosis on radiograph, synovial fluid behind lesion on MRI (unstable lesion), or lateral femoral condyle/patella location [S10, p.402]
4. **Overuse injuries are self-limited**: Osgood-Schlatter, Sinding-Larsen-Johansson, and most patellar tendonitis cases resolve with non-operative management in >90% of cases [S10, p.403]
5. **Return to sport timing**: For tibial tubercle fractures, no sooner than 3 months; for meniscal repair, 6 months minimum; for OCD drilling/fixation, 4-6 months [S10, pp.399-403]
6. **Compartment syndrome risk**: Tibial tubercle fractures risk compartment syndrome due to injury to the anterior tibial recurrent artery — admit overnight for monitoring [S10, p.399]
7. **Eccentric strengthening**: Central to rehabilitation for all overuse knee injuries — particularly patellar tendonitis [S10, p.404]

## Injury Categories and Management

### Tibial Tubercle Avulsion Fractures
- **Presentation**: Sudden onset pain at jumping/sprinting initiation; knee effusion, soft tissue swelling, extensor lag, difficulty bearing weight [S10, p.399]
- **Imaging**: AP with internal rotation view to profile tubercle; CT for intraarticular extension; MRI rarely needed
- **Non-operative**: <2 mm displacement or acceptable closed reduction → long-leg cast in extension × 6 weeks, then progressive weight-bearing and PT
- **Operative**: Any displaced fracture → ORIF ± arthroscopy for meniscal/intraarticular evaluation; non-weight-bearing × 4-6 weeks post-op
- **Return to play**: ≥3 months, after full painless ROM, appropriate strength, neuromuscular control
- **Complications**: Recurvatum deformity, leg length discrepancy, stiffness, bursitis, painful hardware, vascular injury [S10, p.399]

### Meniscal Injuries
- **Most common mechanism**: Non-contact twisting injury
- **Tear type distribution**: Complex 28%, vertical 16%, discoid 14%, bucket-handle 14%, radial 10%, horizontal 8%, oblique 5%, fray 3%, root detachment 2% [S10, p.399]
- **Clinical tests**: McMurray test (pain/pop with flexion + internal/external rotation then extension); Thessaly test (joint line pain on single-leg stance with 5° and 20° knee flexion while rotating trunk)
- **Non-operative**: Non-displaced partial tears in the red-red zone — functional bracing + PT
- **Operative**: All tears in red-white and white-white zones → attempt repair
- **Post-meniscectomy protocol**: Immediate weight-bearing → PT at 2 weeks → return to sport when full ROM and strength achieved
- **Post-repair protocol** (Table 33.3): 5 phases over 20+ weeks — partial WB in locked brace → progressive WB → full ROM → sport-specific drills at 5-6 months → return to sport at 6 months [S10, p.401]

### Discoid Meniscus
- **Prevalence**: 3-5% of population; lateral >> medial; often cause of isolated lateral meniscus symptoms
- **Watanabe Classification**: Type I — stable, complete (covers full tibial plateau); Type II — stable, partial (covers ≤80%); Type III (Wrisberg variant) — unstable, no posterior meniscotibial attachment [S10, p.400]
- **Management**: Observation for minimally symptomatic; surgery (saucerization + rim preservation + repair) for symptomatic
- **Arthroscopic findings**: 70% have associated tears; 28% have peripheral rim instability

### Osteochondritis Dissecans (OCD)
- **Definition**: Focal idiopathic alteration of subchondral bone with risk for instability/cartilage disruption — ages 10-15 most common [S10, p.401]
- **Location**: Posterolateral aspect of medial femoral condyle most common
- **Stages**: (1) cartilage softening with intact surface → (2) early cartilage separation → (3) partial detachment → (4) osteochondral separation with loose body
- **Imaging**: Weight-bearing AP + lateral + tunnel/notch views; MRI for characterization; radiographs reliable for monitoring healing
- **Non-operative**: Stable lesions in patients with open physes → restricted weight-bearing + bracing + close radiographic follow-up; 50-75% healing rate [S10, p.403]
- **Operative**: Unstable lesions or older patients → arthroscopic drilling (stable), ORIF (unstable but fixable, 85% healing), cartilage restoration (>2cm × 2cm unfixable)
- **Return to sport**: 4-6 months after radiographic confirmation of healing

### Overuse Injuries

**Osgood-Schlatter Syndrome** (tibial tubercle traction apophysitis)
- Males > females; 12-15 yo male / 8-12 yo female; bilateral in 20-50%
- Enlarged tibial tubercle with soft tissue swelling; pain with resisted knee extension
- Treatment: Rest, ice, activity modification, NSAIDs, patellar tendon strapping, quadriceps stretching → >90% complete resolution [S10, p.403]
- Surgery (ossicle excision) reserved for skeletally mature patients with persistent symptoms

**Sinding-Larsen-Johansson Syndrome** (inferior patellar pole apophysitis)
- Tenderness at inferior patella pole; pain with resisted extension
- Usually self-limited; responds to activity modification + NSAIDs + PT
- Surgery rarely indicated; symptoms rarely persist past skeletal maturity [S10, p.404]

**Patellar Tendonitis / Jumper's Knee**
- Degenerative process (not inflammatory) — can present in adolescence and adulthood
- Stages: Pain after activity → pain during activity → pain at rest → performance deterioration
- Positive Basset's sign: tenderness at distal patella pole in full extension, no tenderness in full flexion
- MRI (if surgery considered): tendon thickening, increased T1/T2 signal, loss of posterior fat pad border
- Treatment: Activity modification, patellar strapping/taping, PT (eccentric strengthening, stretching)
- **Corticosteroid injection CONTRAINDICATED** — risk of patellar tendon rupture
- PRP injections: mixed results, not yet standard of care
- Surgery (debridement): reserved for refractory cases; 80-90% return to some level of play; anticipate 4-6 months of continued discomfort [S10, p.404]

## Mainstays of Overuse Injury Treatment
1. NSAIDs
2. Rest and activity modification
3. Offloading apophyses via tendon strapping
4. Focused stretching program
5. Initiation of eccentric strengthening exercises

## Return-to-Sport Functional Tests
See appendix — single-leg stance (60 sec), single-leg stance on BOSU, 45° single-leg squat, 45° single-leg squat on BOSU, clockwise/counterclockwise quadrant hop, single-leg hop for distance, 6-meter timed hop, crossover hop for distance [S10, p.405]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Time-based return to play** → Always use functional criteria (ROM, strength, neuromuscular control)
2. **Missing compartment syndrome risk** in tibial tubercle fractures → Admit overnight for monitoring
3. **Meniscectomy instead of repair** → Repair whenever possible in pediatric population
4. **Corticosteroid injection for patellar tendonitis** → Contraindicated; risk of tendon rupture
5. **Rushing OCD rehabilitation** → Radiographic confirmation of healing required before return to impact activities
