# Wrist Complex

## Summary
The wrist complex is the final link in the shooting chain — the joint and muscle group that controls ball spin, release angle, and the snap that translates upper-body force into a soft arc on the basket. It is also the joint that fails first under fatigue: late-game wrist droop is one of the most common reasons a previously accurate shooter goes cold in the fourth quarter.

The wrist is mechanically tied to the elbow and shoulder above ([[concept-anatomy-elbow-complex]], [[concept-anatomy-shoulder-girdle]]); a clean wrist follow-through cannot be produced if the elbow is flaring or the shoulder is unstable. Fix the proximal joints first; the wrist usually self-corrects.

## When to Use
This anatomy matters to coaching decisions involving:
- Shooting follow-through quality (the "in-the-cookie-jar" hold)
- Late-game accuracy decay (fatigue affects the wrist before the legs)
- Layup finishes (touch on glass; soft-finger control near the rim)
- Ball-handling at speed (wrist as the steering element of the dribble)
- Pass touch (chest pass, bounce pass, lob — all driven by wrist snap)

## Key Principles
1. **Snap, then hold.** The shooting wrist accelerates through release, then holds the follow-through position long enough for the fingers to verify the release was on line. Athletes who don't hold also don't feel — they cannot self-correct.
2. **Fingertip touch beats palm contact.** The fingertips, not the palm, control the ball at release. Train this with fingertip pushups and grip-isolation exercises.
3. **Equal extension and flexion.** Most basketball players overdevelop wrist flexors (gripping) and underdevelop wrist extensors (the cock-back). Train extension explicitly.
4. **Pronator/supinator balance controls release rotation.** Side spin on the ball usually comes from a pronator-dominant forearm, not from the wrist. Address with neutral-grip carries and supinated curls.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG / SG**: highest shooting + ball-handling volume; the wrist is the limiting factor on shot count per session
- **SF**: catch-and-shoot windows demand fast wrist preparation
- **PF / C**: hook shots, layup touch, and dribble-handoff catches all stress the wrist; underbuilt at the position group

## Variations
### Radiocarpal Joint
The proximal wrist joint between the radius and the carpal bones. Provides most of the flexion / extension range used in the shooting motion.

### Midcarpal Joint
Between the proximal and distal carpal rows. Adds to total range and is the source of much wrist "wobble" when the radiocarpal is stiff.

### Distal Radioulnar Joint
Where the radius rotates around the ulna. This is the source of pronation / supination — the rotation that causes side spin on a poorly released shot.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Letting the wrist break sideways at release** → ball rotates side-to-side instead of back-to-front; arc is flat and rim-clanking. Correction: hand-on-block drill (palm vertical to rim) until the muscle memory holds.
2. **Dropping the follow-through immediately** → no feedback loop on what the wrist actually did. Correction: hold the wave-goodbye position for one full second after release.
3. **Treating the wrist like a hinge** → ignores the rotational element. Correction: explicit pronator/supinator work; check for ball coming off the index finger, not the middle finger.
4. **Training only flexion (curls, grippers)** → posterior wrist underdeveloped; wrist tires and droops late. Correction: reverse curls, finger-extension band work, fingertip pushups.
