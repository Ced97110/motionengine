# Pick-and-Roll Defense

## Summary
Defending the two-man pick and roll is one of the most difficult defensive maneuvers players must contend with. Herb Brown's system requires your team's collective mindset to be focused on stopping penetration and forcing the dribbler away from the goal. The screener's defender carries an enormous responsibility — he must contact show and step out to stop ball reversal while remaining aware of roll and pop options. [S1, pp.127-128]

The system uses several interlocking techniques (show, trap, switch, squeeze, drop) chosen based on the screen location, personnel matchups, and game situation. All variations share a common foundation: on-ball pressure is mandatory, and weak-side rotation must be disciplined.

## When to Use
- Every time the offense initiates a ball screen — wing, high, elbow, drag, step-up
- Specific techniques vary by screen location and score/clock situation
- Switch-heavy approach preferred in late-game or short-clock situations
- Trapping (aggressive show or soft double) used to stop dribble penetration first

## Key Principles
1. **Force the dribbler away from the goal** — the primary objective of every PnR coverage
2. **Stop penetration first** — all rotations are triggered after penetration is contained
3. **Ball pressure is non-negotiable** — the dribbler's defender must be physical and belly or body up to the ball handler to fight over the screen
4. **Screener's defender contacts show** — step out aggressively to stop ball reversal to the other side of the floor
5. **Rotate by flight of the ball** — if the ball is advanced to the weak side, recover to your own man; if the ball stays strong side or goes back to the screener, rotate early from the closest weak-side big man
6. **Staggered drag screens** — the defender on the second screener must stay connected to stop or trap dribble penetration; the first screener's defender can let his man through and then shrink to the middle to pick up the roll man [S1, p.127]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (on-ball)**: Belly/body up to the dribbler; fight to get over the screen by making himself thinner; be physical
- **SG (off-ball)**: Rotate toward the ball according to the flight of the pass; recover to own man on weak-side kick-ahead
- **SF**: Provides weak-side help from elbow/box; rotates toward the ball if it stays strong side or returns to screener
- **PF (screener's defender)**: Contact show and step out to stop ball reversal; aware of roll vs. pop; shrinks to the middle of the lane if ball handler gets by to pick up the roll man
- **C (screener's defender, alt.)**: Same as PF; if X4 cannot recover to a popping 4, X5 must sprint and pre-rotate; tilt/shadow option also available [S1, p.128]

## Variations
### Aggressive Show / Soft Double-Team
Many teams aggressively show or softly double-team the ball on wing/side PnR until dribble penetration is stopped. Then rotate by flight of the ball. If ball is kicked to weak side, recover; if it stays strong or goes back to screener, rotate aggressively from the nearest weak-side big man. [S1, pp.127-128]

### Forcing Sideline / Baseline Trap
Force the wing PnR sideline to initiate a baseline trap and rotation sequence. When the screener pops, X4 must recover; if beaten, X5 pre-rotates. On a skip pass to the popping guard, the nearest weak-side defender rotates. [S1, p.127, Figs. 8.33-8.34]

### Strong Side Empty
On wing or high PnR when not forcing sideline, the strong side is left empty — defenders collapse and help from weak side. [S1, p.128, Figs. 8.35-8.36]

### Transition Drag / Step-Up Screen
Big guarding a small guard on a drag or step-up screen fights over the screen. Switch similar-sized players (e.g., SF and PG) on step-up PnR. [S1, p.128, Figs. 8.37-8.38]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Dribbler's defender fails to belly up** → gives the ball handler a free path over the screen; correction: be physical, press the dribbler's hip before contact
2. **Screener's defender shows too late** → allows the ball to be reversed easily; correction: contact show on the first dribble off the screen
3. **Rotating before penetration is stopped** → leaves open cutters; correction: contain first, then rotate by flight of the ball
4. **Second screener's defender drifting** → allows dribble penetration through staggered screens; correction: stay connected to the second screener [S1, p.127]
