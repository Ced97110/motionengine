# Rear Shoulder Roll (Back Flip Chain Step 1)

## Objective
Adapt the brain and vestibular system to backward rotation safely, protecting the neck and skull, as the foundational first step of the back flip progression chain.

## Setup
- 1 athlete
- Soft surface recommended (grass, mat)
- No equipment required
- Speed: slow and controlled — this is NOT an explosive drill

## Execution
1. Stand with **one foot out front**, squat toward the ground.
2. With spine curled forward, **gently drop back onto your glutes**. The rear leg can slip behind you if it feels natural.
3. Continue momentum by **pushing through your strongest leg**, encouraging the body to roll backward.
4. **Swing your legs over your head** as you roll over your **strongest shoulder** — keep the head tucked in.
5. As you roll over the shoulder and legs approach the floor behind you, allow the **braced arm of that shoulder to guide you** back up.
6. As feet touch down, use momentum to finish the roll naturally **in a standing position**. Push up with hands if needed.

## Coaching Points
- "Do not roll directly back, over your head and neck — use your shoulder." [S9, p.179 tip]
- Head must stay tucked in throughout — the neck should bear NO weight
- Roll over the arm and shoulder, NOT the neck, to protect the joints
- Speed is irrelevant at this stage — this is about pattern, not explosiveness
- Similar to *ushiro ukemi* (rear rolls) found in Japanese martial arts [S9, p.178]
- When performed correctly, it mirrors the front shoulder roll but in reverse

## Progressions
1. **Beginner (Regression)**: Alter form freely to find what feels safe and natural; focus only on tucked head and shoulder roll
2. **Intermediate**: Master the key points slowly with complete control
3. **Advanced**: Pick up speed once form is locked in — still not explosive, just faster

## Common Mistakes
1. **Rolling over the back of the neck** → cervical spine injury; always redirect over the shoulder
2. **Head not tucked** → skull impact risk; keep chin to chest throughout
3. **Going too fast too soon** → loss of control; master slow before adding speed
4. **Not using the guiding arm** → fails to control landing from the shoulder roll
