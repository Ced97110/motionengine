# Shooter Pivot Fundamentals

## Summary
Every shooter must master the art of pivoting in order to get their shot off quickly with minimal defensive pressure. The pivot is actually more fundamental than passing or dribbling — players who cannot pivot cannot get into proper position to dribble or pass without committing a turnover. Shooters who want to release the ball before a defender can close out must master the proper footwork: getting feet and hands ready *before* the catch, then pivoting efficiently to square up to the basket.

Three distinct pivot methods enable shooters to square up quickly off the pass. Coaches should choose one or two methods, teach them comprehensively, demonstrate them thoroughly, and give players numerous supervised repetitions to develop proficiency. [S6, pp.141-160]

## When to Use
- Any time a player receives a pass on the perimeter and intends to shoot
- Coming off any type of screen (down screen, flare screen, back screen, stagger screen, ball screen)
- Stationary catch-and-shoot situations in half-court offense and zone offense
- Any catch from any direction where the shooter needs to square shoulders to the basket quickly

## Key Principles
1. **Get feet and hands ready before the catch**: The shooter prepares steps *before* receiving the pass so they are balanced and can execute proper footwork instantly. Hands are already in the shooting pocket so the passer has an exact target (like a baseball catcher giving a pitcher a target).
2. **Rise straight up, come straight down**: All shooters should rise and descend vertically. Any forward drift should be only slightly toward the target — never sideways or backward.
3. **Pivot stops unwanted momentum**: When cutting laterally or away from the basket, the inside-heel pivot stops the original momentum so that new momentum can shift toward the basket. Allowing original momentum to continue causes falling away or drifting on the shot.
4. **See the basket as soon as possible**: Proper footwork via the pivot allows the shooter to pick up the basket sooner and release more accurately.
5. **Shooting hand and guide hand in position pre-catch**: Hands are in near-exact shooting pocket position before the ball arrives — this also gives the passer a clear, specific target.

## Three Pivot Methods

### Method 1: Two-Step Hop
As the ball is passed to the shooter, the receiver takes a hop with both feet simultaneously while squaring up to the basket. Either foot can become the pivot foot. Simple and fast — allows immediate squaring with one motion. [S6, p.143]

### Method 2: Designated (Dominant) Pivot Foot
The coach teaches every shooter to always use the same designated pivot foot regardless of where on the floor they are located. This builds deep proficiency — the shooter develops one highly refined pivoting habit. Strong for players who play the same position/spot repeatedly. [S6, p.143]

### Method 3: Inside Foot / Inside Heel Pivot (Preferred)
The shooter pivots off the foot that is the *inside foot* in relationship to the court — the foot closest to the center of the court or the basket depending on direction of travel and body orientation:
- **Left side of court, back to basket**: right foot is inside foot → pivot foot
- **Left side of court, facing basket**: left foot is inside foot → pivot foot
- **Right side of court**: mirror image of the above

Critically, the pivot occurs off the *inside heel* (not the ball of the foot). Pivoting off the inside heel:
- Stops the cutter's lateral or outward momentum completely
- Allows a smooth, simple rotation to face the basket
- Gets the feet and legs directly below the body for a balanced, vertical jump
- Enables the shooter to see the basket sooner and release more quickly [S6, pp.143-145]

> Former pro player Antoine Walker's three-step shooting formula: "Get my feet set" → "Get my hands all ready to go" → "Get my legs up under me." The inside heel pivot accomplishes all three. [S6, p.145]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Pivot method applies when driving off screens or receiving throwback passes; also must know which pivot creates triple-threat vs. immediate shot
- **SG**: Primary beneficiary — must have one reliable pivot method mastered for catching off all screen types
- **SF**: Must know inside-foot pivot rules for both sides of the court and adjust based on back-to-basket vs. facing-basket orientation
- **PF**: When slipping screens or popping to perimeter (pick-and-pop), must use reverse pivot off inside foot to maintain sight of ball, basket, and defender
- **C**: When setting back screens and slipping to perimeter, must choose front pivot (immediate shooting threat) or reverse/inside pivot (triple-threat passer/driver) based on read

## Stationary Shooting Exception
For stationary shooters already facing the basket (especially in zone offenses), coaches may allow the shooter to use their preferred dominant pivot foot:
- Most **right-handed** shooters prefer the **left foot** as pivot foot
- Most **left-handed** shooters prefer the **right foot** as pivot foot

The stationary shooter sets up with pivot foot slightly ahead of the free foot, pivot foot facing the basket, free foot opening toward the passer. Hands are up and in shooting position before the pass is caught. As the catch is made, the heel of the pivot foot touches the floor, the free foot steps up, and the ball enters the shooting pocket. [S6, pp.150-152]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not getting feet/hands ready before the catch** → Shooter must catch first, then orient — costing precious milliseconds and allowing defenders to close out
2. **Pivoting off the ball of the foot instead of the heel** → Momentum is not stopped; shooter drifts away from or sideways to the basket
3. **Using the outside foot as pivot foot when cutting** → Rotation is awkward, slower, and requires more steps to square up
4. **Allowing lateral cut momentum to continue into the shot** → Causes falling away or drifting sideways — fundamentally changes shot angle
5. **Not rising straight up** → Landing too far forward (more than a slight drift toward basket) or sideways disrupts accuracy and repeatability
6. **Hands not in shooting pocket pre-catch** → Passes are harder for the passer (no target) and slower to shoot (ball must travel further to pocket)
