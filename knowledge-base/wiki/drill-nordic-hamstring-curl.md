# Nordic Hamstring Curl

## Objective
Develop elite eccentric hamstring strength through a demanding body-lowering exercise that is one of the most evidence-supported tools for hamstring injury prevention and posterior chain development in basketball.

## Setup
- 1 athlete, 1 partner to hold ankles (or a Nordic hamstring machine)
- **Alternatives if no machine available**:
  - Partner-assisted: Partner holds the athlete's ankles in place
  - Power rack: Athlete positions ankles under the squat pad or a weighted barbell
- Perform on a padded surface to protect the knees

## Execution
1. Start in a kneeling upright position with hips extended, gluteals contracted, and knees flexed to approximately 90° (a).
2. Keeping the **torso rigid and hips extended**, allow the knees to extend to lower the torso forward (b) — descend **as slowly as possible** under control.
3. When the descent is no longer controllable, allow the body to fall to the floor in a controlled fashion.
4. **Catch the body with hands** in a push-up position (c).
5. Give just enough of a push off the floor to get the torso back to the point where the hamstrings can pull the body back to the starting position.
6. Return under hamstring power to the upright position.

## Coaching Points
- **Keep the torso rigid throughout** — do not let the hips flex at any point
- Maintain **hip extension** the entire set — allowing hips to flex takes tension off the hamstrings
- When extending the glutes, no pressure or contraction should be felt in the lower back
- The slower the descent, the greater the eccentric stimulus
- **Inhale** during the downward movement; **exhale** after the sticking point of the upward movement

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Use a resistance band anchored overhead to assist the return; focus on maximum eccentric descent time
2. **Intermediate**: Unassisted with a push-off assist from the floor; aim for 3-5 reps with 4-6 sec descent
3. **Advanced**: Minimal push-off assist; full hamstring-powered return; load with a plate held at the chest

## Common Mistakes
1. **Hips flexing during descent** → reduces hamstring load → cue "glutes squeezed, body rigid"
2. **Too fast descent** → loses eccentric benefit → cue "fight gravity every inch"
3. **Large push-off assist** → cheats the return → aim to minimize hand push, use only what's needed to re-engage hamstrings
