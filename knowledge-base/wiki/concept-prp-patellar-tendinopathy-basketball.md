# PRP for Patellar Tendinopathy (Jumper's Knee) in Basketball

## Summary
Patellar tendinopathy ("jumper's knee") is one of the most prevalent overuse injuries in basketball due to the sport's extraordinary jumping demands. Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injection has emerged as a treatment for cases refractory to conservative care. Current evidence is generally supportive, though a 2019 level-I RCT has introduced uncertainty [S10, Ch.45]. The condition is classified using the modified Blazina scale and assessed with the VISA-P outcome score.

## When to Use
- Patellar tendinopathy that has failed first-line conservative management (eccentric loading programs, physical therapy, NSAIDS, ESWT)
- Athletes seeking an accelerated return to play
- When surgery is being considered as the next step

## Key Principles
1. PRP contains concentrated growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β, VEGF, IGF-1) that may promote tendon cell anabolic activity and reduce catabolic processes [S10, Ch.45]
2. Leukocyte content matters — LR-PRP (leukocyte-rich) was used in most positive patellar tendinopathy studies
3. Two injections appear to be the typical protocol in positive studies; one injection showed mixed results
4. Ultrasound guidance is recommended for accurate injection placement
5. PRP should be considered a safe option with a favorable risk-benefit profile vs. surgery [S10, Ch.45]

## Clinical Evidence (Level-I RCTs)

| Study | Comparison | n | Injections | Follow-up | Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vetrano et al. (2013) | LR-PRP vs. ESWT | 23 vs. 23 | 2 | 12 months | **PRP significantly better** VISA-P, VAS, Blazina |
| Dragoo et al. (2014) | LR-PRP + dry needling vs. dry needling | 10 vs. 13 | 1 | 6 months | **PRP better** at 12 weeks; no difference at 26 weeks; Lysholm better in control at 26 weeks |
| Scott et al. (2019) | LR-PRP vs. LP-PRP vs. saline | 19 vs. 19 vs. 19 | 1 | 12 months | **No significant difference** between groups at any timepoint |
| Clarke et al. (2011) | Skin-derived tenocyte cells | 33 vs. 27 | 1 | 6 months | **Tenocytes significantly better**; 1 late tendon rupture in cell group |

[S10, p.568]

## Clinical Recommendation
> There is sufficient evidence to recommend PRP for patellar tendinopathy refractory to first-line conservative treatment. It is considered a safe treatment option. However, the Scott et al. (2019) level-I RCT raises doubts about the benefits of PRP for patellar tendinopathy and warrants careful patient counseling. [S10, p.572]

## Common Mistakes
1. Using PRP as a first-line treatment before adequate conservative management has been trialed
2. Ignoring PRP preparation variability — LP-PRP may not be equivalent to LR-PRP for patellar tendinopathy
3. Expecting uniform long-term results — benefits appear most consistent at 12-week follow-up; long-term data are weaker
