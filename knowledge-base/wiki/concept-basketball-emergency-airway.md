# Basketball Emergency Airway Management

## Summary
Airway compromise is a time-critical emergency. A player with a compromised airway and circulation has as little as 3-4 minutes before permanent cellular damage occurs [S10, p.165]. Tongue falling back is the most common cause of airway obstruction when an athlete collapses. Recognizing obstruction, applying the chin lift, placing an oropharyngeal airway, and operating a bag-valve-mask device are core competencies for every basketball medical staff member [S10, pp.167-168].

## When to Use
- Step A of the C-ABCD protocol for any collapsed athlete
- Any athlete with abnormal breathing sounds or altered consciousness
- As part of cervical spine management — airway may need attention while C-spine is being protected

## Key Principles

### Recognizing Airway Problems
1. **Quick screen**: Ask the athlete their name — a coherent response indicates airway and breathing are functioning [S10, p.167]
2. **Partial obstruction sounds**: stridor, gurgling, whistling, gasping
3. **Severe obstruction signs**: intercostal retraction, accessory muscle use, agitation, altered level of consciousness
4. **Cyanosis** (purplish discoloration of nail beds and lips) — indicates inadequate oxygenation; harder to detect in athletes with dark skin [S10, p.167]

### Chin Lift Technique
1. Place index finger under athlete's mandible
2. Gently pull lower lip down with thumb
3. **Critical**: Do NOT hyperextend or flex the neck — possible C-spine injury
4. Tongue will often fall forward with the chin lift, relieving obstruction [S10, p.168]

### Oropharyngeal Airway Placement
1. Position the airway concavity upward (upside down) toward the roof of the mouth
2. Insert along the roof of the hard palate until the soft palate is reached
3. Simultaneously slide in and rotate 180° so concavity faces down over the tongue [S10, p.168]
4. Provides a channel past the tongue to maintain the airway open

### Bag-Valve-Mask Ventilation
1. Most fundamental airway management skill in on-court settings
2. Use when athlete cannot breathe spontaneously
3. Provides both ventilation and oxygen supplementation [S10, p.168]
4. Create a seal: mask over mouth and nose, jaw-thrust to maintain airway without neck extension

### Definitive Airway (When BVM Fails)
If it is not possible to maintain the airway and ventilate with a BVM, a definitive airway is required:
- Orotracheal tube
- Nasotracheal tube
- Surgical airway (cricothyroidotomy or tracheostomy) [S10, p.168]

### Breathing Assessment (Step B)
After airway is secured:
1. Look and listen simultaneously — chest symmetric + breath sounds over mouth
2. Asymmetrical chest movement → suspect chest wall injury
3. Auscultate both lung fields — blood or air blocks sound transmission
4. Tracheal deviation on palpation → suspect tension pneumothorax
5. Use bag-valve-mask for assisted ventilation; auscultate to verify adequate air entry in both fields [S10, p.168]

## Common Mistakes
1. Hyperextending the neck during chin lift → maintain neutral C-spine alignment at all times
2. Not having oropharyngeal airways in the medical kit → essential equipment for any basketball venue
3. Failing to recognize cyanosis in dark-skinned athletes → look specifically at nail beds and lips
4. Assuming normal breathing based on any chest movement → auscultate both fields to verify equal air entry
5. Diagnosing tension pneumothorax without palpating the trachea → deviated trachea is the key field sign
