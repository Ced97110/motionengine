# Jumping Pull-Up (Muscle-Up Chain — Step 2)

## Objective
Teach explosive elliptical pulling mechanics by using leg drive to replicate the kipping backswing, training the nervous system to pull *in* to the bar rather than straight up.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Low horizontal bar (athlete stands in a semi-squat position while gripping), OR a sturdy box/platform to stand on beneath a full-height bar
- No additional equipment needed

## Execution
1. Grip an overhead bar at shoulder width, standing in a semi-squat beneath it — not hanging.
2. **Leap up and back** (not straight up) to emulate the backswing of the swing kip from Step 1.
3. As you explode upward, simultaneously pull down and *in* with the arms — since you jumped up and back, you must pull yourself *in* toward the bar, not just up.
4. Use the combined momentum of the jump plus the arm pull to carry yourself up until the jaw is above the bar.
5. Lower yourself quickly but under control.
6. Repeat [S9, p.15].

## Coaching Points
- **"Jump up and back, not straight up"** — the back-and-in direction of the jump is what differentiates this from a standard jumping pull-up and teaches kipping mechanics
- "Pull in, not just up" — the elliptical path of the pull (up and in) is the key neurological pattern for the muscle-up
- Athletes who do standard jumping pull-ups (straight up) will not transfer this skill correctly to the kipping pull-up chain
- Watch for: athletes jumping straight up and pulling vertically — redirect them to feel the behind-the-bar backswing position
- Lower under control — the eccentric builds grip and shoulder tendon strength [S9, p.15]

## Progressions
1. **Beginner/Regression**: Use a lower bar or higher platform so more leg power assists the pull. Focus on jumping higher [S9, p.15].
2. **Intermediate**: Begin without pre-gripping the bar — jump up from standing, grab the bar mid-jump, and immediately pull up/in.
3. **Advanced**: Increase bar height so more distance must be covered. Progress to single-leg takeoff (alternate legs each rep) to reduce assistance and increase demand [S9, p.15].
