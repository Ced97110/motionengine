# Front Outside Pivot (and Square-Up) Post Move

## Summary
The front outside pivot (and square-up) is a post move used when the post player cannot determine the defender's position by feel — typically because the defender is not making physical contact or is playing straight up rather than fronting from either the high or low side. The post player makes a front pivot **away from the basket and away from the defender** with either foot, swinging the free foot 180° back toward the basket to end in a square-up position facing both the basket and the defender [S6, pp.195-196].

This move is closely related to the Sikma inside pivot but uses a front pivot in the opposite initial direction. Both moves produce the same end result — a square-up position with the dribble still available — but take different initial paths [S6, p.196].

## When to Use
- When the defender is NOT making physical contact and cannot be felt or seen easily
- When the defender is playing straight-up behind the post player without committing to a side
- When the post player needs to face up to locate the defender visually
- As a universal tool for younger post players who have not yet learned to read the defender by feel

## Key Principles
1. **Same universal catch routine** — Two-foot simultaneous hop, chin the ball, look over the top shoulder [S6, p.195].
2. **Front pivot AWAY from the basket and defender** — Since the defender is behind the post player (between the post player and the basket), a front pivot that moves away from the basket actually moves away from both [S6, p.196].
3. **Free foot swings 180° back toward the basket** — The full 180° swing lands the post player in a square-up position, facing the basket and the defender [S6, p.196].
4. **Either foot can be the pivot foot** — The choice of which foot to use as the pivot foot determines which direction the player squares up (toward the baseline or toward the middle) [S6, pp.196-197].
5. **Dribble has NOT been used** — This is critical: after squaring up with the front outside pivot, the post player still has the live dribble available and can attack in any direction [S6, p.196].
6. **Attack reads after squaring up** — Once facing the basket:
   - Defender playing tight → use front crossover pivot or blast move
   - Defender playing softly → rise up for jump shot OR use dribble to attack the basket
   [S6, p.196]

## Footwork Detail — Pivot Toward Baseline (Left Side of Lane)
- Post player on offense's left side of lane, back to basket
- Pivot foot = right foot
- Free foot (left) swings 180° around the right pivot foot, moving away from the basket/defender then back around to face the basket
- Result: square-up position facing the basket and X5, with right foot as pivot foot

## Footwork Detail — Pivot Toward Middle (Left Side of Lane)
- Same starting position (left side of lane)
- Pivot foot = left foot
- Free foot (right) swings 180° toward the middle of the free-throw lane
- Result: square-up position facing the basket and X5 at the middle

## After Squaring Up: Attack Options
Once in the square-up position, the post player attacks the same way a perimeter player would after facing up:
- **Front crossover pivot**: attack in the opposite direction from the chosen pivot foot (same as the jab-and-go crossover for perimeter players)
- **Blast move**: attack in the same direction as the chosen pivot foot
- **Jump shot**: if the defender gives space, rise up and shoot without a dribble
- **Drive to basket**: use the dribble to advance the ball toward the basket

## Comparison to the Sikma Inside Pivot
| Feature | Front Outside Pivot | Sikma Inside Pivot |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Initial pivot direction | Away from basket/defender | Toward basket/defender |
| Pivot type | Front pivot | Reverse pivot |
| End position | Square-up facing basket | Square-up facing basket |
| Space created | Less immediate separation | Creates separation from defender |
| Dribble used? | No | No |

Both moves arrive at the same square-up position but via opposite initial pivot directions [S6, p.196].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using the dribble before squaring up** → Eliminates the primary post-facing-up advantage; correction: complete the full pivot and square-up first, then read and attack.
2. **Pivoting toward the basket on the initial move** → That is the reverse-pivot post move, not the front outside pivot; they accomplish the same result but are different techniques.
3. **Not committing to a full 180°** → Post player ends up sideways, not truly facing the basket; correction: the free foot must complete the full 180° swing.
