# Elbow Tendon Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Tendon injuries about the elbow occur in basketball from eccentric loading mechanisms. Distal biceps tendon ruptures (1.2 per 100,000 in the general population) predominantly affect the dominant extremity and are exacerbated by anabolic steroid use. Triceps tendon ruptures are rare (0.8% of all tendon ruptures) but can be devastating to basketball players due to loss of elbow extension strength. Both benefit from surgical repair in competitive athletes to restore pre-injury strength levels. [S10, pp.286-287]

## 25.3.3.1 Distal Biceps Tendon Injuries

### Epidemiology and Risk Factors
- Incidence: 1.2 per 100,000 in general population
- More common in the dominant extremity
- Increased risk with smoking
- Anabolic steroid use increases risk of **bilateral** rupture [S10, p.286]
- Classified as: partial or complete; acute (<4 weeks) or chronic

### Mechanism
- Unexpected, eccentric loading of the biceps with elbow flexed at 90°
- Tendon avulses from the radial tuberosity [S10, p.286]

### Diagnosis
- **History**: sudden, painful tearing sensation over front of elbow
- **Exam**: ecchymosis and swelling in antecubital fossa; proximal migration of muscle belly (complete rupture); tenderness in antecubital fossa; inability to palpate tendon
- **Weakness**: primarily in supination; lesser deficit in elbow flexion
- **Hook Test**: examiner hooks index finger under the lateral side of the distal biceps tendon while the athlete actively supinates; inability to hook = complete rupture [S10, p.286]
- **Biceps Squeeze Test**: squeeze biceps muscle belly; observe for passive supination (positive = intact tendon) [S10, p.286]
- **Imaging**: Radiographs usually normal (may show radial tuberosity avulsion); MRI in FABS (flexion, abduction, supination) position best delineates partial vs. complete rupture [S10, p.286]

### Treatment
- **Partial ruptures**: nonoperative (immobilization + PT) — risk of activity-related pain and supination strength loss
- **Complete ruptures in athletes**: anatomic surgical reattachment indicated — restores flexion and supination strength [S10, p.286]

### Rehabilitation (Post-Surgical)
1. **Week 1**: Immobilization in 90° of flexion and supination
2. **Week 1+**: Transition to hinged elbow brace with progressive extension block
3. **Month 5**: Strenuous lifting and unrestricted activities permitted [S10, p.286]

## 25.3.3.2 Triceps Tendon Injuries

### Epidemiology and Risk Factors
- Only 0.8% of all tendon ruptures [S10, p.286]
- Usually avulsion from tendon insertion; occasionally intramuscular tear
- Loss of extension strength = devastating to basketball players
- Risk increased by steroid use and metabolic disorders [S10, p.286]

### Mechanism
- Elbow extension activity with sudden, eccentric triceps contraction
- Classic: fall on outstretched hand [S10, p.286]

### Diagnosis
- **History**: sudden pain with elbow extension activity
- **Exam**: swelling, ecchymosis, tenderness over olecranon; weakness in elbow extension
- **Note**: Complete tears may preserve some extension due to intact lateral tendon expansion — can mask diagnosis [S10, p.286]
- **Imaging**: Radiographs for bony avulsion; MRI differentiates partial from complete [S10, p.286]

### Treatment
- **<50% tear (nonoperative)**: 4 weeks immobilization in 30° extension → 4 weeks ROM → 8 weeks strength. Not commonly chosen in elite basketball players [S10, p.287]
- **Partial and complete tears in athletes**: surgical repair indicated — repair within **3 weeks** of injury (after 3 weeks, reconstruction likely needed)
- Post-repair expected outcomes: 4/5–5/5 strength; 99% endurance recovery vs. uninjured arm [S10, p.287]

### Rehabilitation (Post-Surgical)
1. **Weeks 0–2**: Immobilization in 30° of flexion
2. **Week 2+**: ROM exercises
3. **Month 4–6**: Return to sport permitted [S10, p.287]

## Key Coaching/Medical Facts
- Biceps tendon ruptures are more common in the dominant extremity
- Anabolic steroids increase risk of bilateral biceps rupture
- Hook test and biceps squeeze test are diagnostic clues for complete biceps rupture
- Triceps ruptures are rare but high-impact for basketball function
- Primary repair within 3 weeks is critical for triceps injuries [S10, p.287]
