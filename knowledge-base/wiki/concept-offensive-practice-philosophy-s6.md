# Offensive Practice Philosophy (S6)

## Summary
This philosophy establishes the guiding principles for designing and running effective offensive basketball practices. It emphasizes that successful coaches are not just great teachers and motivators — they must also have the right tools, the right environment, and the right culture to allow players to learn and improve. Every drill must be game-realistic, every player must be constantly engaged, and accountability must be built into every minute of practice. [S6, pp.1-6]

The philosophy synthesizes ideas from coaching basketball, baseball, and football at multiple levels, plus observations from top coaches at high school, college, and pro levels.

## When to Use
- When designing a practice plan at any competitive level
- When evaluating whether current drills are generating real skill improvement
- When building or rebuilding a program's practice culture
- When addressing player effort, focus, or competitive intensity issues in practice

## Key Principles
1. **Make practices as game-realistic as possible.** Use fundamental and breakdown drills in all phases. Simulate game passes, cuts, and shot locations. [S6, p.3]
2. **The practice facility is the basketball coach's classroom.** Create an environment conducive to learning and teaching. Pay attention to every detail with every player. [S6, p.3]
3. **Be enthusiastic.** Players must see that the coach loves to teach. Assume players know nothing and start with the basics — be thorough. [S6, p.3]
4. **Plan your work, work your plan.** Have a detailed practice plan and follow it; be flexible only when special circumstances demand. [S6, p.3]
5. **Use the whole-part-whole method.** Teach the complete skill, isolate its parts, then reassemble into the full game action. [S6, p.3]
6. **Demand attention at all times.** Players must give coaches their eyes and ears. Do not allow standing around — every player and coach is involved in every drill. [S6, p.4]
7. **Make practices more demanding than games** — both physically and mentally. Players accustomed to harder practice will be more composed in competition. [S6, p.3]
8. **Establish drills requiring concentration.** The habit of concentrating in drills transfers to game focus. [S6, p.3]
9. **Reward correct performance more than punishing errors.** Consistently recognize those who perform correctly. Constructively correct mistakes without personalizing criticism. [S6, p.4]
10. **Set pre-set quantity AND quality standards for every shooting drill.** Specific attempts required, specific makes required. This enforces game speed and intensity. [S6, p.5]
11. **Shoot free throws after strenuous drills.** Players must get accustomed to shooting when winded and fatigued. Shoot two FTs at a time (as in a game). [S6, p.5]
12. **Combine drills to include transition.** Drill design should offer frequent opportunities for offense-to-defense and defense-to-offense transition. [S6, p.5]
13. **Have managers record practice statistics.** Post them so players see their results matter. Set individual and team standards. [S6, p.5]
14. **Organize competition: individual, small group, and team.** Most competitive drills should have a winner and a loser with light penalties (sprints, push-ups, sit-ups). [S6, p.5]
15. **Critique fairly and positively.** Before criticizing, first ask: "What did you do correctly?" Then: "What did you do incorrectly?" [S6, p.5]
16. **Never omit conditioning.** Build conditioning into drills rather than ending practice with a conditioning sprint. [S6, p.5]
17. **"Perfect practice does make perfect."** The practice plan is the coach's lesson plan. [S6, p.5]
18. **Keep practice lengths consistent.** Shorter, lighter practices the night before and after games. On rare occasions, cancel practice or replace it with a non-basketball activity (volleyball, movie, pizza) mid-season for a mental break. [S6, p.6]
19. **Never end a drill on a negative note.** Do not end on a missed shot or a turnover. The last activity of practice should be positive and motivating. [S6, p.6]
20. **Do not finish practice with conditioning work.** End with a rewarding activity that makes players eager for tomorrow's practice. [S6, p.6]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Every player is engaged in every drill at game speed — no standing around. Every player is responsible for cutting to the ball, making proper passes, aggressive offensive rebounding, and accurate outlet passing, regardless of which role they occupy in any given drill rotation.

## Variations
### Mid-Season Mental Break
When players are worn down mid-schedule (halfway or two-thirds through), cancel practice or replace it with a completely different activity: volleyball, Wiffle ball, movie, or team pizza. This restores physical and mental energy. [S6, p.6]

### One-and-One Free Throw Reward/Penalty System
When a player earns a water break (by doing something positive), the remaining players shoot one-and-one free throws. Miss the front end = full-court sprint. Make front, miss back = half-court sprint. Make both = water break too. Consistent rewards and penalties reinforce accountability. [S6, p.4]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Drill without teaching** → A drill is only as good as how it is taught. Coach actively during every rep, not just at the start. [S6, p.4]
2. **Ending practice with conditioning** → Conditioning is important but should not be the final activity; end on a positive, motivating note. [S6, p.6]
3. **Ending a drill on a miss** → Always finish shooting drills on a make. Never conclude a scrimmage on a missed shot or turnover. [S6, p.6]
4. **Allowing players to stand around** → Every player must be active. Downtime in practice is wasted repetition. [S6, p.4]
5. **Only punishing errors, never rewarding correct play** → Consistently reward correct performance to build positive culture. [S6, p.4]
