# Time Surfing — Skill Training Method

## Summary
Time surfing is Paul Wade's primary programming method for skill-based movements in *Explosive Calisthenics*. Instead of counting sets and reps, the athlete sets a **timer for a defined window** (typically 5 minutes for beginners) and practices the technique repeatedly — taking natural rest breaks between attempts — until the timer expires [S9, pp.14-15].

This approach is designed for movements that require neural coordination more than muscle power (e.g., kip-ups, flips, complex agility patterns), where tracking rep counts is less useful than simply accumulating quality practice attempts. Think of it as "treating skill training like *practice*, not like a *workout*." [S9, p.12]

For basketball coaches, time surfing is the appropriate method for any complex motor skill that a player has the physical capacity for but hasn't yet mastered — for example, a new footwork pattern, a complex cut-and-finish sequence, or any coordination-heavy agility drill.

## When to Use
- When the athlete can perform several reps of the target exercise (i.e., has enough power to attempt it)
- When the athlete is progressing quickly through the skill
- When fitting multiple exercises into a single training session
- When a structured timed approach suits the training environment better than ad hoc consolidation training [S9, p.15]

**Do not use** time surfing when the athlete can barely perform the technique once — use [[concept-consolidation-training]] instead.

## Key Principles
1. **Set a time window, not a rep goal** — "5 minutes is good for a beginner, but you can stretch the amount of time if you like" [S9, p.15]. Advanced athletes may use 10+ minute windows.
2. **Do NOT perform continuous reps** — Resting between attempts is essential. Continuous reps for 5 minutes = one exhausting set. That is not time surfing [S9, p.14]
3. **Rest until ready** — After each rep, shake loose, reflect on technique, breathe, then go again. Rest duration is self-regulated by feel, not a fixed clock
4. **Singles are ideal** — For most skill movements, performing one rep, resetting, and then going again allows maximum focus and prevents form breakdown
5. **Aim for perfect reps, not more reps** — "Don't get in the habit of watching the clock and trying to squeeze more reps in" [S9, p.15]. If you want more volume, add time to the window, don't rush reps
6. **Think like a practitioner, not a competitor** — The mindset is Zen-like practice, not athletic competition. Each rep should feel like a conscious, deliberate attempt to improve technique

## Execution
1. Complete a thorough warm-up
2. Set a timer: 5 minutes (beginner), 7-10 minutes (intermediate), 10+ minutes (advanced)
3. Perform one rep of the target skill
4. Rest naturally (10 seconds to 1+ minute depending on difficulty) until ready
5. Perform another rep
6. Continue until timer expires
7. Optional: add another timed block for a second exercise

**Example throughput**: A conditioned athlete with 10-second breaks can accumulate ~5 reps per minute = 50 reps in a 10-minute window [S9, p.15]

## Coaching Points
- "Time surfing is a better way to train for a skill. It permits you to be thoughtful, subjective, and explore what you're doing, without recording numbers or worrying about beating your last performance." [S9, p.15]
- "Allows an athlete to get lots of reps in, avoids fatigue, and dumps the need to do any counting" [S9, p.15]
- Watch for form degradation — if technique breaks down before the timer expires, stop and rest longer or call it done for this block
- This method fits naturally into a structured training session because it has a defined start and end time

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: 5-minute window, singles only, 30-60 seconds between reps
2. **Intermediate**: 7-10 minute window, singles or occasional doubles, 15-30 seconds between reps
3. **Advanced**: 10+ minute window, singles, 10-second natural rest between most reps

## Limitations
- Limits total practice time to a small daily window — some athletes benefit from practicing a skill *throughout* the day, not just in one block
- Works best when the athlete can already perform the movement fairly consistently. If they can barely do it once, [[concept-consolidation-training]] is more appropriate [S9, p.15]
- The defined time window can create a subtle pressure to squeeze reps in, undermining the quality-over-quantity principle
