# Jump Roll (Front Flip Chain — Step 3)

## Objective
Develop explosive upper-body catching ability and plyometric entry into forward rotation — bridging controlled rolls and the eventual explosive handspring take-offs required for the front flip.

## Setup
- **Players**: 1 (solo)
- **Equipment**: Soft surface recommended, especially for beginners
- **Space**: Any open floor area

## Execution
1. Stand with feet symmetrical; bend forward aiming the palms toward the floor.
2. Jump *upward* (not forward) as you descend, allowing the torso to fall while dropping the hands to the floor.
3. Land on the palms with arms fairly straight, just as the feet leave the floor — "you are catching yourself with your palms."
4. Let the braced arms bend to guide the movement as you roll over — head tucked, taking virtually no weight.
5. Roll over the upper back without veering to either side.
6. Finish all the way to a standing position (by this stage in the chain, you should have enough momentum).

**Key distinction from the dive roll**: In the dive roll, gymnasts dive up and *forward*; in the jump roll, you build power and explosive strength by jumping *up* more than forward. This builds the vertical component of front flip power. [S9, p.150]

## Coaching Points
- "Jump UP — not out. You are training vertical power, not horizontal reach."
- Arms catch and control, not crash — brace and guide the weight smoothly.
- Head tuck is non-negotiable — it must be tucked before the hands contact the floor.
- Aim to roll completely to standing position without momentum loss.
- The higher the hands are from the floor when the feet leave the ground, the more plyometric the demand.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner (Regression)**: Begin the jump with the palms already braced on the floor — this reduces upper-body power demands by eliminating the catching component.
2. **Intermediate**: Standard execution — jump up, catch with palms, roll to standing.
3. **Advanced**: Increase the height of the hands above the floor at the moment of take-off — this maximizes the plyometric demand on the upper body.
