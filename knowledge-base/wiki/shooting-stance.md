# Shooting Stance

## Summary
Just as a house is built on a foundation, every shot is built from the ground up. The shooting stance is the single most important starting point for developing a consistent, repeatable shot. Proper stance establishes balance, power source, and the "shot line" — the critical alignment from toe to basketball that directs every shot toward the target. This stance should be identical whether shooting a free throw, a mid-range jumper, or a three-pointer. [S5, pp.10-11]

## When to Use
- On every shot attempt — stationary catch-and-shoot, off-the-dribble pull-up, free throws
- As the foundation for all form shooting drills
- When evaluating and correcting a player's shooting mechanics

## Key Principles
1. **Build from the ground up** — foot position is the foundation; everything else is built on top of it [S5, p.10]
2. **Shoulder-width base** — feet too narrow causes poor balance; feet too wide reduces quickness and explosiveness [S5, p.10]
3. **Stagger the feet** — for a right-handed shooter, slide the left (non-shooting) foot back until its toes are even with the arch of the right (shooting) foot [S5, p.10]
4. **Lead foot points at the target** — the shooting-side foot (same side as the shooting hand) always faces the basket, creating the shot line [S5, p.10]
5. **The Shot Line** — toe, knee, hip, elbow, shoulder, wrist, and basketball should all be aligned as close to a straight line as possible toward the target; this eliminates unnecessary compensating movements [S5, p.10]
6. **Bend at both knees AND hips** — bending only at the knees leaves heels on the floor and disrupts balance; the shoulders should be in front of the feet, and the head in front of the shoulders [S5, pp.10-11]
7. **"Consistency leads to greatness and consistency starts with the feet"** [S5, p.10]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Assume this exact stance before every shot regardless of distance or game situation. The stance must become automatic through thousands of repetitions.

## Variations
### Hip-Bend Practice Drill
From a stationary stance, lean forward and attempt to reach as close to the basket as possible without moving the feet. This forces the natural hip-bend position. Hold for a few seconds, repeat several times until the feeling is familiar. [S5, p.10]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Base too narrow** → Poor balance, reduced shot range → Widen to shoulder-width and stagger feet properly [S5, p.10]
2. **Base too wide** → Sluggish, unable to get shot off quickly → Bring feet to shoulder-width [S5, p.10]
3. **Bending at knees only, not hips** → Heels stay on floor, body is too upright, shooter is thrown off balance on the catch → Actively lean head and shoulders forward during the stance [S5, pp.10-11]
4. **Lead foot not pointed at target** → Shot line is broken, ball goes left or right → Align the toes of the shooting-side foot directly at the basket before catching [S5, p.10]
