# Rear Press Roll (Back Flip Chain Step 2)

## Objective
Progressively load the arms and shoulders during backward rotation, transitioning from pure momentum-based rolling to upper-limb-controlled backward rolls — Step 2 of the back flip chain.

## Setup
- 1 athlete
- Soft surface recommended (grass, mat)
- No equipment required
- Symmetrical foot stance (unlike the rear shoulder roll's staggered stance)

## Execution
1. Stand with **feet symmetrical**, squat toward the ground.
2. With spine curled forward, **gently drop back onto your glutes**.
3. Roll back, bringing legs over the head. Simultaneously **slip palms either side of your head** and **push through the arms**.
4. Let braced arms guide the movement as you roll over — **keep the head tucked** and out of the way; the head should take virtually no weight.
5. Roll over the back, trying **not to veer to either side** (stay centered).
6. Finish the roll in a **seated position**; as feet touch down, **push up with hands** into a crouching or standing position.

## Coaching Points
- Arms are braced beside the head — palms flat, elbows bent
- The head should take virtually NO weight — arms handle all the load
- Keep centered: veering to one side causes uncontrolled shoulder landing
- "Backwards rolls exist primarily to get the athlete's brain and nervous system used to spinning 360 degrees in a short space of time." [S9, p.180]
- This drill directly trains the arm mechanics that will be needed in the back handspring (Step 7)

## Progressions
1. **Beginner (Regression)**: Minimal arm force — use arms for control and neck protection only; let momentum do the work
2. **Intermediate**: Gradually increase arm press force; push up to seated position with arms
3. **Advanced**: Push up to near-standing with a semi-press motion; perform **see-saw drills** (rear press roll immediately into front press roll, repeated for reps)
4. **Advanced combination**: Mix and match with shoulder rolls, rear shoulder rolls, and front rolls

## Common Mistakes
1. **Head taking weight** → neck injury risk; brace arms fully to keep head off floor
2. **Veering to one side** → uncontrolled roll; focus on symmetrical arm placement and centered roll
3. **Not finishing to standing** → reduces transfer to back handspring pattern; push up to complete the roll
4. **Skipping to see-saw drills too early** → only add the see-saw variation after the basic roll is solid
