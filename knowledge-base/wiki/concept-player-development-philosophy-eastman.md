# Player Development Philosophy (Kevin Eastman)

## Summary
Kevin Eastman (team) presents a comprehensive framework for developing pro and high-level players. The central premise is that true development requires two things simultaneously: knowing *what* to work on and *consistently* working on it day in and day out. A quality skill-development program alone is not enough — players must understand their game is unfinished and coaches must bring energy and genuine interest every single day. [S7, pp.299-305]

Eastman's philosophy emphasizes shared responsibility between coach and player, game-speed repetition, conditioning as the prerequisite for skill development, and rigorous charting of progress to maintain motivation.

## When to Use
- When designing individual player workout programs for any level
- When trying to create buy-in from players who don't see the need to keep improving
- When building a season-long skill-development culture
- When evaluating whether your practice sessions produce enough quality repetitions

## Key Principles

### The Theory of Two
1. It takes a coach **two minutes** to explain a fundamental.
2. It takes a player **two weeks** of daily practice to become somewhat comfortable with it.
3. It takes **two months** before the player can call it his own and use it effectively in a game.
> "Improvement is a process that takes time." [S7, p.299]

### Keys to a Quality Player-Development Program
1. **Practice at game speed (or faster).** Players must train skills at the rate they will occur in a game — e.g., two-ball drills or a toss-back device for shooting.
2. **Improvement is a three-step process:** Proper conditioning → Effective practice → Improved skills. The foundation of skill development is conditioning.
3. **Eliminate workout killers:** fatigue and boredom. Gradually condition and rotate a variety of drills for the same fundamental.
4. **Have a written workout plan every day.** Work on these three areas daily: conditioning, dribbling/ball handling, and shooting.
5. **Chart progress.** Players need visible proof of improvement (e.g., 12 made shots Monday → 16 Thursday). [S7, p.301]

### Bullet Basketball
Give each player only two or three quick teaching points per fundamental — enough to remember and apply. Example for shooting:
- Be ready on the catch (knees bent, hands up, feet ready)
- Use the same exact footwork on every shot
- Hold a perfect follow-through until the ball reaches the rim
[S7, p.300]

### Covering the Basics
- **Basketball is a game of precision.** Attend to every small detail — shoulder-to-shoulder off screens, straight-line drives past defenders.
- **Pound dribble.** Any drill requiring dribbling uses a hard, quick pound dribble; the more control in the hand, the more options available.
- **Play low to high.** Every player should be down in a stance and ready — knees bent, feet ready on the catch, no wasted motion into the shot.
- **Basketball is a shoulders game.** Cutter's shoulders below the screener's; post player's shoulders below the defender's; "shoulders–hips" when going by a defender.
- **Feet first, ball second.** Get the footwork advantage first, then use the ball to create separation. *"It's a physical hand and forearm fight to get open, but it's a foot fight to score."*
- **Find your feet on all shots.** The quality of footwork determines the quality of the shot. *"Point 10 toes at the rim."* Same footwork on every shot. [S7, pp.302-303]

### Workout Musts (Beyond Standard Skills)
- **Weak-hand development.** Better opponents will force the weak hand.
- **Footwork and balance.** Be balanced on every shot and every move.
- **Playing through contact.** Use workout partners to simulate real defensive contact.
- **Contesting shots.** Practice under game conditions — have workout partners actively contest every shot. [S7, p.301]

### Seven Keys to a Quality Shooting Workout
1. Incorporate catch-and-shoot (spot-up) shots
2. Incorporate cut-catch-and-shoot shots
3. Incorporate shots off the dribble
4. Focus on perfect form (perfect feet, perfect follow-through)
5. Contest all shots
6. Take game shots from game spots at game speed
7. Chart makes and misses [S7, p.302]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions:** Hard work (price of admission), Competitiveness (attitude that won't quit), Commitment (year-round dedication to skill, team success, conditioning, and rehabilitation)

## Coaching Responsibilities
- "Bring it" every day — be enthusiastic, filled with energy, genuinely interested in improvement
- Study the game and your system thoroughly; knowledge leads to respect
- Catch players doing something right
- Tell players the truth verbally, on video, and with statistics
- Cultivate trust and mutual respect — "You can't motivate anyone without trust"

## Creating Buy-In From Players
1. Thoroughly study the game (knowledge → respect)
2. Thoroughly study your system (show you know how they improve in it)
3. Catch them doing something right
4. Bring energy, enthusiasm, work ethic, and genuine interest to workouts
5. Tell players the truth — verbally, on video, and with statistics [S7, p.301]

## Studying the Game
Players must actively study — watching film honestly, reading articles, talking to other players, observing how great players structure their workouts. Key questions: How long are their sessions? How many shots per day? What dribbling drills? [S7, pp.303-304]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not enough shots in practice** → Tape practice and count; it is almost always less than needed. In pickup games players average only 3.2 shots per game to 7 (≈12.8/hr) — far too few. [S7, p.300]
2. **Generic teaching points** → Use bullet basketball: 2-3 specific cues per fundamental only.
3. **No contact in workouts** → Always contest shots and simulate defensive pressure.
4. **No charting** → Players who can't see their improvement lose motivation.
5. **Boredom and monotony** → Rotate a variety of drills for the same fundamental; keep workouts fresh.
