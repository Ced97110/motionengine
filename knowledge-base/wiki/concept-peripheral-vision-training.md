# Peripheral Vision Training

## Summary
Peripheral vision — seeing events outside the direct focus of gaze — is critical for basketball players reading the floor, seeing cutters, picking up defensive rotations, and avoiding turnovers. Peripheral vision is processed by **rods** (nerve cells along the outside portion of the eyes), which register information more slowly than **cones** (nerve cells at the center of the eye). However, this peripheral reaction time is **trainable**: athletes who consistently practice reacting to stimuli in their peripheral field get faster at doing so. [S8, pp.216-222]

Likewise, practice reacting to **central** vision stimuli also improves central vision reaction time. The two systems are somewhat independent and should each be trained. Basketball players need both — they must read a defender in front of them (central) while seeing a cutter from the corner of their eye (peripheral).

## When to Use
- In the second half of a training mesocycle (weeks 4–6) when complexity increases
- As a component of agility ladder drills with visual reaction components
- During film sessions — teach players to consciously expand their visual field on the court
- Year-round as a supplement to other SAQ training

## Key Principles
1. **Cones = central vision, faster**: Processing stimuli seen directly in focus is inherently faster
2. **Rods = peripheral vision, slower but trainable**: Peripheral reaction time improves significantly with targeted practice
3. **Coach semicircle method**: Place one coach directly at the end of the ladder and one or two coaches at 45° and 90° angles — athlete must react to stimuli coming from the periphery [S8, p.216]
4. **Cross-training effect**: Training peripheral reactions also speeds up central reactions, and vice versa
5. **Cognitive load integration**: Combine with a demanding physical task (ladder footwork) to simulate real game cognitive-motor demands

## Training Methods
- **Single coach, end of ladder**: Basic — stimuli in central vision
- **Coach semicircle (2 coaches)**: Intermediate — stimuli from 45°–90° off center
- **Multiple coach semicircle (3 coaches)**: Advanced — simultaneous stimuli from multiple peripheral angles
- **Odd/even filter**: Adds selective attention layer — only respond to odd numbers, even numbers, or specific signals

## Common Mistakes
1. **Always training with frontal stimuli** → never develops true peripheral awareness
2. **Moving too slowly to notice the peripheral signal** → peripheral reaction benefits require game-speed execution
3. **Not varying stimulus location** → rotate coach positions every session to prevent habituation
