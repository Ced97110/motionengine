# Qualities of Good Players and Teammates

## Summary
Being a great teammate is a learnable skill. Herb Brown's framework identifies 20 specific behaviors that transform individual players into cohesive team members. The list spans defense, offense, communication, and character — reflecting Brown's belief that basketball IQ and unselfishness are as important as physical talent.

The framework applies at every level: youth players learning to share the ball, college players executing within a system, and professionals maintaining focus through a long season. pro greats like an iconic scorer and Joe Dumars exemplified being "coaches on the floor" — players who understood what championship-caliber basketball required and delivered it every possession.

## When to Use
- Distributed to players at the start of each season as a team standards document
- Referenced in player evaluations and individual meetings
- Used in film sessions to highlight great teammate plays (charges taken, screens set, defensive communication)
- Reinforced after team breakdowns to identify which quality was missing

## Key Principles
1. **Always strive to make your teammates better players.** Elevation of others is the highest individual contribution.
2. **Play intelligently** within the team framework. Display basketball street smarts.
3. **Think first about team success** — not personal statistics or recognition.
4. **Act constructively** during practices and games. Negative body language poisons the team.
5. **Encourage your teammates** at all times, especially after mistakes.
6. **Repeat calls and signals on the floor** to help teammates stay aligned.
7. **Be an active defender both on and off the ball.** Deny and stop dribble penetration. See the ball on the weak side; see your man on the ball side.
8. **Concentrate, be focused, and react quickly** and instinctively to changing situations.
9. **Remain aware of time, score, and situation** at all times.
10. **Limit turnovers.** Strive for at least a 2:1 assist-to-turnover ratio.
11. **Demonstrate good shot selection** — take shots within your offensive role.
12. **Shoot a high percentage** — quality over quantity.
13. **Force the fast break but not the bad shot.** Transition offense must be disciplined.
14. **Become an outstanding free-throw shooter.** FTs are free points; missed FTs are unforced errors.
15. **Do the necessary extra things** — take charges, set screens, make the extra pass.
16. **Rebound the ball.** Know where the ball is going before you jump — don't jump at the peak of the shot.
17. **Retain your focus, concentration, and poise** through the game's emotional swings.
18. **Be consistent and make intelligent basketball decisions** — not just athletic plays.
19. **Be supportive of teammates and coaches** — even when disagreeing privately.
20. **Represent your team, school, or organization** in a positive and respectful manner.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Sets the tone for communication on the floor; models basketball IQ and court vision.
- **SG/SF**: Active denial defense; quick transition back; disciplined shot selection.
- **PF/C**: Rebounding effort and communication; setting legal screens; making the extra pass out of double-teams.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Ball-watching on defense** → Active defenders move on the pass, not the catch; always track both the ball and your man.
2. **Poor shot selection** → Taking difficult contested shots when open teammates exist violates team play.
3. **Negative body language** → Visibly pouting or blaming after turnovers breaks team cohesion and hurts the coach's authority.
4. **Jumping at the wrong time on rebounds** → Know where the ball will come off before leaving your feet — positioning beats athleticism.
