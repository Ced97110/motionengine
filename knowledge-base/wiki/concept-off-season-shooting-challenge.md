# Off-Season Shooting Challenge

## Summary
The Off-Season Challenge is a goal-based cumulative shooting framework that assigns players a total shot target to complete during the off-season. Unlike daily workouts, which prescribe reps per session, the off-season challenge gives players full autonomy over how they distribute their volume across the months — the only requirement is completing the total. The system uses structured tracking charts so athletes can monitor daily progress against long-term goals.

There are two families of challenges: **General Shooting Challenges** (10,000–20,000 game shots mixing all scoring moves) and **Three-Point Challenges** (1,000–4,000 three-pointers *made* at game speed). [S3, pp.186-197]

## When to Use
- Off-season (summer, winter break) when no team practices are scheduled
- As a motivational accountability tool for individual players committed to skill improvement
- To set measurable, concrete shooting goals that can be reviewed at the start of the next season
- Coaches can use completion of these challenges as a team-building recognition system

## Key Principles
1. **Track cumulative progress daily** — use the provided chart (date + shots per move category + daily total) so athletes see the running total climb toward the goal
2. **Game speed only** — all shots come off one or two dribbles; spot shots are included but not the primary volume driver
3. **Alternate directions** — every dribble-move series alternates L and R; log them separately to identify weak-hand deficits
4. **Scale to the athlete** — start with the 10,000-shot or 1,000-three-point challenge; elite and college-prep players target 20,000 or 4,000
5. **5,000 free throws accompany every general shooting challenge** — FTs are part of the development package, not an afterthought
6. **Three-point challenges track MAKES, not attempts** — this forces game-speed shooting and honest percentage accountability

### Move Menu (all challenges)
**Shot Fake (SF) Series:**
- SF Stride Stop
- SF Stride Stop Step-Through
- SF Big Hop
- SF Hop Back
- SF Hop Back to Roll Out Dribble
- SF Hop Back to Crossover Dribble

**Rip Step Series:**
- Rip Step Stride Stop
- Rip Step Stride Stop Step-Through
- Rip Step Big Hop
- Rip Step Hop Back
- Rip Step Hop Back to Roll Out Dribble
- Rip Step Hop Back to Crossover Dribble
- Rip Step Pull-Up 3PT

All shots alternate L/R; one or two dribbles; any spot on the floor.

### Three-Point Category Breakdown (equal distribution)
| Category | 1,000 Target | 2,000 Target | 3,000 Target | 4,000 Target |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Spot 3s | 400 make | 500 make | 750 make | 1,000 make |
| Rip Step Pull-Up 3s | 200 make | 500 make | 750 make | 1,000 make |
| Transition 3s | 200 make | 500 make | 750 make | 1,000 make |
| Curl/Fill/Fade Off Screen | 200 make | 500 make | 750 make | 1,000 make |

> **Important:** These are *makes*, not attempts. A player shooting 40% from three must take 2,500 attempts to make 1,000 three-pointers.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Focus on Rip Step Pull-Up 3PT and transition three-point categories; SF/Rip dribble-move series from the top and wings
- **SG**: Full menu; specialize in curl/fill/fade-off-screen category
- **SF**: Full menu; emphasize both corner spot 3s and transition categories
- **PF**: Rip Step and SF series from midrange; if three-point shooting is part of role, add 3PT challenge
- **C**: Can substitute midrange moves for 3PT categories in general shooting challenges

## Common Mistakes
1. **Shooting all volume in casual fashion (no dribble, stationary)** → Every rep must simulate a game situation with at least one dribble
2. **Frontloading or backloading the off-season** → Distribute work throughout; use the chart to ensure even weekly pacing
3. **Ignoring the free-throw component** → 5,000 FTs in a summer = the most targeted FT development possible
4. **Not tracking the three-point challenge by category** → Tracking spot 3s vs. pull-up vs. transition separately reveals where the player's three-point game is weakest
5. **Choosing a volume level too high for the athlete's commitment** → Better to complete the 10,000 challenge with quality than abandon the 20,000 challenge halfway through
