# Perimeter One-on-One Intelligence

## Summary
There is a critical difference between **knowing how to make a move** and knowing **when to make it**. Perimeter one-on-one intelligence is the ability to read a defender in real time and select the correct action — drive, pull-up, kick-out, or wait — within the spatial constraints of motion offense spacing. This intelligence is developed through deliberate practice with a live defender and is the foundation for elite individual offense [S15, p.215].

As noted in the source: *"Patience is required (for both coaches and players) because players will make many mistakes before they become intelligent one-on-one players. Once players have developed this intelligence, they will make all other players on your squad better."* [S15, p.215]

## When to Use
- Every half-court possession in a motion offense
- Whenever a player receives the ball in their scoring zone
- When the ball is driven to a dead end in the paint (scoring at the end of the dribble)

## Key Principles
1. **15-foot lane spacing is the operating zone**: The motion offense requires teammates to maintain 15-foot spacing — the one-on-one player must create their shot in this space [S15, p.215].
2. **Three-dribble limit builds efficiency**: Players should be able to create a quality shot in three dribbles or fewer — more dribbles collapse the defense.
3. **Begin with a rocker step**: The rocker step (jab step read) is the universal starting point for all one-on-one perimeter moves.
4. **Read the defender's reaction**: Separation — not the move itself — is the goal. If separation exists, shoot. If not, read again.
5. **Don't take a bad shot**: Creating separation is the objective; only shoot when a quality shot exists.
6. **Create open teammates**: Elite one-on-one players force defenders to help, opening teammates for catch-and-shoot opportunities.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG/SF**: Master at least 2–3 dribble move combinations from their scoring spots.
- **All perimeter players**: Develop the patience to read the defender rather than forcing a predetermined action.
- **Teammates off-ball**: Maintain spacing (15 feet) so the one-on-one player has room to operate.

## Variations

### Front-Foot-to-Pivot-Foot Defense
Defender stays on the shooter's shooting shoulder rather than forcing direction changes — teaches the attacker to use the in-and-out dribble or crossover to get the defender shifting weight.

### Make-It/Keep-It Offense
Offensive player stays on offense as long as they create separation — win or lose the shot. Extends competitive reps and rewards good decision-making.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Taking a bad shot after 3 dribbles** → Correction: kick the ball out to an open teammate when defense is solid.
2. **Telegraphing the move** → Correction: use the rocker step to make the defender commit before attacking.
3. **Using too many dribbles** → Correction: 3-dribble limit in practice; 1–2 dribbles are often enough.
4. **Playing in a lane that is too wide** → Correction: stay in the 15-foot lane — it forces decision-making.
