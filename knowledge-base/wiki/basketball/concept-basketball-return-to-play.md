# Basketball Return-to-Play Algorithm

## Summary
When a basketball player is injured, a systematic field triage and return-to-play algorithm ensures the player returns to competition as quickly as possible — but more importantly, as safely as possible. The process begins the moment an injury occurs on the court or practice floor, continues through diagnosis and treatment, and concludes only when the player is fully cleared for contact. The S&C coach, physiotherapist, and team physician must collaborate at every stage [S10, pp.128-130].

## When to Use
- Any injury that takes a player off the court during practice or competition
- Serious injuries (hamstring tear, severe ankle sprain, meniscal injury) requiring weeks of absence
- Planning a progressive return from a surgical procedure
- Managing an injured player's minutes when returning from a rehabilitation plan

## Key Principles

### Field Triage
1. **Inform the head of medical team immediately** when any injury occurs — triage begins
2. **Minor injuries** (strain, sprain): head physiotherapist evaluates and initiates treatment; physician evaluates if needed
3. **Diagnosis drives the plan**: after quick diagnosis, a treatment plan is communicated to all medical staff AND the coach — including estimated time out of game
4. **Player transparency**: the player must be fully informed about the diagnosis, treatment plan, and estimated timeline
5. **Non-orthopedic injuries**: eye and facial injuries require immediate specialist consultation (dentist, ophthalmologist) — these specialists should have pre-established relationships with the team [S10, p.128]
6. **Competition evaluation**: medical staff must quickly assess whether a player can return to the game; superficial lacerations/bruises can be treated immediately on court or in the locker room [S10, p.130]
7. **Physician knows the player**: personality awareness is essential — some players under-report, some over-report injury severity [S10, p.130]

### Rehabilitation Phases (Serious Injuries)
**Phase 1: Diagnosis and Acute Treatment**
- Diagnose the injury and its severity; each injury has its own healing timeframe
- Physiotherapy + modalities to decrease inflammation, swelling, and pain
- Rest, ice, compression, elevation (RICE)

**Phase 2: Range of Motion + Strengthening**
- ROM exercises combined with progressive strengthening
- Medical team (PT, S&C coach) co-manages this phase

**Phase 3: Basketball-Specific Exercises**
- S&C coach initiates basketball-specific exercise program
- This phase is the S&C coach's primary responsibility for court return

**Phase 4: Progressive Return to Practice**
- Start with non-contact practice
- Gradually increase practice time and contact load
- No regression = progress to next level

**Phase 5: Medical Clearance**
- Player must be pain-free with full range of motion before full return
- Additional imaging only if mild pain or hesitation persists
- Physician, in partnership with the coach and player, may allow early return with explicit limitations (e.g., minute restriction) [S10, p.130]

## College Basketball Return-to-Play Context
- General management: RICE + activity modification + progressive ROM/strength restoration
- Athletes resume sport when they have restored ROM, normal strength, and good joint stability
- Prevention focus: strength, flexibility, and proprioceptive training [S10, p.136]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Rushing the player back** → Only return when fully pain-free with full ROM; minute restrictions must be strictly followed
2. **Missing the diagnosis** → Quick assessment under game pressure can miss severity — always follow up with proper imaging post-game when in doubt
3. **Excluding the player from the plan** → Player buy-in improves compliance; they must understand the timeline and rationale
4. **S&C coach isolation** → The S&C coach must be looped into every stage of the rehabilitation plan, not just brought in at Phase 3
5. **Ignoring specialist injuries** → Eye injuries, dental injuries, and facial trauma require specific consultants; orthopedic surgeons cannot manage all injury types alone
