# ABCDE Primary Survey Protocol for Basketball Emergencies

## Summary
The ABCDE protocol (Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure) is the systematic framework used by team physicians to rapidly assess and manage life- or limb-threatening injuries on the basketball court. Each letter represents a sequential step in the primary survey, with the goal of identifying immediately reversible threats to life before proceeding to definitive care or transfer. This protocol must be known and rehearsed by every member of the medical team before the season begins.

The framework originates from Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS) and has been adapted for sports medicine settings. Every basketball team with a medical team member should have this protocol established as a pre-agreed emergency action plan. [S10, pp.169-174]

## When to Use
- Any athlete who collapses, loses consciousness, or presents with severe distress on the court
- After major trauma (hard foul, collision, fall from height, head-first contact)
- When an athlete cannot answer simple questions coherently
- After any cardiac or respiratory event

## Key Principles

### A — Airway
1. Assess whether the airway is patent and protected
2. Use head tilt–chin lift if no cervical spine injury suspected
3. Bag–valve–mask (BVM) ventilation: 1 breath every 5 seconds; 2 breaths per 30 chest compressions if CPR also ongoing
4. Pulse oximetry to assess ventilation adequacy — values near or below 92% are worrisome; normal is ~100%
5. Avoid strong scents near an unconscious athlete — they can trigger reflexive sudden head extension

### B — Breathing
1. Assess respiratory rate, effort, and chest symmetry
2. Tension pneumothorax requires immediate needle decompression (2nd intercostal space) before transfer
3. Respiratory arrest: start CPR and transfer immediately

### C — Circulation
1. **Do not delay chest compressions to check pulse** — pulse identification during compression is difficult even for trained professionals
2. Palpate the carotid artery (under the angle of the jaw) — more reliable than radial or femoral at low blood pressure
3. If carotid pulse is absent AND signs of shock/cardiac arrest: initiate CPR immediately
4. Assess heart rate and rhythm — should be regular with no skipped or extra beats
5. Normal resting HR: 60–100 bpm adults; up to 140 bpm in children under 5; ~120 bpm pre-puberty
6. **Trained athletes may have resting HR as low as 40 bpm** — do not misinterpret bradycardia as pathological without context
7. Athletes have better compensatory mechanisms for hypovolemic shock — imminent shock may be harder to recognize
8. Obvious bleeding: apply pressure dressings, elevate lower extremities (if no complicating injuries), commercial tourniquet for extremity bleeding, IV normal saline (0.9%) as first choice; Ringer's lactate as alternative
9. For shock: 1,000–2,000 cc normal saline; if no response, consider non-hemorrhagic causes
10. Significant hemorrhage may require blood transfusion at a medical facility

### D — Disability (Neurological)
1. Suspect significant head injury if: athlete cannot answer simple questions coherently; open/depressed/basal skull fracture signs; 2+ vomiting episodes; amnesia >30 min
2. Bleeding from ears, nose, or mouth + periorbital bruising = suspect skull fracture and closed head injury → transfer immediately
3. Quick neurological exam: pupil size and light reaction, eye movements, grip strength, toe movement, ability to follow commands
4. Unilateral pupil dilation or poor light response = intracranial injury (usually ipsilateral to enlarged pupil)
5. Use the **Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)** to estimate severity (see table below)
6. Check all extremities: look for bleeding, swelling, deformity; palpate and log-roll each limb
7. Check neurovascular status distal to any extremity injury (sensation, pulses)
8. Immobilize fractures with prefabricated or vacuum splints; if vascular injury → emergent transfer
9. Dislocations: attempt field reduction if experienced; if unable, immobilize comfortably

### Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)
| Category | Response | Score |
|---|---|---|
| **Eye Opening** | Spontaneous | 4 |
| | To voice | 3 |
| | To pain | 2 |
| | None | 1 |
| **Verbal** | Oriented | 5 |
| | Confused | 4 |
| | Inappropriate words | 3 |
| | Incomprehensible sounds | 2 |
| | None | 1 |
| **Motor** | Obeys command | 6 |
| | Localizes pain | 5 |
| | Withdraws | 4 |
| | Flexion | 3 |
| | Extension | 2 |
| | None | 1 |

**GCS 13–15** = mild injury; **9–12** = moderate; **≤8** = severe (transfer immediately) [S10, p.170]

## Player Responsibilities
*(This concept applies to the medical team, not positional players)*
- **Team Physician**: leads the ABCDE survey, makes transfer decisions, performs field procedures
- **Athletic Trainer/Assistant**: assists with BVM, stabilization, equipment retrieval
- **Coaching Staff**: clear the court, keep players away, call 911 if directed

## Conditions Requiring Immediate Transfer

| Category | Condition | Initial Action |
|---|---|---|
| Airway | Airway compromise | Secure airway; transfer immediately |
| Airway | Anaphylaxis | Epinephrine IM; transfer immediately |
| Breathing | Respiratory arrest | Start CPR; transfer |
| Breathing | Tension pneumothorax | Needle decompression; transfer |
| Circulation | Cardiac arrest | CPR + AED; transfer |
| Circulation | Severe dehydration | Fluids; transfer |
| Circulation | Internal organ injury | IV fluids; NPO; transfer |
| Circulation | Myocardial infarction | Aspirin; transfer |
| Disability | Suspected spine injury | C-spine immobilization on spinal board; transfer |
| Disability | Loss of consciousness | C-spine immobilization; may need CT |
| Disability | Open fracture | Sterile dressing + splint; antibiotics; transfer |
| Disability | Acute compartment syndrome | Splint; transfer; NPO |
| Environment | Heat illness | Cool patient; fluids; remove clothing; transfer |
| Environment | Hypothermia | Warm patient; dry clothes; transfer |
| Other | Hypoglycemia | Glucose PO or IV; may need glucagon IM |
| Other | Hyponatremia | Fluid restrict; transfer for sodium labs |
| Other | Seizure | Logroll to side; benzodiazepine if >30 min; transfer |
| Other | Eye injury | Eye shield; avoid pressure; transfer |
| Other | Dental injury | Transport tooth in medium; transfer |

## First Aid Skills for the Sideline Physician
1. **Suturing and wound care**: sterile technique for lacerations; antiseptic, local anesthetic, suture materials, needle driver, scissors, forceps, gauze; sterile adhesive strips or surgical glue for small wounds
2. **Athletic taping**: ankle, wrist, finger sprains; patellofemoral pain; foot problems
3. **Splinting**: immobilize joint distally and proximally, centering the area of injury
4. **Applying pressure bandages**
5. **Protective devices**: eye shields, protective padding
6. **Ice and heat application, stretching, simple massage**

## Reducing Dislocations on the Field
- Most common: shoulder (>90% anterior)
- Attempt reduction before pain/spasm/swelling become too significant
- **Always assess neurovascular status** before and after any reduction attempt
- Hip dislocation = emergency (avascular necrosis risk) → heavy sedation usually required at facility
- Knee dislocation: can attempt field reduction but needs vascular evaluation regardless
- Patella: extend knee, manually guide patella medially back into trochlear groove
- Finger PIP joints: assess, reduce, buddy tape
- Always consider associated fractures — further evaluation required even if athlete returns to play

[S10, pp.172-173]

## Special Issues
- **Muscle cramps/spasms**: Fine needling with acupuncture needles to the contracted muscle (e.g., gastrocnemius, hamstring) can rapidly resolve spasm and allow return to play [S10, p.173]
- Pre-travel meetings with athletes allow the physician to learn individual medical histories, medications, and build rapport that improves emergency preparedness

## Common Mistakes
1. Delaying chest compressions to find a pulse → start CPR within 10 seconds of assessment
2. Using strong scents near a head-injured athlete → can trigger reflexive neck extension
3. Assuming a trained athlete's low HR indicates pathology → athletes can have resting HR of 40 bpm
4. Attempting repeated dislocation reductions after initial failure → limited attempts; immobilize and transfer
5. Allowing return to play after dislocation without further evaluation → always refer for imaging
