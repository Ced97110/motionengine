# ACL Injury Biomechanics in Basketball

## Summary
Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injuries are among the most serious and costly injuries in basketball, occurring 2–4× more frequently in women (0.094 per 1000 athletic exposures) than men (0.02 per 1000 AEs). The majority (60–70%) occur via a noncontact mechanism, with 90% during single-leg activity. Rebounding is the most frequently cited activity causing injury. ACL injuries are of particular concern because of the high rate of concurrent meniscal injury (65%), lifetime cost approaching $100,000 per injury, and early development of knee osteoarthritis (signs of joint degradation in up to 48% of athletes within 10 years).

The primary biomechanical risk factor is dynamic lower extremity valgus — specifically knee abduction moment — which is the single best predictor of ACL injury in female athletes. A second ACL injury risk is also very high: 25% of surgically reconstructed patients suffer a second injury within 12 months of return to play.

## When to Use
- When assessing landing mechanics during jump training and on-court skill work
- When designing neuromuscular training programs, especially for female athletes
- When screening return-to-play readiness after ACL reconstruction
- In pre-season movement screening to identify high-risk athletes

## Key Principles
1. **Noncontact dominant**: 60–70% of basketball ACL injuries occur without direct contact; yet up to 50% of injured female athletes had a collision or push within 1 meter immediately prior — indirect contact is a major factor [S10, pp.42-43]
2. **Dynamic lower extremity valgus** is the primary high-risk movement pattern — the knee abduction moment (external torque pushing the knee into valgus) is the single best predictor of ACL injury in female athletes [S10, p.43]
3. **Shallow knee flexion at landing** is an independent ACL risk factor — strong quadriceps contraction with foot anterior to center of mass dramatically increases ACL loading [S10, p.43]
4. **Injury within 40 ms of landing** — ACL failure is represented by rapid knee abduction and internal rotation within 40 ms of initial ground contact [S10, p.43]
5. **Hamstring:quad ratio matters** — the ACL resists anterior tibial translation; strong quadriceps promote it; hamstring contraction generates a protective effect. Athletes with reduced total hamstring strength have higher ACL injury risk [S10, p.44]
6. **Re-injury risk is very high** — 25% of reconstructed patients suffer a second injury (same or opposite limb) within 12 months of return to play; transverse plane hip kinetics and frontal plane knee kinematics are key second-injury predictors [S10, p.44]
7. **Prevention program success is limited in basketball** — first three published RCTs in women's basketball showed no significant ACL injury reduction; two recent studies (Omi et al.; Bonato et al.) showed the first reductions using hip-focused neuromuscular training [S10, p.44]

## Player Responsibilities (Biomechanical Targets)
- **All positions**: Learn to land with good knee stability (knees out, not together), on flexed knees, with hips back so knees stay behind toes
- **Guards (PG/SG)**: Higher jump frequency increases exposure; land technique must be drilled daily
- **Centers (PF/C)**: Rebounding is the highest ACL injury activity — box-out and landing mechanics must be reinforced

## Optimal Landing Technique (Coaching Cues)
- "Keep knees out" — prevent dynamic valgus collapse
- "Land on bent knees" — avoid stiff, shallow-flexion landings
- "Hips back" — keep hips behind knees to reduce anterior tibial shear force

## Variations
### Hip-Focused Prevention Program (Omi et al.)
3×/week, 20 min per session; education + neuromuscular training emphasizing strength, balance, and technique focused at the hip. Successfully reduced ACL injury rate in women's basketball over 12 years [S10, p.44]

### 5-Part Warm-Up Prevention Program (Bonato et al.)
30-min warm-up protocol: general muscular activation → active mobility/flexibility → strengthening → plyometrics → agility. Reduced lower limb injuries in elite female basketball players [S10, p.44]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stiff-legged landings** → Coach landing with >45° knee flexion; avoid heel-dominant landing with foot anterior to COM
2. **Knee valgus under fatigue** → Late-game and fourth-quarter fatigue amplifies valgus collapse; must train landing mechanics under fatigued conditions
3. **Returning to play too early** → 25% re-injury rate within 12 months; return-to-play criteria must include biomechanical screening, not just time-based protocols
4. **Neglecting the hip** → Low hip strength is directly associated with greater lower extremity valgus during single-leg jump landings — hip strengthening is non-negotiable [S10, p.45]
