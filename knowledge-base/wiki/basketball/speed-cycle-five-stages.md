# The Five-Stage Speed Cycle

## Summary
Most athletes who want to be faster spend virtually all of their training time on **Stage V: Movement Speed** — improving how fast muscles physically execute a movement. According to Paul Wade, this is a critical training error because Stage V is only one of five components that determine how fast an athlete actually performs in competition. The other four stages — which involve mental awareness, perception, recognition, and decision-making — account for the majority of what makes athletes truly fast.

Understanding and training all five stages of the speed cycle is the most direct path to becoming a faster, more reactive basketball player. [S9, p.11-13]

## The Five Stages

### Stage I: Pre-Perception Awareness
Becoming aware that you *may* need to move fast — before the triggering event occurs. In basketball: reading offensive body language, weight shifts, or ball-handling tendencies that signal what is about to happen. This is the stage Wayne Gretzky famously exploited ("play where the puck is going to be"). [S9, p.12]

**Training method:** Sensory openness, mental focus, and opponent/situation study. Study your opponent's tendencies, weight shifts, and cues before they act.

### Stage II: Perception Speed
The moment you first become consciously aware of an event requiring fast movement — seeing a pass, a drive, a screen being set. Primarily a visual sense; other senses contribute as well. [S9, p.12]

**Training method:** Visual sense training — eye exercises (figure-8 eye circuits), peripheral vision development, sensory mediation, hearing sharpness training.

### Stage III: Recognition Speed
The split-second the brain correctly identifies *what* is happening. Ambiguous stimuli (e.g., is that a screen or a cut?) must be resolved almost instantly. Without training, the brain wastes precious time here. [S9, p.12]

**Training method:** Reflex channeling — drilling specific responses to specific stimuli thousands of times so recognition happens automatically, bypassing conscious thought.

### Stage IV: Decision Speed
Having recognized the event, the brain must choose the correct response. In basketball: help or stay? Switch or fight through? Shoot or pass? This is a brainstem-level process during game situations, not a deliberate upper-cortex decision. [S9, p.13]

**Training method:** Reflex channeling — drilling preferred responses to specific situations until the correct decision fires automatically (see [[reflex-channeling-hebbs-rule]]).

### Stage V: Movement Speed
The neuromuscular execution of the chosen action — how quickly the body actually moves. This is the stage nearly all physical training addresses. [S9, p.13]

**Training method:** Explosive calisthenics chains, plyometrics, SAQ training, strength training.

## Why Most Athletes Miss 80% of Speed
> "Most athletes who want to be fast spend up to 100% of their training time exclusively on stage V: building greater movement speed. That's awesome, but it's also pretty misguided—athletes who do this are missing out on the other four stages. Damn, that's 80% of what makes you truly fast!" — Paul Wade [S9, p.13]

## Basketball Applications

| Stage | Basketball Example | Training Method |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-Perception | Reading a ball-handler's hip rotation before a drive | Film study, on-court opponent scouting |
| Perception | Seeing a skip pass in the air | Peripheral vision drills, sensory training |
| Recognition | Identifying whether a screen is a flare or down screen | Defensive drill repetition, game exposure |
| Decision | Choose to switch or fight through | Repetition of defensive principles in live drills |
| Movement | Close-out sprint execution | Plyometrics, SAQ, explosive calisthenics |

## Key Principles
1. **Train all five stages** — design practice to include awareness, perception, and decision components, not just physical movements
2. **Anticipation creates "free" speed** — even a split-second pre-read makes a player appear faster than their movement speed alone would suggest [S9, p.14]
3. **Stages III and IV require reflex programming** — they cannot be improved by thinking faster; they need thousands of drilled repetitions to become automatic
4. **Visual training is trainable** — peripheral vision, figure-8 eye circuits, side-on TV watching all develop the perceptual hardware
5. **Instincts and gut feelings are real inputs** — olfactory/tactile senses that manifest as "gut feelings" should not be suppressed; they are pre-perception signals [S9, p.17]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Only training movement speed** → Spend deliberate time on all five stages, especially anticipation/reading skills
2. **Treating defense as purely physical** → "Staying in front of the ball handler" begins with Stage I-II reads, not Stage V footwork
3. **Practicing only in controlled drills** → Live competitive drills force stages I-IV; closed drills only develop stage V
