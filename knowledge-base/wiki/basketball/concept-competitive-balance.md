# NBA Competitive Balance and the Power of Parity

## Summary
The NBA exhibits a structural pull toward competitive balance — bad teams tend to improve and good teams tend to decline toward a .500 winning percentage over time. This regression to the mean is driven by the draft system (bad teams pick first), personnel shuffling, and natural talent distribution. However, the pull is asymmetric: bad teams climb to .500 faster than good teams decline, and historic franchises (Boston, LA, Chicago, Philadelphia) have shown the ability to maintain excellence for extended periods.

For coaches, understanding competitive balance has strategic implications: a team's current record may not accurately reflect its true talent level, point differential is a better indicator of future performance, and parity means that consistent systems and player development are more reliable than chasing short-term record improvements.

## When to Use
- When evaluating whether to make drastic roster changes based on a poor season
- When projecting next season's performance for roster planning
- When understanding that regular season win-loss records may be "lucky" or "unlucky" relative to point differential
- When building a long-term franchise strategy

## Key Principles
1. **Regression to .500**: Over one year, competition draws teams approximately one-third of the way to .500 [S16, p.111]
2. **Bad teams improve faster**: Teams winning 10–20% of games have a 96% historical chance of improving the next season; teams winning 80–90% have a 100% chance of declining [S16, Table 9.1]
3. **Five-year convergence**: After five years, most bad teams reach approximately .500 ("ultimate fate"), but elite franchises can maintain excellence above .500 for a decade [S16, pp.111-112]
4. **Big-market advantage**: Classic NBA powerhouses (Boston, LA, Chicago, Philadelphia) resist parity pull — consistent with sports economists' finding that big-market teams stay ahead regardless of draft and free agency rules [S16, p.112]
5. **No historical trend toward imbalance**: NBA competitive balance since the 1960s shows no obvious trend up or down — dynasties (Celtics, Bulls, Lakers) appear periodically but don't represent structural degradation [S16, p.113]
6. **Fan preference**: Research suggests fans prefer games where one team has a 60–65% win probability — the sweet spot between domination and bland parity [S16, p.109]
7. **Playoff vs. regular season**: Playoff predictability (Lakers 2000–02, Bulls 1990s) is a separate issue from regular season balance; dominant playoff teams may be tanking regular season games [S16, p.113]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Overreacting to a bad season** → Historical data shows most bad teams improve naturally; avoid panic trades that cost future assets
2. **Trusting regular season record over point differential** → A team outscoring opponents but with a losing record may be "unlucky" and due for improvement (see [[concept-bell-curve-method]])
3. **Assuming dynasty = structural imbalance** → The NBA has had dynasties since the 1960s without a trend toward increasing imbalance; a few repeat champions is historically normal
