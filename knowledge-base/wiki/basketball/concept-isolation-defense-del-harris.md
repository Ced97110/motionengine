# Isolation Defense (Del Harris)

## Summary
Del Harris outlines three defensive responses to teams that isolate one or two skilled one-on-one players. The decision must be made pregame as part of the scouting plan, and all five defenders must understand their rotations before the game. The three options are: (1) accept the 1v1 with weak-side help, (2) trap from the top with rotations, and (3) push to the baseline and trap there. A fourth option is shifting to a 2-3 zone formation around the on-ball defender. [S7, p.259]

## When to Use
- Against a team's primary isolation scorer (e.g., a dominant wing who initiates every half-court possession)
- When the isolated player is significantly better than your on-ball defender in 1v1 situations
- When scouting reveals predictable isolation spots (wing, corner, high post)

## Key Principles
1. Decide the strategy pregame — don't improvise during the game on isolation coverage
2. All rotations must be pre-assigned and practiced so execution is automatic
3. The direction you push the ball handler determines who traps and who rotates

## Coverage Options

### Option 1: Accept the 1v1 (Standard Coverage)
- Let the on-ball defender play 1v1
- Weak-side defenders maintain help positions at elbows and boxes
- Applicable when you trust your defender or the isolation player is not dominant

### Option 2: Trap from the Top
- X2 (on-ball defender) influences ball handler TOWARD the middle of the paint
- X1 (adjacent defender) comes off his man to trap from the top
- X3 and X4 rotate to cover the two open perimeter players

### Option 3: Push to Baseline Trap
- X2 shades the ball handler toward the baseline
- X5 (near the basket) steps out to trap with X2 in the corner
- Defense rotates BEHIND X5: X4 fronts the post (5) and X3 V-cuts back to cover 4

### Option 4: Shift to Zone
- Four defenders fill zone spots around X2 (on-ball defender)
- Typically a 2-3 zone formation around the isolated player
- Zone positioning allows simultaneous help and trap-on-penetration coverage
- **Must be pre-planned with a verbal signal** — cannot be improvised on the fly
- Players fill the four zone spots, ready to give help or trap on penetration

## Player Responsibilities (Option 3 Example)
- **X2**: On-ball defender; shades ball to baseline side
- **X5**: Steps out to form the baseline trap with X2
- **X4**: Fronts the low-post player (5) after X5 vacates
- **X3**: V-cuts back to cover the weakest perimeter threat (4)

## Common Mistakes
1. **No pregame plan** → Isolation defense breaks down when players are unsure which option to use; decide and communicate before the game
2. **Wrong trap direction** → If you want to push baseline, X2 must shade that direction from the start — late adjustments give up the first step
3. **Zone without a signal** → Shifting to zone without a verbal signal creates confusion; always designate a call
4. **Rotations arriving late** → X4 and X3 must read X5's trap action and move simultaneously, not after the ball is passed
