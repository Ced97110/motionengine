# Stretch-Shortening Cycle (SSC) in Basketball

## Summary
The **stretch-shortening cycle (SSC)** describes the mechanism by which a rapid **eccentric (lengthening) muscle contraction** immediately preceding a **concentric (shortening) muscle contraction** produces a more forceful and explosive movement. The SSC is the physiological basis of plyometrics and explains why athletes pre-load their bodies before explosive actions [S2, p.5].

In basketball, the SSC is exploited constantly: the slight squat before a vertical jump, the wind-up before a chest pass, the hip load before a lateral cut. Teaching players to use and train this mechanism deliberately — through structured plyometric progressions — is one of the highest-leverage improvements a strength coach can make.

## When to Use
- When introducing plyometric training to players who have completed a strength base
- When coaching jumping mechanics (emphasize the dip/load before takeoff)
- When explaining *why* pre-stretching improves explosive performance
- When designing programs for players who need a quicker first step or higher vertical jump

## Key Principles
1. **Pre-stretch produces more force** — Tendons and muscles prestretched before a concentric contraction produce a more forceful output than a concentric contraction initiated from rest [S2, p.5].
2. **SSC = eccentric load + concentric explosion** — The eccentric (lengthening) phase stores elastic energy in the muscle-tendon unit; the concentric phase releases it, amplifying total force output [S2, p.5].
3. **Strength base required first** — SSC/plyometric training is only safe and effective after an adequate strength foundation has been established (see [[basketball-physical-qualities-hierarchy]]). Tendon and tissue strength must support landing forces.
4. **SSC is synonymous with plyometrics** — The two terms describe the same training concept. Plyometric exercises explicitly train athletes to load eccentrically and explode concentrically [S2, p.5].
5. **Quick ground contact = better SSC utilization** — The shorter the time between the eccentric load and concentric push-off, the more elastic energy is preserved (less is lost as heat). Training for rapid ground contact times maximizes SSC benefit.
6. **Basketball applications** — Jump before a rebound, first step on a drive, lateral shuffle, push-off on a back-cut. All are SSC-driven movements.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Learn and practice the pre-stretch/load position before all explosive movements — dip before jumping, load hips before cutting.
- **Coaches/trainers**: Progress plyometric volume and intensity carefully, starting with low-impact landing practice before adding reactive exercises.

## Variations
### Slow SSC (>250ms amortization)
Box jumps with a controlled land and reset. Used for beginners to develop landing mechanics and initial reactive strength.

### Fast SSC (<250ms amortization)
Depth jumps, bounding, and reactive drills. Used with advanced players to maximize RFD and first-step quickness.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Loading before strength is established** → Without sufficient lower-extremity strength, plyometric landing forces overwhelm the soft tissues and joints, causing injury. Strength first, SSC second.
2. **Excessive amortization time** → If a player pauses too long at the bottom of a squat jump (amortization phase), elastic energy is dissipated as heat and the SSC benefit is lost. Emphasize quickness of the reversal.
3. **Neglecting landing mechanics** → Athletes must be able to absorb and control landing forces before progressing to reactive plyometrics. Poor landing mechanics cause ACL and ankle injuries.
