# Productive Practice Structure (the head coach)

## Summary
the head coach's practice philosophy centers on game-speed, game-rhythm repetition within a fast-paced, no-dead-time structure. Practice begins with three-man two-ball shooting, flows into 5-on-0 full-court offensive rehearsal, then transitions to defensive and offensive breakdown drills. The philosophy emphasizes detail (timing, spacing, cuts, screen angles, on-time/on-target passing) and maximum use of every minute through a master practice calendar [S7, pp.277-285].

The system is built on the belief that what is emphasized in practice determines what happens in games — drilling creates automaticity. A unique feature is naming every drill so it can be called by name on subsequent days without re-explanation, eliminating transition waste.

## When to Use
- Season-long daily practice structure at any competitive level
- When managing a long season with varying player minute loads
- When integrating both starters and bench players at game speed
- When time is limited and efficiency must be maximized

## Key Principles
1. **Begin with shooting** — three-man, two-ball shooting drill at game speed and game rhythm after warm-up.
2. **5-on-0 full-court rehearsal** — players flow through the offense with attention to timing, spacing, setups, hard cuts, screen angles, and passing.
3. **Explore all options within each set** — don't just rehearse the first read; explore drive-and-kick, second-side attacks, and dribble penetration off play calls.
4. **No breaks between drills** — one drill flows immediately into the next; players sprint from drill to drill.
5. **Name every drill** — give each segment a unique name so next day it requires zero re-explanation: "just call it by its name."
6. **Flexibility** — shorter but more intense practices late in the season; adjust for minutes played the night before.
7. **Bench players need extra work** — players not playing heavy minutes get additional 3-on-3 or individual skill time; otherwise they coast through shortened practices.
8. **Assistant coaches own player development** — each assistant has a detailed teaching plan for assigned players covering PnR recognition, post moves, shooting, on-ball defense, and ball handling.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: sprint from drill to drill; shoot game shots at game speed.
- **PG**: in 5-on-0, lead offense through all options including drive-and-kick on the second side.
- **Bench players**: maintain preparation mentality identical to starters; always be ready.

## Variations
### Short Practice (Late Season)
Shorter total duration but more intense density; bench players fill extra time with 3-on-3 or individual skill work rather than resting.

### 5-on-0 With Specific Option Call
Coach calls not just the play but the exact option sought — e.g., "side PnR, pass ahead, drive-and-kick on second side" — to rehearse multiple offensive concepts simultaneously.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Breaks between drills** → Eliminate all dead time; one drill flows into the next.
2. **Rehearsing only the primary read** → Force players to explore all options within each set, including penetration counters.
3. **Coasting bench players** → Assign specific extra work (3-on-3, individual skills) so bench players are always improving.
4. **Generic drill descriptions** → Name every drill uniquely so it self-explains on repeat days.
