# Jumper's Knee Rehabilitation

## Summary
Jumper's knee is an inflammatory condition presenting as anterior knee pain at or around the patella, typically at the inferior aspect of the patellar tendon at its insertion into the tibial tubercle. It is caused by repetitive stress on the extensor mechanism of the knee during jumping activities and is formally categorized as patellar tendinopathy, tendinosis, or tendinitis [S2, p.140].

The condition is rarely tied to a single injury event. Rather, it develops from the cumulative eccentric loading on the quadriceps during jump landings — the eccentric (deceleration) phase places significantly greater stress on the patella than the concentric (take-off) phase. Risk factors include poor physical conditioning, quadriceps and hamstring weakness, gluteal weakness, and poor flexibility in the hip flexors, quadriceps, hamstrings, and ankles [S2, p.140].

## When to Use
- When a player presents with anterior knee pain that is worse after jumping activities
- As a prevention-oriented knee conditioning program for high-volume jumpers (PF, C, and high-usage wing players)
- During the return-to-play progression after a patellar tendon injury

## Key Principles
1. **Protect and rest first**: Use a patella strap or device to alleviate stress; rest appropriately to prevent further tissue damage [S2, p.140]
2. **Reduce inflammation**: Ice to decrease pain and blood flow, reducing bruising
3. **Restore range of motion**: Both passive and active knee ROM must be regained
4. **Restore strength**: Focus on the extensor muscles (quadriceps), hamstrings, and hip/ankle/core musculature
5. **Restore flexibility**: Emphasize hip flexors, hamstrings, quadriceps, knees, and ankles
6. **Restore proprioception**: Train the entire lower extremity neuromuscular system
7. **Progress eccentric loading**: Eccentric strength is the key deficiency in jumper's knee; prioritize controlled eccentric exercises [S2, p.144]
8. **Progress to plyometrics last**: Only after strength and proprioception are restored should jumping activities be reintroduced

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Maintain quadriceps and hamstring strength year-round. High-volume jumpers (posts and high-usage perimeter players) should perform preventive eccentric work during pre-season.

## Rehabilitation Exercise Progression
The full exercise sequence for jumper's knee rehabilitation is [S2, p.140]:
1. **Mat walk** — balance during forward movement on unstable narrow surface
2. **Single-leg stance on unstable surface** — balance and proprioception in minisquat
3. **Eccentric leg press** — controlled eccentric quadriceps loading with sub-body-weight resistance
4. **Body-weight squat** — bilateral strength restoration baseline
5. **Step-down** — eccentric quad emphasis, readiness test for return to running
6. **Box jump** — power restoration with reduced landing impact forces

## Common Mistakes
1. **Returning to play too quickly** → Pain resolution does not equal tissue healing; complete the full progression
2. **Skipping eccentric work** → Eccentric strength is the critical deficiency; it must be specifically addressed [S2, p.144]
3. **Uneven loading** → During bilateral exercises, do not shift weight to the healthy leg; maintain equal load on the injured side
4. **Ignoring flexibility** → Hip flexor and hamstring tightness contributes to patellar tendon stress; stretch these regularly [S2, p.140]
5. **Going directly to plyometrics** → Box jumps and hopping must come after strength baseline is established, not at the start of rehab

## Variations
### Prevention Protocol
For healthy players: eccentric leg press, body-weight squats, step-downs, and box jumps as part of regular pre-season conditioning.

### Post-Surgical or Severe Tendinosis
Begin with mat walk and seated proprioception before weight-bearing exercises; consult medical staff for modified loading parameters.
