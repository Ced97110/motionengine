# Orthobiologics for Cartilage and Meniscal Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Orthobiologics — biological substances that promote healing of musculoskeletal tissues — have become increasingly popular for treating cartilage and meniscal injuries in basketball athletes. These include Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP), Bone Marrow Aspirate Concentrate (BMAC), Mesenchymal Stem Cells (MSCs), Adipose-Derived Stem Cells (ASCs), and fibrin clots. While evidence supports several of these interventions for specific conditions, standardized protocols and high-quality athletic-specific studies remain lacking. The medical team must weigh current evidence, injury type/grade, athlete goals, and season timing when selecting a biologic approach [S10, pp.609-613].

The role of orthobiologics in meniscal injuries is even less established than in cartilage injuries. Three biological barriers impede meniscus healing: the avascular nature of the meniscus (only 10–30% is vascularized), the presence of synovial fluid with pro-inflammatory cytokines, and repetitive mechanical load [S10, p.611].

## When to Use
- Focal articular cartilage defects (ICRS grade II–IV), particularly in younger athletes seeking to avoid surgery
- Early-onset knee osteoarthritis in basketball players wishing to extend career
- Meniscal tears — especially white-zone (avascular) tears where standard repair has poor healing rates
- As a surgical adjunct during cartilage or meniscal repair procedures
- Off-season or post-season when recovery time is available

## Key Principles

### BMAC (Bone Marrow Aspirate Concentrate)
1. FDA-approved for intra-articular single-step MSC delivery [S10, p.609]
2. Contains hematopoietic stem cells, endothelial progenitor cells, and PDGFs in addition to MSCs
3. Produces higher concentrations of VEGF, PDGF, TGF-β, BMP-2, and BMP-7 than PRP alone, promoting chondrocyte proliferation and MSC differentiation [S10, p.609]
4. Best outcomes reported in patients with **lower grades of knee degeneration** and **smaller lesions (<8 cm²)**; larger lesions show inferior results [S10, p.610]
5. When used as a surgical adjunct to cartilage repair: improved outcomes in isolated chondral lesions <8 cm² at 24 months; hyaline-like tissue confirmed on MRI/histology [S10, p.610]
6. For meniscal repair: BMAC augmentation showed macroscopic and histologic superiority over PRP and no-augmentation controls in rabbit models [S10, p.612]
7. Standardized guidelines for harvest site, carrier, dosing, number of treatments, and volume remain **poorly characterized** [S10, p.610]

### MSCs (Mesenchymal Stem Cells)
1. Possess high plasticity, immune suppression, anti-inflammatory properties, and self-renewal capacity — considered the most promising stem cell option for articular cartilage repair [S10, p.610]
2. Sources: bone marrow, peripheral blood, adipose tissue, synovium
3. ASCs (Adipose-Derived Stem Cells) are gaining popularity due to: easier harvest (liposuction aspirate or infrapatellar fat pad), minimal morbidity, up to **300× more stem cells per volume** vs. BMAC, and better phenotype maintenance over culture passages [S10, p.610]
4. Clinical evidence shows improvement in IKDC, KOOS, Tegner, VAS for focal cartilage defects; some studies show hyaline-like tissue on biopsy [S10, p.611]
5. MSCs + PRP combined showed improvement across all KOOS domains at 12 months; corticosteroids showed improvement only in pain/function subscores [S10, p.611]
6. Most current studies lack control groups; confounders limit conclusions about MSC-specific contribution [S10, p.611]

### PRP (Platelet-Rich Plasma)
- For meniscal repair augmentation: a randomized controlled trial found statistically significant functional improvement, lower failure rates, and better healing on second-look arthroscopy at 42 months with PRP-augmented repairs vs. isolated suture repair [S10, p.612]
- Isolated meniscal repairs augmented with PRP: significantly decreased failure rates at 3 years
- PRP augmentation of meniscal repairs with **concomitant ACL reconstruction**: no difference in failure rates vs. controls [S10, p.612]
- Significant heterogeneity in PRP preparation techniques remains a major limitation

### Fibrin Clot
- Effective adjunct for meniscal repairs — especially complete radial tears of the lateral meniscus traditionally treated with meniscectomy
- Complete radial tears repaired with fibrin clot: all menisci completely healed on MRI at average 71 months (van Trommel et al.) [S10, p.612]
- Horizontal cleavage tears: significant functional improvement but only 70% healing rate on second-look at 12 months [S10, p.612]

### ACS (Autologous Conditioned Serum)
- Products: Regenokine (US), Orthokine (Europe)
- Shown efficacy primarily for osteoarthritis; commonly used in European professional athletes
- No sufficient evidence to support efficacy specifically in athletes [S10, p.609]

## Player Responsibilities
Not applicable — this is a sports medicine/team physician concept.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Applying orthobiologics without injury grading** → Always stratify by defect size, location, grade (ICRS), and patient factors (age, activity level) before selecting a biologic
2. **Expecting BMAC to work equally across defect sizes** → Outcomes are significantly worse for lesions >8 cm²; counsel athletes accordingly
3. **Using PRP for meniscal repairs with simultaneous ACL reconstruction** → Evidence shows no benefit in this combined setting; reserve for isolated repairs
4. **Neglecting standardization** → Inconsistent PRP preparation techniques make outcomes unpredictable; use validated classification systems (e.g., PAW classification)
5. **Assuming biologic = non-surgical** → Many biologics work best as surgical adjuncts, not standalone treatments

## Variations
### BMAC + Collagen Matrix
For ICRS grade IV lesions: BMAC cells transplanted onto a collagen I/III matrix. At 24 months: significant improvements in VAS, IKDC, KOOS, Lysholm, Marx, SF-36, Tegner [S10, p.609].

### BMAC + Hyaluronic Acid Scaffold
HA-based scaffolds soaked in BMAC for grade 4 chondral lesions — outcomes correlated with lesion size; significantly better IKDC scores for lesions <8 cm² [S10, p.610].

### BMAC + PRP on Collagen Scaffold
For large full-thickness cartilage lesions: improved outcomes at 1–2 years but MRI/histology showed repair tissue inferior to native hyaline cartilage [S10, p.610].

### PRP Injection for Meniscal Tears (Non-operative)
Percutaneous injections of PRP for intrasubstance (grade 2) meniscal tears using fluoroscopy guidance — three injections over 7 days; 60% showed improvement in outcomes [S10, p.612].
