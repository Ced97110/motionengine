# Postactivation Potentiation (PAP) for Basketball

## Summary
Postactivation potentiation (PAP) is a physiological phenomenon where a maximal or near-maximal muscular contraction acutely enhances subsequent explosive performance. In basketball, PAP can be used strategically as a pregame or pre-practice warm-up protocol to acutely improve sprint speed and jump power — the primary physical qualities that determine first-step quickness, rebounding, and shot-blocking [S11, p.20].

Traditional warm-ups consisting of prolonged static stretching before competition impair muscular performance, reducing force and power output (Sole et al.). PAP-based warm-ups reverse this effect by activating fast-twitch muscle fibers and potentiating neuromuscular output [S11, p.20].

## When to Use
- **Pregame warm-up**: Apply PAP protocol 5–12 minutes before tip-off to peak jump and sprint performance at game start.
- **Pre-practice explosive work**: Use a brief PAP stimulus before plyometric drills to amplify the training effect.
- **French Contrast Method**: Sequences heavy compound lift → plyometric → assisted/resisted variation → sport-specific explosive action in one superset (Hernández-Preciado et al.) [S11, p.20].

## Key Principles
1. **Heavy contraction potentiates explosive output**: A maximal or near-maximal eccentric-concentric or isometric contraction acutely elevates neuromuscular readiness for subsequent explosive actions [S11, p.20].
2. **Sprint performance improves after a heavy squat**: McBride et al. demonstrated that performing 1 set of a back squat at 90% 1RM improved 40-meter sprint time [S11, p.20].
3. **Agility performance improves after a graded squat protocol**: A back squat protocol at 50%, 60%, and 90% 1RM (3 reps each) before a 10-meter agility shuttle showed a trend toward improved times [S11, p.20].
4. **Both load and contraction frequency elicit PAP**: The magnitude of potentiation is influenced by the intensity of the conditioning contraction and the number of contractions performed [S11, p.20].
5. **French Contrast Method improves CMJ baseline**: Using the French contrast sequence (heavy lift + jump + assisted variation + sport action) improved countermovement jump levels above baseline (Hernández-Preciado et al.) [S11, p.20].
6. **Static stretching alone hurts performance**: Replace pre-competition static stretching sessions with dynamic warm-up + PAP protocols [S11, p.20].
7. **Timing matters**: The PAP effect typically peaks 5–12 minutes after the conditioning contraction. Too short = still fatigued; too long = potentiation dissipated.

## Sample PAP Warm-Up Protocol
1. Dynamic warm-up (5 min): leg swings, hip circles, walking lunges, arm circles.
2. **PAP stimulus**: Back squat — 1 set × 4 reps @ 90% 1RM (or 3 sets × 3 reps @ 50%/60%/90% 1RM).
3. Rest 5–8 minutes (allow fatigue to dissipate while potentiation remains elevated).
4. **Explosive activity**: Countermovement jumps × 3–5 reps OR 10-meter sprint × 2–3 reps.
5. Proceed to game/practice.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Applying PAP with insufficient recovery time** → If the athlete moves directly from the heavy set to the explosive activity, residual fatigue will offset potentiation. Wait 5–12 minutes.
2. **Using PAP with untrained athletes** → Less trained athletes have lower twitch contractile properties and may not benefit from PAP. Reserve for intermediate to advanced athletes.
3. **Using PAP as a substitute for proper program-length resistance training** → PAP is an *acute* warm-up tool, not a substitute for the chronic adaptations that come from periodized resistance training.
4. **Continuing pre-competition static stretching** → The evidence is clear: prolonged static stretching reduces force and power output before competition [S11, p.20].
