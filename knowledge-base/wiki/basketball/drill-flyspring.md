# Flyspring / Bounder (Front Flip Chain — Step 7)

## Objective
Execute a perfectly symmetrical front handspring (block jump off *both* feet, land on *both* feet simultaneously) — developing total-body bilateral power required for the standing front flip.

## Setup
- **Players**: 1 (solo)
- **Equipment**: Open floor; mat or padding for early attempts
- **Space**: 10-15 feet for approach run

## Execution
1. Take a run-up to build momentum.
2. Perform a *block jump* off *both* feet simultaneously as you throw your hands down — unlike the front handspring (single-leg kick-off), both feet block and leave the ground together.
3. Kick up hard through both legs, pushing both feet off the ground while allowing the torso to fall and dropping the hands to the floor.
4. Land on the palms with arms nearly straight as the feet leave the floor — catch yourself symmetrically.
5. Let the jump momentum carry the legs above the torso.
6. Allow forward momentum to turn you over; extend through the arms and hands to gain extra height.
7. Land on *both* feet simultaneously and symmetrically. Step forward only if needed to dissipate extra momentum. [S9, pp.158-159]

**Key distinction**: In the front handspring (Step 6), one leg kicks and the other assists — the feet may land apart (unsymmetrically). In the flyspring, *both* feet block simultaneously and *both* feet land symmetrically. This demands significantly more total-body power. The flyspring is also called a *bounder*. [S9, p.158]

## Coaching Points
- "Both feet leave the ground together — this is the critical difference from the front handspring."
- Block with equal force from both legs — any asymmetry will cause off-axis rotation.
- Symmetrical landing is the goal — feet together, weight balanced.
- Progress by gradually reducing the run-up steps — the ultimate version is a standing flyspring.
- Arms extend to maximize height out of the handstand phase.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner (Regression)**: Try *back-drop flysprings* first — set up with both feet together but finish in the back-drop position (as in Step 5) with padding. This removes the full rotation demand while training bilateral take-off.
2. **Intermediate**: Standard flyspring with run-up; land in squat if needed.
3. **Advanced**: Reduce run-up steps progressively until the flyspring can be performed from standing. Note: the standing flyspring requires huge power and is *not* a prerequisite for the front flip. [S9, p.159]
