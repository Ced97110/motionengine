# Combination Scoring Moves

## Summary
A complete scorer cannot rely on being a stationary shooter alone. The most effective offensive players chain together shot fakes, jab steps, and step backs into fluid combination sequences that force defenders into impossible choices. The key is NOT to think through combinations consciously during games — each individual move must be so deeply drilled that it becomes second nature, freeing the scorer to read the defense instinctively.

As the author notes: "Even the greatest scorers have an understanding of what moves are their very best. When the game is on the line, they'll go to that move." — [S5, p.107]

## When to Use
- In one-on-one isolation situations against tight, adaptive defenders
- When the initial move (shot fake or jab) does not create the expected advantage
- Against lockdown defenders who are coached not to bite on the first fake
- To build an unpredictable offensive repertoire that keeps defenders off balance

## Key Principles
1. **Do not consciously string moves together in-game** — combination moves must be drilled until automatic; thinking slows execution and telegraphs actions [S5, p.107]
2. **Master individual moves before combining** — become proficient at each component skill separately before linking them
3. **Identify your best 1-2 moves and perfect them** — elite scorers narrow their arsenal and master a few moves deeply rather than being mediocre at many [S5, p.107]
4. **Develop a counterattack for every move** — if the defender takes away your primary option, you must be ready with the counter; e.g., if the step back is contested, rise up and go [S5, p.107]
5. **Master each move across all catching situations**: (a) straight-on one-two step catch, (b) on-the-move catch pivoting on nonshooting foot, (c) on-the-move catch pivoting on shooting foot [S5, p.107]

## Favorite Combination Sequences

### Shot Fake → Drive → Step Back
If the defender bites on the shot fake and you drive into the lane, but help defense contests the pull-up → execute a step back to create space for the shot.

### Jab → Shot Fake → Drive
If the defender doesn't bite on the initial jab (stays put) → add a shot fake; if the defender still doesn't retreat → drive by the frozen defender.

### Jab and Shot Fake (Simultaneous)
Jab-step at the defender while simultaneously raising the ball as on a shot fake. Forces the defender to account for both the drive threat and the shot simultaneously, causing soft closeouts. Optional: add a **head fake** (tilt head back) to further sell the move. Best for midrange post players. [S5, p.109]

### Jab, Jab, Jumper
Double jab to get the defender back on their heels → open jump shot. Even elite lockdown defenders (Joe Dumars, Dennis Rodman, Michael Cooper, Bruce Bowen) bite on multiple fakes. Keep jabs short and quick throughout. [S5, p.105]

### Jab, Jab, and Go
Double jab to freeze the defender (who was conditioned to expect the shot after the first possession) → drive past the paralyzed defender. Use a long last step along the defender's hip, arm bar to protect the ball, and push the dribble out ahead for maximum distance. [S5, p.107]

### Catch → Shot Fake → Jab → Shot Fake Again → Weak-Side Step Back → Crossover → Pull-Up
An example of a full combination sequence to illustrate that the possibilities are endless — but this should never be pre-planned; it must flow from reading the defense. [S5, p.107]

## Player Development Model (an elite scoring wing Method)
1. Watch tape and study the defense
2. Go to the gym and visualize a defender
3. Drill the same move repeatedly solo
4. Grab a teammate for live defense
5. Work the move against live defense until mastered
6. Deploy the move in a game [S5, p.107]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Pre-planning combination sequences** — trying to execute rehearsed combos consciously slows the offensive player down and reveals intent → drill until automatic, then read and react
2. **Trying to learn all moves at once** — results in being inefficient and mediocre at everything → master one move at a time across all catching situations [S5, p.107]
3. **Not developing a counter to the primary move** — if a defender takes away the initial look and there is no counter, the possession is wasted → always develop the complementary move alongside the primary
