# Orthobiologics for MCL Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Medial collateral ligament (MCL) injuries are the most common knee ligament injuries in basketball. Between 2009-2014, 35 MCL injuries were recorded in female and 33 in male collegiate and high-school basketball players. Unlike the ACL, the MCL has good spontaneous healing potential and is typically managed non-operatively. However, these injuries can lead to chronic pain, laxity, joint instability, and possibly osteoarthritis — particularly in competitive athletes where time loss is significant [S10, pp.593-594].

Orthobiologics — particularly PRP — have been investigated to enhance MCL healing and accelerate return to play, but the clinical evidence is very limited and mixed.

## When to Use
- Grade I-III MCL injuries where prolonged time loss is unacceptable (elite-level athletes, mid-season)
- Adjunct to rehabilitation when standard conservative management is not meeting recovery timelines
- **Caution**: Evidence does NOT support routine PRP use for MCL injuries; clinical data is mostly case reports

## Key Principles

### Preclinical Evidence
1. Early animal studies showed promising MCL healing enhancement with PRP [S10, p.594].
2. Yoshioka et al.: Significantly improved structural properties of rabbit MCLs treated with LP-PRP (PRGF) vs. controls [S10, p.594].
3. **Dose-dependent risk**: LaPrade et al. found that:
   - Single dose of platelet-poor plasma (PPP) OR 2× PRP dose — did NOT accelerate healing
   - **4× PRP dose** — significantly NEGATIVE effect on collagen orientation and ligament strength at 6 weeks [S10, p.594]
4. Amar et al.: No histological or biomechanical differences between PRP-treated MCLs and controls in a rat model [S10, p.594].

### Clinical Evidence
5. Clinical evidence is limited — mostly case reports [S10, p.594].
6. Lundblad et al. (20 elite football players with MCL injuries): No differences in lay-off times between PRP and non-PRP treated players with grade II MCL injuries [S10, p.594].
7. Major limitations in existing studies: no standardization of PRP type, injection number, timing from injury, MCL area involved, or post-injection protocol.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Applying excessive PRP doses** → 4× dose showed negative effect on collagen orientation; stick to standard doses if using PRP [S10, p.594].
2. **Expecting PRP to replace rehabilitation** → MCL has good intrinsic healing potential; structured rehabilitation is the primary treatment [S10, p.593].
3. **Using LR-PRP** → may increase local inflammation; LP-PRP is preferable when biologics are used.
