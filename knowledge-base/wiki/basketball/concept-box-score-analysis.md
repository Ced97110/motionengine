# Box Score Analysis Framework

## Summary
A basketball box score provides a complete summary of one game — the unit of time that matters most to coaches, players, and scouts. While a game recap emphasizes the last two minutes or a scoring run, the box score shows the full foundation of a game: pace, efficiency, and individual contributions across all forty-eight minutes [S16, p.192].

Dean Oliver's framework for reading a box score proceeds in three steps: (1) calculate pace and team efficiency ratings to determine whether offense or defense decided the game; (2) identify which of the Four Factors (shooting, rebounding, turnovers, free throws) drove the efficiency difference; and (3) use individual statistics to identify which players executed those factors [S16, pp.193-197].

As coaches Dean Smith and Frank McGuire noted: "A quick glance at three figures (our points per possession, opponent's points per possession, and total possessions) helps the coach to determine any changes he must make" [S16, p.193].

## When to Use
- **Morning after a game**: Quick pace/efficiency check to understand the game's story before detailed film review
- **Scouting**: One game box score provides enough information to understand a team's offensive and defensive tendencies
- **Halftime adjustments**: Check offensive rebounds, turnovers, foul differential, and individual usage patterns to guide second-half changes
- **Player evaluation**: Track individual floor% and offensive ratings over multiple games to identify efficiency trends

## Key Principles

### Step 1: Calculate Pace
1. Estimate possessions using the formula: **Poss = FGA − OR + TOV + 0.4 × FTA** [S16, p.193]
2. Do this for both teams — the two estimates should be within 1-2 possessions of each other
3. Divide each team's points by their possessions to get the **offensive rating** (points per possession)
4. Compare both ratings to the league average (~105 per 100 possessions in the NBA era) — both above average means offense won; both below means defense won

### Step 2: Diagnose the Four Factors
Once you know whether the game was won offensively or defensively, identify *which* of the four factors explains the differential [S16, p.195]:
- **Field goal shooting**: Did a team shoot unusually well or poorly?
- **Offensive rebounding**: Did offensive rebounds extend possessions significantly?
- **Turnovers**: Did turnovers cost one team possessions? (turnovers per possession, league avg ≈ 22%)
- **Free throws**: Did one team dominate at the foul line, and how well did they convert?

### Step 3: Individual Analysis
5. Look at top scorers for the winning team first, then trace how they generated their points (shooting, free throws, assists)
6. For defensive analysis: identify which players held assignments in check vs. which got beaten — look at fouls drawn, blocked shots, and defensive rebounds as proxies
7. Calculate **individual offensive rating** (points produced per scoring possession) and **floor%** (scoring possessions / total possessions used) for more detailed analysis
8. Compare each player's offensive rating to the opponent's team defensive rating — players rated *above* the opponent's defensive rating helped the offense; those rated *below* hurt it [S16, p.197]

### Step 4: Usage and Balance
9. Calculate **% Team Possessions** used by each player while on the floor (Sc. Poss / Team Poss × 100)
10. A player using 30%+ of team possessions with below-average efficiency is a major drag on the offense even if they score a lot of points [S16, p.197]
11. A team with 3-4 players handling 20%+ of possessions at above-average efficiency is well-balanced and hard to stop defensively

## Player Responsibilities
This is a coaching/analysis framework applied to evaluating all positions. No position-specific duties.

## Variations
### Quick Morning Read (2 minutes)
- Calculate possessions and team ratings
- Check if game was offensive or defensive
- Note top scorer efficiency and any outlier individual performances

### Detailed Pregame/Scouting Analysis
- Calculate all individual offensive ratings and floor percentages (Table 16.2 format)
- Map which factors each team relied on offensively
- Identify defensive matchup tendencies from foul counts and blocked shots
- Use quarter-by-quarter line scores to find "the run that decided the game"

### Halftime Adjustment Read
- Focus on offensive rebounds (easy to correct with coaching emphasis)
- Check foul counts by player (substitution decisions)
- Look at turnover counts vs. pace to see if pressure defense is affecting the offense
- Identify if one player is dominating possessions inefficiently [S16, p.197]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Focusing only on points scored** → A player who scores 25 points on 34% of team possessions with a 94 offensive rating is *hurting* the team, not helping it [S16, p.197]
2. **Ignoring pace** → A team scoring 110 points is impressive in an 85-possession game but average in a 110-possession game; always normalize to per-possession [S16, p.193]
3. **Crediting the last run** → Box scores don't isolate decisive runs. The game recap emphasizes the 3rd quarter, but the box score shows whether that quarter reflected real quality differences or random variance
4. **Ignoring offensive rebounds in halftime adjustments** → Offensive boards are one of the most coachable stats; a halftime emphasis to "go to the glass" can flip a game [S16, p.197]
5. **Evaluating defense from the box score alone** → Official stats (blocks, steals, defensive rebounds) are incomplete proxies for defensive contribution; forced misses and forced turnovers require additional tracking [S16, p.195]

## Case Study: Charlotte vs. Milwaukee, May 20, 2001 (Game 7)
- **Pace**: ~87 possessions
- **Team ratings**: Charlotte 109.9, Milwaukee 120.3 — both above average, offense decided the game [S16, p.196]
- **What won it for Milwaukee**: Free throw performance (94% on 35 attempts, spread across 5 players) plus 3rd quarter offensive rebounding (7 offensive boards on 12 misses)
- **What hurt Charlotte**: Jamal Mashburn used 34% of team possessions at a 94.5 offensive rating — below even Charlotte's own offensive average. His nine assists could not offset the drag of his inefficient shooting [S16, p.197]
- **Halftime read**: Milwaukee was down 3 at half with only 4 offensive rebounds. Coach George Karl apparently told his big men to attack the glass — they responded with a dominant 3rd quarter [S16, p.197]
