# Reactive Strength for Basketball

## Summary
Reactive strength is the ability to quickly transfer eccentric force into concentric explosive output — the defining athletic quality for basketball actions like second jumps, cutting, and absorbing and redirecting contact. It is quantified by the **Reactive Strength Index (RSI)** = jump height ÷ ground contact time (derived from the drop jump) [S11, p.19].

Explosive basketball athletes require optimal muscle elasticity. Reactive strength is reliant on eccentric force development, elastic energy storage and release, and muscle reflexive properties. The **amortization phase** — the transition from eccentric to concentric, when zero velocity occurs — is the critical bottleneck: a longer amortization phase causes elastic energy to dissipate and ground reaction forces to decrease, hindering explosiveness [S11, p.19].

## When to Use
- **Second-jump rebounding**: Center lands after a missed shot and must immediately jump again — reactive strength determines whether they reach the apex in time.
- **Cutting and change of direction**: Eccentric braking, isometric planting, and concentric propulsion phases must transition rapidly. Female athletes with faster agility times exhibit shorter ground contact braking times [S11, p.14].
- **Disguised cuts**: A cutter pausing mid-cut to freeze a defender still requires explosive concentric output after storing isometric energy — reactive strength enables this [S11, p.20].

## Key Principles
1. **RSI = Jump Height / Contact Time**: The reactive strength index captures both height (power output) and contact time (energy transfer efficiency). Training should reduce amortization phase duration [S11, p.19].
2. **Eccentric strength drives RSI**: Lower body eccentric strength is highly related to overall jump performance (Bridgeman et al.). Combining eccentric resistance training with concentric-focused work has positive effects on jump performance [S11, p.15].
3. **Elastic energy is stored in the muscle-tendon unit**: Stronger muscles contract and store elastic energy better than weaker muscles. This energy is created in the eccentric phase and released in the concentric acceleration phase [S11, p.16].
4. **Muscle architecture matters**: Jump height, peak force, and peak velocity are all related to greater vastus lateralis thickness and gastrocnemius pennation angle. Resistance training improves muscle thickness, pennation angle, and fascicle length [S11, p.16].
5. **Heavy loads and type II fiber recruitment**: Heavy loads require type II fiber recruitment and are more effective for eliciting neural adaptations that facilitate strength improvements, which underpin reactive strength [S11, p.15].
6. **Amortization phase = direction change from eccentric to concentric**: Any increase in amortization phase duration dissipates stored elastic energy and reduces ground reaction force. Training should minimize this transition time through plyometric and drop-jump work [S11, p.19].

## Training Methods
### Drop Jump / Depth Jump
Step off a box and immediately jump maximally upon landing. The brief ground contact trains the rapid eccentric-to-concentric transition. Intensity varies by box height (12–30 inches). See [[lower-body-plyometrics-basketball]].

### Countermovement Jump (CMJ)
The standard measure of reactive strength in basketball S&C. The pre-stretch (eccentric countermovement) amplifies concentric power. Lower body eccentric strength is highly correlated with CMJ performance [S11, p.15].

### Eccentric Overload Training
Accentuated eccentric loading (e.g., slow-tempo negatives, flywheel training) improves muscle architecture, strength, power, and velocity more than concentric-only training [S11, p.15].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Training only concentric strength** → Reactive strength requires eccentric capacity as its foundation; neglecting it limits RSI gains.
2. **Prolonged ground contact during plyometrics** → Cue athletes to "get off the floor fast" — think of the floor as a hot surface. Long amortization phases negate the training stimulus.
3. **Skipping the eccentric phase in squats and lunges** → The eccentric phase of weight-room exercises directly builds the eccentric strength that underlies reactive strength. Never skip or rush the lowering phase.
