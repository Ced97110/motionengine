# Patellar Tendinopathy (Jumper's Knee) in Basketball

## Summary
Patellar tendinopathy, or "jumper's knee," is the most prevalent tendinopathy in basketball. Estimated prevalence of current or prior patellar tendinopathy was **55% in professional basketball players** with a mean symptom duration of 32±25 months — meaning most professional athletes compete with active symptoms [S10, p.493]. The condition is characterized by progressive patellar tendon pain leading to recurrent or long-standing impairment of athletic performance.

Basketball demands eccentric muscle forces 1.5–2.0× higher than maximal isometric forces, and ground reactive forces up to 10× body weight during jumping — directly explaining why this condition is so prevalent in the sport [S10, p.493].

## When to Use
- Any basketball player presenting with activity-related anterior knee pain
- Players with a history of high training volume, hard court surfaces, or rapid volume increases
- Pre-participation screening for at-risk players (high BMI, quad/hamstring inflexibility, flat foot)

## Key Principles

### Etiology and Risk Factors
1. **Training volume** has a linear relationship with jumper's knee prevalence — more volume = higher risk [S10, p.493]
2. **Floor firmness** is an extrinsic risk factor — harder, firmer training surfaces increase risk [S10, p.493]
3. **Inferior pole of the patella** is the most common site — subjected to highest tensile forces during impact [S10, p.493]
4. **Anthropometric risk factors**: high BMI, large abdominal circumference, limb-length discrepancy, flat foot arch [S10, p.493]
5. **Intrinsic risk factors**: quad/hamstring weakness and inflexibility, patellar malalignment, patella alta, abnormal laxity [S10, p.493]
6. Affects men approximately **twice as frequently** as women [S10, p.493]

### Grading Systems (Table 40.1)

**Blazina Classification:**
- Stage I: Pain only following activity, no functional impairment
- Stage II: Pain during and after activity, satisfactory performance
- Stage III: Prolonged pain during/after activity, progressively increasing difficulty performing

**Ferretti Classification:**
- Grade I: Pain following intense activity only, no functional impairment
- Grade II: Moderate pain during sports, no performance restriction
- Grade III: Pain with slight performance restriction
- Grade IV: Pain with severe performance restriction
- Grade V: Pain during daily activity, unable to participate at any level

**Roels Classification:**
- Grade I: Pain at infrapatellar/suprapatellar region after training/event
- Grade II: Pain at beginning of activity, disappears with warm-up, returns after
- Grade III: Pain during and after — unable to participate
- Grade IV: Complete tendon rupture

[S10, p.494]

## Diagnosis
- **Primary clinical diagnosis** based on history and physical exam — imaging not required to confirm [S10, p.494]
- **Presentation**: Activity-related anterior knee pain at distal pole of patella; insidious onset; may progress from activity-only to rest pain
- **Warm-up phenomenon**: Pain may improve with repeated loading but increases the day after energy-storage activities
- **Key exam finding**: Tenderness at distal pole of patella with knee in terminal extension; pain reduced with knee flexion
- **Provocative test**: Single-leg decline squat to ~30° of flexion reproduces symptoms [S10, p.494]
- **Dose-dependent pain** is a hallmark — pain increases as magnitude and rate of load application increases

### Imaging
| Modality | Sensitivity | Specificity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ultrasonography | 58% | 94% | Hypoechogenicity at inferior pole; tendon thickening; calcifications |
| MRI | 78% | 86% | Hyperintensity proximal patellar tendon; rules out intra-articular pathology |

**For high-level basketball players: MRI should be first-line** due to ability to rule out additional pathologies [S10, p.494]

## Management — Nonoperative

### Step 1: Load Modification
- First step: reduce pain and avoid symptom aggravation
- Up to 24h of pain provocation after a training session is acceptable during rehabilitation [S10, p.495]

### Step 2: Isometric Loading
- Entry point for high-symptom athletes (especially during the basketball season)
- Isometric contractions provide analgesia without significant tendon stress

### Step 3: Isotonic Loading
- Progressive transition to concentric/eccentric loading

### Step 4: Eccentric Exercises (Gold Standard)
- **Mainstay of nonoperative management** with strong evidence support [S10, p.495]
- Use a **decline board** — decline board eccentric squats are superior to flat-surface eccentrics
- Perform with a mild level of discomfort (not sharp/severe pain)
- Minimum **20 training sessions at 15-repetition maximum** for effective therapy
- Program duration: **6 weeks to 1 year** depending on severity [S10, p.495]
- Eccentric loading stretches tendon more than concentric → more mechanotransduction → more collagen → improved healing [S10, p.495]
- Returning to elite-level competition after tendinopathy may require **6 months or longer** [S10, p.495]

### Extracorporeal Shock Wave Therapy (ESWT)
- 1500 impulses at 14 kV (0.18 mJ/mm²) at point of maximal tenderness — no anesthesia required
- Superior pain relief and VISA scores vs. conservative treatment at 2–3 year follow-up
- Recurrence: 13% ESWT vs. 50% conservative (p=0.014)
- Mechanism: Hyperstimulation analgesia + growth factor stimulation (IGF-1, TGF-β, interleukin) [S10, p.496]

### Injection Therapies
- **Corticosteroids**: NOT recommended — limited long-term relief, increased rupture risk [S10, p.496]
- **PRP**: Recommended as 2–3 consecutive infiltrations of leukocyte-rich PRP (5 mL, spaced 10–15 days); multiple injections superior to single; combine with rehabilitation [S10, pp.496-497]
- **USGET** (Ultrasound-Guided Galvanic Electrolysis): Promising — 2-week interval galvanic current application destroys degenerative tissue and triggers localized inflammatory healing response [S10, p.496]
- **Hyaluronic acid**: Mean of 2 injections — 54% excellent, 40% good results at 25.7 months [S10, p.497]
- **Sclerosing agents** (polidocanol): Inhibit neovascularization; Level IV evidence for pain improvement during activity [S10, p.497]

## Management — Operative
- Indicated for the ~10% refractory to nonoperative care after adequate trial [S10, p.497]
- **Open approach**: Midline incision; longitudinal tendon incision; posterior degenerative tissue debrided; distal 5mm of patella resected for bleeding surface; tendon/paratenon closed with resorbable sutures; possible suture anchor
- **Arthroscopic approach**: Diagnostic arthroscopy; resect inferior pole synovial tissue; debride posterior zone of proximal patellar tendon; resect distal 5mm of patella with burr
- Both techniques can be augmented with leukocyte-rich PRP injection at conclusion
- **Post-op**: No restriction of WB or ROM; at ~10 days start eccentric squats on inclined board + strengthening
- **Return to sport**: ~3 months when patient demonstrates functional recovery without pain [S10, p.498]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping the isometric loading phase during the season** → Use isometrics first when eccentrics are too painful; they provide analgesia while maintaining some tendon loading [S10, p.495]
2. **Underestimating program duration** → Players and coaches expect a 4–6 week fix; reality is 6 months to 1 year for resolution of severe cases [S10, p.495]
3. **Single PRP injection** → Multiple injections are superior; recommend 2–3 spaced 10–15 days apart [S10, p.497]
4. **Using corticosteroid injections** → Provides short-term relief but increases tendon rupture risk; not recommended [S10, p.496]
