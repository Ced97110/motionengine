# Tendinopathy Rehabilitation for Basketball Players

## Summary
Tendinopathy — including jumper's knee (patellar tendinopathy) and Achilles tendinopathy — is among the most prevalent overuse injuries in basketball. Controlled tendon loading is the best-supported treatment. Effective rehabilitation combines individualized progressive tendon loading exercises with activity modification and a graded return-to-sport phase that gradually improves the tendon's tolerance to sport-specific demands. The principles described here apply to tendinopathies at any location in the upper or lower extremity, with anatomical and biomechanical adjustments as needed [S10, Ch.59].

## When to Use
- Any basketball player presenting with patellar or Achilles tendon pain during jumping, sprinting, or direction changes
- In-season pain management when full rest is not possible
- Post-season recovery from chronic overuse
- Return-to-play planning following a diagnosis of tendinopathy

## Key Principles
1. **Controlled tendon loading is first-line treatment** — progressive mechanical stress remodels tendon structure and reduces pain [S10, p.756]
2. **Pain-monitoring model** — use the NPRS (0–10) scale to guide loading intensity; ≤2/10 pain during and ≤2/10 the next day is acceptable for continued progression [S10, p.756]
3. **Intensity classification drives recovery days** — Light (daily), Medium (2 days recovery), High (3 days recovery); classified by pain during, pain after, and athlete's RPE (Borg CR10) [S10, p.756]
4. **Plyometrics mimic sport demands** — jumping, sprinting, and direction changes must be included in Phase 3; patellar tendon forces reach 6.5× bodyweight during running and jumping [S10, p.756]
5. **72-hour recovery window** — the tendon may require up to 72 h to fully recover after high-load activities; avoid back-to-back high-intensity plyometric sessions [S10, p.756]
6. **Symptom resolution ≠ full recovery** — tendon structure, mechanical properties, and muscle-tendon function may be altered for a year or more; continue maintenance loading after return to play [S10, p.757]
7. **Isometric exercises for in-season pain** — isometric contractions have been used to manage in-season pain; however, recent RCTs show no superiority over isotonic exercise for acute pain relief [S10, p.759]
8. **Individualize the protocol** — no single protocol is superior; use clinical judgment based on athlete presentation, risk factors, and imaging findings [S10, p.757]

## Three-Phase Framework

### Phase 1: Isometric / Low-Level Loading
- Begin with isometric exercises (e.g., wall sits, isometric knee extension for patellar tendon; isometric plantarflexion for Achilles)
- Performed on a level surface if highly irritable
- For Achilles: encourage ankle pumps throughout the day during prolonged sitting
- Frequency: can be performed daily if pain stays ≤2/10 (Light intensity)

### Phase 2: Isotonic Loading (Eccentric + Concentric)
- **Patellar tendon protocols:** Eccentric Decline Squat (single-leg squat on 25° decline board — increases quadriceps activity and patellar tendon load) OR Heavy Slow Resistance (leg press, squat, hack squat at heavy loads and slow speeds) [S10, p.757]
- **Achilles tendon:** Progress from bilateral to unilateral heel raises; add incline or step to increase ROM; add quick rebounding heel raises to prepare for plyometrics [S10, p.759]
- Perform 3 days per week
- Avoid deep knee flexion if bony changes are observed on imaging (patellar tendon) [S10, p.759]
- **Insertional Achilles:** Avoid dorsiflexion loading; consider heel lifts and avoiding uphill running [S10, p.760]

### Phase 3: Plyometric / Return to Full Participation
- Initiate when pain with daily activities does not exceed 2/10 NPRS
- Begin submaximal; progress by increasing speed or height [S10, p.756]
- Include jumping, sprinting, and directional changes
- Use training diaries to monitor response
- Continue maintenance loading program after return to sport [S10, p.757]
- Preferred: complete plyometric sessions in a single session rather than back-to-back days at lower volumes

## Intensity Classification Table (Table 59.5)
| Category | Pain During (NPRS) | Pain After Next Day (NPRS) | RPE (Borg CR10) | Recovery Days |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Light** | 1–2 | 1–2 | 0–1 | 0 (daily) |
| **Medium** | 2–3 | 3–4 | 2–4 | 2 |
| **High** | 4–5 | 5–6 | 5–10 | 3 |

*Patellar examples:* Light = free throw shooting; Medium = stationary jump shots 20 min; High = half-court scrimmage 20 min [S10, p.756]
*Achilles examples:* Light = straight line dribbling 15 min; Medium = dribbling/passing drills with directional changes 20 min [S10, p.756]

## Selected Loading Exercises

### Patellar Tendon (Table 59.6)
- **Phase 1–2:** Wall sits, Spanish squat, decline squat, isometric knee extension
- **Phase 2–3:** Step-ups, bodyweight squat, lunge, leg press, weighted squat, isotonic knee extension [S10, p.757]

### Achilles Tendon (Table 59.6)
- Isometric plantarflexion, decline heel raise, bilateral heel raises, eccentric heel raises, unilateral heel raises, seated heel raises, rebounding heel raise, step-up with plantarflexion [S10, p.758]

## Risk Factors to Address

### Patellar Tendinopathy
- Ankle dorsiflexion ROM deficit
- Hip extension, external rotation, and abduction strength deficits
- Posterior thigh and quadriceps flexibility deficits
- Quadriceps activation deficits
- Greater bodyweight / poor body composition [S10, p.758]

### Patellar Landing Biomechanics
- Athletes often use a "stiff" landing pattern (excessive hip and knee flexion at initial contact) — limits available ROM to dissipate ground reaction forces, increasing patellar tendon load
- Knee valgus upon landing increases strain on the medial tendon — the most common site of pathology [S10, pp.758–759]

### Achilles Tendinopathy
- Lower leg tendinopathy history
- Fluoroquinolone medication use
- Plantar flexion weakness
- Abnormal gait patterns
- Altered tendon structure on ultrasound increases symptom risk [S10, p.760]

## Adjunctive Treatments (When Loading Alone Fails)
- Shockwave therapy
- Platelet-rich plasma injections
- Noxious electrical stimulation at pain site (in-season pain management)
- Surgery (last resort)
- Even post-surgery, tendon loading exercises are essential to restore health and prepare for return to sport [S10, p.760]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stopping loading when symptoms resolve** → Continue maintenance program; structural alterations persist for 1+ year after symptom resolution [S10, p.757]
2. **Using only eccentric contractions for Achilles** → Comprehensive programs with isometric, concentric, and eccentric contractions are equally effective [S10, p.759]
3. **Applying mid-substance Achilles protocols to insertional pathology** → Avoid dorsiflexion loading for insertional Achilles tendinopathy; use heel lifts [S10, p.760]
4. **Relying solely on isometric exercises for pain relief** → Recent RCTs show no difference between isometric and isotonic in acute pain relief [S10, p.759]
5. **Back-to-back plyometric days** → Tendon needs 72 h recovery after high-intensity loading [S10, p.756]
