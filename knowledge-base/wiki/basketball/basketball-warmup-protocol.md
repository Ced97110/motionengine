# Basketball Warm-Up Protocol

## Summary
A fundamentally sound warm-up program is essential for injury prevention, alignment correction, and performance. Herb Brown emphasizes that warm-ups should never be performed at 100% speed — because basketball involves constant change of direction and velocity, athletes rarely sprint at full speed in a single direction during games. The warm-up should mimic the multi-directional, change-of-pace demands of the sport.

Consistency is the key: players who perform the same warm-up routine before every practice and competition experience less soreness, fewer injuries, and improved overall performance. Even injured players who cannot practice should perform the warm-up on the sideline, as it provides rehabilitative movements and corrects alignment errors before they become ingrained. [S1, pp.227-232]

## When to Use
- Before every practice session
- Before every competition (a mini-version suffices)
- Before summer workouts
- For injured players on the sideline (rehabilitative value)
- After practice: some exercises restore "normal feel" to muscles and flush lactic acid

## Key Principles
1. **Never warm up at 100% speed.** Injury prevention, proper technique, and stamina maintenance all require sub-maximal effort in warm-up.
2. **Keep movements simple, functional, and easy to follow.** The routine must be reproducible every day.
3. **Routine breeds results.** Consistency is more important than any single exercise.
4. **Correct alignment errors in the warm-up** before they become ingrained habits.
5. **Static stretching belongs after practice**, not before — it shuts the body down. Dynamic movement is the warm-up.
6. **Cool-down after practice**: walk or ride a stationary bike with no resistance for 5 minutes to flush lactic acid and reduce next-day soreness.
7. **Power flows upward**: generate force from legs → core → extremities in all passing and throwing motions.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: same warm-up protocol — this is a team-wide routine.

## Advanced Warm-Up Drills

### Drill 1: Single-Cone Shuffle
- Place one cone at the free-throw line, one at half-court, one at the far free-throw line.
- Sprint toward the first cone, then shuffle around it (no crossing feet) and sprint toward the second cone — shuffle in the opposite direction.
- Finish at the third cone in the original shuffle direction.
- **Coaching cues**: Keep arms active and pumping as in a sprint. Do not drop the head and eyes when transitioning from shuffle to forward run.

### Drill 2: Wide-Cone Shuffle
- Same structure as Drill 1 but use two cones 3 feet apart at half-court and two cones 6 feet apart at the far free-throw line.
- Requires more lateral distance to shuffle around each set.
- **Variations**: Figure-8 pattern through cones; coach stands in front and points or calls direction (adds a reaction component).

### Drill 3: Partner Cone Drill
- Two teammates 10 feet apart perform the initial cone drill together at the free-throw line and half-court.
- After passing half-court, one player receives the ball and the other becomes the live defender all the way to the basket.
- **Purpose**: Forces players to work on footwork under competitive pressure.

### Drill 4: Multiple-Cone Shuffle
- Place four cones across the free-throw line and four at half-court.
- Sprint from the baseline to the first set of cones, then weave through (in front of the first cone, behind the last cone).
- Sprint to the next set and shuffle in the opposite direction.
- **Variation**: Run on the diagonal after the first shuffle set, requiring work on both left and right sides.

## Supplemental Exercises
These exercises prepare the body for basketball-specific movements and improve flexibility, strength, balance, and coordination.

### 1. Leg Swings (Front to Back)
- **Purpose**: Loosen hip flexors and hamstrings; work on hip alignment and core strength.
- **Setup**: Stand on a step so the swinging leg does not touch the ground.
- **Execution**: Swing one leg front to back, maintaining neutral pelvis and upper-back position. Swing as high as possible in both directions without losing posture.
- **Coaching cues**: "Keep the knee and foot pointed straight forward." Avoid pointing the foot or knee outward when the leg swings behind the body.
- **Diagnostic note**: Athletes who struggle with the back phase are more prone to lower-back and hamstring problems.

### 2. Leg Swings (Side to Side)
- **Purpose**: Hip flexibility and core stability; expose lower-back and kneecap issues.
- **Setup**: Same step as front-to-back swings.
- **Execution**: Cross leg swings — swinging leg crosses the standing leg to both front and back sides. Keep pelvis square; do not overarch the lower back to allow the leg to clear.
- **Diagnostic note**: Athletes with lower-back problems or kneecap pain struggle to swing one leg behind the other and have difficulty keeping feet pointed forward.

### 3. Step-Ups
- **Setup**: Step no higher than 10 inches.
- **Purpose**: Warm up the knees and promote optimal leg alignment.
- **Execution**: One leg at a time, maximum 15 repetitions. Knee must stay in line with the toes throughout.
- **Diagnostic cues**:
  - Knee falling inward → tendency toward knee tendonitis
  - Knee swaying outward → risk of cartilage problems
- **Benefit**: Strengthens leg and hip muscles; activates joint receptors for proper alignment, efficiency, and power.

### 4. Balance Board
- **Setup**: Teeter-totter style balance board.
- **Execution**: Stand with knees unlocked and core activated. Maintain athletic arm position without using upper body to assist balance. Balance must come from legs and core.
- **Progressions**: Add basketball-specific upper-body movements (chest passes, outlet passes, shooting motions) while maintaining balance. Upper-body movement quality should not suffer from being on the board. As balance improves, the board moves less.

### 5. Tennis Ball Sit-Ups
- **Setup**: Lie on a bench with feet toward the ceiling (or knees slightly bent if hamstrings are tight).
- **Execution**: Crunch up and switch a tennis ball from one hand to the other behind the legs. Return to start between reps. Perform both clockwise and counterclockwise.
- **Progressions**:
  - Two balls → adds coordination demand
  - Three balls (two in one hand) → maximum core endurance and hand coordination
- **Benefit**: Directly trains core muscles; also increases hamstring flexibility.

### 6. Wall Tosses
- **Setup**: Solid wall at various distances.
- **Balls used**: Start with light rubber medicine ball → basketball → tennis ball (progressively lighter/faster rebound).
- **Movements**: Chest passes, outlet passes, one-hand baseball passes, side rotation passes.
- **Coaching cues**: "Keep knees unlocked. Generate power upward from legs, through core, to extremities." All movements use proper technique with the core as the power source.

## Common Mistakes
1. Warming up at full speed → increases injury risk and wastes energy needed for practice
2. Crossing feet during shuffles → poor footwork mechanics that transfer to games
3. Dropping head during cone transitions → loss of court vision habit
4. Overarching the lower back in leg swings → loses core activation and risks injury
5. Letting knee drift inward on step-ups → tendonitis risk; must be corrected immediately
6. Using the upper body for balance on the balance board → defeats the purpose; balance comes from legs and core
7. Static stretching before practice → shuts the body down; save it for after practice
