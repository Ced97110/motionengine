# MCL, LCL, and Posterolateral Corner (PLC) Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Collateral ligament injuries are generated by forces that push the knee sideways. MCL injuries are more common in basketball, while LCL/PLC injuries are less frequent but often misdiagnosed — leading to chronic instability and potentially career-threatening consequences. Accurate grading (Hughston classification) and thorough clinical examination are critical for appropriate management. The vast majority of isolated MCL tears are treated conservatively, while LCL/PLC Grade III injuries typically require surgery [S10, pp.339-342].

## When to Use
- Evaluating any basketball player with medial or lateral knee pain following a contact or rotational mechanism
- Diagnosing combined ligamentous injuries (e.g., ACL + lateral meniscus + PLC)
- Making conservative vs. surgical treatment decisions for collateral ligament injuries

## Key Principles
1. MCL tear rates: collegiate 0.87–1.02/10,000 AE; high school 0.3–0.62/10,000 AE [S10, p.340]
2. MCL injury in association with ACL injury in basketball (1.2%) is less common than in football (3%) or American football (2.8%) [S10, p.340]
3. LCL/PLC injuries are commonly misdiagnosed — true incidence is underestimated; misdiagnosis leads to chronic laxity and potential career jeopardy [S10, p.341]
4. ACL or PCL reconstruction may be compromised if lateral/posterolateral injuries are neglected — identify and treat simultaneously [S10, p.341]
5. MRI tends to overestimate MCL lesion severity — management should be led by clinical examination and functional progression [S10, p.340]

## Hughston Grading System (Both MCL and LCL/PLC)
- **Grade I**: laxity <5 mm (or 0–5 mm for LCL) — sprain, treated conservatively
- **Grade II**: laxity 6–10 mm (or 5–10 mm) — moderate tear, treatment complex
- **Grade III**: laxity >10 mm — complete tear [S10, pp.340-341]

## MCL Injuries

### Mechanisms
1. Direct contact on lateral knee with valgus force
2. Noncontact rotational injury: flexion + valgus + external rotation [S10, p.340]

### Diagnosis
- Palpate MCL from femoral insertion to tibia in supine position
- **Valgus stress test at 30°**: isolated laxity = isolated superficial MCL lesion
- **Valgus stress test at 0°**: laxity present = more severe injury involving other knee structures [S10, p.340]
- MRI for severe medial laxity in full extension, surgical planning, identifying associated meniscal injuries; beware: MRI overestimates severity

### Treatment by Grade
- **Grade I** (sprain): RICE ± hinged knee brace; early physiotherapy; RTS 2–3 weeks [S10, p.340]
- **Grade II**: similar to Grade I; brace up to 3 weeks; RTS ~6 weeks [S10, p.341]
- **Grade III** (complete tear):
  - Protective hinged brace; minimum 4 weeks immobilization; discontinued 4–6 weeks
  - Progressive rehabilitation and sport-specific exercises over 3–6 weeks following brace discontinuation
  - RTS rarely before 10 weeks [S10, p.341]
  - Surgery considered for: complete medial ligamentous complex disruption (including posterior-oblique ligament/POL), failure of conservative treatment — especially in pivoting sports like basketball where residual rotational instability is unacceptable [S10, p.341]

## LCL and Posterolateral Corner (PLC) Injuries

### Mechanisms
- Most common: noncontact — hyperextension + varus + external rotation
- Less common: direct medial trauma [S10, p.341]

### Clinical Examination
- Compare to contralateral knee (some players have physiologic lateral laxity)
- Suspect LCL/PLC with: swelling/hematoma/tenderness in posterolateral knee, hyperextension of injured knee
- **Varus stress at 30°**: LCL tear
- **Varus stress at 0°**: suspicious for PLC injury + cruciate ligament injury
- **Dial test** (key test): prone position, knees together, external rotation force at 30° and 90° flexion:
  - >10° side-to-side difference at 30° only → isolated PLC injury
  - >10° at both 30° and 90° → combined PCL + PLC injury
  - >10° at 90° only → PCL instability [S10, p.342]
- **External rotation recurvatum test**: external tibial rotation in hyperextension
- **Posterolateral drawer test**: suspected popliteal tendon + popliteofibular ligament injury
- **Reverse-pivot shift test**: reduction of posterior lateral tibial subluxation near extension [S10, p.341]

### Treatment by Grade
- **Grade I**: conservative management
- **Grade III**: surgical management
- **Grade II**: complex decision based on laxity degree, knee alignment, combined injuries, time from injury, playing level [S10, p.341-342]
- Neurovascular assessment mandatory if knee dislocation suspected (may have reduced spontaneously) [S10, p.341]

## Common Mistakes
1. Missing LCL/PLC injuries because they are less common — always examine posterolateral structures in multiligament injury [S10, p.341]
2. Failing to assess dial test at both 30° and 90° → misinterpret PLC vs. combined PCL+PLC [S10, p.342]
3. Performing ACL/PCL reconstruction without addressing LCL/PLC → reconstruction may fail [S10, p.341]
4. Comparing only to a textbook norm for lateral laxity — some players have physiological lateral laxity; always compare to contralateral knee [S10, p.341]
5. Over-bracing Grade I MCL and delaying physiotherapy → slow recovery; Grade I allows early return in 2–3 weeks with early physiotherapy [S10, p.340]
