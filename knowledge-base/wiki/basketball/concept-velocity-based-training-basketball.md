# Velocity-Based Training (VBT) for Basketball

## Summary
Velocity-based training (VBT) is an advanced resistance training method that assigns target movement velocities rather than fixed percentage-of-1RM loads to elicit specific training adaptations. A linear encoder or other velocity-measuring device provides real-time feedback on bar speed, allowing the coach to autoregulate load based on the athlete's current readiness. In a team environment, VBT feedback also serves as a powerful motivational tool. [S11, Ch.11]

VBT is a preferred preseason training method for basketball because the sport demands explosiveness and rate of force development (RFD). As the preseason progresses, target velocity zones shift from lower velocities (maximal/accelerative strength) toward higher velocities (strength-speed, speed-strength), reflecting the transition from strength to power.

## When to Use
- Preseason and in-season phases when power and explosiveness are the priority
- When the coach wants to autoregulate intensity based on daily athlete readiness (fatigue, travel, game load)
- As a motivational tool in team training environments
- When transitioning from %1RM-based programming to output-based programming

## Key Principles
1. **Velocity zones guide training zone selection** — not %1RM (though the two are correlated)
2. **Earlier preseason = lower velocity targets** (accelerative strength, 0.45–0.75 m/s)
3. **Later preseason = higher velocity targets** (strength-speed to starting strength, 0.75–1.5+ m/s)
4. **Olympic lifts: record PEAK velocity**, not average — peak velocity reflects explosive intent
5. **Olympic lift volume: 2–5 sets** — lower volume required to generate maximum peak velocity; never train to failure
6. **All reps: maximum explosive intent** — "intent to move as fast as possible" is the coaching cue
7. **Autoregulation:** If the athlete cannot reach the target velocity zone, reduce load; if they consistently exceed it, increase load

## Force-Velocity Training Zones

| Zone | Velocity Target | Training Quality |
|---|---|---|
| Absolute strength | 0.15–0.35 m/s | Maximum force, highest loads |
| Circa max strength | 0.35–0.45 m/s | Near-maximal strength |
| Accelerative strength | 0.45–0.75 m/s | Transitional zone — early preseason focus |
| Strength-speed | 0.75–1.0 m/s | Power development |
| Speed-strength | 1.0–1.5 m/s | Explosive power — late preseason focus |
| Starting strength | >1.5 m/s | Reactive/ballistic power |

*Source: Reprinted from NSCA, Advanced Power Techniques, D.N. French, in Developing Power (McGuigan, ed., 2017), p.191. Cited in [S11, Ch.11]*

## Olympic Lift Peak Velocities

| Exercise | Peak Velocity |
|---|---|
| Snatch from floor | 1.52–1.67 m/s |
| Snatch power pull | 1.81 m/s |
| Snatch power shrug | 1.45 m/s |
| Hang snatch | 1.96 m/s |
| Power clean | 1.2–1.32 m/s |
| Hang clean | 1.3–1.4 m/s |
| Power shrug | 1.15 m/s |

*Source: J.B. Mann, "Power and Bar Velocity Measuring Devices and Their Use for Autoregulation," NSCA Hot Topic Series, 2011. Cited in [S11, Ch.11]*

**Note:** For Olympic lifts, record **peak** velocity (not average, which is used for squat/bench/deadlift). Olympic lifts should always be explosive; do not train to failure.

## Preseason VBT Progression
- **Early preseason (Weeks 1–2):** Higher loads, lower velocity targets (accelerative strength zone, 0.45–0.75 m/s); traditional set/rep schemes (4–6 sets × 3–5 reps)
- **Mid-preseason (Weeks 3–4):** Shift toward strength-speed (0.75–1.0 m/s); loads decrease, velocity targets increase
- **Late preseason (Weeks 5–6):** Speed-strength and starting strength zones (1.0–1.5+ m/s); lower loads, maximum velocity intent

## Accentuated Eccentric Loading (Advanced VBT Variant)
For experienced athletes: hold dumbbells (≈20% bodyweight) during the eccentric phase of drop jumps, release at the bottom, then perform an unloaded vertical jump. The eccentric overload enhances neuromuscular activation. Bridgeman et al.: 1 set of 5 loaded drop jumps was superior to unloaded drop jumps for CMJ improvement. Habitual use improves strength, power, reactive strength, and sprinting performance. [S11, Ch.11]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using average velocity for Olympic lifts** → peak velocity is the correct metric; average velocity underestimates explosive output
2. **Training Olympic lifts to failure** → technical breakdown occurs; never grind through these movements
3. **Not adjusting load to meet velocity target** → if the athlete can't reach the zone, reduce load; VBT is autoregulative
4. **Maintaining off-season velocity zones through the preseason** → zones must progress toward higher velocities as the season approaches
