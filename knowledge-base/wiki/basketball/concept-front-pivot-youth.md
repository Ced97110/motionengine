# Front Pivot

## Summary
The front pivot is a fundamental footwork technique where a player rotates 180 degrees by bringing the non-pivot foot between themselves and their defender, turning toward the basket. It is one of two primary pivot techniques in basketball (the other being the [[drill-reverse-pivot-youth|reverse pivot]]) and is used in a wide range of offensive and defensive situations including catch-and-face, box-out setups, and passing out of pressure.

The front pivot keeps players closer to the basket at the conclusion of a scoring fake, making it the preferred pivot when the player wants to maintain position near the rim or when the player needs to observe their defender for a longer time before making a decision.

## When to Use
- After a jump stop or stride stop to face up for a pass or shot
- At the end of a scoring fake when you want to stay close to the basket
- By defensive rebounders against a quicker opponent (allows watching the assignment longer)
- By perimeter players trying to box out on the perimeter
- When a player wants to maintain vision on the defender's tactics before committing

## Key Principles
1. Establish the pivot foot firmly before lifting the free foot
2. Pick up the free foot and swing it 180 degrees between yourself and the defender
3. Keep the ball protected — bring it to chin/triple-threat position just before pivoting
4. Stay low throughout; do not rise up and expose the ball
5. "Stay closer to the basket" — the front pivot does not move you away from the hoop like the reverse pivot does

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Master both front and reverse pivots; use the front pivot when proximity to the basket is the priority

## Variations
### Defensive Front Pivot (Box-Out)
Used by on-ball defenders boxing out the shooter — swing the free foot in front to seal the offensive player from the ball's flight path. Most effective against quicker opponents.

### Perimeter Front Pivot
Used by perimeter players to face up after catching a pass on the move, establishing triple-threat position quickly.

## Common Mistakes
1. Moving the pivot foot before establishing it → travel call; reinforce "plant and stay"
2. Bringing the ball to palm height instead of chin → ball exposure; cue "ball to chin"
3. Rising up during the pivot → loss of balance; cue "stay low, chair sit"
4. Pivoting too slowly → allows the defender to recover; cue "snap the foot around"
