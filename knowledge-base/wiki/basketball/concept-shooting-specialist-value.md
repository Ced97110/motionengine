# Shooting Specialist Value

## Summary
Pure shooting specialists who don't create their own shot extensively can still be highly valuable — *if* their shooting is efficient enough. Dean Oliver's *Basketball on Paper* (S16) evaluates Reggie Miller and Chris Mullin as the prototypical shooting specialists, finding that Miller in particular was remarkably valuable and consistent throughout his career. The key insight is that **if shooting is the most important aspect of the game** (as argued in chapter 6 of S16), then elite specialists who do it exceptionally well can approach All-Star value even without other dominant skills.

## Key Findings: Reggie Miller
- 15-year career Off. Rtg.: **121** (career average well above league average)
- Floor%: **0.54** career
- Win-loss record: **9.4-2.2 per 82 games** — "a very good figure" [S16, p.291]
- Miller posted between **9-11 wins and 1-3 losses** in nearly every season from his 3rd year forward — *"Consistency is definitely a sign of greatness"* [S16, p.291]
- Peak single season: **10.7-1.5** (1991, Off. Rtg. 129)
- His defense improved significantly mid-career — he became a solid defender on mid-to-late 1990s Pacers teams [S16, p.290]
- Career Def. Rtg.: **110** (slightly above average)

## Key Findings: Chris Mullin
- 16-year career Off. Rtg.: **115**
- Floor%: **0.54** career
- Win-loss record: **88-49 (0.640)** career
- Peak single season: **11.3-3.3** (1991, Off. Rtg. 120)
- Never reached Miller's peak offensive ratings (120 vs. 129)
- Good player, but didn't show the same sustained greatness as Miller [S16, p.291]

## Comparative Perspective: Miller vs. Rodman
- **Miller** ≈ best pure *shooting* role player ever: ~9.6 wins, 2.3 losses per 82 games
- **Dennis Rodman** ≈ best pure *non-shooting* role player ever: ~8.3 wins, 2.0 losses per 82 games
- These two opposite-style extreme players end up with **similar win-loss records** [S16, p.292]
- Neither contributed as much as superstars (Jordan: 14.9-2.3 per 82 games) [S16, p.292]

## Key Principles
1. **One elite skill can sustain an All-Star-level career** — if it's shooting (the most impactful skill), even limited defenders can produce 9-10 win seasons [S16, p.291]
2. **Specialists don't need to create** — Miller and Mullin knew their shots and made them without creating shots off the dribble extensively [S16, p.290]
3. **Consistency across years signals greatness** — posting 9-11 wins every season for 15 years is more impressive than an occasional spectacular season [S16, p.291]
4. **Defense has a floor** — both Miller and Mullin were subpar defenders early in their careers; Miller improved his D substantially, which elevated his overall value [S16, p.290]
5. **Opposite-skill specialists have similar values** — the best shooter (Miller) and the best non-shooter/rebounder (Rodman) produced nearly identical win-loss contributions, suggesting the system fairly values complementary skills [S16, p.292]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Labeling specialists as "one-dimensional" and undervaluing them** → If the one dimension is shooting (the most important element), specialists can be extremely valuable; evaluate via Off. Rtg. and win-loss, not scoring average alone [S16, p.290]
2. **Overrating consistency in less impactful skills** → Being consistently average at a low-value skill is not the same as being consistently elite at a high-value skill
