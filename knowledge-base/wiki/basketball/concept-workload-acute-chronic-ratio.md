# Workload Acute-to-Chronic Ratio

## Summary
The acute-to-chronic workload ratio (ACWR) is a tool for quantifying the relationship between recent training load (acute = last 1 week) and long-term training load (chronic = rolling average of the past 3–4 weeks). It represents the training load an athlete is doing relative to what they have been prepared to handle. A ratio ≥1.5 indicates a dangerous workload spike associated with significantly elevated injury risk [S12, p.13].

In basketball specifically, the shortened pre-season (3 weeks in the NBA) makes ACWR management critical: athletes arrive from an off-season with low chronic workloads and coaches must resist the temptation to spike loads rapidly [S12, p.12].

## When to Use
- Daily monitoring of all training loads (sessions + games)
- Transition from off-season → pre-season (highest risk spike period)
- Return-to-play decisions after injury
- Planning load distribution across congested fixture schedules
- Managing players who miss sessions and then return to full training

## Key Principles
1. **Threshold for elevated injury risk: ACWR ≥ 1.5** — When the acute load exceeds chronic load by 50% or more, injury risk rises substantially [S12, p.13]
2. **Avoid spikes >10% per week** — Regardless of the absolute ratio, week-to-week load increases should not exceed 10% [S12, p.12]
3. **Low chronic workload = high spike risk** — The off-season creates a low training base; the first weeks of camp must start conservatively even if players feel ready [S12, p.12]
4. **Individual calculation** — ACWR is most meaningful when calculated for each athlete individually rather than using team averages [S12, p.13]
5. **Apply to both external and internal load** — Calculate ACWR using both microtechnology player load (external) and S-RPE training load (internal) for a complete picture [S12, p.13]
6. **Z-score complement** — Use ACWR alongside Z-score monitoring (flag Z ≤ −1.5) for a comprehensive early-warning system [S12, p.13]

## Calculation Framework
- **Acute load** = sum of session loads (S-RPE × duration) over the last 7 days
- **Chronic load** = average weekly load over the past 3–4 weeks
- **ACWR** = Acute load ÷ Chronic load
- **Interpretation**: Ratio 0.8–1.3 = "sweet spot" (low injury risk + high fitness); ≥1.5 = elevated injury risk; <0.8 = potential undertraining/detraining

## Player Responsibilities
- **All players**: Provide accurate session RPE (6–20 Borg scale) within 30 minutes of every session completion — the raw data for internal load calculation
- **S&C Coach**: Calculate individual ACWRs weekly; flag any player approaching 1.3 as a caution; adjust training for any player at ≥1.5
- **Head Coach**: Receive weekly ACWR summary; understand that a player flagged at ≥1.5 needs load reduction even if they report feeling fine

## Common Mistakes
1. **Applying the 10% rule in absolute terms only** — A player returning from injury with a very low chronic load can spike their ratio dramatically with what looks like a modest absolute load increase [S12, p.12]
2. **Only tracking team-level load** — Individual players respond very differently; positional demands (back-court vs. front-court) and playing time create large within-team variation [S12, p.13]
3. **Ignoring game load in ACWR** — Match loads often exceed practice loads significantly; games must be included in acute load calculations [S12, p.13]
4. **Using ACWR as the only metric** — ACWR quantifies load exposure but not the athlete's response; always pair with fatigue monitoring (CMJ, wellness) [S12, p.12]
