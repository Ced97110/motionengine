# Triangle Offense — Foundational Principles

## Summary
The Triangle Offense, developed by Tex Winter and famously implemented by Phil Jackson with the pro team (the iconic scorer/the top defensive wing era) and team (Bryant/O'Neal era), is a continuity half-court system built around a "sideline triangle" formed by a wing, post, and corner player. It combines read-and-react decision making with precise spacing to create high-percentage shots for all five players. The system proved effective across nearly 60 years, from college basketball in the 1960s and 1970s through multiple pro championships. [S7, pp.97-109]

## When to Use
- Primary half-court offense against man-to-man defense
- When your personnel includes a versatile post player who can pass, screen, and shoot
- When you want a system that keeps all five players involved and does not depend on one iso scorer
- Late-game situations where patient, precise execution is required

## Key Principles
1. **The sideline triangle is the core formation** — formed by the strong-side wing (3), post (5), and corner guard (1) — three players creating a triangular passing relationship on one side of the floor.
2. **The second pass drives the offense** — after the entry pass to the wing, four distinct passing targets exist (post, weak-side guard, weak-side wing, strong-side corner), each opening a different scoring action.
3. **Spacing vocabulary:** Specific spots must be understood by all players:
   - **Fan spot / Garden spot:** The open shooting area on the weak side where the weak-side guard (2) spots up — ideal for punishing double-teams on the post. "A nice and open spot on a court for shooting."
   - **Freeze spot:** A weak-side position where a player occupies their defender while waiting for a possible screen — keeps the defense honest without clogging the lane.
   - **Moment of truth / Line of truth:** The critical passing window and its triggering position (see [[concept-triangle-offense-pressure-releases]]).
4. **All actions have continuity built in** — if no second-pass option produces a shot, the ball can always be rotated to form the triangle on the other side of the floor. "We are always in our offense and ready to operate!"
5. **The passer is the first cutter** — immediately after a pass to the post, the passer cuts to free the post player from their defender.
6. **"It's the execution that counts"** — Winter/Jackson's cardinal rule: technique and precision matter more than athleticism in running this system. [S7, pp.97-109]

- **3 (SF/wing):** Strong-side wing, second-pass decision maker
- **5 (C/post):** Strong-side post, hub of post passing game
- **1 (PG/guard):** Strong-side corner, safety valve and screen target
- **2 (SG/guard):** Top of the circle / weak-side, garden spot shooter, defensive balance
- **4 (PF/wing):** Weak-side wing, freeze spot holder, backdoor option

### How the Triangle Side Switches
When all second-pass options are exhausted: 2 passes to 4 → 4 passes to 1 → 1 passes to 3 who has re-entered the corner on the new side. OR 4 cuts to the low post, 5 moves to the weak-side wing, and 1 receives at the top of the lane — new sideline triangle forms on the other side. The offense never dies.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (1):** Entry passer to the wing; fills the corner; acts as the corner option in the fourth look; dribble weave and pressure-release ball handler.
- **SG (2):** Spots up at the garden/fan spot; receives second-look passes; defensive balance safety.
- **SF (3):** Primary second-pass decision maker; split cutter; rebound screen cutter; wing screener.
- **PF (4):** Weak-side operations — freeze spot, backdoor cuts, elbow flashes, screen-the-screener, weak-side screen on 3's cuts.
- **C (5):** Post hub — receives post entry, initiates split cuts, executes fan passes to garden spot, sets screen-and-rolls on corner passes.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not completing cuts after a pass** → turnover when the passer has committed. Always finish every cut.
2. **Forgetting to spot up at the garden spot** → removes the safety valve when 5 is doubled. 2 must always find the open weak-side spot.
3. **Post holding the ball without action** → defenders collapse; the system requires immediate reading and action after the entry.
4. **Not using the continuity reset** → coaches must teach that every failure to score is a transition to the other side, not a stall.
