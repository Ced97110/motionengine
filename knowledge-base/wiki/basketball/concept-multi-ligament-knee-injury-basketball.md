# Multi-Ligament Knee Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Multi-ligament knee injuries (MLI) are among the most devastating injuries a basketball player can sustain. Although often associated with high-energy trauma, MLI in basketball frequently occurs during low-velocity twisting and pivoting motions at landing — the hallmark movements of the sport. Surgical reconstruction is the standard of care, and outcomes have improved significantly with modern single-stage anatomic reconstruction and structured staged rehabilitation protocols.

The lower extremity accounts for **62.4% of all basketball injuries**, with knee injuries comprising **17.8% of all athletic injuries** in the sport [S10, p.363].

## Classification

### Schenck Knee Dislocation Classification System [S10, p.364]
| Class | Anatomic Pattern |
|---|---|
| KD I | Single cruciate + collaterals |
| KD II | ACL + PCL, intact collaterals |
| KD III-M | ACL + PCL + MCL |
| KD III-L | ACL + PCL + LCL |
| KD IV | ACL + PCL + MCL + LCL |
| KD V | Dislocation + fracture |

- "C" modifier = concomitant arterial injury
- "N" modifier = concomitant neural injury
- "Dislocation" implies rupture of **both** cruciate ligaments [S10, p.364]

## Prevalence and Epidemiology
1. Incidence estimated at **0.072 per 100-patient years** — not as rare as previously thought [S10, p.364].
2. **Sports-related MLI accounts for up to 47%** of multi-ligament injuries in some cohorts [S10, p.364].
3. **Female basketball players** have ACL injury rate **4× greater than males** (p < 0.01), with increased meniscal and collateral ligament tears [S10, p.364].
4. Most common MLI pattern: **KD III-M** (medial side + cruciate) — medial injuries 52%, lateral 28%, isolated bicruciate only 5% [S10, p.364].
5. Basketball players are **4.95× more likely** to experience an ACL tear than football players (95% CI: 2.61–8.76) [S10, p.364].
6. **Low-energy trauma** (typical of basketball) accounted for 49.7% of knee dislocations defined by bicruciate involvement [S10, p.364].

## Diagnosis

### History and Physical Examination
1. Thorough and systematic history and physical examination is mandatory [S10, p.365].
2. Video of the injury is helpful when available.
3. Focus: swelling, alignment, passive/active ROM, knee stability — may be limited by pain.
4. Concomitant meniscal and ligament injuries can be missed in the acute period [S10, p.365].

### Neurovascular Assessment (Critical)
1. **Neural injury incidence: 19–25%; vascular injury incidence: 5–21%** in MLI cohorts [S10, p.365].
2. **PLC (posterolateral corner) injury → 42× greater odds of peroneal nerve injury and 9.2× greater odds of popliteal artery injury** [S10, p.365].
3. Compare injured to contralateral limb: palpate posterior tibial and dorsalis pedis pulses bilaterally; assess skin color, capillary refill, pulse symmetry [S10, p.365].
4. **Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI) < 0.9 warrants angiography** [S10, p.365].
5. Do not hesitate to pursue angiography when peroneal nerve injury is present [S10, p.365].

### Diagnostic Imaging
**Stress Radiograph Diagnostic Criteria** [S10, p.366]:

| Test | Threshold | Diagnosis |
|---|---|---|
| PCL stress | Side-to-side > 8 mm | Grade 3 PCL injury |
| PCL stress | Absolute difference > 11 mm | Combined PCL + PLC injury |
| Valgus stress | Side-to-side > 3 mm | Grade 3 MCL injury |
| Valgus stress | Absolute difference > 9.8 mm | Posteromedial corner injury |
| Varus stress | Side-to-side > 2.7 mm | Grade 3 FCL injury |
| Varus stress | Absolute difference > 4 mm | Posterolateral corner injury |

- If patient guarding prevents stress radiographs, use mini C-arm under anesthesia [S10, p.366].
- **MRI is imperative**: provides information on meniscal/cartilage injuries and individual ligaments [S10, p.366].
- Failing to address a concurrent collateral ligament injury increases cruciate graft failure risk due to increased force transmission [S10, p.366].

### Concomitant Injuries
- Meniscal injury: 37.3% of MLI cases; cartilage injury: 28.3% [S10, p.365].
- In some series, meniscal or chondral injury present in **76% of cases** [S10, p.365].

## Treatment Principles

### Key Treatment Recommendations (Fact Box) [S10, p.370]
1. **Ligament reconstruction is superior to repair** — anatomic reconstruction provides greater stability, earlier ROM, and better kinematic restoration.
2. **Single-stage surgery is superior to multi-stage surgery** — reduces instability, restores physiologic force transmission, allows early rehabilitation.
3. **Early surgical fixation (within 3 weeks) is superior to chronic fixation** — associated with higher Lysholm scores (90 vs. 82), higher IKDC scores (47% vs. 31%), higher sports activity scores [S10, p.370].

### Graft Selection
- **ACL**: BTB autograft preferred; BTB allograft for age >55 or patellar tendon compromise on MRI [S10, p.366].
- **PCL**: Anatomic double-bundle reconstruction — Achilles tendon allograft (anterolateral bundle) + tibialis anterior allograft (posteromedial bundle) [S10, p.366].
- **MCL**: Autograft hamstring reconstruction [S10, p.366].
- **FCL/PLC**: Semitendinosus autograft or allograft [S10, p.366].
- **Repair preferred only for**: capsular avulsion or hamstring tendon avulsion [S10, p.366].

### Repair vs. Reconstruction Evidence
- Repair showed greater anteroposterior translation (6.67 mm vs. 3.20 mm) vs. reconstruction [S10, p.367].
- PLC repair failure rate: 40% vs. 6% for single-stage reconstruction (p = 0.04) [S10, p.367].
- Posteromedial corner repair failure: 20% vs. 5% for reconstruction [S10, p.367].

### Single-Stage Reconstruction Evidence
- LaPrade et al. (194 patients, mean 3.5-year follow-up): significant improvement in Lysholm (41→90) and WOMAC (44→3); no difference in arthrofibrosis rates between medial/lateral injuries [S10, p.369].

### Timing
- Surgery within 3 weeks recommended (when swelling improved, 0–90° of flexion achieved) [S10, p.370].
- Early surgery allows early rehabilitation and reduces arthrofibrosis risk.

## Rehabilitation

### Four Core Rehabilitation Principles (Mueller et al.) [S10, p.370]
1. **Protect the surgical reconstruction** and restore joint range of motion
2. **Manage the scarring process**
3. **Minimize muscular atrophy** and restore preinjury muscular strength
4. **Utilize return-to-sport testing** to guide decision-making

### Phases of Periodization [S10, p.371]
1. **Muscular Endurance Phase** — begins ~week 8 postoperatively when ROM fully restored; each phase ≥ 6 weeks
2. **Muscular Strength Phase**
3. **Muscular Power Phase**

### Phase-by-Phase Treatment Algorithm [S10, pp.371-372]

| Phase | Weeks | ROM | Gait | Key Physical Exam | Key Exercises |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | 0–2 | 0–90° knee flexion; patellofemoral ROM | Non-weight-bearing | — | Patellofemoral mobilization |
| Phase 1 | 2–8 | 0–10° contralateral side | Protected weight-bearing | Swelling within 1 cm of opposite knee | Four-way SLR, ankle pumps, gluteal sets |
| Phase 3 (Endurance) | 8–15 | Full active ROM = contralateral | Full WB unassisted ×20 min | Quad index >80%; anterior reach within 8 cm | Leg press 2.5× BW; single-leg squat 15 reps; walk-run; DorsaVi single-leg squat |
| Phase 3 (Strength) | 15–21 | — | — | Quad index 90%; girth within 1 cm; H:Q ratio >60% | Leg press 2.8× BW; single-plane speed/agility; DorsaVi box drop |
| Phase 4 (Power) | 21+ | — | — | Quad within 1 cm contralateral | Leg press 3.1× BW; multiplane agility T-test within 90%; DorsaVi single-leg vertical jump within 90% |
| Return to Sport | 9–12 months | — | — | Multidisciplinary decision | Vail Sports Test/SANE/IKDC <5% difference from contralateral |

### Early Phase Key Points
- **Immediate active ROM** advocated to prevent residual stiffness (most common complication) [S10, p.370].
- Athletes should reach **0–90° of flexion within 2 weeks** [S10, p.370].
- Avoid aggressive flexion training for first 4 weeks; limit extension to neutral for 6 weeks [S10, p.370].
- Non-weight-bearing: 4–6 weeks in most protocols [S10, p.370].
- **Patellofemoral mobilization** initiated immediately; hypomobility of proximal pole of patella can lead to extensor mechanism dysfunction [S10, p.370].
- Arthofibrosis risk: suprapatellar arthrofibrosis increases patellofemoral contact force by up to 80 N [S10, p.371].

### Basketball-Specific Return-to-Play Progressions [S10, p.372]
- **Lateral movement, jumping, and pivoting** introduced gradually in final rehabilitation phase.
- Progress: **single-plane agility → multiplane agility**.
- Return-to-play decision: multidisciplinary (physical therapist, athletic trainers, surgeon).
- Use objective scoring: **Vail Sports Test, SANE, IKDC** prior to release.
- **Lunging exercises**: use cautiously in PCL and PLC injuries — weight-bearing flexion >45° disproportionately loads the PCL [S10, p.371].

## Outcomes

### General Population
- Good functional outcomes at short-, mid-, and long-term follow-up: Lysholm 84±17, IKDC-2000 73±19, Tegner activity score 4 [S10, p.373].
- **42% radiographic osteoarthritis** at long-term follow-up (10–18.8 years); risk increased for patients >30 at surgery [S10, p.374].

### Elite Athlete-Specific Outcomes
- **56% of professional football players** (comparable biomechanical demands) returned to play at mean 388±198 days [S10, p.374].
- Elite athletes with single-stage reconstruction **within 40 days**: 19/24 returned to sport; only 8/24 reached preinjury sports activity level [S10, p.374].
- NBA injuries to the knee account for **18.1% of total games missed** — highest proportion of any structure [S10, p.373].
- Post-traumatic OA: a greater problem for retired NBA players; average NBA career ~6 years [S10, p.374].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Underestimating neurovascular risk** → always assess ABI, peroneal nerve, and pulses bilaterally — especially with PLC injury [S10, p.365].
2. **Choosing repair over reconstruction** → higher failure rates (20–40% vs. 5–6%) [S10, p.367].
3. **Delaying surgery beyond 3 weeks** → lower Lysholm, IKDC, and activity scores vs. early surgery [S10, p.370].
4. **Immobilizing the knee after surgery** → arthrofibrosis is the most common complication; immediate ROM is key [S10, p.370].
5. **Returning to basketball before objective tests are passed** → use T-test, DorsaVi, Vail Sports Test, IKDC [S10, p.372].
6. **Missing concurrent meniscal or cartilage injury** → present in 37–76% of MLI cases; failing to address increases morbidity [S10, p.365].
