# Basketball Ankle Sprain Epidemiology and Prevention

## Summary
Among male and female basketball players of all ages, the lateral ankle sprain is the single most common injury diagnosis. Basketball players change motion every 2.0–2.82 seconds and jump 35–46 times per game — frequent direction changes and jumping are directly linked to ankle injuries [S10, p.206]. This page covers the epidemiology, mechanisms, concomitant injuries, and prevention strategies specific to basketball ankle injuries.

For rehabilitation and exercise progressions, see [[ankle-sprain-rehabilitation]] and [[ankle-strengthening]].

## When to Use
- Designing ankle injury prevention programs for any level of basketball
- Evaluating a player after an inversion or eversion ankle injury
- Determining the need for radiography using Ottawa rules
- Advising players with a prior ankle sprain history on protective measures

## Key Principles

### Mechanism of Injury
1. **Most common mechanism**: Inversion injury with foot in slight plantar flexion — most commonly occurring through contact (player stepping on foot of another player) [S10, p.206]
2. **Additional mechanisms**: Awkward landing directly on court, twisting during cutting/turning/pushing off [S10, p.206]
3. **Lateral ligamentous complex (LLC) most commonly injured** — anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL) first, then calcaneofibular ligament (CFL) [S10, p.206]
4. **High ankle sprains (syndesmotic)**: From dorsiflexion with eversion or external rotation; more serious — injures deltoid ligament, anterior tibiofibular ligament, interosseous membrane [S10, p.207]

### NCAA Ankle Injury Breakdown
- Men: LLC injuries 79.97%; deltoid ligament 7.24%; anterior tibiofibular syndesmosis 7.01% [S10, p.206]
- Women: LLC tears 83.47%; syndesmotic injuries 7.05%; deltoid ligament 5.57% [S10, p.206]

### Concomitant Injuries to Rule Out
- Osteochondral lesions of the talar dome [S10, p.207]
- Fractures of anterolateral process of calcaneus
- Os trigonum fractures
- Superior peroneal retinaculum (SPR) tears with peroneal tendon subluxation
- Avulsion fractures of navicular and base of 5th metatarsal [S10, p.207]

### Prevention — External Ankle Support (Evidence-Based)
1. **Mid- or high-top sneakers** — standard for all players [S10, p.207]
2. **Ankle bracing or taping** — has been shown to decrease risk of ankle sprains [S10, p.207]
3. **Prior sprain = highest risk indicator** — athletes with history of ankle sprain should always wear external support on that ankle [S10, p.207]
4. **External support reduces recurrence** — studies show athletes with prior sprain who wear brace or tape have lower incidence of reinjury [S10, p.207]

### Time Loss Data (NCAA)
- 41.2% women / 43.8% men: Time loss <3 days [S10, p.204]
- >60% of ankle injuries: Time loss <7 days [S10, p.204]
- ~30% of injuries: Time loss >7 days [S10, p.204]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Report any prior ankle sprain history to medical staff at start of season; wear prescribed bracing/taping; participate in proprioceptive training programs
- **Guards**: Highest ankle injury rate at collegiate level — must be diligent about prevention protocols [S10, p.204]

## Variations

### 5th Metatarsal Stress Fractures (NBA)
- Most common NBA foot stress injury: 5th MT stress fracture (18.4%) [S10, p.207]
- Treated surgically 100% of the time with open reduction internal fixation [S10, p.207]
- 42% of players unable to return to play despite surgical management [S10, p.207]
- Mechanism: Avulsion of peroneus brevis attachment at base of 5th metatarsal from strong inversion load [S10, p.207]

### Avulsion Fractures Mimicking Lateral Ankle Sprain
- Anterolateral calcaneus fractures: foot abducted and plantar flexed, tension on bifurcate ligament [S10, p.207]
- Navicular avulsion fractures: similar presentation to low-ankle sprain [S10, p.207]
- Flake sign on plain radiograph: small bone fragment avulsion from fibula where SPR previously attached [S10, p.207]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Missing concomitant injury** → Thorough clinical evaluation of both ankles + advanced imaging for related pathologies when clinical picture doesn't match expected recovery
2. **Failing to protect prior sprain history** → Prior sprain is the strongest predictor of reinjury; all returning athletes with ankle history must wear external support
3. **Missing 5th metatarsal stress fracture** → Presents like a lateral ankle sprain; palpate base of 5th metatarsal; use Ottawa rules to determine if X-ray needed
4. **Under-treating high ankle sprains** → Syndesmotic injuries are more serious and recover more slowly than LLC sprains; do not treat as routine lateral ankle sprains
