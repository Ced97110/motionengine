# Basketball Acceleration and Deceleration

## Summary
Acceleration and deceleration are the two most important physical competencies in basketball. Every scoring opportunity, defensive stop, and transition moment is shaped by a player's ability to reach top speed quickly (accelerate) and to stop or change direction under control (decelerate). *Basketball Anatomy* repeatedly emphasizes that the ability to beat an opponent to the basket depends on the ability to accelerate, decelerate, and change direction — and that these abilities are built primarily through lower-body strength training. [S2, pp.13-27]

Deceleration is equally or more important than acceleration in basketball because cutting, landing, and change-of-direction all require eccentric (braking) force production. Insufficient deceleration strength leads to increased injury risk (ACL, hamstring strains) and reduced cutting sharpness.

## When to Use
- **Offensively:** First step off the dribble to beat a defender; cutting to the basket off a screen; driving to the rim on a show-and-go.
- **Defensively:** Closing out on a shooter; lateral slides to cut off dribble penetration; recovering after a ball-reversal.
- **Transition:** Getting ahead of the defense on the break; getting back in transition to prevent an easy basket.

## Key Principles
1. **Force into the floor = acceleration** — the ability to apply force into the ground determines first-step quickness; this is trained through squats, deadlifts, and hip thrust [S2, pp.11-19].
2. **Eccentric strength = deceleration** — the ability to absorb force on landing and during cuts requires strong quads, glutes, and hamstrings trained eccentrically; reverse step-down is the primary drill [S2, pp.22-23].
3. **Anterior/posterior balance** — proper ratios of quad-to-hamstring strength prevent muscle strains and ligament injuries; the Romanian deadlift is the primary corrective exercise [S2, p.17].
4. **Single-leg specificity** — cuts and landings happen on one leg; unilateral training (reverse lunge, reverse step-down) directly prepares these single-leg deceleration demands [S2, pp.20-23].
5. **Lateral plane** — direction changes are multi-planar; lateral hip abductor strength (band lateral walk) is essential for sideways acceleration and cutting [S2, pp.26-27].
6. **Strength endurance** — basketball requires these explosive actions repeatedly throughout a 32-40 minute game; strength endurance work (band backward walk) ensures quality is maintained late in games [S2, pp.24-25].

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG:** Lateral acceleration and change-of-direction are critical for creating off the dribble; prioritize band lateral walks and reverse step-down.
- **SG/SF:** All-direction acceleration for scoring and defensive rotations; full leg-strength programme applies.
- **PF/C:** Vertical acceleration (jumping) and lower-body deceleration for box-outs and post positioning; emphasize deadlift, hip thrust, and calf pop-up.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Training only acceleration, not deceleration** → add eccentric-focused exercises (RDL, reverse step-down) and plyometric landing drills.
2. **Neglecting lateral strength** → band lateral walks should be a year-round staple for all positions.
3. **Quad dominance without posterior balance** → pair every squat session with RDL or hip thrust to maintain safe strength ratios.
4. **Ignoring second-jump ability** → calf pop-up and plyometric rebounding drills build the Achilles elasticity needed for consecutive jumps.
