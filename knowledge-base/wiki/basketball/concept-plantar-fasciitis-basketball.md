# Plantar Fasciitis in Basketball

## Summary
The plantar fascia is highly vulnerable in basketball due to the sport's ballistic demands — repeated jumping, running, cutting, and pace changes generate significant plantar stress. Research shows significantly increased plantar peak pressure in women basketball players during three-point shots and layups [S10, p.570]. Plantar fasciitis can present acutely or as a chronic condition with acute exacerbations. Management progresses from conservative care to injectable treatments when first-line measures fail. PRP has emerged as the preferred injectable, with high-quality evidence demonstrating better long-term outcomes than corticosteroids with fewer complications [S10, pp.570-579].

## When to Use Injections
- After failure of first-line conservative management (rest, plantar fascia-specific stretching, NSAIDs, foot orthosis, shock wave therapy)
- When early return to play is required
- Corticosteroids for short-term relief (< 3 months); PRP for sustained long-term relief

## Key Principles

### Conservative Care (First Line)
1. Plantar fascia-specific stretching — most important conservative intervention
2. Rest from provocative loading activities
3. NSAIDs for acute inflammation
4. Foot orthosis for biomechanical support
5. Extracorporeal shock wave therapy (ESWT) for chronic cases [S10, p.570]

### Corticosteroids
1. Still widely used — 89% of AOFAS surgeons perform injections (average 13.9/year) [S10, p.571]
2. Short-term pain relief up to 3 months [S10, p.571]
3. Ultrasound guidance reduces recurrence rates [S10, p.571]
4. **Major concern**: 53.7% rate of plantar fascia rupture and fat pad atrophy with repeated injections [S10, p.571]
5. Use judiciously — rupture risk is clinically significant and can be career-threatening

### Botulinum Toxin Type A (BTX-A)
1. One RCT (Elizondo-Rodriguez et al.) compared BTX-A injection to gastrocnemius vs. dexamethasone to plantar fascia in 36 patients
2. BTX-A group showed more rapid AND sustained functional improvement [S10, p.571]
3. Mechanism likely involves reducing muscle tension transmitted to plantar fascia via gastrocnemius-Achilles-plantar fascia kinetic chain

### PRP
1. Most level-I and level-II studies report favorable outcomes of PRP vs. corticosteroids [S10, p.571]
2. **Meta-analysis (Singh et al., 10 studies)**: PRP associated with improved pain AND functional scores at 3 months (p=0.04 and p=0.03); no differences at 1, 6, 12 months [S10, p.571]
3. **Meta-analysis (Yang et al., 9 RCTs)**: Greater long-term (24-week) pain relief with PRP vs. corticosteroids (p=0.03); no short-term differences [S10, p.571]
4. PRP vs. PPP (Malahias et al.): Significant improvement in both groups at 3 and 6 months; no significant difference between modalities [S10, p.571]
5. Large-scale high-quality studies still warranted due to small sample sizes in existing studies

### Amniotic-Derived Products (mDHACM, c-hAM)
1. **Cazzell et al. (2018, n=145)**: 76% VAS decrease in mDHACM vs. 45% in saline at 3 months (p<0.0001); greater FFI-R reduction (p=0.0004) [S10, p.571]
2. **Zelen et al. (2013, n=45)**: AOFAS scores 51.6 (0.5 mL mDHACM) and 53.3 (1.25 mL mDHACM) vs. 12.9 (saline) at 8 weeks (p<0.001) [S10, p.572]
3. **Hanselman et al. (2014)**: Two injections of cryopreserved human amniotic membrane (c-hAM) → greater FHSQ pain score at 18 weeks vs. corticosteroid (p=0.0113) [S10, p.572]
4. Early results are encouraging but insufficient for formal clinical recommendations [S10, p.572]

| Study | n | Follow-up | Favorable for PRP? | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Acosta-Olivo et al. (2016) | 28 | 4 months | No | No difference in VAS, AOFAS, FADI |
| Jain et al. (2015) | 60 | 12 months | Yes | PRP better VISA, AOFAS at 12 months; no difference at 3 or 6 months |
| Mahindra et al. (2016) | 75 | 3 months | Yes | PRP higher AOFAS at 3 months |

## Clinical Recommendation
> High-quality evidence supports the use of PRP injections for plantar fasciitis, with slightly greater efficacy compared to corticosteroids and a presumed lower complications rate (no rupture or atrophy risk). [S10, p.579]

## Common Mistakes
1. Multiple corticosteroid injections → dramatically increases plantar fascia rupture risk (53.7%) and fat pad atrophy
2. Using PRP before adequate conservative management
3. Expecting PRP to produce short-term pain relief equal to corticosteroids — PRP benefits are primarily at 24+ weeks
4. Failing to address the gastrocnemius-Achilles-plantar fascia kinetic chain through stretching
