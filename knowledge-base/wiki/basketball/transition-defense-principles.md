# Transition Defense Principles

## Summary
Outstanding transition defense is the trademark of a great defensive team. Stopping opponents from getting quick fastbreak scores and easy early-offense baskets severely limits their total offensive production. Herb Brown's system assigns each retreating defender a clear priority: cover the goal first, then stop the ball, then pick up the nearest free opponent — not necessarily your personal assignment.

Brown devotes 40–60% of practice time to defense and rebounding, and insists all five players rebound defensively instead of leaking perimeter players early. This prevents second-chance points and keeps proper defensive floor balance. [S1, pp.168-172]

## When to Use
- Every possession after an offensive shot, made basket, or turnover
- When the opponent pushes the pace or plays a zone offensively (forcing mismatch recovery)
- Particularly critical after free throws — teams often run after both makes and misses
- Any dead-ball situation where the opponent can create an advantage before your team sets up

## Key Principles
1. **First defender back retreats to the paint** — cover the goal and build the defense outward from there; never chase the ball laterally while leaving the basket open.
2. **First big man back picks up the first inside offensive player** — regardless of individual assignment; big men must protect the rim together.
3. **All other defenders level back to the line of the ball** — get at least even with the ball before engaging; never trail the play from behind.
4. **Stop the ball** — once the goal is covered, the next priority is to contain and pressure the dribbler to buy time for teammates to recover.
5. **Match up by proximity, not assignment** — each retreating defender picks up the nearest free man closest to the basket; communicate verbally: "I've got 4!"
6. **Leave the perimeter man free last** — if someone must go unguarded while sprinting back, it must be the opponent farthest from the ball and basket on the perimeter.
7. **Set up the defensive triangle** — after the first pass is made in tandem defense, the third man back goes to the weak-side area to form a triangle, covering both wings.
8. **Establish weak-side and ball-side help** — both coverage angles must always be present before any individual contest.
9. **Contest every shot and block out** — all five defenders must contest and rebound; no uncontested looks allowed.
10. **No aimless steals in the backcourt** — flailing at the ball in transition leaves teammates outnumbered and exposed; prioritize positioning over gambling.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PF/C (first big back)**: Sprint to paint, take the first offensive big man coming to the rim regardless of assignment. Cover the goal; build defense outward.
- **PG/SG/SF (retreating perimeter)**: Sprint to get level with the ball. Match up with the nearest free opponent. Communicate all switches aloud. Stop any dribble penetration — the nearest perimeter defender is responsible for picking up penetrators leaking out.
- **Third man back**: While first two defenders engage in tandem, fill the weak-side area of the court to establish the defensive triangle.
- **All five players**: Contest the shot, block out, and rebound defensively. Perimeter players do NOT leak early from rebounds — five players rebound defensively every possession.

## Variations
### Tandem Defense (2-on-1 / 3-on-2 situations)
First two defenders form a tandem: one at the rim, one stopping the ball. Third man fills the weak side. Communicate constantly as the play develops.

### Against Zone Offense Running the Floor
Players are rarely matched up against their own individual opponents when the opponent plays zone and runs. In this case: sprint back, pack the paint, and build the defense out from in front of the goal. Do not attempt to match up — there isn't time. [S1, p.170]

### Defensive Rebounding to Prevent Transition
Rebound with all five players (including perimeter players) to deny second chances. Offensive aggressiveness on the boards also prevents opponents from running, since they must box out rebounders. [S1, p.171]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Perimeter player leaks early on a shot** → Correct: all five players box out and rebound before transition begins.
2. **Defender sprints back to his assigned man who is already covered** → Correct: pick up the nearest free man closest to the basket; never leave someone open to find your original man.
3. **Gambling for steals in the backcourt** → Correct: position first — flailing at the ball leaves teammates further outnumbered.
4. **Big man chases his own assignment instead of the first rim threat** → Correct: first big back takes the first offensive big regardless of assignment; rim coverage is priority one.
5. **Defenders trail the play from behind** → Correct: level with or below the line of the ball before engaging.
