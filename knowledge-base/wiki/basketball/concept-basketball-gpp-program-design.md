# General Physical Preparedness (GPP) for Basketball

## Summary
The GPP (General Physical Preparedness) microcycle is the foundation of any basketball resistance training program. It lasts four weeks and establishes the core stability, technique, and fundamental movement competency necessary before external loading is introduced. For high school athletes, the entire off-season may need to be devoted to GPP — a young athlete who excels on the court will not necessarily demonstrate excellent weight room skill [S11, p.302]. Even bodyweight exercises can and should be modified to match the athlete's current ability.

GPP is an extension of a shorter postseason recovery program. For experienced college and professional athletes, GPP essentially becomes the warm-up protocol; for novice athletes, it is a full training phase requiring deliberate attention.

## When to Use
- First 2-4 weeks of any off-season program
- Any athlete new to resistance training regardless of age
- After injury or extended layoff
- High school athletes: may occupy the full off-season
- Professional rookies: focus on technique and external loading acclimation

## Key Principles
1. **Technique before load** — purposefully refrain from introducing external loads when technique is lacking; mechanical advantage must be established first [S11, p.302]
2. **Bodyweight modifications** — an athlete unable to do a proper floor push-up should first perform from an elevated surface (bench, box, or wall press); this maintains proper positioning without compensation [S11, p.302]
3. **High frequency, low intensity** — repeated exposures to basic movement patterns at high frequency have a profound effect on development; neural adaptations come from repetition, not weight [S11, p.302]
4. **Core stability emphasis** — building enough core stability to support safe loading is the first goal; unstable surfaces, plank variations, and anti-rotation exercises are central [S11, pp.302, 307]
5. **Unilateral movements** — single-leg and single-arm exercises are prioritized to expose bilateral strength deficits common in basketball athletes [S11, p.307]
6. **Controlled tempo** — 3:1:1 to 1:3:1 tempo on all primary lifts to build eccentric strength and body awareness [S11, p.307]
7. **Load range: 53-76% 1RM** — moderate loading with higher rep ranges (8-12) in a 3-4 set structure [S11, p.307]

## Sample GPP Structure (Weeks 1-2)

### Guards — Day 1 (Lower Body Focus) [Table 10.1, S11]
- Front squat (eccentric focus): 4 sets, 8-10 reps, 53%→70-76%, tempo 3:1:1
- One-arm cable row (split stance): 4 sets, 8-10 reps
- Barbell hip thrust hold (5-sec end hold): 4 sets, 8-10 reps
- Isometric pull (rack, split stance): 3 sets, 5-6 reps
- Leg curl (stability ball): 3 sets, 10-12 reps
- RDL with DB: 3 sets, 8-10 reps

### Guards — Day 2 (Upper Body Focus) [Table 10.1, S11]
- Bench press (3-sec isometric hold at bottom): 4 sets, 8-10 reps, 53%→70-76%
- DB split squat (3-sec hold at bottom): 4 sets, 8-10 reps
- Prone Y's, T's, I's (1-5 lb): 4 sets, 8-10 reps
- Lat pulldown: 3 sets, 10-12 reps
- Pallof press (5-sec isometric hold): 2 sets, 5-6 reps

### Guards — Day 3 (Velocity-Based) [Table 10.1, S11]
- Deadlift (concentric only): 4 sets, 6-8 reps, 53%→76-85%
- Push press or jerk: 4 sets, 6-8 reps, tempo 2:X:X
- Bosch hip extension hold: 4 sets, 8-10 reps
- Side plank (5-sec hold): 3 sets, 15-20 reps

### Bigs — Key Differences [Table 10.2, S11]
- Front squat: sit-down-to-box variation (eccentric focus)
- Half-kneeling one-arm cable row (vs. split-stance for guards)
- DB walking lunge (vs. isometric pull for guards)
- Single-leg squat Bulgarian (vs. DB split squat for guards)
- Drop step wood chop, low to high (instead of Pallof press)

## GPP Exercise Selection Rationale
- **Eccentric focus**: builds tissue tolerance and deceleration capacity
- **Single-leg stance**: exposes and corrects bilateral deficits before loading
- **Hip thrust hold**: develops glute activation essential for jumping and defense
- **Isometric holds**: builds tendon and ligament strength not captured by dynamic reps
- **Shoulder/rotator cuff work (Prone Y's, T's, I's)**: injury prevention for shooting and rebounding actions

## Common Mistakes
1. **Loading too quickly** → establish 3+ weeks of technique competency before adding significant external load [S11, p.302]
2. **Skipping unilateral work** → bilateral exercises mask single-leg deficits that cause injury during cutting and jumping [S11, p.307]
3. **Ignoring isometric holds** → the isometric phase builds stability that dynamic-only training cannot provide [S11, p.307]
4. **Using adult programs for youth** → younger athletes need bodyweight-to-low-load progressions with very high technique emphasis [S11, p.302]
