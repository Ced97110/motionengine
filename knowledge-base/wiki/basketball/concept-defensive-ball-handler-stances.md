# Defensive Ball-Handler Stances

## Summary
When guarding a live-ball offensive player (one who has not yet used their dribble), a defender can choose from three distinct initial stances. Each stance is defined by the relationship of the defender's front foot to the attacker's pivot foot and free foot. The core principle is that **an effective defensive stance must eliminate two of the attacker's three options** — drive right, drive left, or shoot — forcing the offense into only one acceptable action that the defense is prepared to stop.

The stance system comes from the youth basketball drills framework [S15, pp.266-270] and is applicable at all levels of play.

## When to Use
- Whenever guarding a ball handler who has not begun or has picked up their dribble
- Situationally: use front-foot-to-pivot-foot when you want to eliminate the shot and the drive away from the pivot foot; use front-foot-to-free-foot (overplay) when funneling a player in a specific direction; use parallel when the attacker has jump-stopped and has not yet established a pivot foot
- Scouting-based: match stance to the attacker's dominant hand and preferred moves

## Key Principles
1. **Attackers have exactly three options**: drive away from pivot foot, drive toward pivot foot, or shoot. The defender's stance must eliminate two of these.
2. **Front-foot-to-pivot-foot stance**: defender's front foot is 3 inches from the attacker's pivot foot, slightly off-center toward the shooting shoulder. Eliminates the jump shot (too close) and the drive away from pivot foot. Only the drive toward the pivot foot is available — and the defender is already on that shoulder.
3. **Front-foot-to-free-foot stance (overplay)**: defender's front foot is placed even with the attacker's free foot, overplayed by half a body toward the free foot. Eliminates the drive to the free-foot side and (if tight coverage) the jump shot. Simplest stance for beginners. Disadvantage: defender must balance between too tight (unstoppable drive if free foot gets outside) and too loose (easy shot).
4. **Parallel stance**: used when the attacker jump-stops with no established pivot foot. Defender meets the stop with a slight overplay, compelling the attacker to declare a pivot foot. Typically overplay right-handed players to their right.
5. **The defender must dictate**: "The defender has dictated to the offense what move is allowed" [S15, p.266]. Reactive defense loses; proactive stance-based defense wins.
6. **Body position universals** (all three stances): crouch with knees bent to near-sitting position, able to touch floor with palms; head directly above center of gravity; body bent slightly at waist with torso forward; arms wide on both sides to discourage lateral movement; head level ("as if carrying a bowl of water on top").
7. **Front foot is the pressure point**: pressure on the foot closest to the ball enables fence-slide interceptions; pressure on the foot closest to the assignment enables gap-closing pivots.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Master all three stances and read which to deploy based on the attacker's dominant hand, tendencies, and the game situation.

## Variations
### Front-Foot-to-Pivot-Foot (Drill #129)
Front foot placed beside the attacker's pivot foot, 3 inches away and 2-3 feet directly across from it. Defender is in a pre-retreat-step position — a jab step doesn't require a reaction. A jab-step crossover is answered with a swing step. A reverse pivot is answered with a swing-step-then-reposition to the new pivot foot. See [[drill-front-foot-to-pivot-foot]].

### Front-Foot-to-Free-Foot (Drill #130)
Front foot placed beside attacker's free foot, overplayed half a body to that side. Jab step in overplay direction doesn't require reaction if attacker can't clear the overplay. Jab-step crossover: slight slide step + retreat step, first step coincides with attacker's first. Reverse pivot: defender needs only a half-step slide. See [[drill-front-foot-to-free-foot]].

### Parallel Stance
Used after an attacker's jump stop before pivot foot is established. Overplay toward the dominant hand to force a pivot foot declaration.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Defender's front foot against the attacker's free foot without overplay** → attacker direct-drives to basket unstoppably; correct by overplaying half a body toward the free foot side
2. **Overplaying the pivot-foot side** in a front-foot-to-free-foot attempt → opens an unstoppable direct drive; never do this
3. **Too loose coverage** in front-foot-to-free-foot → attacker shoots uncontested; maintain tight coverage
4. **Head bobbing on slides** → maintain a level head, always; loss of head stability = loss of reaction speed
5. **Watching only the ball or only the man** (help-side) → must see both with peripheral vision simultaneously
