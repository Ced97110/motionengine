# Triangle Offense Philosophy

## Summary
The triangle offense — also called the triple-post offense or sideline triangle offense — is a comprehensive offensive system developed by Tex Winter and popularized by Phil Jackson with the pro team (6 championships) and team (5 championships). It is not a series of set plays but a **philosophy**: a consistent framework of rules and concepts that guides every offensive possession. The offense is "a sideline triangle on one side of the court and a two-man game on the other side, in which the offensive options are dictated by the positioning and reactions of the defenders." [S7, pp.89-96]

Jackson emphasizes that "highly structured, non-spontaneous basketball does not win championships." The triangle gives players a framework within which they read and react, eliminating the need for the coach to call a specific play or isolation on every possession. It is adaptable to any personnel, requires no specialized position assignments, and works at all levels of competition.

## When to Use
- As a complete half-court offensive system for any team with well-rounded players
- When you want to reduce dependence on a single point guard to initiate every play
- When playing against high-pressure defenses that try to trap the point guard
- When you need a system that releases players to read and react rather than execute scripted plays

## Key Principles

### The Seven Principles of the Sound Offense [S7, pp.90-91]
1. **Penetration.** Players must penetrate the defense. The best way is the fast break — basketball is a full-court game from baseline to baseline.
2. **Spacing.** Players must space themselves to make it most difficult to defend, trap, and help. Recommended distances: high school 12-15 ft, college 15-18 ft, pro 15-20 ft. Proper spacing exposes individual defensive vulnerabilities AND ensures an open player whenever the defense tries to trap.
3. **Ball and player movements.** Players and ball must move with purpose. Effective off-the-ball movement is underappreciated — there is only one ball and five players, meaning each player will have the ball less than 20% of possession time.
4. **Options for the ball handler.** The more options a smart player has to attack a defender, the more successful he will be. All five teammates must be moving to maximize the ball handler's choices.
5. **Offensive rebounding and defensive balance.** On all shots, players must go strong for the rebound while retaining court awareness to prevent the opponent's fast break.
6. **Versatile positioning.** The offense must allow any player to fill any spot on the court, regardless of traditional position. All positions are interchangeable.
7. **Use individual talents.** The offense must allow the team to exploit the skill sets of its best players — without sacrificing team play.

### Additional Core Principles
- **Two-guard offense.** Reject sole dependence on a point guard for all ball-handling decisions; a two-guard approach distributes the responsibilities and prevents defensive overload on one player.
- **Rules-based, not play-based.** The offense is run "by where the ball is positioned on the court and by how the defense is moving" — not by called plays.
- **Complete players.** The system runs better when players are well-rounded in movement, ball handling, screening, and shooting. "Basketball played at its best is a reflexive sport."
- **Team before individual.** Players must "submit their personal ambitions to the best interests of the group."

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions:** Interchangeable — any player can fill any spot. The offense removes traditional positional constraints.
- **PG/Ball Handler:** Initiates action; reads the defense and makes decisions within the framework; does NOT make all decisions alone.
- **Wings:** Must space properly (15-20 ft apart) and move with purpose off the ball; must be capable shooters, screeners, and drivers.
- **Post (C):** Masters the "line of deployment" — shaping up on the post so the forward can pass to the open side when the defender overplays.
- **Forwards:** Fill multiple roles — corner, wing, post, weakside — based on ball position and defensive reaction.

## Variations
### Triangle Offense vs. Isolated Star System
The primary misconception is that the triangle is designed around a gifted player. In reality, "what the triangle really does is help players who aren't so gifted contribute to a team's success at the offensive end." Elite stars have thrived in the system but would excel in any system. The triangle empowers the other players.

### Adaptation by Personnel
The triangle set is adjustable to the personnel each season without altering the essence of the offense. The only necessary adjustment is tailoring the series of options based on individual talents.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Players hold the ball too long** → Every player must move the ball within 3 seconds of receiving it; each extra second makes the player less effective.
2. **Off-ball players stand** → Effective off-the-ball movement is more important than most players think; 80% of the time players are away from the ball.
3. **Relying on one ball handler** → A single-point-guard orientation can be destroyed by defensive pressure; the two-guard approach distributes responsibilities.
4. **Position-specific thinking** → Players must be willing to fill any spot, not retreat to their traditional position.
