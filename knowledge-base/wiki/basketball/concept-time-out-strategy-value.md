# Time-Out Strategy: When They Have Maximum Value

## Summary
The value of a time-out is not fixed — it depends entirely on the game situation. The key insight is that time-outs (and the act of *having* them available) are most valuable when the odds of winning can change the most. That occurs at the end of close games and early in games when one team is opening a significant lead. [S16, pp.230-231]

Oliver illustrates this with four case studies showing how the same action (calling a time-out) has wildly different values depending on score and time remaining. Dean Smith's legendary time-out management in the 1993 UNC-Michigan NCAA Final — including forcing Michigan to use a time-out mid-game, contributing to Chris Webber's infamous technical — is presented as a masterclass in time-out strategy.

## When to Use
- **High value**: End of close games where the next possession could change the outcome
- **High value**: Early in a game when a significant lead (15+ points) is developing — could prevent a 10-0 run from becoming a game-changer
- **Low value**: Large leads late in games (winning by 22 with 3 minutes to go)
- **Low value**: Early in game with tied score and long time remaining (individual possession has minimal win-probability impact)
- **Teaching contexts**: At youth/developmental levels, using time-outs to teach is valuable regardless of game impact

## Key Principles
1. **The core principle**: Having time-outs available is most valuable when win probability can swing most dramatically — not necessarily when you are in the most immediate distress [S16, p.230]
2. **Tied game, last possession**: If a player is falling out of bounds with the ball and the game is tied with 10 seconds left — call the time-out. The possession has enormous win-probability value.
3. **Large lead, last seconds**: If winning by 12 with 10 seconds left — the time-out has near-zero value. The team will win regardless.
4. **Opponent's run, medium time**: If up 12 with 3 minutes left when the opponent cuts it to 2 points in 40 seconds — having a time-out is now extremely valuable (sub in ball handlers, foul shooters, install a stall offense) [S16, p.230]
5. **Large lead, opponent's run**: If up 22 when an 10-0 run makes it 12 with 40 seconds left — the time-out has relatively low value; you are still likely to win. Save it for a larger swing.
6. **Forcing opponents to use time-outs**: Pressuring an inbounds pass at the right moment can burn a valuable opponent time-out — more effective early when they still have many possessions left where that time-out could have mattered [S16, p.231]
7. **Dean Smith principle**: Save time-outs for end-of-game close situations. Smith consistently saved time-outs even while opponents made mid-game runs — a strategy that made observers nervous but was mathematically sound [S16, p.231]

## Game Situation Examples

| Situation | Time-Out Value |
|---|---|
| Player falling OOB, winning by 12, 10 sec left | Nil — will win anyway |
| Player falling OOB, tied, 10 sec left | Very high — possession is game-deciding |
| Up 12, opponent goes on 10-0 run, 40 sec left | High — need to stop momentum |
| Up 22, opponent goes on 10-0 run, 40 sec left | Low — still likely to win |
| Mid-game, tied, opponent about to get inbounds | Moderate — force them to burn one |

## Common Mistakes
1. **Calling time-outs reactively during runs** → This is sometimes justified, but if the lead is large, the statistical impact is small. Save it for when possession win-probability is highest.
2. **Not forcing opponent time-out usage early** → Surprise pressure on routine inbound passes in a close mid-game situation can burn an opponent's future late-game time-out [S16, p.231]
3. **Using time-outs at lower levels like a pro** → At developmental levels, using a time-out to teach a lesson is valuable even if game impact is minimal — context matters

## The 1993 UNC-Michigan NCAA Final — Case Study
With 15:16 left in the second half in a close game, Dean Smith called for a surprise double-team on Michigan's routine inbounds pass, forcing a Michigan time-out. Later, down by 2 with ~10 seconds left, Michigan had no time-outs left. Chris Webber called a time-out his team didn't have — a technical foul that changed the game's outcome. Smith's mid-game pressure, informed by scouting (Michigan's tendency to take inbounds passes for granted), directly set up this decisive moment. [S16, p.231]
