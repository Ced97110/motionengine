# Gluteus Maximus

## Summary
The gluteus maximus is the largest and most powerful muscle in the human body. It is the primary driver of hip extension and horizontal propulsion — the muscular engine behind every jump, sprint stride, drive past a defender, and defensive slide. Basketball Anatomy names it verbatim as the primary muscle trained by the hip thrust: "Primary muscles: gluteus maximus, hamstrings (semitendinosus, semimembranosus, biceps femoris), adductor magnus" [S2, pp.18-19].

When the glute max is chronically inhibited (often by tight hip flexors — see [[concept-anatomy-hip-flexor-complex]]), vertical jump height drops, first-step quickness degrades, and stabilizing force at the hip shifts to secondary muscles that are more prone to strain. [S2]

## When to Use
This anatomy matters to coaching decisions involving:
- First-step acceleration and driving past defenders (horizontal propulsion)
- Vertical jump — every take-off and landing
- Defensive slide and recovery — the glute drives lateral explosiveness
- Rebounding — boxing out requires glute engagement to hold ground against contact
- Post-play footwork — sealing, drop-stepping, and power finishes

## Key Principles
1. **Largest muscle in the body** — the glute max has the greatest force-production capacity of any single muscle; training it directly lifts every explosive basketball action. [S2]
2. **Horizontal and vertical propulsion** — the glute max extends the hip in both planes; different drills emphasize each (hip thrust for horizontal; squat for vertical). [S2]
3. **Reciprocal inhibition with hip flexors** — chronically tight anterior chain inhibits glute activation. Address hip flexor mobility alongside glute strengthening. See [[concept-anatomy-hip-flexor-complex]].
4. **Strength before power** — per S2's foundational periodization principle, build glute mass with heavy loading before converting to jump- and sprint-specific power work. [S2]
5. **ACL context** — glute strength (including the adjacent glute medius) is central to ACL injury prevention, particularly for female athletes; a weak posterior chain lets the knee collapse inward under landing force (dynamic valgus). [S2]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG / SG**: first-step and drive-and-kick acceleration; sprint transition
- **SF**: slashing drives, transition sprints, rebounding contact
- **PF**: boxing out, rebounding, post-seal — glute engagement under contact
- **C**: post-seal power, up-and-under pivots, rim-running — highest total glute load per possession

## Variations
### Hip Thrust (the canonical training movement)
Motion's hip thrust exercise names the glute max as the primary muscle trained [S2, pp.18-19]. See [[exercise-hip-thrust]] for execution details.

### Sprint and Jump Application
Plyometric and sprint-based drills convert raw glute strength into basketball-transferable power. The five jumping skills (see [[concept-five-jumping-skills]]) all rely on glute drive. [S2]

### Rehabilitation Context
Post-injury glute activation protocols (single-leg stance, band lateral walk, side-lying clam) are central to S2's rehabilitation sequences. See [[drill-band-lateral-walk]]. [S2]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Quadriceps-dominant training only** (squats with hip flexor bias) → underdeveloped posterior chain; hamstring overuse and strain risk.
2. **Stretching glutes without strengthening** → a lengthened-and-weak glute cannot hold its role under load.
3. **Ignoring the glute medius (the stabilizer)** → focusing only on max strength while neglecting lateral stabilization creates knee-valgus risk.
4. **Hip thrust form errors** → posterior pelvic tilt at lockout maximizes glute engagement; anterior tilt transfers load to the lower back.
