# Pregnancy, Postpartum, and Basketball

## Summary
As more women participate in basketball at all levels, pregnancy during an athletic career has become more common. The evidence base now supports exercise — including resistance training — during uncomplicated pregnancies. However, contact sports including basketball carry collision and trauma risks that must be weighed individually. Postpartum return to sport is highly individual and not governed by a single arbitrary timeline [S10, Ch.66].

## When to Use
- Managing a pregnant basketball player's training program
- Designing a postpartum return-to-sport protocol
- Counseling breastfeeding athletes on training safety
- Educating coaching staff on physiologic changes affecting performance

## Key Principles

### During Pregnancy
1. **Contact sports carry collision risk**: ACOG recommends avoiding contact sports (including basketball) during pregnancy due to the risk of abdominal and fetal trauma [S10, Ch.66].
2. **Noncontact conditioning is safe**: Pregnant female basketball players may continue noncontact aerobic conditioning and resistance training.
3. **Resistance training is recommended**: No evidence that weightlifting increases miscarriage risk. Weight training during pregnancy is supported [S10, Ch.66].
4. **Physiologic changes begin early**: Increased heart rate (rest and activity), increased cardiac output, increased minute ventilation, and increased oxygen consumption begin in the first few weeks — which may cause some women to experience a decline in exercise tolerance.
5. **Highly fit athletes may improve VO₂ max postpartum**: A small number of studies suggest well-conditioned athletes who maintain fitness during pregnancy may experience a post-pregnancy increase in VO₂ max [S10, Ch.66].
6. **Ligamentous laxity increases**: Due to relaxin and elevated estrogen — avoid ballistic activities and unnecessary joint loading.
7. **Pelvic floor training is recommended**: Especially for basketball players experiencing increased pelvic floor load during jumps and high-impact activities — reduces urinary incontinence and pelvic floor complications [S10, Ch.66].
8. **Center of gravity shifts**: Weight gain and center-of-gravity changes increase fall risk.
9. **Caloric intake monitoring**: Elite athletes continuing to train should be monitored closely to ensure adequate caloric intake.

### Postpartum
1. **Return to sport is individualized**: Recovery time varies enormously — time to return to prepregnancy fitness ranges from 2 to 24 months (mean: 11 months in fit soldiers) [S10, Ch.66].
2. **Early heavy activity carries risk**: Early return to heavy physical activity may increase risk of urinary incontinence and pelvic prolapse in the general population.
3. **Pelvic floor recovery first**: Athletes whose delivery involved significant pelvic floor stress (large baby, operative vaginal delivery) should minimize intra-abdominal pressure activities for the first months while retraining the pelvic floor.
4. **Diastasis recti awareness**: Occurs in some pregnancies and may persist postpartum, causing core weakness, hip pain, and back pain. Insufficient evidence currently exists for optimal rehab.
5. **Cesarean birth requires obstetric clearance**: Abdominal fascia regains less than 60% of tensile strength in the first 6 weeks post-surgery. Gradual progression is essential [S10, Ch.66].
6. **General rule**: Return to exercise gradually, progressing time, frequency, and intensity as tolerated.

### Breastfeeding
1. **Intense exercise does not impair milk production**: As long as caloric needs are met and hydration is maintained [S10, Ch.66].
2. **High-volume aerobic activity may improve breast milk**: Some evidence suggests greater quantity and quality.
3. **BMD caution**: Breastfeeding may contribute to increased bone resorption and decreased BMD, hypothetically increasing stress fracture risk. This BMD loss is typically restored after cessation of breastfeeding [S10, Ch.66].

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Communicate with medical staff and coaching staff about pregnancy status early. Follow individualized medical guidance rather than team-standard return-to-play timelines.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Assuming pregnancy = no training** → Noncontact aerobic conditioning and resistance training are safe and recommended during uncomplicated pregnancies.
2. **Using a 6-week postpartum cutoff** → The postpartum period is highly variable; some athletes need 2+ years to return to prepregnancy fitness.
3. **Neglecting pelvic floor training** → Basketball's high-impact jumping load makes pelvic floor training critical during and after pregnancy.
4. **Ignoring breastfeeding BMD effects** → Coaches and medical staff should be aware that breastfeeding athletes may have temporarily reduced BMD.
