# college-era full-court press (Run-and-Jump Full-Court)

## Summary
The college-era full-court press is a man-to-man full-court press incorporating run-and-jump defensive tactics. It was developed in part from notes Ed Krinsky obtained from the originating head coach at UNC and refined over decades of coaching. Krinsky and Herb Brown used it whenever a **forward scored** (field goal or free throw). The press's core mechanism is forcing the dribbler into a trap, then rotating the trapper's defender to cover the free man in the direction where the trap help came from. [S1, pp.183, 182]

## When to Use
- After a forward scores — personnel-keyed trigger for variety and surprise
- When you want man-to-man pressure with run-and-jump trapping principles
- When the nearest defender has a short enough distance to effectively jump the dribbler without committing to a full trap
- When you want to disrupt the opponent's ball handler without fully committing to a zone press

## Key Principles
1. **Force the dribbler into a trap.** Every defender works to funnel the ball handler toward help. [S1, p.183]
2. **Run-and-jump** when the nearest defender is close enough to get the ball out of the handler's hands — not too far to be effective. [S1, p.177]
3. **Rotate after the ball escapes the trap.** The player who was guarding the dribbler rotates **in the direction of where the trapper came from** — covering the free man in that direction. [S1, p.183]
4. **Fake the trap when you can't complete it.** If the defender cannot reach the dribbler in time, he fakes and feints to keep the offense off balance. [S1, p.181]
5. **Free-man rule.** After a trap and a pass, take the free man in the direction of the help — not your originally assigned player. [S1, pp.181-182]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Primary ball pressurer — initiates the run-and-jump; forces ball handler to turn or pick up dribble
- **SG**: Nearest help defender; executes the jump on the dribbler; rotates into the former ball defender's spot after the trap breaks
- **SF/PF**: Level with the ball; available to trap or to cut off passing outlets; communicate every switch
- **C**: Responsible for the deepest free man; protects the basket against lob or chip passes

## Variations
### Without Trapping (Pure Run-and-Jump)
Defenders run and jump — changing who is guarding the ball handler — without fully committing to a trap. The ball handler is forced into split-second decisions. Used when personnel don't support consistent trapping. [S1, p.177]

### Run-and-Trap on the Baseline
Some coaches prefer to run-and-jump on the perimeter but commit to full traps when the ball reaches the baseline — where the sideline and baseline together create a natural third defender. [S1, p.177]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Trapper's original defender not rotating** → when the trap is beaten, the trapper's defender must rotate to cover the free man in the direction of the help
2. **Fake trap too timid** → the fake must be convincing enough to make the dribbler hesitate and make a poor decision
3. **Players running back to covered assignments instead of free men** → always take the nearest free man in the direction of the help
