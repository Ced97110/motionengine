# Screening Basics — the source coach (pro Coaches Playbook)

## Summary
the source coach (Motion Offense assistant, worked with Stockton/Malone and Williams/Boozer) presents a complete screening system organized around roles: screener, screen receiver, and passer. The system emphasizes that screens are valuable only when combined with reading the defense and adjusting. Knowing what to do with the screen is more important than the screen itself. [S7, pp.51-54]

## When to Use
- Any half-court set featuring on-ball or off-ball screens
- When attacking man-to-man or switching defenses
- As the foundational framework before installing specific plays

## Key Principles
1. **Screen angle is paramount** — Present the screen to the defender at a 90-degree angle to give him the widest space to cover when chasing the screened player.
2. **Screener must see the ball handler** to best time the screen.
3. **Establish a solid, wide, low stance** at the proper angle for the ball handler to use.
4. **After setting: pivot to face the ball and present a target** for the ball handler.
5. **Screen receiver: don't move too quickly before the screen is set** — this tips the defense and ruins the timing.
6. **Don't cut the screen at full speed** — reading the defense, using a change of pace, and going the opposite way is often better than blowing by at full speed.
7. **Screener goes opposite the screened player** as a general rule — if the screened player goes baseline, screener goes high.

## Player Responsibilities
- **Screener**: See teammate, establish stance, angle the screen at 90°, pivot to face ball after setting, read and move opposite the cutter.
- **Screen Receiver**: See the screener, set up the defender, use the screen's angle to get an open shot or drive, read the defense.
- **Passer/Ball Handler**: See the full court, prioritize the screener, read defensive reactions, make crisp passes away from the defender, use lob pass if necessary.

## Variations

### Screened Player Reads (Four Options)

**Curl:** If the defender trails the screened player, he circles (curls) around the screener and cuts to the basket. Cut as close as possible to the screener — brush his shoulder — to prevent the defender from getting through. [S7, p.53]

**Curl and Slip:** If the screened player curls and the defender trails, AND the screener's defender leaves to help, the screener cuts (slips) to the basket to receive the ball. [S7, p.53]

**Bump and Pop:** If, during the curl, the screened player sees his defender going over the screen to beat him, and the screener adapts with a drop step, the screened player pushes himself off the screener (bumps) and hops/steps away (pops). This elude move uses the screener's back or hip as a launching point. [S7, p.54]

**Bump and Slip:** If, after the bump-and-pop, the screener's defender hedges/shows out to cover the now-open bumped player, the screener quickly slips to the basket to receive a pass from the screened player. [S7, p.54]

### Passer Becoming a Passer
After the screen, if the ball handler is picked up by help or by a recovering defender, an open shot or drive may not be available. At that point the ball handler transitions to a passer role and looks for the screener's movement or other teammates.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Screen set at wrong angle** → Defender has an easy path; use 90-degree angle to maximize space for the screened player.
2. **Screener does not pivot to face ball after setting** → Loses the roll/pop option and the passing connection with the ball handler.
3. **Screen receiver cuts too quickly before screen is set** → Tips the defense; be patient and set up the defender first.
4. **Screened player always cuts the same way** → Defense anticipates; must vary curl, pop, and change of pace.
5. **Ball handler doesn't look for the screener after the screen** → Screener almost always has a pass option; ignoring them is a missed opportunity.
