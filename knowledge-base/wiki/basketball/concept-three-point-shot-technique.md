# Three-Point Shot Technique

## Summary
The three-point shot has become a vital part of modern basketball — a 33% three-point percentage is the mathematical equivalent of a 50% two-point percentage. However, players must earn the right to shoot threes by first mastering the midrange game. The principles of shooting the three-pointer are the same as those for the midrange jump shot; the primary variable is distance, which demands more deliberate footwork, greater leg involvement, and even sharper balance.

Mastering the three requires competence in three distinct footwork situations: straight-on catch-and-shoot, shooting on the move (from either direction), and the pull-up three off the dribble. Each situation demands specific footwork adjustments while maintaining consistent shooting mechanics.

## When to Use
- After establishing a reliable midrange game
- Inside-out passes (post feeds kicking to corner/wing shooters)
- Skip passes across the court
- Transition pull-up when a layup opportunity isn't available
- Drive-and-kick situations (shooter receives the kick-out)
- Off screens (cross-screen, flare screen) from the three-point arc
- Late shot-clock situations requiring any available three

## Key Principles
1. **Master the two before the three.** A player whose form changes at the three-point line is not ready to shoot threes. Same form = ready; different form = build strength first.
2. **Establish readiness 3–5 feet behind the arc.** Space out pre-catch so the one-two step generates full momentum into the arc and the ball.
3. **Leg power is essential from downtown.** The biggest, strongest muscles are in the legs. The one-two step is the mechanism for using them. "Step into the three — your legs do the work."
4. **Straight-on (right-handed):** Non-shooting (left) foot lands first; ball arrives as the left foot hits; right (shooting) foot completes the base. Step slightly more pronounced than midrange.
5. **Heel to toe on every step.** Transfers weight forward. Flat-footed landing slows the shot and prevents proper pivot.
6. **On the move — moving toward shooting hand (right-handed, L→R):** Step aggressively with the left foot, opening toward the target (heel-to-toe). Pivot on left foot, bring right foot around into the shot. Do not overstride.
7. **On the move — moving away from shooting hand (right-handed, R→L):** Right foot steps in with a heel-to-toe open step toward the target and becomes the pivot. Left foot must "stub" into a balanced base. Without the stub, the left side leads and forces a body twist, pushing the shot offline.
8. **"Loading the gun" when going weak-side.** When dribbling with the weak hand (or moving in the weak direction), let the ball come up into the weak hand and bring it across to the shooting hand. Keep the shooting elbow tucked on the shot line.
9. **Hard last dribble on the pull-up three.** The harder the bounce, the quicker and higher the shot — critical for getting adequate height on the three-pointer.
10. **Freeze the follow-through until the ball passes through the net.** Dropping the follow-through indicates the shooter wasn't fully locked in on swishing the shot. If hands move, feet move — balance was compromised.
11. **Shoot, stay, then react.** The longer the shot, the longer the rebound. Staying gives the shooter a chance to track the board or be an outlet; being in place also supports transition defense.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Most likely to shoot the three off the dribble (pull-up in transition or shot clock pressure).
- **SG/SF**: Primary catch-and-shoot three-point threats; master on-the-move footwork for off-screen threes.
- **PF/C**: Developing a straight-on or corner catch-and-shoot three is a powerful modern weapon; do not attempt until form is identical inside and outside the arc.

## Variations
### Straight-On Catch-and-Shoot
Classic one-two step into the arc. Most common three — inside-out passes, skip passes. [S5, pp.72-74]

### Pull-Up Three Off the Dribble
Same one-two step, picking up the ball going strong-hand or loading the gun going weak-hand. Typically seen at end of fast breaks with a numbers advantage but no clear layup path. [S5, pp.73-74]

### On-the-Move (Catch)
Moving laterally then squaring to shoot. Left-to-right uses the standard non-shooting foot pivot. Right-to-left requires the toe stub to prevent the body from twisting. Most common in cross-screen situations designed by coaches for good shooters. [S5, pp.75-77]

### On-the-Move (Dribble)
Same footwork as on-the-move catch, but ball comes up off the last dribble into the shot pocket. Open the hip and shoulder of the inside foot toward the basket for peripheral vision of potential defenders. [S5, pp.77-78]

### Shuffle-Step Three
Lateral shuffle (defensive-slide motion) along the arc into the shot. Most difficult because there is no forward momentum. Required in shot-clock situations. Practice with faking elements (jab step, shot fake before elevating). [S5, p.79]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Shooting threes before mastering the midrange** → Forces altered form to compensate for lack of strength; poor mechanics become ingrained.
2. **Stepping with the shooting foot first (straight-on)** → No momentum or power generated into the shot.
3. **Failing to space out behind the arc** → No room for the one-two step; shooter is already at the line when the ball arrives.
4. **Altering elbow bend or release mechanics to compensate for distance** → Inconsistency; "be a lifter, not a slinger or a pusher." Maintain the same release regardless of distance.
5. **Not stubbing the toe when moving right-to-left (right-handed)** → Left side leads; body twists during shot; miss left or right.
6. **Soft last dribble on pull-up three** → Reduces jump height and shot quickness; last dribble should always be hard.
7. **Dropping the follow-through** → Dancing feet, lack of focus; freeze until the ball passes through the net.
8. **Becoming a one-dimensional three-point shooter** → If all you practice is the three, the three is all you can do; develop a complete scoring arsenal.
