# Chronic Ankle Instability in Basketball

## Summary
Chronic lateral ankle instability (CAI) is among the most common orthopedic problems in basketball players. When conservative management (physical therapy, proprioceptive training, peroneal strengthening) fails, surgical intervention is warranted. The most common surgical technique is the Brostrom procedure, which restores the anatomic lateral ligamentous complex (ATFL and CFL) while preserving normal kinematics. Return to play follows a structured, individualized rehabilitation protocol incorporating sport-specific basketball movements.

Injury prevention is equally critical: the most important risk factor for ankle sprain is a previous ankle sprain, and the only intervention proven by Cochrane review to significantly reduce recurrent sprains is a semi-rigid external ankle orthosis [S10, pp.462-464].

## When to Use
- Surgical management is reserved for patients with little to no improvement after prolonged physical therapy and strengthening
- Athletes who cannot return to pre-injury function despite full conservative care
- Players returning from CAI surgery need a structured, phased return-to-play program
- External bracing should be considered for all players returning after any ankle sprain

## Key Principles

### Surgical Management
1. **Brostrom Procedure** is the gold standard — repairs ATFL and CFL in their normal anatomic locations; 26-year follow-up data shows excellent results [S10, p.462]
2. **Gould Modification** augments the Brostrom repair by incorporating the inferior extensor retinaculum secured to the fibula — reinforces the ATFL repair, limits inversion, stabilizes the subtalar joint, and increases repair strength by ~50% [S10, p.462]
3. **Suture anchors** in the Gould-modified Brostrom are effective in returning high-demand athletes to pre-injury function [S10, p.463]
4. **Anatomic lateral ligamentous reconstruction** (using autograft or allograft tendon) is indicated when tissue quality is inadequate — outcomes equivalent to anatomic repair [S10, p.463]
5. **Suture tape augmentation** improves isokinetic peroneal strength and postural control but has no demonstrated effect on proprioception [S10, p.463]

### Return to Sport Timeline (Post-Brostrom)
- **2 months**: Return to personal training
- **3 months**: Return to team training
- **4 months**: Return to play (83.3% of elite athletes achieve pre-injury level)
- **8 months**: All athletes resume participation
- Institutional protocol: 6 weeks protected weight-bearing + 8–12 weeks rehabilitation [S10, pp.463-464]

### Return-to-Play Criteria
- No pain or swelling with therapeutic activity
- Restored ankle stability, strength, balance, and postural control
- Successful completion of sport-specific activities (e.g., cutting exercises, agility drills, double jumps in basketball)
- Multidisciplinary decision: physician + physical therapist + team training staff + player [S10, p.463]

### Injury Prevention Principles
1. **Most important risk factor**: Previous ankle sprain (fivefold increase in re-injury risk) [S10, p.464]
2. **Most effective prevention**: Semi-rigid ankle orthosis (air-cast brace) — the only Cochrane-supported intervention to significantly reduce recurrent sprains [S10, p.464]
3. **Athletic taping**: Does NOT reliably prevent recurrent sprains; loses inversion resistance with prolonged activity; expensive and requires skilled application [S10, p.464]
4. **Proprioception + peroneal strength**: Fully rehabilitating the initial injury by focusing on these two qualities is the most predictable way to prevent recurrent instability; balance/wobble board training is effective [S10, p.464]
5. **High-top shoes**: No proven difference in ankle injury rates vs. low-top shoes in prospective study [S10, p.464]
6. **Air cells in shoe heels**: Associated with 4.3× higher injury rate [S10, p.464]
7. **Pre-exercise stretching**: Players who did not stretch before a game were 2.6× more likely to injure an ankle [S10, p.464]
8. **NBA prehabilitation programs**: Emphasize overall fitness, balance, and landing with a wide-based stance [S10, p.464]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Maintain single-leg balance/proprioception training year-round; wear semi-rigid ankle brace when returning from sprain; report ankle instability symptoms early
- **Guards (PG, SG)**: Higher cutting and first-step demands increase re-injury risk; aggressive proprioception and peroneal strengthening essential
- **Bigs (PF, C)**: Higher jump-landing forces; landing with wide base and proper ankle positioning is critical

## Common Mistakes
1. **Returning to play too early after a sprain** → complete full rehabilitation (proprioception + peroneal strength) before return; one sprain dramatically increases the next
2. **Relying on taping alone** → taping loses effectiveness during activity; semi-rigid brace is evidence-based
3. **Skipping proprioceptive training** → balance board training is one of the most predictable re-injury prevention strategies
4. **Surgery without adequate conservative trial** → surgery reserved for failed non-operative management
5. **Poor graft tensioning in reconstruction** → "appropriate tensioning of the graft is paramount" to success [S10, p.463]

## Variations
### Conservative Management
Full rehabilitation program: PRICE → range-of-motion → proprioceptive training → peroneal strengthening → sport-specific training → return to play.

### Suture Tape Augmentation
Can augment native tissue or a Brostrom repair with a suture tape device; improves peroneal strength and postural control without added surgical complexity.
