# Team First, Players Second: The Analytical Framework

## Summary
A foundational principle of basketball analysis: understand the *team* first and its individual players second. This is especially important when the goal is winning (as opposed to player development). When evaluating why a team over- or under-performed, the first questions must be at the team level — was it the offense or defense? Which of the four factors changed? Only once those questions are answered does it make sense to ask which players drove those changes.

This principle was demonstrated in the analysis of the 2002 Toronto Raptors, a 42-40 team that had been expected to compete for the Eastern Conference Finals. By first establishing that the team's problem was *offensive* (not defensive — defense actually improved), the analyst could focus the entire individual-player investigation on offensive contributions and declines rather than spreading attention across all aspects of the game [S16, pp.318-321].

## When to Use
- End-of-season review: before looking at any individual stat lines, chart team offensive/defensive ratings vs. league average
- Mid-season slump analysis: identify whether the losing streak is offensive or defensive in nature before making lineup changes
- Roster evaluation: understanding team strengths first prevents overvaluing a player who fits one gap while ignoring more critical needs
- Player development priorities: when the team has a clear offensive deficiency, that defines which player skills to develop

## Key Principles
1. **Team wins and loses, not individuals** — the frame of reference must be team performance first [S16, p.318].
2. **Identify offense vs. defense** — use offensive and defensive ratings compared to league average. A two-point swing in either direction is significant.
3. **Apply the four offensive factors** — FG%, OREB%, TOV%, FTA/FGA — to identify the *mechanism* of offensive change before looking at players.
4. **Use time-series data** — moving five-game average ratings and cumulative net-point charts reveal *when* the team changed, pointing to the most impactful players or events.
5. **Statistical significance check** — when a player misses games, compare team performance with vs. without them using significance testing before concluding they were the cause of a slump.
6. **Look for multiple causes** — the Raptors' 2002 season had both an overall offensive decline AND a mid-season collapse/recovery; these required separate explanations [S16, p.320].

## The Diagnostic Sequence
1. Compare this season's win-loss record to expectations and prior season
2. Chart offensive and defensive ratings vs. league average
3. Identify which side of the ball declined
4. Apply the four offensive (or defensive) factors to find the mechanism
5. Use moving-average time-series charts to find when changes occurred
6. Only now examine individual player statistics for the players most likely responsible
7. Significance-test with-vs.-without data for key players who missed games

## Common Mistakes
1. **Starting with star players** → Easy to blame the obvious star for a team's problems; the team-first framework often reveals the real culprit is elsewhere.
2. **Ignoring the defense improved** → When offense declines but defense improves, trade-offs in roster construction or playing style may explain it — not failure.
3. **Confusing absence with performance decline** → Vince Carter's team actually had similar ratings with and without him; the two mid-season periods were offset by other factors [S16, p.322].
