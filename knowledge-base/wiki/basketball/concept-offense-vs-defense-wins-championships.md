# Offense vs. Defense — What Wins Championships?

## Summary
The conventional coaching wisdom "Defense Wins Championships" is **not supported by historical data.** Analysis of 387 NBA playoff series from 1974–2002 shows that the team with the better **offensive** rating won more often than the team with the better **defensive** rating. This holds under both standard ratings and [[concept-uncorrelated-ratings]] that account for garbage time [S16, pp.141-142].

Of the NBA's 50 greatest players as named by an NBA blue-ribbon panel in 1996, only three had career scoring averages below 15 points per game, and only Bill Russell, Dave DeBusschere, and (arguably) Nate Thurmond built their reputations primarily on defense [S16, p.141].

## When to Use
- Program-building decisions: where to allocate development time and resources
- Roster construction: valuing offensive vs. defensive specialists
- Challenging received wisdom about defensive identity teams

## Key Principles
1. **Offense wins more playoff series than defense.** 387 series (1974–2002): better offensive team won 62%, better defensive team won only 57% [S16, p.142].
2. **Uncorrelated ratings strengthen the result.** When adjusting for garbage-time slack, offensive advantage predicts wins at 63%, defensive advantage at 59% — the offensive edge grows slightly [S16, p.142].
3. **Anecdote misleads.** Fans remember great defensive moments in championships (1997 Bulls, 2001 Lakers), but the same championships were decided by elite offensive performances — Jordan's 38-point flu game, Kobe's put-back — not defensive stops [S16, p.142].
4. **Recruiting confirms it.** Players advance to higher levels because they can score, not because they guard. The player who can "break down any defense" gets recruited over the one who "sticks to his man like glue" [S16, p.141].
5. **"Defense Wins Championships" is a partial truth at best.** Both offense and defense matter; defense is just a *weaker* predictor. A team that is dominant offensively should not sacrifice offense to build defense [S16, pp.141-142].

## Data Summary
| Metric | Better Defense Wins | Better Offense Wins |
|---|---|---|
| Standard ratings (387 series) | 57% | **62%** |
| Uncorrelated ratings | 59% | **63%** |

## Important Nuance
This analysis covers **regular-season** offensive and defensive ratings predicting **playoff** series winners. It does not study whether teams specifically *improve* their defense in the playoffs. It is possible that teams that enter the playoffs with average offense but improve defensively do well — but the data show that the teams with *already-better* regular-season offense win more often.

## Common Mistakes
1. **"Defense wins championships" as coaching philosophy justifying neglect of offense** → correction: invest heavily in offensive skill development; defense matters but offense matters more
2. **Survivorship bias** — we remember championship teams' great defensive moments because championships are memorable → correction: control for this by counting all 387 playoff series, not just finals
3. **Ignoring uncorrelated adjustment** → correction: even after accounting for garbage time, offense still predicts better than defense
