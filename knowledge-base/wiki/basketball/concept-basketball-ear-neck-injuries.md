# Basketball Ear and Neck Injuries

## Summary
Ear injuries in basketball, while not a major source of injury, do occur and include auricular hematomas, ear lacerations, and occasional tympanic membrane perforations. Neck injuries range from common muscular strains to rare but catastrophic spinal cord injuries (SCI). Head/neck injuries in NCAA basketball comprise 13.9% of injuries across all NCAA sports. Upper extremity injuries in NCAA basketball are less common (40%) relative to lower extremity injuries (60%). Medical personnel must be prepared to recognize, manage, and appropriately refer all ear and neck injuries [S10, pp.219-223].

## When to Use (Relevance)
- Sideline management of ear trauma (blunt blow to the pinna)
- Neck pain evaluation following collision or fall
- Emergency preparedness for catastrophic spinal cord injury on the court

## Key Ear Injuries

### 1. Auricular Hematoma
- Blunt trauma → blood accumulates in subperichondrial space of the auricle
- Perichondrium supplies cartilage nutrients → hematoma → cartilage necrosis → fibrocartilage replacement → **"cauliflower ear"**
- **Management**: drain by scalpel incision or needle drainage; suture bolster dressing × 1 week to prevent reaccumulation
- Complications uncommon; rarely requires specialty referral [S10, p.219]

### 2. Ear Lacerations
- Simple lacerations: repaired by sports physician
- Rule out: middle ear injuries, skull/facial fractures
- Refer to otolaryngology/plastic surgery if: extensive skin or cartilage injury
- **Important**: ear lacerations with cartilage involvement require antibiotics in addition to repair (higher infection risk than facial lacerations) [S10, pp.219-220]

### 3. Tympanic Membrane (TM) Perforation
- Rare in basketball; mechanism = direct blow to the ear
- Diagnosis: direct visualization with otoscope; most common rupture site = pars tensa
- Small perforations: analgesics + keep ear dry; heal in 2–6 weeks
- Large perforations (≥25% of TM area) or those complicated by infection/hearing loss → otolaryngology referral
- Antibiotic ear drops only for concurrent infection [S10, p.220]

## Key Neck Injuries

### 1. Neck Strains
- Incidence unknown (likely underreported)
- Mechanism: acute muscular stretch or compensatory mechanism
- Symptoms: unilateral pain lateral to cervical spinous processes, no distal radiation, no upper extremity weakness (if these present → consider cervical spine injury)
- Exam findings: increased hypertonicity, tenderness to palpation, unilateral ROM limitation
- Full neurologic exam required; imaging generally not warranted if NEXUS criteria negative:
  - No posterior cervical midline tenderness
  - No painful distracting injury
  - No focal neurologic deficit
  - No intoxication
  - No altered mental status [S10, p.220]
- **Treatment**: ice × 24–48 h → motion restriction to non-tender ROM → anti-inflammatory medications → gradual ROM restoration → strengthening of cervical paraspinals → full return to play [S10, pp.220-221]

### 2. Cervical Spine Injuries (SCI)
- Life-threatening but rare in basketball
- ~9% of all US spinal cord injuries are sports-related [S10, p.221]

## Emergency Action Plan (EAP) for Suspected SCI

### Pre-Event Preparation
- Develop written EAP for catastrophic events (cardiac, pulmonary, SCI)
- All medical personnel must know their role, including spine boarding technique
- Detailed facility map: entrances/exits, emergency vehicle routes, AED locations [S10, p.221]

### On-Court Management Protocol
1. Initiate EAP
2. Apply BLS (Basic Life Support): airway, breathing, circulation
3. Proceed to ACLS (Advanced Cardiac Life Support) or ATLS (Advanced Trauma Life Support) as indicated
4. Thorough neurologic examination
5. **Immobilization**: 6-man lift preferred over log roll for spinal immobilization
6. Transport to nearest ED for advanced imaging
7. Equipment removal (e.g., face protection): discuss approach in EAP pre-event [S10, p.221]

### Return to Play After SCI
- Case-dependent on extent of spinal cord involvement and sequelae
- "Stingers" (brachial plexopathy) — well-established protocols; not frequently seen in basketball
- True SCI: return to unrestricted play criteria not well established; team approach and shared decision-making recommended [S10, p.221]

## Key Principles
1. Auricular hematoma must be drained promptly to prevent cauliflower ear — do not delay
2. Cartilage ear lacerations require antibiotic coverage beyond what typical facial lacerations need
3. Neck strain: rule out spinal cord or nerve root involvement before conservative treatment
4. **Any suspected SCI**: initiate EAP, BLS, immobilize, transport — do not move the athlete without proper protocol
5. EAPs should be rehearsed regularly before the season begins

## Common Mistakes
1. **Missing auricular hematoma** or delaying drainage → permanent cauliflower ear deformity
2. **Not prescribing antibiotics for cartilage ear lacerations** → increased infection risk
3. **Returning a player with unresolved neck strain** before full neurologic clearance
4. **Inadequate EAP preparation** → chaotic response to rare but catastrophic SCI events
5. **Using a log roll when a 6-man lift is available** → increased spinal displacement risk
