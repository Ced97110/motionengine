# Basketball Analytics Philosophy (Dean Oliver)

## Summary
Dean Oliver's *Basketball on Paper* establishes a foundational philosophy for understanding basketball through statistics: no single number can capture a player's or team's quality, and any analysis that attempts to reduce everything to one "magic" ranking number ends discussions rather than opening insight [S16, pp.6-7]. The goal of all analysis should be oriented toward helping a *team win* — not optimizing for fantasy leagues, individual awards, or popularity contests.

Oliver draws heavily on tools from mathematics, statistics, and engineering (significance testing, standard deviations, statistical correlations, conservation equations) to build models that account for the reality of teamwork, which is the central feature distinguishing basketball from more independently-measurable sports like baseball [S16, pp.4-7].

## When to Use
- When evaluating players for contracts, rotations, or playing time decisions
- When trying to determine whether a team performs better or worse with specific players on the court
- When designing statistical tracking systems for practice or game analysis
- When interpreting box-score statistics — understanding their limits and what they leave out

## Key Principles
1. **Multiple metrics over one magic number** — "Reducing quality to one number has a tendency to end a discussion, rather than open up a world of insight" [S16, p.6]. Use many measures: points produced, defensive stops, rebounding, assist-to-turnover ratio, shooting percentage, etc.
2. **Winning-team orientation** — All analysis must be built around what helps a *team* win, in the short term or long term. Individual stats matter only insofar as they contribute to winning [S16, p.3].
3. **Teamwork must be accounted for** — Fantasy league statistics ignore teamwork, but real basketball is fundamentally about teamwork. Formulas for individual points produced and individual defensive stops must explicitly incorporate the contributions of teammates [S16, pp.5-6].
4. **Context dependence** — Awards like MVP and Rookie of the Year are popularity contests with quality as a contextual constraint, not pure performance rankings. Players should be evaluated against themselves over time and relative to their situation [S16, p.6].
5. **Facts over opinion** — Analysis backed by data is more credible than pure opinion, even if opinion can be valuable. The combination of facts + analysis + contextual argument is the ideal form of basketball reasoning [S16, p.4].
6. **Statistical significance matters** — Significance testing can determine whether a team genuinely plays better or worse with/without certain players, rather than relying on small sample noise [S16, p.6].
7. **Conservation thinking** — Points scored and wins created are conserved quantities that can be modeled like fluid dynamics: one player's production comes at some cost to others' opportunities [S16, p.7].

## Variations
### Fantasy League Application
The formulas for individual points produced and individual defensive stops can be applied to fantasy league rosters, though they were not designed for that purpose. More valuably, the framework can be used to rewrite fantasy league rules to better reflect the reality of teamwork [S16, p.5].

### Contract Negotiation Application
Understanding multiple dimensions of player value gives a negotiator more arguments and counterarguments than an opponent who relies on one statistic. Signing a mediocre player to a top-dollar contract restricts roster flexibility and can force a coach to give that player inappropriate status [S16, pp.4-5].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using one number to rank players** → Use multiple metrics that capture different dimensions of contribution (scoring, rebounding, defense, playmaking, efficiency).
2. **Treating MVP votes as pure performance rankings** → They are votes with quality as a constraint; evaluating relative quality within a season is a valid and normal part of the process [S16, p.6].
3. **Ignoring teamwork in individual statistics** → Raw box-score stats don't account for teammates' contributions. Formulas that incorporate team context are more accurate [S16, p.5].
4. **Confusing fantasy league optimization with real winning** → Fantasy leagues don't capture teamwork, defense, or the interactions between players that determine real games [S16, p.5].
