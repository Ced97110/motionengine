# Single-Leg Bounds

## Objective
Increase hip extension and flexion strength, improve ankle-muscle stiffness, and enhance leg power and stride length through continuous single-leg hopping with sprint-quality mechanics.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Open flat surface — field, track, or court
- No equipment required
- Measure a set distance (e.g., 30 meters) for progress tracking

## Execution
1. Get a **slow running start** and begin hopping continuously on a **single leg**.
2. Focus on leg recovery mechanics mirroring sprint mechanics:
   - **Heel to gluteus** on the recovery swing
   - **High knee** drive on each bound
   - **Pawing action** as the leg prepares to contact the ground again
3. Cover a set number of hops and measure the distance covered.
4. In subsequent sets, attempt to cover the same number of hops but with **greater distance** (fewer hops for the same distance = progress).
5. Complete sets on both legs.

## Coaching Points
- "Sprint mechanics, not hop mechanics" — the leg recovery cycle should look like the athlete is sprinting, not bouncing.
- Heel-to-glute recovery is critical — a lazy recovery (low heel) shortens stride and reduces power.
- Arm action coordinates with each bound — opposite arm drives forward with each bound.
- Progress is measurable: record the distance covered in X hops across different sessions.
- Unilateral loading exposes and corrects leg strength asymmetries critical for injury prevention.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: 6 hops per leg, slow start, focus on recovery mechanics
2. **Intermediate**: 10 hops per leg, moderate speed, measure distance each set
3. **Advanced**: 15–20 hops per leg, competition format — maximize distance per hop count
