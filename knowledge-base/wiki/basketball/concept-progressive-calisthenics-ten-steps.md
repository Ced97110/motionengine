# The Ten Steps: Progressive Calisthenics Progression System

## Summary
The Ten Steps is the organizational framework for every movement chain in the Explosive Six system. Each of the six movement families is divided into ten progressively harder exercises — Step 1 is the most accessible, Step 10 (the "Master Step") is the elite target. The system is built on a foundational insight: athletes build speed and power by *mastering steps they can do well*, not by advancing to steps they cannot [S9, pp.16-18].

This principle has direct implications for basketball skill development as well. The same logic that applies to learning a back flip — don't rush the progression — applies to learning a post move, a shooting form correction, or a defensive footwork pattern. Coaches can apply the Ten Steps framework to any skill development sequence.

## When to Use
- When designing progressive athletic development programs for any explosive quality
- When explaining to athletes why they should not skip ahead in a learning sequence
- When evaluating athlete readiness to advance to a harder exercise or technique
- As a model for structuring any basketball skill progression from simple to complex

## Key Principles
1. **Step 1 is the floor, not the goal**: Even experienced athletes benefit from starting at Step 1 — it builds body wisdom, joint health, and training momentum [S9, p.17].
2. **A chain is a series of progressively harder techniques**: Each step prepares the athlete for the next. The skills compound, not just the difficulty [S9, p.16].
3. **Master and milk each step**: Spending quality time at each step — not just doing it once — is what builds the speed and power. "You become a better athlete by taking the time to practice the progressions you *can* do well" [S9, p.12].
4. **Advancing demonstrates, not builds, quality**: Moving up a step is a test of qualities already developed, not the mechanism of developing them [S9, p.18].
5. **Regressions are valid tools**: Easier versions of a technique (regressions) are always available when athletes hit a wall or need to rebuild a pattern [S9, p.11].
6. **Ten steps is the optimal number**: Fewer than ten misses key developmental steps; more than ten fragments the focus unnecessarily [S9, p.17].

## The Step Structure

```
Step 1-2:   Beginner
Step 3-5:   Intermediate (building)
Step 5-7:   Intermediate (consolidating)
Step 8-9:   Advanced
Step 10:    Master Step (elite target)
```

The difficulty increases *gradually* — not in sudden jumps — so each step is achievable from the previous one with dedicated practice [S9, p.16].

| Chain | Master Step |
|---|---|
| Jumps | Suicide Jump |
| Power Pushups | Superman Pushup |
| Kip-Ups | No-Hands Kip-Up |
| Forward Rotations | Front Flip |
| Backward Rotations | Back Flip |
| Explosive Bar Work | Muscle-Up |

## Backward Rotation Progression Example
The back flip is the Master Step of the backward rotation chain. A deconditioned person attempting it directly would almost certainly fail. The progression chain:
1. Bridge mobility exercises (mobilize and strengthen spine, shoulders, limbs)
2. Backward rolls (basic)
3. Backward rolls using hands
4. Bridge kick-overs
5. Monkey flip variations (learn backward rotation side-on)
6. Back handspring (jump and rotate over on hands)
7. Transitional exercises
8. Back flip [S9, pp.11-12]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Trying the Master Step first** → Start at Step 1. Even partial competence at each step is more valuable than a single failed attempt at Step 10 [S9, p.12].
2. **Moving to the next step as soon as you can do it once** → "At the heart of this method is learning to love each progression." Master the step before advancing [S9, p.12].
3. **Skipping steps in the middle** → Most athletes who struggle to advance failed to milk earlier steps. Return and spend more time there [S9, p.18].
4. **Thinking the Master Step is the end** → There is no end point in bodyweight arts. Master Steps are goals to guide training, not finish lines [S9, p.20].

## Basketball Coaching Applications
The Ten Steps framework translates directly to basketball skill development:
- A player learning the post game should master the catch and chin (Step 1) before drop steps (Step 5) before advanced combination moves (Step 10).
- A defender learning pick-and-roll defense starts with basic positioning (Step 1) before learning trap rotations (Step 7+).
- A shooter correcting mechanics should fix grip/elbow (Step 1-2) before adjusting release or arc (Step 6-8).

This mirrors the [[concept-whole-part-whole-teaching]] method — isolate the fundamental step, master it, then integrate.
