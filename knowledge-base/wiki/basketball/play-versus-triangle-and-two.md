# Versus Triangle-and-Two (Half-Court Set)

## Overview
The triangle-and-two invites non-shooting post players (O3, O4, O5) to shoot while denying guards (O1, O2) the ball. This set counters it by having O1 and O2 dive to the low blocks (occupying the baseline defenders X4 and X5 and/or screening them), while the three unguarded players (O3, O4, O5) occupy the open shooting zones: baseline corners, wings, and top of key. Patient ball movement among the three creates 2-on-1 advantages against any single zone defender.

## Formation
- **Defenders**: X4 and X5 cover baseline in tandem; X3 covers middle and top; X1 and X2 man-to-man on guards
- **O1 and O2**: Guards — dive to their same-side low-block positions (not attempting to score)
- **O3**: Has ball — attacks from perimeter; occupies wings, corners, or top with O4 and O5
- **O4**: Perimeter — occupies top of key or wing
- **O5**: Perimeter — occupies corner or baseline
- **Open zones**: Baseline corners, wings, top of the key (all unguarded)

## Phases

### Phase 1: Initial Ball Movement and Dive
- **O1 passes to O5** (wing or high post)
- **O1 and O2 dive to their same-side low blocks** simultaneously
  - This creates congestion and forces X4 and X5 (who must follow/react)
  - O1 and O2 can **screen the baseline defenders** (X4/X5) or **cross-screen each other** to get open

### Phase 2: Identifying and Occupying Open Zones
With X1 and X2 following O1 and O2 to the blocks, the **three unguarded players (O3, O4, O5)** occupy:
- **Baseline corners** (left and right)
- **Wings** (left and right)
- **Top of the key**

### Phase 3: 2-on-1 Attack
**O3 (with ball)** creates two-on-one advantages:
- **Right side**: O3 + O5 (corner) vs. X4 (baseline defender)
- **Top**: O3 + O4 (top) vs. X3 (middle defender)
- **Left side**: Same as right but X5 is the baseline defender

**Ball movement with good passes** between O3, O4, and O5, plus **"taking up slack toward the basket"** establishes shooting range for open shots.

## Key Coaching Points
- **O1 and O2 diving to the blocks is the heart of the play** — they occupy zone defenders and create 2-on-1 opportunities for the three perimeter players
- **"Putting the right shooter in the right spot"** — O3, O4, O5 should be the team's actual perimeter shooters (the defense is inviting them to shoot; make them pay)
- **Patience** — 2-on-1 advantages develop through ball movement, not quick shots
- **Attack the offensive boards** — O1 and O2 at the blocks are in prime rebounding position; second-chance points are critical against this defense
- The triangle-and-two is a **mind game** — the counter-attack is discipline and putting non-guarded players in open shooting zones
- **2003 NCAA Tournament example**: Auburn held Syracuse to 1-point margin using this defense — the lesson: prepare for it before tournament time [S13, p.255]

## Common Mistakes
1. O1 or O2 trying to score from the block (playing into the defense's plan) → their job is to occupy and screen, not score
2. O3/O4/O5 crowding the same zone → spread to separate zones to force X3 to cover multiple areas
3. Quick shots without moving the ball → defense doesn't have to shift; hold and find the 2-on-1
4. Ignoring offensive rebounding → massive wasted advantage with O1/O2 at the blocks
