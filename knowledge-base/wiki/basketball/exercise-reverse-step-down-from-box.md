# Reverse Step-Down From Box

## Objective
Develop single-leg eccentric strength, proprioception, and overall body control for cutting, deceleration, layup mechanics, and stability during contact.

## Setup
- **Players:** 1
- **Location:** weight room or court area
- **Equipment:** secure box 12–24 inches (30–60 cm) high; height depends on strength level
- **Safety:** Maintain neuromuscular control of the knees — do not let the knees buckle in or out. [S2, p.22]

## Execution
1. Stand with both feet firmly on top of a secure 12–24-inch box.
2. Keeping the left leg on the box, slowly step back off the box with the right leg, foot plantar-flexed (pointed) toward the floor. Slowly descend toward the floor by bending at the left hip and knee, keeping the trunk as erect as possible while maintaining the pointed foot toward the floor.
3. Touch — don't bounce — off the floor with the toes of the right foot before changing direction. The instant the toes touch, extend both hip and knee to return to the straight-leg starting position.
4. Without resting, continue for the prescribed repetitions on the same leg.
5. After completing, repeat with the right leg remaining on the box and the left leg stepping back. [S2, pp.22-23]

## Coaching Points
- **"Touch, don't bounce"** — the brief ground contact should be controlled; momentum from a bounce removes the eccentric training stimulus.
- **"Knee over toe, not inside"** — lateral knee collapse is the primary safety concern; cue "knee out" if necessary.
- The trunk must stay erect — forward lean indicates insufficient hip and quad strength; regress to the [[exercise-reverse-lunge]].
- Progress box height only when proper technique and knee control are consistent across all repetitions.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner:** [[exercise-reverse-lunge]] on flat ground — establishes the movement pattern without the height challenge.
2. **Intermediate:** Reverse step-down from a 12-inch box.
3. **Advanced:** Reverse step-down from a 24-inch box; add light dumbbells for increased load.

## Concepts Taught
- [[leg-strength-for-basketball]] — single-leg strength is the most sport-specific form of lower-body strength in basketball
- [[basketball-acceleration-and-deceleration]] — eccentric deceleration on one leg mirrors cutting mechanics

## Basketball Application
This single-leg exercise assists in both horizontal and vertical body propulsion, essential for running past and outjumping an opponent as in taking a layup. It also assists with deceleration from a single-leg position during cutting activities, proprioception, and overall body control — important during violent contact that may occur during play. [S2, pp.22-23]

**Primary muscles:** quadriceps (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis, vastus intermedius), gluteus maximus, gluteus medius

**Secondary muscles:** hamstrings (semitendinosus, semimembranosus, biceps femoris), gluteus minimus, adductor magnus
