# Basketball Pre-Participation Evaluation (PPE)

## Summary
The pre-participation evaluation (PPE) is a comprehensive history and physical exam performed annually for all basketball athletes. It is the standard of care despite limited evidence for reducing overall morbidity and mortality. Its primary value is detecting conditions that predispose athletes to injury or sudden cardiac death (SCD), with secondary value in general health counseling and system entry for adolescents. Up to 75–88% of medical or orthopedic conditions can be detected by history and physical exam alone [S10, pp.91-101].

Basketball players face elevated SCD risk: 6.25 per 100,000 for male African American college athletes, rising to **19 per 100,000 for NCAA basketball players specifically** [S10, p.93]. Basketball has become a contact sport with increasing traumatic injury rates, making the PPE particularly important for this population.

## When to Use
- **Ideally**: 6–8 weeks before the start of the season (allows time to evaluate, treat, and rehabilitate identified problems)
- **At minimum**: annually for all players; at each new level of competition
- NCAA: comprehensive PPE on entry, updated history/physical annually
- AHA recommendation: cardiac screening every 2 years at new competition levels, annual blood pressure readings

## Key Principles
1. **Primary goal = do not withhold competitors, but promote long-term health and safety** [S10, p.92]
2. **History is the foundation**: 75–88% of conditions identified by history + physical alone — medications, supplements, past surgeries, family history must all be documented
3. **Cardiac screening is highest priority**: SCD is the leading cause of exercise-related death; HCM and congenital coronary artery anomalies are the most common US causes [S10, p.87]
4. **EKG debate**: AHA recommends against routine EKG in asymptomatic athletes (poor sensitivity, high false-positive rate); however, professional basketball leagues support routine EKG; EKG sensitivity for SCD conditions = 87.5% vs. 18.8% for AHA 14-point history alone [S10, pp.94-95]
5. **Musculoskeletal history targets lower extremity**: Two-thirds of all basketball injuries occur in the lower extremity; focus on prior sprains, fractures, stress reactions, and ligament tears [S10, p.96]
6. **Concussion eligibility is symptom-based, not count-based**: Do not disqualify based on number of prior concussions alone — base decisions on current symptoms [S10, p.99]
7. **FMS has limited PPE value**: Functional Movement Screening has some rehabilitation utility but limited evidence as a PPE injury prediction tool [S10, p.96]
8. **Sickle cell screening is NCAA-mandated**: All incoming NCAA athletes must have a one-time sickle cell screen; exercise-induced death from sickle trait has decreased since implementation [S10, p.99]

## PPE Sections

### 1. Cardiac History and Exam
**AHA 14-Point Screening** (personal and family history):
- Personal: elevated BP, exertional dyspnea/fatigue, exertional chest pain, prior heart murmur, unexplained syncope/near-syncope with exercise
- Family: disability from heart disease in close relative <50, premature sudden death <50, known HCM, dilated cardiomyopathy, long QT, Marfan, arrhythmias [S10, p.94]

**Physical Exam Components**:
- Brachial artery BP (both arms, sitting); recheck if >160/100 adults or >95th percentile in children after 15-min rest
- Femoral pulse palpation (rule out aortic coarctation)
- Heart auscultation standing + supine (Valsalva) — grade III–VI systolic, diastolic, or valsalva-louder murmurs → echocardiogram
- Marfan syndrome screening: pectus excavatum, arachnodactyly, arm span > height, scoliosis, Steinberg sign, wrist sign

**SCD Red Flags Requiring Cardiologist Referral**: Suspected HCM → ECG, echocardiography, and/or cardiac MRI before return to play

### 2. Musculoskeletal History and Exam
- Prior sprains, strains, fractures, dislocations, tears
- Length of time away from sport (severity indicator)
- Stress fractures: tibia, tarsal, navicular most common in basketball (repetitive jumping + landing) [S10, p.98]
- Lower crossed syndrome assessment (Table 8.2): overactive hip flexors + lumbar extensors → weak abdomen + glutes — common in basketball athletes [S10, p.97]
- General screen: inspection, palpation, ROM, strength, neurovascular assessment; spine via ROM + provocative testing
- Individual joint special tests for known or suspected abnormalities

### 3. Neurological History and Exam
- Prior head/neck injuries, concussions, headaches, radicular symptoms, neuropraxia, seizures
- Concussion assessment: total number, frequency, severity, recovery time, most recent event
- Baseline concussion testing: cognitive assessment, balance evaluation, symptom score, concussion history (NCAA recommends one-time pre-participation assessment)
- VOMS (vestibular ocular motor screening) for contact sport athletes — not mandatory but useful for return-to-play
- Conventional neuro exam: cranial nerves, UE/LE dermatomes, gross motor, deep tendon reflexes

### 4. Medications and Supplements
- All prescribed, OTC, and supplements must be documented
- TUE (therapeutic use exemptions) must be on file
- Governing agency banned substance lists (NBA, WADA, FIBA) reviewed annually [S10, p.93]

## Basketball-Specific Common Injuries (PPE Focus)
| Injury | Notes |
|---|---|
| Lateral ankle sprain | Most common; conservative Tx, 7–10 day recovery |
| Jumper's knee (patellar tendinopathy) | Overuse; jumps 50+ times per game |
| Osgood-Schlatter disease | Young players; patellar tendon apophysitis |
| ACL tear | Noncontact (squatting, jumping, shuffling); women at higher risk due to wider Q-angle |
| Stress fractures | Tibia, tarsal, navicular; repetitive floor impact |

## PPE Methodology Options
| Format | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|
| Office-based (single provider) | Continuity of care, privacy, record-keeping | Limited time, higher cost, less sport expertise |
| Team-based (multi-station) | Time efficiency, cost-effective, athlete-staff communication | Requires closed rooms for privacy per AAP/ACSM |

## Athletes with Disabilities
- PPE format does not change significantly; address unique features of each disability
- Down syndrome: cervical spine radiographs of atlanto-axial joint recommended before participation
- Functional exam is individualized per athlete and pathology
- Prosthetics: prosthetist may augment evaluation [S10, pp.99-100]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Relying on AHA history alone to clear basketball players** → EKG sensitivity (87.5%) far exceeds AHA 14-point history (18.8%) for SCD conditions; consider EKG for this population [S10, p.95]
2. **Disqualifying based on concussion count** → Eligibility should be symptom-based, not count-based
3. **Performing PPE within days of season start** → 6–8 weeks lead time is needed to evaluate and rehabilitate identified issues
4. **Skipping musculoskeletal functional testing** → Single-leg squat, drop-box testing, and Y-balance have some validity for lower extremity injury prediction in basketball players
