# Six Individual Offensive Skills Framework

## Summary
Every coaching staff should organize their individual offensive skill development program around six core categories. This framework ensures that no area of offensive fundamentals is neglected and that daily practice systematically covers the full range of skills players need to succeed. The framework comes from years of observation across high school, college, and pro coaching practices.

These six categories are not isolated — they interact. A shooting drill becomes better when it incorporates passing, pivoting, cutting, and rebounding. Transition is the bridge that connects offense and defense, and must be practiced as integral to both. [S6, pp.1-2]

## When to Use
- When designing a season-long individual skill development program
- When auditing existing practice plans for coverage gaps
- When building a pre-practice routine (see [[drill-daily-pre-practice-routine-s6]])

## Key Principles
1. **Basic fundamentals station drills** — foundational technique work: stance, footwork, hand placement, body control
2. **Shooting drills** — interior and perimeter shooting; must be game-realistic with movement, pressure, and time constraints
3. **Dribbling drills** — hesitation, crossover, through-legs, behind-back, and advance dribbles; practiced in lines and game-like situations
4. **Pivoting, passing, and catching drills** — footwork before and after receiving the ball; the pivot is the foundation of all offensive actions
5. **Offensive rebounding drills** — overcoming box-outs, reading ball flight, anticipating where missed shots fall
6. **Transition drills** — defense-to-offense AND offense-to-defense transition; must be an integral part of both offensive and defensive practice

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Every player must be proficient in all six skill areas, not just the skills traditionally associated with their position.
- **Post players**: Post-up moves practiced individually daily, with and without defensive opposition.
- **Perimeter players**: Shooting in shooting position at the catch; catching techniques for quick release.

## Common Mistakes
1. Neglecting transition in offensive practice → transition must be explicitly built into every practice plan, not assumed.
2. Only shooting with stationary players → require cutters and passers to simulate game-realistic movement.
3. Skipping pivoting/passing/catching drills in favor of more shooting → these are the prerequisite skills for shooting to work under pressure.
4. Post-up moves only practiced by post players → all players need post footwork for offensive rebounding and interior scoring.
