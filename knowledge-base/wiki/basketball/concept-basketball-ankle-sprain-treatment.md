# Basketball Ankle Sprain Treatment and Return to Play

## Summary
Ankle sprains are the most common basketball injury, but with proper management most players return quickly. The primary treatment principle — supported by NATA 2013 guidelines — is **functional rehabilitation rather than immobilization** for Grade I and II sprains. Grade III sprains require short-term immobilization before functional training begins. Return-to-play requires the injured limb to perform at ≥80% of the uninjured limb on functional testing.

Achilles tendon ruptures carry a much more guarded prognosis, with up to 39% of basketball players failing to return to play. [S10, pp.451-453]

## When to Use
- Immediately after diagnosis of ankle sprain at any level
- During return-to-play decision-making after ankle injury
- When planning surgical vs. non-surgical management for high-grade injuries

## Key Principles

### Initial Evaluation
1. Thorough history — mechanism and foot position at time of injury formulate differential diagnosis [S10, p.450]
2. Lateral ankle sprain mechanism: foot in **plantarflexed and inverted** position [S10, p.450]
3. Syndesmotic (high ankle) injury mechanism: foot in **dorsiflexed and everted** position [S10, p.450]
4. Assess overall limb alignment (genu valgum, cavovarus) [S10, p.450]
5. Evaluate shoe wear: midsole flexibility, collar height, outsole traction [S10, p.450]
6. Ottawa Ankle Rules: plain radiographs if unable to bear weight OR focal bony tenderness [S10, p.450]
7. MRI for chronic ankle pain despite conservative treatment; to rule out Lisfranc, syndesmosis, osteochondral lesions, or bony stress injuries [S10, p.450]
8. Weight-bearing CT scan to characterize Lisfranc injuries and aid in surgical planning [S10, p.450]
9. Special tests (ankle drawer, syndesmosis stress) are difficult in the acute stage — delay 5 days [S10, p.450]

### Treatment by Grade (NATA 2013 Guidelines)

#### Grade I and II Ankle Sprains
- **Functional rehabilitation** preferred over immobilization [S10, p.451]
- Mainstays: Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation (R.I.C.E.) [S10, p.451]
- Balance training and neuromuscular training throughout rehabilitation [S10, p.451]
- **56% of NBA players sustaining an ankle sprain did not miss any games**; median games missed = 2 [S10, p.452]

#### Grade III Ankle Sprains
- Immobilize ≥10 days in a boot or below-knee cast [S10, p.451]
- Begin functional training once pain and edema improve [S10, p.451]
- Return-to-play requires injured limb functional performance ≥80% of uninjured limb [S10, p.452]

### Return-to-Play Timelines by Type (NBA Data)
- **Lateral sprain**: median 2 games missed, median 8 days [S10, p.452]
- **Medial sprain**: median 3 games missed, median 8 days [S10, p.452]
- **High (syndesmotic) sprain**: median 5 games missed, median 24 days [S10, p.452]
- European professional players: mean 84 days for ankle sprains [S10, p.452]
- High school: <7 days (51.7%), 7–21 days (33.9%), >22 days (10.5%) [S10, p.452]

### Achilles Tendinopathy (Non-Rupture)
- Conservative first: rest, ice, NSAIDs, stretching, heel lifts [S10, p.452]
- Flatfoot deformity: medial longitudinal arch support [S10, p.452]
- Night splints for equinus [S10, p.452]
- **Avoid corticosteroid injections** — risk of Achilles rupture [S10, p.452]

### Achilles Tendon Rupture
- High-grade partial or complete tears: **surgical repair** indicated [S10, p.452]
- Surgical approaches: minimally invasive, percutaneous, or open [S10, p.452]
- Postoperative: traditional non-weight-bearing 4–6 weeks OR accelerated early weight-bearing protocol [S10, p.452]
- Return-to-play outcomes: **70.8% return-to-play rate**, mean 10.5 months, with lower efficiency ratings [S10, p.452]
- At 1 and 2 years post-op, NBA players performed worse (74.8%, 77.7%) than MLB players (87.8%, 112.4%) [S10, p.452]

### Stress Fractures
- Low-risk (calcaneus, lateral malleolus, cuneiforms, cuboid): nonoperative management [S10, p.446]
- High-risk locations (proximal fifth metatarsal, navicular, sesamoid, talus, medial malleolus): often require surgical intervention [S10, p.446]
- NBA players (76 stress fractures): mean 25.1 games missed; 38% managed surgically; **30% did not return to play** [S10, p.453]
- Fifth metatarsal (Jones fractures, zones II and III): intramedullary screw fixation + BMAC; radiographic healing 7.5 weeks; mean return to play 9.8 weeks [S10, p.453]

### Lisfranc Injuries
- Medial column instability (most common basketball pattern): mean return to play **7.5 months** [S10, p.452]
- Primary arthrodesis: 97% patient satisfaction at 5.2 years; 44.8% returned to high-impact activity [S10, p.452]
- Open reduction internal fixation: 94% returned to recreational sport; 66% returned to previous level at 2.9 years [S10, p.452]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Prolonged immobilization** for Grade I/II sprains → leads to strength and proprioceptive deficits that increase recurrence risk [S10, p.461]
2. **Premature return to play** before ≥80% functional symmetry — especially for high-ankle sprains [S10, p.452]
3. **Corticosteroid injections for Achilles tendinopathy** → risk of rupture [S10, p.452]
4. **Treating all ankle sprains as equivalent** — medial and high sprains require longer timelines than lateral sprains [S10, p.452]
