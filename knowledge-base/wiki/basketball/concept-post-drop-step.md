# Post Drop Step — Back-to-Basket Moves

## Summary
The drop step is the foundational back-to-basket post move and a "lost art" according to an iconic post player. Every other back-to-basket move builds on the drop step. The post reads defender position (high side, low side, behind), starts the drop step while the ball is still in the air, and exploits the rim's protection to complete the finish. a legendary post player of the an elite team of that era was the greatest master of the drop step. [S7, pp.42-46]

## When to Use
- Any time the post player catches the ball at the low or mid post with his back to the basket
- When the defender is positioned on the high side or low side (sealed in place by the catch)
- At the high post when immediately attacking before the defense sets

## Key Principles
1. Start the drop step while the ball is **still in the air** — but do not release contact with the defender (releasing allows a steal). [S7, p.42]
2. The post must set himself **above the block** to have room to drop-step toward baseline or middle. [S7, p.42]
3. After the drop step, the body should be **parallel to the backboard** — this uses the rim's protection to block the weak-side helper. [S7, p.42]
4. The buttocks block out the defender's body throughout the entire drop step. [S7, p.44]
5. After a power-shot with a defender behind, **lean torso and arms forward** — not vertical — to prevent a block from behind. [S7, p.44]

## Drop Step to the Baseline

### Defender on High Side — Shoot with One or No Dribble
- Post has sealed defender on the high side with arm bent at 45°, leaning on defender's chest or overplaying elbow.
- While ball is in the air: make a **long drop step with the baseline foot**, toes pointed toward baseline, touching heel then toes.
- "Hook" the back foot around the defender's back foot, turn front foot to baseline, rotate body parallel to the board.
- Finish with a dunk or power shot. The buttocks block out the defender. [S7, p.44]
- If distance requires it: one dribble between the legs (protects ball, avoids defensive recovery), bounce very hard, bend legs, jump with full strength. [S7, p.44]

### Defender Behind — Fake Middle, Power Dribble, Shoot
- Defender is behind the post (between him and the basket). Post holds ball with elbows outside, checks inside lane over his head.
- Bring ball to the **outside shoulder** (midcourt side) — defender reads middle fake and slides in that direction.
- As defender reacts: drop step to the baseline, hook the defender's rear foot, point the drop-step toe toward the basket.
- Simultaneously make a strong **power dribble with both hands** and shoot a power shot or dunk. [S7, p.44]

### Defender Recovers to Baseline — Countermoves
If the defender recovers to the baseline after the drop step:
1. **Jump hook**: Step into the lane with the same drop-step foot. One dribble inside the body. Toes point toward sideline. Jump on two feet. Bring ball laterally away from defender and over head, protect with forearm/shoulder. Release with wrist flip. Stay low on legs throughout — if legs straighten, power is lost. [S7, p.44]
2. **Hook shot**: Can be taken with or without a dribble. Toes of forward foot point toward basket. Jump off front foot. Bring ball outside with arm nearest midcourt, starting slightly above the shooting shoulder with the corresponding knee bent. Nonshooting arm (bent ~45°) lifts ball up; release at shoulder level. Shooting arm extends overhead, ball released with wrist/finger flip. Turn head to watch basket in advance — if the high-leg foot points at the basket, the body follows. [S7, pp.44-45]
3. **Jump shot**: If the player is squared up with enough space and the defender is far enough away. [S7, p.45]
4. **Up-and-under**: If the defender fully recovers under the basket and tries to block the shot. Fake a shot (ball slightly above forehead, two hands, legs bent, eyes on rim). When defender is in the air: long balanced stride through with the outside foot (midcourt-line foot), bring ball from ear to ear (not to chest — no time). Slim the body to get under the defender and shoot. [S7, p.45]

## Drop Step to the Middle

### Defender on Low Side — No Dribble or One Dribble
- Sealed defender on the low side; make a drop step to the middle using the same baseline-drop-step technique.
- Finish options: dunk, power shot off glass, jump hook, hook, jump shot, up-and-under. [S7, p.46]

### Defender Recovers on Middle Drop Step
- Defender slides up and recovers: post uses the foot nearest the baseline as a pivot foot, reverses, takes one dribble (in front of body for protection), goes up for a dunk or shot off the glass. [S7, p.46]

## The Spin Move
If the defender stays behind and pushes with the forearm:
- Quickly spin around the defender, shoot immediately if possible (no dribble). Most common to baseline, also possible to middle.
- Keys: stay low; use the foot nearest baseline or midcourt line as the pivot foot; rotate on the heel; point toe of pivot foot toward basket; take a long step with the other leg.
- Keep the ball at chest level, in the middle of the body throughout the entire spin — prevents the defender from slapping the ball from behind. [S7, p.46]
- Greatest practitioner: Hakeem Olajuwon — "great footwork, agility, and quickness." [S7, p.46]

## High Post Back-to-Basket Moves
At the high post, two immediate options after catching with back to the basket:
1. **Drop step and drive**: Same technique — long quick step, dribble once, shoot off glass or dunk.
2. **Spin and drive**: Fake one side, spin, shoot after no dribble or one hard dribble.
- Make sure the lane is free — the move is so quick there is little chance for a countermove if a help defender is in the lane. [S7, p.47]

## Common Mistakes
1. Starting drop step before ball is in the air while still holding contact → turnover (defender steals during release).
2. Post too close to the baseline when setting up → no room to drop step, forced away from basket.
3. Keeping body vertical after drop step with defender behind → defender blocks shot from behind. Lean forward.
4. Legs straightening during jump hook → lost power and strength, easier for defender to bump post away.
5. Bringing ball to chest level during up-and-under step-through → loses time and space. Keep ball ear-to-ear.
