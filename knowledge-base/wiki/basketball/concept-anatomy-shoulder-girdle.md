# Shoulder Girdle

## Summary
The shoulder girdle is the linkage of joints, ligaments, and muscles that positions the arm in space and controls the release of the basketball. It is not a single joint — it is a coordinated system of the glenohumeral (ball-and-socket) joint, the scapulothoracic articulation, and the acromioclavicular and sternoclavicular joints. For shooting, the shoulder girdle's job is to deliver a stable, repeatable release point above the head; for defense and rebounding, its job is to absorb and redirect contact.

A common shooting flaw — elbow flair, where the shooting elbow drifts laterally away from the midline — almost always traces to scapular instability or limited shoulder external rotation, not to elbow mechanics. The fix lives at the shoulder girdle, not the elbow.

## When to Use
This anatomy matters to coaching decisions involving:
- Jump-shot release alignment (elbow flair, release height)
- Free-throw repeatability (release point variance)
- Catch-and-shoot windows (shoulder rotation speed under defense)
- Defensive contesting (vertical arm extension without leaving feet)
- Rebounding contact (absorbing impact through the scapular plane)

## Key Principles
1. **Scapular stability precedes arm strength.** The scapula must be set against the rib cage before the arm can deliver consistent force. Train scapular pushups, prone Y-T-W holds, and wall slides before loading overhead lifts.
2. **External rotation lives above 90° of abduction.** Shooters need free shoulder external rotation in the release position. Limited mobility here forces the elbow outward — the visible "flair" is often a downstream symptom.
3. **Posterior chain of the shoulder is undertrained.** The infraspinatus, teres minor, and lower trapezius are the decelerators on follow-through. Weakness here shows up as wrist droop or "tired arm" late in games. See [[concept-anatomy-wrist-complex]] for the distal continuation.
4. **The kinetic chain runs through here.** Force generated by the legs and core ([[concept-anatomy-glute-max]] and [[concept-anatomy-core-outer]]) is delivered to the ball through the shoulder girdle. A weak link here leaks the lower body's work.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG / SG**: highest shooting volume; release-point variance is a primary performance signal
- **SF**: catch-and-shoot off-screen actions stress shoulder rotation under load
- **PF / C**: post hooks and contested putbacks load the shoulder under contact; rebounding adds cumulative impact

## Variations
### Glenohumeral Joint
The ball-and-socket joint where the humerus meets the scapula. Greatest range of motion in the body and most prone to instability. Coaches see consequences as: dislocation history, recurrent impingement, painful arc on overhead motion.

### Scapulothoracic Articulation
The scapula glides on the rib cage. Not a true joint but functions as one. Its position determines arm-overhead capacity. Coaches see consequences as: scapular winging, asymmetric shoulder height, "rounded" shooting posture.

### Rotator Cuff (Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres Minor, Subscapularis)
Four small muscles that center the humeral head in the socket. They are stabilizers, not prime movers. Weakness or imbalance shows up as shoulder pain after high-rep shooting or contact play.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Training only the deltoids and pecs** → unbalanced shoulder; posterior cuff under-recovers and follow-through droops late in sessions. Correction: Y-T-W endurance work + face-pulls + band external rotations.
2. **Stretching a tight shoulder without strengthening the cuff** → mobility gains without control; defenders push the player off line. Correction: strengthen through the new range, don't just stretch.
3. **Ignoring scapular position in shooting workouts** → release point varies by 2-4 inches across reps. Correction: cue "set the shoulder blades down and back" before the catch, hold through release.
