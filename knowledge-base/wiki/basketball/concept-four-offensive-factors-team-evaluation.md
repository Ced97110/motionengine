# The Four Offensive Factors: Team Evaluation Framework

## Summary
When diagnosing why a basketball team's offense declined from one season to the next, four key factors reveal where the problem lies: field goal percentage (FG%), offensive rebounding rate (OREB%), turnover rate (TOV%), and free throw rate (FTA/FGA). Tracking these four metrics reveals the specific mechanism of offensive change and points coaches toward the right corrective actions.

This framework was applied in Basketball on Paper to the 2001–2002 Toronto Raptors, where the team's offensive rating dropped from ~3 points above league average to ~2 points below. The four-factor breakdown showed the decline was concentrated in turnovers (14% → 16% of possessions) and getting to the free-throw line (0.28 → 0.26 FTA/FGA), while shooting percentage and offensive rebounding were essentially unchanged [S16, p.319].

## When to Use
- End-of-season offensive review to identify why a team's scoring efficiency changed
- In-season monitoring of offensive trends after a losing streak or plateau
- Comparing two seasons of the same team to isolate regression or improvement
- Player personnel decisions — which factor(s) did a departing/arriving player most affect?
- Scouting opponents to identify their offensive vulnerabilities

## Key Principles
1. **FG% (field goal percentage)** — Measures shot quality and shot-making. A decline here suggests worse shot selection, better opponent defense, or loss of a high-efficiency scorer.
2. **OREB% (offensive rebounding rate)** — Measures ability to extend possessions. Stable OREB% while other factors decline means rebounding is not the problem.
3. **TOV% (turnover rate)** — Turnovers per 100 possessions. An increase here is particularly costly because it eliminates scoring opportunities entirely. Rule of thumb: each extra percentage point of turnovers costs approximately one point of offensive rating.
4. **FTA/FGA (free throw rate)** — Free throw attempts per field goal attempt. Declining FTA/FGA often signals less aggressive attacking of the paint, more perimeter shooting, or opponent adjustments that take away drives.
5. **Sequence of diagnosis**: Team record → Offensive/Defensive rating comparison → Four-factor breakdown → Individual player analysis. Always start with the team, then go to players [S16, p.318].
6. **Defense vs. Offense first**: Before breaking down players, determine whether the team's decline is offensive or defensive. The corrective actions are entirely different [S16, p.319].

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Most directly influences TOV% through ball-handling decisions and pace management; also affects FTA/FGA through driving frequency
- **SG/SF**: Heavily influence FG% through shot selection (2PT vs. 3PT mix) and FTA/FGA through aggressiveness to the basket
- **PF/C**: Dominate OREB% and contribute to FG% through high-percentage interior attempts; also affect FTA/FGA through post aggression

## Variations
### Defensive Four Factors
The same framework applies to defense, tracking opponent FG%, opponent OREB%, forced TOV%, and opponent FTA/FGA. A team can improve its defensive rating while the offensive rating declines — as the 2002 Raptors demonstrated [S16, p.319].

### Moving-Window Analysis
Plotting five-game moving averages of offensive and defensive ratings through the season reveals streaks, slumps, and inflection points tied to injuries or roster changes. Cumulative net-point charts are especially revealing for identifying when a team's fortunes shifted [S16, p.320].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Looking at players before the team** → First establish whether the problem is offense, defense, or both; then identify which offensive factor declined; only then examine individual players.
2. **Assuming the best player's absence caused a slump** → The 2002 Raptors' decline when Vince Carter went out in February was offset by a recovery when he went out again in March — showing the team's problems were deeper than one player [S16, p.322].
3. **Treating FG% as the only shooting metric** → Shot volume from different zones, 2PT vs. 3PT mix, and FTA/FGA together tell a more complete story than FG% alone.
4. **Ignoring turnover rate increases** → A 2-percentage-point increase in TOV% (14% → 16%) is a meaningful offensive regression equivalent to roughly losing 2 points of offensive rating [S16, p.319].
