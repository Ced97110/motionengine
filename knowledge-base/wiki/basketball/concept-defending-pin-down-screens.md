# Defending Pin-Down Screens

## Summary
The pin-down screen occurs when a player sets a downward screen on a defender near the low block, allowing a teammate to relocate — often to the top of the key or wing — for a catch-and-shoot. Pin-down screens are effective at relocating players and creating mismatches on switches. Dallas's use of the pin-down screen was highlighted as a "tremendous offensive tool" in the 2011 NBA Finals. Because the offensive player is always looking to catch and shoot, the closer the defender stays to the screened player, the better the defense [S13, p.207].

## When to Use
- Whenever the offense uses pin-down actions to free a shooter from the low block to the perimeter
- Against teams that run two consecutive pin-down screens (screen-the-screener) to create layered looks
- As part of any half-court man-to-man defensive package where pin-down actions are scouted

## Key Principles
1. **Communicate early** — the defender guarding the screener must yell "Screen!" or "Watch the screen!" the moment the screener sets into line with the defender being screened.
2. **Break down and get small** — the screened defender must shorten stride, lower their center of gravity, and reduce their profile before contact.
3. **Maintain hand contact** — keep a hand on the offensive player being screened to feel direction changes and maintain awareness.
4. **Push opponent away from the screen** — do not trail; push the cutter away from the screen to reduce their catch-and-shoot opportunity.
5. **Follow as closely as possible** — the screen occurs in a tight space; staying glued to the cutter is the only way to contest the catch-and-shoot.
6. **Recognize the second screen** — teams often use a pass-and-second-pin-down sequence (e.g., O1 passes to O2 then re-screens for O4 at the low block). Both X1 and X4 must execute the same break-down technique on the second action.
7. **Avoid switches that create mismatches** — pin-downs are designed to produce favorable switches; communicate constantly to prevent giving the offense a size advantage [S13, p.207].

## Player Responsibilities
- **Screened defender (X2 or X4)**: Break down, get small, maintain hand contact on the offensive player, push them away from the screen, and recover as closely as possible to contest the catch-and-shoot.
- **Screener's defender (X4 or X1)**: Force the screener (O4 or O1) down and low so they cannot get a favorable screening angle. After screening, if the screener catches the ball at a cleared-out position, be in position to recover.
- **Ball defender**: Keep vision on both the ball and weak-side screen actions; communicate screen calls to teammates.

## Variations
### Double Pin-Down
After O1 passes to O2 and sets a first pin-down on X4, O1 sets a second pin-down at the low block, freeing O4 for a low-post position. X4 must break down, get small, maintain contact, and push O4 away on the second screen. X1 must step directly in the passing lane in front of O4 to prevent the pass [S13, p.207].

### Pin-Down Creating a Switch Mismatch
Pin-downs are especially effective when the offense hunts a switch. If X1 and X4 switch, the offense gains a size mismatch in the post. Communicate and fight through the screen rather than defaulting to a switch [S13, p.207].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Standing tall when the screen comes** → Break down and get small well before contact.
2. **Losing hand contact with the offensive player** → Maintain a guiding hand on the cutter's hip or back at all times.
3. **Switching automatically** → Pin-downs are designed to create mismatches on switches; fight through rather than switch unless mismatched talent requires it.
4. **Not calling the screen** → The screener's defender must verbalize the screen immediately to give the screened defender a chance to react.
