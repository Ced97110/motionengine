# General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS) in Basketball Training

## Summary
The General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS), first outlined by Hans Selye in 1936 and refined in 1956, is the fundamental model explaining how the body responds and adapts to training stress. Also known as the **supercompensation cycle**, it is the scientific basis for progressive overload in basketball strength and conditioning programs. Understanding GAS helps coaches and trainers apply the right training stimulus — enough to disrupt homeostasis and drive adaptation, but not so much that exhaustion and injury result. [S2, pp.2-3]

## When to Use
- **Program design**: Use GAS to time loading and recovery weeks (e.g., 3 weeks of progressive load followed by a deload week)
- **Player assessment**: Recognize when a player is in the exhaustion phase (overtraining) vs. the resistance phase (normal fatigue)
- **Off-season training**: Maximum supercompensation opportunity exists when competitive demands are absent
- **In-season management**: Reduced training volume prevents exhaustion phase while maintaining the supercompensation baseline

## Key Principles
1. **Alarm phase**: A training stimulus (new exercise, increased load, greater volume) disrupts the body's homeostasis. Performance temporarily decreases. [S2, p.2]
2. **Resistance phase**: The body recovers from the stimulus and repairs itself, returning toward the homeostasis baseline. Adequate rest and nutrition are critical here. [S2, p.2]
3. **Supercompensation phase**: The body adapts by rising *above* the previous homeostasis baseline, becoming more capable of handling the stimulus that originally disrupted it. This is the training effect — the goal of every workout. [S2, p.2]
4. **Exhaustion/detraining phase**: If training stress is too high, too frequent, or recovery is inadequate, the body cannot supercompensate and drops below homeostasis. This leads to overtraining, injury, or detraining. [S2, pp.2-3]
5. **Stimulus must exceed homeostasis**: If training stress is too low, little or no adaptation occurs. Players must be progressively challenged with greater weight, higher jumps, or faster running speeds. [S2, p.3]
6. **Individual assessment matters**: Because each athlete's baseline and recovery capacity differs, training programs must be individualized based on physical deficits, needs, and goals. [S2, p.3]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Players must commit to recovery — sleep, nutrition, and active recovery — to allow the resistance and supercompensation phases to complete properly.
- **Coaches/trainers**: Responsible for timing training stimuli correctly so that the next stimulus hits during the supercompensation phase, not the exhaustion phase.

## Variations
### Short GAS Cycles (Micro)
Weekly loading: a hard training day (alarm phase) → rest/light day (resistance phase) → hard training 48-72 hrs later (hitting during supercompensation).

### Long GAS Cycles (Macro/Periodization)
Multi-week blocks: 3-4 progressive loading weeks followed by a deload week. Pre-season loading blocks followed by in-season maintenance.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Training too frequently without rest** → Body stays in alarm or exhaustion phase; no supercompensation occurs → injury and performance decline.
2. **Training too lightly** → Stimulus does not disrupt homeostasis → no adaptation → wasted training time. [S2, p.3]
3. **Applying the same stimulus repeatedly** → Body adapts to that stimulus; it no longer disrupts homeostasis → plateau. Progressively increase weight, volume, or intensity.
4. **Confusing soreness with overtraining** → Normal DOMS (delayed-onset muscle soreness) occurs during the alarm/resistance phase; true overtraining shows as prolonged performance decline, mood disturbance, and increased injury risk.
