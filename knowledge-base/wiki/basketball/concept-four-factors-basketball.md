# The Four Factors of Basketball Success

## Summary
The Four Factors is Dean Oliver's framework for identifying the offensive and defensive components that determine a team's efficiency — and therefore its wins and losses. Rather than looking at points scored directly, the Four Factors decompose offensive and defensive performance into four measurable categories that coaches can directly influence and that are available from any standard box score [S16, pp.193-202].

Every game can be diagnosed by examining each team's performance on these four factors relative to league average. The factors can be applied to the whole season for strategy evaluation, or to a single game for halftime and post-game adjustments.

## When to Use
- **Post-game analysis**: First step in understanding why the team won or lost before detailed film review
- **Halftime adjustments**: Two-minute check of which factor is most out of line and most correctable
- **Scouting**: Identify an opponent's offensive strengths and defensive weaknesses across the season
- **Season-long strategy**: Design offensive systems to exploit your team's factor strengths; design defensive systems to attack opponents' factor weaknesses
- **Individual accountability**: Connect individual player actions (offensive rebounds, turnovers, fouls) to team-level factor performance

## Key Principles

### The Four Factors (Offensive and Defensive)

**Factor 1: Field Goal Percentage (Effective FG%)**
- Offensive: How efficiently is the team converting field goal attempts?
- Defensive: How well is the team limiting opponent shooting efficiency?
- NBA average: ~42-47% depending on era
- *Coaching cue*: "Shot quality matters more than shot quantity" — a team that takes fewer but higher-percentage shots often outscores one that takes more low-percentage attempts

**Factor 2: Offensive Rebound Percentage**
- Offensive: What fraction of the team's missed shots does the offense recover? = OR / (OR + opponent DR)
- Defensive: What fraction of opponent misses does the defense rebound? = DR / (DR + opponent OR)
- NBA average: ~28-32% offensive rebound rate
- *Coaching cue*: "Go to the glass" — offensive boards directly extend possessions and are among the most coachable adjustments at halftime [S16, p.197]

**Factor 3: Turnover Rate**
- Offensive: Turnovers as a percentage of possessions = TOV / Poss
- Defensive: Opponent turnovers forced as a percentage of opponent possessions
- NBA average: ~14-22% depending on era
- *Coaching cue*: "Protect the ball" — turnovers are the most direct way to give the opponent free possessions

**Factor 4: Free Throw Rate and Free Throw Shooting**
- Offensive: How often does the team get to the line, and how well do they convert? = FTM / FGA (rate) and FT% (efficiency)
- Defensive: How often does the team foul the opponent, and how lucky/unlucky is the opponent from the line?
- *Coaching cue*: "Attack the paint" — getting to the line generates free points and forces defensive fouls; free throw defense is largely about limiting opponents' aggression into the paint

### Weighting the Factors
Not all four factors are equally important. In general order of impact:
1. Field goal shooting (most important)
2. Turnovers
3. Offensive rebounding
4. Free throw rate/shooting

However, this weighting can vary by game situation. In the Charlotte-Milwaukee Game 7 case, free throw performance was the decisive factor because both teams shot around 45% from the field [S16, p.195].

### Possession Calculation Formula
All factor calculations begin with possessions:
**Poss = FGA − OR + TOV + 0.4 × FTA**
[S16, p.193]

Both teams' possession estimates should be within 1-2 of each other (they share the same possessions). Average the two for the most accurate game pace.

## Player Responsibilities
- **Guards (PG, SG)**: Primary contributors to Factor 3 (turnover rate) and Factor 1 (shooting efficiency from perimeter). Ball handlers directly control turnover rate.
- **Forwards (SF, PF)**: Primary contributors to Factor 2 (offensive/defensive rebounding). Physically positioned to control the glass.
- **Centers (C)**: Critical to Factor 2 (rebounding) and Factor 1 (interior shooting efficiency, shot-blocking on defense)
- **All positions**: Contribute to Factor 4 (free throw rate) by attacking aggressively and defending without fouling

## Variations
### Single-Game Diagnosis
Used post-game or at halftime to determine which factor was most decisive and what adjustment is most available:
1. Check if both teams' ratings were above/below average → offense vs. defense game
2. Find the factor most out of line with league average
3. Identify individual players driving that factor deviation
4. Make coaching adjustment targeting the most coachable factor [S16, p.197]

### Season-Long Strategic Planning
Track each factor across many games to identify:
- Which factor is the team's biggest offensive strength (double down on it)
- Which factor is the team's biggest defensive weakness (design schemes to minimize opponent exploitation)
- Which individual players are the best and worst contributors to each factor

## Common Mistakes
1. **Focusing only on scoring without possessions context** → A team scoring 110 points in 88 possessions (125 offensive rating) played much better than one scoring 110 in 110 possessions (100 rating)
2. **Neglecting offensive rebounds at halftime** → Offensive boards are highly coachable; a 3-minute conversation at halftime about going to the glass can directly flip Factor 2 in the second half [S16, p.197]
3. **Over-weighting free throw performance luck** → A team hitting 94% from the line (as Milwaukee did in the Charlotte game) is partially lucky; don't build a game plan around opponent free throw inaccuracy [S16, p.195]
4. **Treating the four factors independently** → They interact: a team that forces turnovers will face fewer field goal attempts (Factor 1 denominator shrinks), and a team that dominates offensive boards reduces the effective significance of their own turnover rate
