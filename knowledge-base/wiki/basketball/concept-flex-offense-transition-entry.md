# Flex Offense — Transition Game and Lane-Filling Entry

## Summary
the head coach (Argentina national team / team) presents the transition game as the first component of the flex offense. The primary philosophy is to attack the defense as soon as possession is gained, pushing the pace without rushing — capturing the essence of John Wooden's "Be quick, but don't hurry." The transition game is structured around three lane-filling rules that handle the most common configurations of fast-break personnel. The transition ends with a standard half-court setup that flows directly into the flex offense. [S7, pp.135-136]

## When to Use
- Immediately upon gaining possession — always look to push before the defense sets
- On every change of possession: made baskets, defensive rebounds, steals, and turnovers
- The structure should be automatic — players execute their lane-filling assignment without a call from the bench
- When a primary or secondary break scoring opportunity exists, improvise and score; do not force the set structure if the layup is open

## Key Principles
1. **Offense starts with defense** — aggressive defense creates fast-break opportunities through quick transitions from one end to the other
2. **Be quick, but don't hurry** — push the pace with controlled decision-making, not reckless haste
3. **Fill proper lanes** — each player has a specific lane-filling assignment based on their position relative to the ball
4. **Create overload situations** — fill the court to create numerical advantages (3v2, 2v1) for high-percentage shots
5. **First trailer posts, second trailer stops outside** — the first trailer (4) cuts into the three-second area and posts low if not receiving; the second trailer (5) stops outside the three-point line
6. **Improvise when easy baskets appear** — the lane-filling structure does not hamper creativity; if a layup is available, take it without completing the formal pattern
7. **Always end in the flex formation** — regardless of which lane-filling pattern is used, the transition ends with 2 and 3 in corners outside the arc, 4 in the low post, 5 outside the three-second lane, and 1 with the ball

## The Three Lane-Filling Rules

### Rule 1: Two Players on the Wing, Same Lane, Ball Side
**Situation**: 2 and 3 both run on the same lateral lane; ball is pushed on the same lane behind them
- **Forward player (2)**: continues running, cuts under the basket to the opposite corner
- **Trailing player (3)**: stays in the same lane, goes to the corner on the same side
- **Opposite wing (4)**: cuts into the three-second area to receive; if no pass, posts up low (first trailer rule)
- **Second trailer (5)**: runs and stops outside the three-point line

### Rule 2: One Player on the Wing, Ball Side
**Situation**: 3 (guard or SF) is behind the ball
- **3**: runs to fill the other open lateral lane, regardless of what the first trailer (4) is doing
- This ensures both lateral lanes are occupied for a proper 3-man fast-break structure

### Rule 3: Trailer High Over the Ball on Lateral Lane
**Situation**: 4 (first trailer) is high over the ball and running on a lateral lane
- **4**: runs to receive the ball and shoot a layup
- **If 4 can't receive and score inside the lane**: 4 posts up on the same side as the ball
- **2 and 3**: run on the lateral lanes in proper positions
- **5**: runs as the second trailer in support

## Standard Flex Setup at the End of Transition
Regardless of which lane-filling pattern was used, the transition resolves into:
- **1 (PG)**: ball in hands at the top of the key
- **2 (SG)**: in the corner outside the three-point line (one side)
- **3 (SF)**: in the corner outside the three-point line (other side)
- **4 (PF)**: low-post position
- **5 (C)**: outside the three-second lane (second trailer position, near high post)

From this setup, the flex offense begins. [S7, p.136]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (1)**: pushes the ball upcourt; reads the break; pushes to corner if no advantage; starts the flex
- **SG (2)**: reads lane assignment based on position relative to ball; sprints to designated corner or lane
- **SF (3)**: same as SG; fills the other open lateral lane if 3 is behind the ball
- **PF (4)**: first trailer — cuts to the three-second area; posts if no catch; sprints in secondary break
- **C (5)**: second trailer — stops outside the three-point line; does not enter the paint until the flex begins

## Common Mistakes
1. **All players bunching in one lane** → correction: as soon as two players are on the same lane, one (the forward one) continues under the basket to the other corner
2. **Trailing center (5) entering the paint** → correction: 5 must stop outside the three-point line; entering the paint takes away post-entry angles for the flex
3. **First trailer (4) bypassing the three-second area** → correction: 4 must always look to receive inside the lane before posting low; skipping this read misses the highest-percentage fast-break finish
4. **Forcing the fast-break structure when a layup is open** → correction: improvise and score the easy basket; the structure is a default, not a mandate when an obvious opportunity exists
5. **Settling into set too slowly** → correction: the transition to the flex setup should be automatic and fast; every second of indecision allows the defense to recover
