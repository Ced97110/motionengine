# Basketball Training Camp Athlete Monitoring System

## Summary
Training camps (7 weeks in collegiate basketball, 3 weeks in the NBA) expose players to high training loads to prepare for the upcoming season. The challenge: prescribe appropriate volumes and recovery periods to optimize adaptation without causing overtraining or injury [S12, p.12].

Key principle: Players who complete more pre-season training sessions have *reduced* in-season injury rates. Teams with the lowest injury burdens also have the greatest competitive success. A multi-disciplinary approach (sport coaches + S&C coaches + sport scientists + athletes) is required to individualize training prescription and prevent large workload spikes (>10%) [S12, p.12].

## When to Use
- Pre-season training camps (all levels from youth to pro)
- Any period of rapid load escalation
- When integrating new players or returning injured players
- The first 2–4 weeks after the off-season break (low chronic workload baseline)

## Key Principles
1. **Assess prior training load history** — Off-season breaks create low chronic workloads; first-week loads must reflect this starting point, not end-of-season fitness [S12, p.12]
2. **Avoid spikes >10%** — Acute-to-chronic ratio ≥1.5 signals increased injury risk [S12, p.13]
3. **Pair load data with fatigue data** — Workload numbers alone are insufficient; fatigue response must be monitored simultaneously [S12, p.12]
4. **Use Z-scores relative to the individual** — Each athlete's response is unique; flag Z-scores ≤ −1.5 as meaningful deviations [S12, p.13]
5. **Calculate the Smallest Worthwhile Change** — SWC = 0.2 × between-subject SD; ensures detected changes are real, not noise [S12, p.14]

## The Complete Training Camp Monitoring System

*Note: This system is directly adapted from Table 2 in [S12, p.13]*

| Tool | Frequency | Purpose | Analysis Method | Interpretation Threshold |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Microtechnology (Player Load)** | Every court session | External load | Z-score vs. individual baseline; Acute:Chronic ratio | Z-score ≤ −1.5; Ratio ≥ 1.5 = elevated injury risk |
| **Session RPE (S-RPE) Training Load** | Every session | Internal load | Z-score vs. individual; Acute:Chronic ratio | Z-score ≤ −1.5; Ratio ≥ 1.5 = elevated injury risk |
| **Wellness Questionnaire** | Daily | Sleep quality, fatigue, soreness | Z-score vs. baseline; SWC relative to reliability | Z-score ≤ −1.5 on any item |
| **Countermovement Jump (CMJ)** | Daily | Neuromuscular fatigue | Z-score vs. baseline; SWC relative to reliability | Z-score ≤ −1.5 OR decrease > SWC |
| **RHR/HRV** | Daily | Autonomic nervous system status | Z-score vs. baseline | Z-score ≤ −1.5 |

**Key abbreviations:**
- S-RPE = Session Rating of Perceived Exertion
- SWC = Smallest Worthwhile Change (0.2 × between-subject SD)
- RHR = Resting Heart Rate; HRV = Heart Rate Variability
- ANS = Autonomic Nervous System

## Player Responsibilities
- **All players**: Complete wellness questionnaire daily (before breakfast, same time each day); perform CMJ test before each training session; wear accelerometer/GPS device every court session
- **S&C Coach**: Calculate daily Z-scores and flag any player at ≤ −1.5 on any measure; track acute-to-chronic ratios weekly
- **Head Coach**: Receive daily flag list and adjust individual player loads accordingly; avoid blanket prescription when individual data is available
- **Sport Scientist**: Establish population-specific ICC and CV for each tool; calculate and update individual SWC values; report trends across the camp

## Variations
### Competition Period Monitoring
During the season, add match load monitoring. Key finding: 10m sprint speed and CMJ height decrease until 24h (ES=0.5) and 48h (ES=0.6) post-match — schedule high-intensity sessions no sooner than 48h after games [S12, p.13]. NBA teams may play up to 5 games in 7 days; positional differences in load demand separate monitoring by back-court vs. front-court players [S12, p.13].

### Congested Fixture Monitoring
Post-season tournaments may have 24h between games. Evidence shows higher fatigue and injury rates during congested fixtures due to spikes in game load [S12, p.13]. CMJ and wellness questionnaires remain the most practical daily tools in this context.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Monitoring without acting** — Data has no value unless coaches adjust loads based on it; create a clear protocol for what happens when a player is flagged [S12, p.12]
2. **Establishing group norms instead of individual baselines** — A tired player may look normal compared to the group but be significantly below their own baseline [S12, p.13]
3. **Ignoring the pre-season load history** — Starting camp at full volume assumes players have maintained a training base during the off-season, which is rarely true [S12, p.12]
4. **Using only subjective tools** — Wellness questionnaires are essential but athletes can manipulate responses; objective measures (CMJ, HRV) provide a check [S12, p.12]
