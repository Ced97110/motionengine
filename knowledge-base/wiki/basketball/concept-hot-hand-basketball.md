# Hot Hand in Basketball

## Summary
The "hot hand" is the belief that a player who has made several consecutive shots has a temporarily elevated probability of making the next shot. Amos Tversky and colleagues conducted landmark research in the 1980s examining whether this phenomenon exists statistically, concluding they found no significant evidence for it. However, the absence of statistical proof is not the same as proof of absence — the tools used may have been insufficient to detect real but subtle streakiness.

For coaches, the practical implication is nuanced: teams should react to apparent streak shooting because if streaks do exist, ignoring them is a competitive disadvantage. But they should also avoid *over*-reacting, since NBA defenders' aggressive response to perceived hot shooters may itself create anti-streak behavior (forcing worse shots).

## When to Use
- When deciding whether to double-team a shooter after consecutive makes
- When designing offensive sets to "feed" a player who appears hot
- When evaluating whether a player's recent performance is predictive
- When analyzing late-game shot-selection decisions

## Key Principles
1. **Tversky's null finding is not proof of absence** — he showed only that his statistical tests found no significant hot hand, not that streaks are impossible [S16, p.105]
2. **Four out of twenty-six shooters** successfully predicted their next shot outcome at statistically significant levels — suggesting *something* was cuing performance, even if subtle [S16, p.106]
3. **NBA players showed anti-streak tendencies** while college players without defense showed slight streak tendencies — suggesting defensive reaction to perceived hot hands may itself suppress streaks [S16, p.105]
4. **Second free throws are made at higher rates than first** — a consistent, measurable finding within the same study data [S16, p.103]
5. **Shot difficulty and shot selection were not controlled** — three consecutive 20%-probability makes would be statistically significant, but Tversky's methodology didn't capture this [S16, p.106]
6. **Team-level analogy**: home court advantage (~3 pts/game in 2000-02 NBA) produces real win% effects (~59%) that Tversky-style tests also wouldn't detect if applied to that data [S16, p.105]
7. **Competitive strategy**: if streaks exist even partially, ignoring them costs you; calibrate your reaction frequency to how often they actually occur [S16, p.107]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Be aware of your recent performance but don't let the hot-hand belief cause you to take lower-quality shots outside your normal range
- **PG**: Recognize when a teammate appears hot and feed them — the cost of being wrong is low if the shot quality remains high
- **Defenders**: Closing out harder on a shooter after consecutive makes is defensively sound even if statistical proof of streaks is weak

## Variations
### Anti-Streak Behavior (NBA Context)
NBA players may show slight *anti*-streak tendencies because defenders and help rotations increase pressure on perceived hot shooters, forcing lower-percentage attempts or passes. The hot hand effect may be partially suppressed by defensive reaction [S16, p.107].

### Free Throw Context
Boston Celtics 1981-82 data showed no player significantly more likely to make their second free throw after making the first. However, the data *did* show that second free throws are made at a higher percentage than first free throws across the team — consistent with the common coaching cue that the second FT is easier [S16, p.103].

### Controlled Shooting Experiments
Cornell players shot 100 shots from their 50% distance without defense. Only 1 of 26 showed significant streakiness. But both shooters and observers predicted next-shot outcomes at better-than-chance rates (4/26 each) — the chance this happened randomly is only ~4% [S16, pp.103-104, 106].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Concluding streaks don't exist** → Tversky proved only that his tests didn't detect them; the tests had known limitations (didn't control shot difficulty, shot selection, or context)
2. **Over-reacting to every hot spell** → NBA data suggests teams already over-react, possibly causing anti-streak behavior; calibrate to actual frequency
3. **Treating all players the same** → Some players may genuinely be more streaky than others; track individual data rather than applying league-wide conclusions
4. **Ignoring context** → A player hitting three shots in a row on difficult contested shots is more meaningful than three open catch-and-shoot makes
