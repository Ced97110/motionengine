# Basketball Defensive Terminology (Herb Brown Glossary)

## Summary
Herb Brown's glossary of defensive basketball terms provides precise, coaching-ready definitions for the concepts that appear throughout his defensive system. Understanding these terms allows coaches and players to communicate quickly and accurately during games and practice. Many terms have multiple synonyms used by different programs — knowing the aliases helps with scouting and cross-team communication.

## Key Principles
1. Every defensive concept has a name — use consistent terminology so players react without thinking.
2. Many terms have aliases; learn them all for scouting purposes.
3. Defensive positioning principles (ball-you-man, I-position, level with the ball) are the foundation of all team defense.

## Glossary of Defensive Terms

### Ball-You-Man
Defensive positioning principle: the defender always keeps his **body between the man he is guarding and the basketball**. Foundational to all help-side defense. [S1, p.234]

### Belly Up / Body Up
Defensive player makes **body contact** with the offensive post player (with or without the ball), forcing him up toward a trapping defender to stop him from reversing the ball to an open man. [S1, p.234]

### Blitz the Pick and Roll
The **screener's defender traps the dribbler** while the dribbler's defender chases over the screen to double-team the ball and stop penetration. Teams may call a color or acronym to signal this. Synonyms: trap the PnR, ice the ball. [S1, p.234]

### Bump / Chuck / Body / Ride
Defender makes **physical contact with an offensive player without the ball** to slow, stop, influence, or impede his cut. Used against screeners and cutters alike. [S1, p.235]

### Close Out
Method by which a defender **recovers to a ball-receiver ready to shoot** after providing weak-side help or rotating. Must be quick but under control to limit dribble penetration. [S1, p.236]

### Contact Show
Pick-and-roll defense technique: the **screener's defender maintains contact with the screener** while jumping out to stop the dribbler, rather than fully hedging or switching. [S1, p.236]

### Cover Down
When a player is beaten on the **baseline on a straight-line drive**, the nearest weak-side player must slide down and cross the lane to cover the goal and stop the drive. [S1, p.236]

### Deny and Overplay
The ball-you-man principle applied to **one pass away**: the defender keeps his body between the man and the ball to deter the penetrating pass. Forces the opponent backdoor or makes him receive the ball farther out. [S1, p.237]

### Digging
The strong-side defender **shrinks off his man** when the post has the ball, to disconcert the post player and/or force him to pass back outside. [S1, p.237]

### Full-Body Show
A defender off the ball gets **his entire body in front of a penetrating dribbler** to stop the ball or force a pass uphill or across the court. [S1, p.240]

### Hand Check
Defensive act of **forcing/controlling/channeling the dribbler or post man** in a desired direction, usually when his back is turned. In the pro, the defender must use his forearm rather than his palm. Allowed against post players below the free-throw line extended with their back to the goal. [S1, pp.240-241]

### Help
Defensive term: **leaving your man to stop penetration** by a teammate's opponent. The foundation of help-side team defense. [S1, p.241]

### I (Defensive Position)
The **weak-side defensive alignment**: one defender on the elbow, the other on the low block. The ball-defender knows his teammates have these spots and are ready to help. Can also exist on the strong side when the ball is on the strong-side wing. [S1, p.241]

### Inverting
**Keeping big men inside and perimeter players outside** by switching defensive positions on the weak side regardless of where opponents set up. [S1, p.242]

### Level with the Ball
When a defender's man is trailing the play and the ball is **in front of the defender**, the defender must get back to the ball side to stop penetration. Used in full-court, transition, and half-court defenses. [S1, p.242]

### Lock and Trail
Defending a **down screen on the strong side**: the defender gets on his man's hip and outside shoulder to ride and force him to curl the screen, avoiding being screened himself. [S1, p.243]

### Lock In / Marry
When a defender guards a good shooter and **does not help or rotate** in a trap or double-team defense. The defender "gloves" or "blankets" the offensive player to deny him the ball entirely. [S1, p.243-244]

### One and Done
**Trap the ball once** in a full- or half-court press. Once the pass exits the trap or the trap fails, the defense sprints back and plays normal defense. [S1, p.245]

### Open and Through
Defensive technique for the **weak-side pin down**: the screener's defender opens up to allow the cutter's defender to cut inside and ball-side of the screen. Big men may step up and back to let their teammate go inside in PnR situations. [S1, p.245]

### Playing Topside
Defending a pin-down screen: the defender **stays on his man's outside shoulder from the beginning of the play**, forcing him away from the screen and the ball rather than letting him curl it. [S1, p.246]

### Coward's Press
Full-court press that **does not seek to trap**; instead slows the offense and burns shot-clock time. May incorporate a one-and-done trap philosophy. [S1, p.236]

## Common Mistakes
1. Losing ball-you-man positioning → defenders must always see both ball and man simultaneously.
2. Closing out out-of-control → leads to easy drive-bys; close out "quickly but under control." [S1, p.236]
3. Helping too far off a good shooter (lock-in situation) → gives up an open look; "marry" the shooter in these situations. [S1, p.243]
4. Failing to level with the ball on transition → ball gets in front of the defense and creates easy layups. [S1, p.242]
