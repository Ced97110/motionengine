# Dynamic Valgus, Hip Weakness, and Patellofemoral Pain

## Summary
In basketball players, patellofemoral pain (PFP) is not only a local knee problem — it frequently originates from deficits in hip muscle strength and core stability that manifest as dynamic valgus during the sport's high-demand landing, cutting, and driving movements. Poor core stability leads to contralateral pelvic drop and homolateral excessive hip adduction and internal rotation (dynamic valgus), which medially displaces the knee relative to the foot and internally rotates the distal femur relative to the tibia, altering patellofemoral kinematics and loading [S10, pp.418-419].

Basketball players with PFP consistently display greater weakness in hip abduction and external rotation compared to pain-free players. This creates a vicious cycle of altered movement patterns that increase lateral patellar tracking and retinacular tension via the iliotibial band.

## When to Use
- Basketball player with PFP and no clear local anatomical cause (normal trochlea, normal patellar height)
- Any player demonstrating dynamic valgus on single-leg squat, landing, or cutting movements
- Designing PFP rehabilitation programs
- ACL injury prevention programming (dynamic valgus is a shared risk factor)

## Key Principles
1. **Kinetic chain thinking**: PFP in basketball players must be assessed from the lumbo-pelvic-hip complex downward, not just at the knee [S10, p.418]
2. **Core = force production AND control**: The musculoskeletal core (spine, pelvis, abdomen) is responsible for lumbopelvic and hip dynamic control — directly upstream of PF kinematics
3. **Dynamic valgus mechanism**: Poor core → contralateral pelvic drop → homolateral hip adduction + internal rotation → medial knee displacement → increased lateral PF pressure
4. **ITB as the transmission belt**: Contralateral pelvic drop increases tension in the lateral patellar retinaculum via the iliotibial band → increased lateral patellar tracking and tilt [S10, p.419]
5. **Sagittal plane consideration**: Lumbar lordosis → compensatory knee flexion → increased PF joint load ("knee-spine syndrome") [S10, p.419]
6. **Hip-first rehabilitation**: Strengthening hip abductors and external rotators is a key component of PFP rehabilitation, not just quadriceps/VMO work

## Biomechanical Cascade
```
Weak Hip Abductors/External Rotators
        ↓
Contralateral Pelvic Drop
        ↓
Homolateral Excessive Hip Adduction + Internal Rotation
        ↓
Dynamic Valgus (knee medial to foot)
        ↓
Internal Rotation of Distal Femur relative to Tibia
        ↓
Increased Q-angle / TT-TG distance
        ↓
Lateral Patellar Translation + Tilt
        ↓
Increased Lateral PF Contact Stress
        ↓
Patellofemoral Pain / Tissue Overload
```

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Land from jumps with controlled knee-over-toe alignment; avoid medial knee collapse
- **Guards**: High dynamic valgus risk during crossover dribbles, first-step drives
- **Bigs**: High risk on post-up power moves, landing from dunks/blocks

## Assessment
- **Single-leg squat test**: Watch for contralateral pelvic drop, homolateral hip adduction/internal rotation (dynamic valgus)
- **Single-leg landing test**: Same dynamic valgus indicators during a jump-land sequence
- **Hip strength testing**: Hip abduction and external rotation strength testing — compare bilateral deficits
- **Functional movement screen**: Overhead squat and hurdle step for lumbopelvic control

## Training Interventions

### Hip Strengthening
- Clamshells (side-lying hip external rotation with band)
- Lateral band walks (hip abduction strength in functional position)
- Single-leg squats with valgus correction cues ("knee over second toe")
- Hip thrust (gluteus maximus for hip extension and anti-internal rotation)
- Romanian deadlift variations (posterior chain, hip external rotators)

### Core Stability
- Anti-rotation exercises (Pallof press) — resisting the rotational forces driving pelvic drop
- Single-leg balance with perturbation — simulates basketball landing demands
- Front plank, side plank — isometric core stiffness under dynamic load

### Movement Re-education
- Jump-landing mechanics cues: "soft knees," "knee over toe," "quiet landing"
- Deceleration drills with valgus correction feedback
- Mirror or video feedback for self-monitoring

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating PFP only locally** → Without addressing hip weakness, symptoms recur
2. **Quad-dominant rehabilitation** → Strengthening VMO alone without hip/core work is insufficient
3. **Ignoring the sagittal plane** → Lumbar lordosis and anterior pelvic tilt need addressing alongside frontal-plane dynamic valgus
4. **Clearing players too early** → Dynamic valgus on landing or single-leg squat must be resolved before return to full basketball activity
