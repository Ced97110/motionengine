# Tendinopathy in Basketball — Pathophysiology and Overview

## Summary
Tendinopathy is a clinical syndrome defined by activity-related pain, diffuse or localized swelling, and impaired performance. It accounts for a substantial proportion of overuse injuries in basketball, where repetitive jumping and pivoting place enormous demands on the Achilles (12% prevalence in basketball) and patellar tendons [S10, pp.491-492]. Tendinopathy is not simply inflammation — it involves complex collagen remodeling, neovascularization, and nerve ingrowth that can become self-perpetuating if not managed properly.

Two distinct pathological processes underlie tendinopathy: **tendinosis** (histopathological collagen disorganization without inflammation) and **peritendinitis** (acute or chronic paratenon inflammation) [S10, p.491].

## When to Use
- When evaluating any basketball player with activity-related tendon pain
- To distinguish tendinopathy from acute rupture, bursitis, or referred pain
- When designing training programs to prevent overuse injuries

## Key Principles

### Collagen Remodeling Cycle
1. Mechanical loading upregulates collagen gene expression and increases collagen protein synthesis — anabolic response peaks at ~24h post-exercise, remains elevated up to 80h [S10, p.492]
2. During the first 24–36h post-exercise, there is a **net loss of collagen** due to degradation — "adequate tendon unloading (~24h) after exercise is helpful in preventing net loss" [S10, p.492]
3. **Restitution time between exercise sessions** is critical — insufficient recovery → microruptures → chronic breakdown
4. Repetitive loading → microruptures → pro-inflammatory cytokines → macrophage/mast cell/lymphocyte infiltrate → early immune system involvement [S10, p.492]

### Myofibroblast and Fibrosis Pathway
5. Tenocytes and fibroblasts transform into **myofibroblasts** under repetitive stress and pro-inflammatory conditions
6. Myofibroblasts facilitate healing but should undergo apoptosis when mechanical stress abates
7. Under constant stress (no recovery) or tissue hypoxia, myofibroblasts continue proliferating → **fibrosis** → chronic tendinopathy [S10, p.492]

### Neovascularization and Pain
8. Hypoxia upregulates VEGF → **microvascular angiogenesis** into the tendon [S10, p.492]
9. Sensory nerve fibers grow adjacent to new blood vessels → release of **substance P** (nociceptive) → pain [S10, p.492]
10. In healthy tendon, repair nerves retract; in tendinopathic tendon, ingrowth nerves **do not retract** → chronic pain signaling + hyperproliferative changes
11. Substance P potentiates tendinopathy via: vasodilation, plasma extravasation, TGF-β stimulation in fibroblasts

## Medical and Pharmacologic Risk Factors
- **Statins** — increase tendinopathy risk [S10, p.492]
- **Fluoroquinolone antibiotics** — increase tendinopathy risk; should be avoided in athletes [S10, p.492]
- **Corticosteroids** (intratendinous injection) — detrimental effects on tendon metabolism; use with extreme caution [S10, p.492]
- **Metabolic disease** — obesity, hypertension, diabetes, hypercholesterolemia, inflammatory arthropathies all contribute to tendon vulnerability [S10, p.492]

## Definitions
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Tendinopathy | Clinical syndrome: activity pain + swelling + impaired performance |
| Tendinosis | Histopathological collagen disorganization, NO intratendinous inflammation |
| Peritendinitis | Acute/chronic paratenon inflammation from repetitive loading |
| Enthesopathy/Insertional tendinopathy | Inflammation at bone-tendon insertion site |

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating tendinopathy as acute inflammation** → NSAIDs have limited long-term value; the underlying process is often non-inflammatory collagen disorganization; treat with loading protocols [S10, p.492]
2. **Allowing insufficient recovery** → Training the same tendon daily without 24h+ recovery creates net collagen loss → injury risk [S10, p.492]
3. **Intratendinous corticosteroid injection** → Short-term relief only; increases tendon rupture risk; avoid in patellar and Achilles tendons [S10, pp.496, 500]
