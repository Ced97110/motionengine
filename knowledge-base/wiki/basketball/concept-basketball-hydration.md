# Basketball Hydration Guidelines

## Summary
Being well hydrated is a key factor for optimal health and exercise performance in basketball athletes. Perspiration dissipates metabolic heat from muscle work, but excessive perspiration reduces plasma volume, elevates body temperature, and depletes critical electrolytes. Studies on basketball and other team sports show that ≥2% hypohydration deteriorates sport performance, with progressive decline as dehydration reaches 1–4% body mass loss. An individualized, event-timed hydration strategy is essential for all levels of basketball play. [S10, pp.74-76]

## When to Use
- Pre-game hydration planning (4+ hours before tip-off)
- In-game hydration during timeouts and quarter breaks
- Post-game recovery fluid replacement
- Practice hydration in hot/humid training environments
- Monitoring player hydration status during training camp and long road trips

## Key Principles

### Dehydration Thresholds
1. **≥2% body mass loss** (hypohydration) deteriorates basketball sport performance [S10, p.75]
2. **Progressive decline** as dehydration progresses from 1% to 4% [S10, p.75]
3. **Anaerobic performance reduced** with progressive dehydration [S10, p.75]
4. **Hypovolemia pathway**: Metabolic heat → sweat → decreased plasma/blood volume → cardiovascular hypotension + increased glycogen consumption + altered CNS function + increased core temperature [S10, p.75]
5. **Game day stress**: Can cause over- or under-hydration — eating/drinking patterns are disrupted by travel, schedule, and anxiety [S10, p.75]

### Pre-Exercise Hydration
- Drink **5–7 mL/kg body weight** (approximately 2–3 mL/kg) of water or a sports drink **at least 4 hours** before exercise [S10, p.75]
- This allows time for excess fluid excretion via urine before competition begins
- **Do NOT over-hydrate**: Expanding extra- and intracellular compartments excessively increases risk of withdrawal from competition without physiological benefit [S10, p.75]

### During-Exercise Hydration
- Sodium- and potassium-containing fluids replace perspiration electrolyte losses [S10, p.76]
- **6–8% CHO-electrolyte beverages** recommended for exercise events lasting more than 1 hour [S10, p.76]
- Sodium stimulates thirst and fluid retention simultaneously
- CHO component provides additional energy source during high-intensity periods

### Post-Exercise Recovery Hydration
- **450–675 mL of liquid per 0.5 kg body weight lost** during exercise [S10, p.76]
- Rapid and complete recovery from dehydration requires this volume to restore plasma volume
- Balanced food and drink to restore electrolytes lost in sweat

### Hyponatremia Risk
- Can develop when sodium is not replaced despite large sweat volume, or when excessive water (without sodium) is consumed before, during, or after events [S10, p.76]
- More likely in athletes who perspire less or over-drink water
- Prevention: Include sodium in recovery fluids; avoid drinking only plain water in large volumes post-exercise

## High-Risk Conditions
- **Limited fluid availability or drinking opportunity**: Moderate risk for hypohydration
- **Requires frequent high-intensity efforts**: HIGH risk
- **Hot/humid environment**: Moderate-to-high risk
- Combined conditions: Maximum dehydration risk [S10, p.75]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Self-monitor sweat rate, pre-game body weight, and urine color
- **Guards**: Highest sprint/shuffle intensity → highest sweat rate per minute of play
- **Bigs**: Lower sprint frequency but more physical contact → still significant sweat loss

## Common Mistakes
1. **Drinking only water** during long games → no electrolyte replacement → hyponatremia risk; use sports drinks for games >1 hour
2. **Skipping pre-game hydration** → starting dehydrated compounds 4-quarter performance decline
3. **Over-hydrating pre-game** → gastrointestinal distress and bloating impair performance
4. **Ignoring match-day eating disruption** → reduced fluid intake on game days due to stress/travel is a known risk factor [S10, p.75]
5. **One-size-fits-all fluid protocol** → individual sweat rates and sodium losses vary widely; personalized approach needed

## Practical Protocol Summary
| Timing | Amount | Type |
|---|---|---|
| ≥4 h before exercise | 5–7 mL/kg body weight | Water or sports drink |
| During exercise (>1h) | Ad libitum based on thirst | 6–8% CHO-electrolyte |
| Post-exercise recovery | 450–675 mL / 0.5 kg BW lost | Na+/K+ containing fluid + food |
