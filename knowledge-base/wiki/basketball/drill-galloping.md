# Galloping

## Objective
Promote good hip projection and back-leg push-off; improve lead-leg mechanics and proper pawing (leg-cycle) mechanics through a galloping movement pattern with the trailing ankle locked.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Open flat surface — court, field, or track
- Distance: 20–30 meters per rep

## Execution
1. Begin galloping forward (like a horse gallop — one lead foot stays in front).
2. Keep the **trailing ankle locked** (dorsiflexed and stiff) to emphasize a **"spring-loaded" landing and take-off**.
3. Drive the hip of the lead leg forward with each stride.
4. Focus on the push-off from the back leg — this is the propulsive phase.
5. Alternate lead legs after each set.

## Coaching Points
- The locked trailing ankle forces the Achilles tendon and calf to act as a spring — this is the same spring-loaded mechanics used in efficient sprinting.
- "Push the ground away" with the back foot — each push-off should be powerful and complete.
- Hip projection is the goal: get the hip of the lead leg forward, not just the foot.
- This drill bridges skipping and sprinting — it teaches the athlete to transition from rhythmic movement to explosive projection.
- Combine with [[drill-skip-for-height]] and [[drill-bounding]] in a speed warm-up sequence.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Slow gallop, focus on locked ankle and push-off sensation
2. **Intermediate**: Moderate speed gallop, 25m, deliberate hip projection
3. **Advanced**: Fast gallop transitions to a sprint at 20m mark — gallop → sprint contrast

- [[stretch-shortening-cycle-plyometrics]] — locked ankle exploits the SSC in the calf and Achilles for elastic energy return
