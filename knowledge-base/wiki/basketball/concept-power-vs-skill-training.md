# Power vs. Skill Training — Twin Training Methodologies

## Summary
Not all explosive exercises are the same. Some build raw physical power — strength × speed — while others require the nervous system to *learn* a complex movement pattern. Treating these two categories the same way leads to suboptimal results and increased injury risk. Explosive Calisthenics (S9, Ch.11) introduces a clear framework for distinguishing **power moves** from **skill moves** and prescribes different training methodologies for each.

The analogy used is computing: power training builds better *hardware* (muscles, tendons, joints, muscle-nerve connections), while skill training upgrades the *software* (nervous system, brain, coordination, equilibrium, timing). Elite athletes need both. The correct sequence is to build hardware first, then layer in software.

## When to Use
- Designing explosive and athletic training programs for basketball players
- Deciding how to train a specific exercise (repetitive drilling vs. skill acquisition approach)
- Explaining to players why more reps ≠ more power for complex movements
- Periodizing athletic development from simple jumps → complex agility → sport-specific skill

## Key Principles

### Defining Power Moves
Power moves are *simple* — typically up-and-down or linear. They build: [S9, p.19]
- Speed-strength (force × velocity)
- Linear performance
- Joint integrity
- Muscle strength and the muscle-nerve connection

**Examples:** Vertical jump, clap push-ups, box jumps, broad jumps. You drill these *over and over* to build power and strengthen joints. The nervous system doesn't need to learn a new complex movement — it just needs more force.

### Defining Skill Moves
Skill moves are *complex* — involving rotation, partial rotation, or multiple movement angles. They build: [S9, p.19]
- Coordination
- Equilibrium
- Timing
- Nervous system and brain adaptations
- Mental focus and body wisdom

**Examples:** Back flip, front flip, kip-up. Many athletes have the physical equipment (power, speed, joint strength) to perform a back flip but cannot — because they lack the *skill*. The nervous system must learn the movement pattern; strength alone is insufficient. [S9, p.18]

### The Hardware vs. Software Analogy
- **Power training = hardware** — Works on muscles, soft tissues, nerves, and bones. Makes the physical body more capable.
- **Skill training = software** — Conditions the nervous system, brain, and mind. Teaches the body *how* to move.
- You need both. Build the hardware first, then upload better software. [S9, p.20]

### Which Explosive Chain Uses Which Method
From the Explosive Six chains: [S9, p.20]
| Exercise | Category |
|---|---|
| Jumps | Power |
| Power pushups | Power |
| Kip-ups | Skill |
| Front flips | Skill |
| Back flips | Skill |
| Muscle-ups | Blend — train as Skill for maximum explosiveness |
| Dip progressions | Neither — train as regular strength (low-moderate reps, low sets) |

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Identify whether the movement being trained is a power move or skill move before selecting a training approach. [S9, p.18]

## Variations
### Power Training Protocol
- Drill repeatedly at maximum effort and speed
- Rest fully between attempts
- Focus on force output, ground contact time, height/distance
- Progress via [[concept-parc-progression-principle]] when ready

### Skill Training Protocol
- Never train to fatigue — skill acquisition requires fresh, alert nervous system
- Focus on *quality* of movement pattern, not volume
- Use progressive step chains (easier progressions develop the movement groove before advancing)
- Confidence is critical — anxious athletes cannot learn movement skills effectively

## Common Mistakes
1. **Training skill moves for reps** → Fatigued nervous system cannot learn or refine complex movement patterns. Skill work must always be done fresh.
2. **Treating jumps like flips** → Vertical jumps can be drilled for volume; back flips cannot. Confusing the categories leads to poor skill acquisition and injury.
3. **Skipping the hardware phase** → Athletes who jump to complex skill work (flips, kip-ups) before building the power base lack the force production to complete the movement safely.
4. **Trying to force skill with repetition** → Repeating a movement you cannot yet do correctly trains the *wrong* pattern. Regress to build the skill groove.
