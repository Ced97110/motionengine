# Concussion Management in Basketball

## Summary
Sports-related concussion (SRC) is a significant medical concern in basketball at all levels — from youth recreation through the NBA and WNBA. SRC affects both genders and all age groups. The cornerstone of modern concussion management is a structured assessment battery at baseline and post-injury, immediate removal from play upon suspicion, and a carefully staged return-to-play (RTP) and return-to-learn (RTL) protocol supervised by a physician. The old "rest is best" model has been replaced by evidence-based early subsymptom threshold aerobic exercise, which reduces delayed recovery.

The NCAA, NBA, and WNBA all maintain formal concussion protocols updated nearly annually. Coaches and athletic trainers must be familiar with the signs, symptoms, and management pathway to protect athletes from the serious long-term consequences of premature return to play. [S10, pp.231-237]

## When to Use
- Any athlete who sustains a blow to the head or body that transmits force to the head
- Any athlete who reports or displays signs of SRC: headache, dizziness, fogginess, visual disturbance, amnesia, balance problems, or behavioral change
- Any athlete where SRC is suspected — err on the side of caution and remove from play
- After any identified SRC to guide the recovery and return-to-play timeline

## Key Principles
1. **Remove immediately**: An athlete with suspected SRC must not return to activity on the same day, and must be cleared by a physician before beginning any RTP protocol. [S10, p.232]
2. **Err on the side of caution**: Delayed reporting leads to significantly more symptoms, greater severity, longer duration, and more time lost. Immediate removal also decreases additional head impacts in the acute period. [S10, p.234]
3. **Early subsymptom threshold aerobic exercise beats complete rest**: Prescribing subsymptom threshold exercise within 1 week of SRC using the Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test (BCTT) reduces incidence of delayed recovery beyond 30 days without worsening symptoms. Target HR = 80% of HR at symptom onset during BCTT, 20 min/day, 6–7 days/week. [S10, p.232]
4. **Recovery timeline**: Most adults and older adolescents resolve SRC symptoms in ~2 weeks. Younger athletes average ~4 weeks. More severe initial symptoms = longer recovery. [S10, p.231]
5. **Headache and depression are risk factors for prolonged recovery** (>1 month). Pre-injury depression and migraine history also increase recovery time. ADHD/learning disabilities do not increase time but may require extra RTL interventions. [S10, p.232]
6. **Return-to-learn before or simultaneously with return-to-play**: School-aged athletes should not return to sport if they cannot return to the classroom. [S10, p.233]
7. **Repetitive SRC compounds risk**: Athletes with one concussion are 3× more likely to suffer another. Repetitive SRC also increases lower-extremity musculoskeletal injury risk, particularly in female athletes. [S10, p.234]

## Assessment Tools

### SCAT5 / mBESS (Balance Error Scoring System)
Balance-based neurocognitive sideline tool. Full BESS uses both firm and foam surfaces. SCAT5 uses modified version (mBESS, firm only). Moderate-to-good reliability. Full BESS preferred for greater diagnostic accuracy. [S10, p.231]

### King-Devick Test (K-D)
2-minute test: athlete reads single-digit numbers on cards or tablet. Positive = time to complete post-injury is longer than baseline. Validated in basketball athletes. Note: learning curve exists — practice baseline is important. Alternative: MULES test (Mobile Universal Lexicon Evaluation System) uses colored images with similar diagnostic promise. [S10, p.231]

### Computer-Based Neurocognitive Testing
Tools: ImPACT, ANAM, Concussion Sentinel. Measure attention span, working memory, sustained/selective attention time, non-verbal problem solving, and reaction time plus symptom scale. Compare post-injury to baseline. Debate exists regarding test-retest reliability. Further research needed. [S10, p.231]

### Vestibular Ocular Motor Screen (VOMS)
5–10 minute evaluation of vestibular and ocular function. Clinician evaluates: smooth pursuits, saccades, convergence, head-stationary object fixation, head-moving object tracking. Positive = symptom provocation (headache, dizziness, nausea). No ceiling effect — can be used serially. Particularly useful for athletes with vestibular-specific deficits. [S10, p.231]

## 6-Stage Return-to-Play Protocol

Each stage takes approximately 24 hours. Complete protocol takes ~1 week. Athlete must be symptom-free before advancing. If symptoms return, drop back to previous stage. [S10, p.233]

| Stage | Activity | Goal |
|-------|----------|------|
| 1 | Symptom-limited activity — normal daily life + subsymptom threshold exercise (BCTT prescription) | Baseline re-establishment |
| 2 | Light aerobic exercise — walking, stationary bike (no resistance training) | Increase heart rate |
| 3 | Basketball-specific exercise — dribbling, shoot-around, free throws (no contact) | Movement + elevated HR |
| 4 | Non-contact basketball drills — passing, offensive/defensive sets, OOB plays | Coordination + cognitive load |
| 5 | Full contact practice — physician clearance required first | Restore confidence and assess function |
| 6 | Return to game | Full competition |

## Return-to-Learn (RTL) Modifications
For school-aged athletes, RTL should occur simultaneously with or before RTP:
- Breaks during the school day
- Reduced in-class and homework assignments
- Extended time for assignments and tests
- Testing in quiet, distraction-free environments
- Limited computer/screen time
- Avoid loud environments (cafeteria, music class)

> "Athletes should not be returned to sport if they are unable to return to the classroom." [S10, p.233]

## Complications

### Postconcussion Syndrome
Defined as symptoms persisting beyond expected recovery window (>2 weeks adults, >4 weeks children). Subsymptom threshold aerobic exercise now preferred over complete rest for prolonged recovery. [S10, p.234]

### Repetitive SRC
- 3× increased risk of subsequent concussion after the first
- Lower threshold force needed for subsequent concussions
- Increased lower-extremity musculoskeletal injury risk (greater in female athletes) — thought to be due to subclinical neurocognitive effects impairing anticipation of contact

### Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy (CTE)
No direct link established between CTE and concussions; research focuses on repetitive brain trauma (RBT). CTE = progressive neurodegenerative disease with behavior, mood, and cognitive symptoms. Exposure to RBT is one of the largest risk factors. Most CTE research has used retired NFL athletes; limited basketball-specific data exists. [S10, p.234]

## Prevention Strategies
1. **Education**: NCAA requires schools to provide educational materials and athlete signature forms. CDC HEADS UP campaign. [S10, p.234]
2. **Rule changes**: NBA restricted area under the basket reduces charge-taking collisions. [S10, p.234]
3. **Legislation**: All 50 US states have concussion legislation. CSIG implemented nationally and internationally. NBA has a formal concussion protocol. [S10, p.234]
4. **Increased athletic trainer coverage**: 86% increase in collegiate ATs from 1995–2005, improving immediate removal and RTP protocol adherence. [S10, p.234]
5. **Primary prevention gap**: Further research needed on risk factors (prior SRC history, female sex), mechanisms (player-to-player contact), and equipment strategies. [S10, p.234]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Report concussion symptoms immediately. Do not hide symptoms to remain in play — delayed reporting significantly worsens outcomes. Wear properly fitted headwear when available. Follow physician-supervised RTP protocol fully.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Same-day return to play** → Never allow; always remove athlete and seek physician evaluation before any return.
2. **Complete rest until fully symptom-free** → Outdated; early subsymptom threshold aerobic exercise is evidence-based and superior.
3. **Skipping RTL planning for school-aged athletes** → RTL must be coordinated with RTP; academic function is a prerequisite for return to sport.
4. **Advancing RTP stages too quickly** → Each stage requires ~24 hours minimum; any symptom recurrence requires dropping back.
5. **Using computer-based neurocognitive tests without a proper baseline** → Without baseline, post-injury comparison is unreliable.
