# One-Arm Back Handspring

## Objective
Reduce arm dependence during the back handspring by landing on only one pivot arm, forcing the athlete to rely primarily on leg power and momentum — the critical transition toward the no-hands back flip (Step 8).

## Setup
- 1 player
- Flat or cushioned surface
- No equipment required
- **Prerequisite**: solid, high back handspring (Step 7) [S9, p.12]

## Execution
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, hands held high.
2. Dip down at knees and hips, swinging arms down and behind. Keep looking forward. Dip "as if sitting in a chair."
3. Explode up and backwards diagonally, swinging hands above you.
4. As you jump, look behind you; slight back arch helps rotation. Hands follow line of vision.
5. As you become inverted, **reach out with ONE arm** to make contact with the floor directly below your head. Keep the other arm pulled closer to your head.
6. The single arm contact is brief — just a momentary pivot.
7. Once legs reach vertical, whip feet down to ground rapidly.
8. When feet are planted, catch balance and extend body again.

## Coaching Points
- This step is NOT strictly required to learn the back flip — "some back-flippers can't do this" — but it is a great training drill because it forces athletes to stop relying on arms [S9, p.12]
- The loaded arm "should really only act as a pivot, for a fraction of a second" — progressively touch it down quicker and with less force [S9, p.12]
- If the two-arm back handspring is already "good and high," this step can be skipped [S9, p.12]
- Watch for compensatory lean — athletes often tilt too far toward the loaded arm

## Progressions
1. **Beginner (Regression)**: Close hands, asymmetrical hands — gradually work toward full one-arm [S9, p.12]
2. **Intermediate**: One-arm contact with significant loading
3. **Advanced**: One arm barely grazes the floor; minimal force transfer through the arm [S9, p.12]
