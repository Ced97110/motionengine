# Rotational Medicine Ball Throw Test (RMBT)

## Summary
The Rotational Medicine Ball Throw (RMBT) measures rotational power capabilities — a highly relevant quality in basketball due to the frequency of rotational actions during play (direction changes, drives, post moves, rebounding rotations). Beyond performance, rotational trunk control is also an important ACL injury risk factor; the RMBT can identify functional rotational asymmetries that may predispose athletes to injury. Both right-to-left and left-to-right throws are collected to detect side-to-side differences [S11].

No normative or descriptive data are currently available for basketball athletes at any level — making pre-/post-testing and left/right symmetry comparison the primary applications.

## When to Use
- Pre-season rotational power baseline
- ACL injury risk screening (identify left-right asymmetries)
- Monitoring rotational power development in response to training
- Post-injury return-to-sport assessment (compare to pre-injury baseline)

## Key Principles
1. **Test both directions** — always collect right-to-left and left-to-right trials; the asymmetry score may matter more than the absolute distance
2. **Use a 3–4 kg (6–8 lb) medicine ball** — heavy enough to require full-body contribution, light enough to move at sport-relevant velocities
3. **Load position is triple flexion** — ankles, knees, and hips flexed; trunk rotated opposite to throw direction; ball held at the outside knee
4. **Full rotational explosion** — drive from legs, through trunk, and out the arms; not an arm-only throw
5. **Standardize starting position** — mark foot position to ensure reproducibility between trials and athletes
6. **≥2 min rest between trials** — rotational power is highly effort-dependent

## Equipment
- 3–4 kg (6–8 lb) medicine ball
- Long tape measure (laid flat, perpendicular to starting line)
- Open space long enough for maximum-effort throws
- Optional: medicine ball with built-in accelerometer (valid and reliable for upper-body power assessment)

## Setup
- Mark starting line clearly
- Tape measure runs perpendicular to the starting line
- Ensure adequate clearance in the throw direction (no bystanders in range)

## Testing Protocol
1. Athlete positions feet perpendicular to the tape measure, parallel to the starting line
2. **Load position:** triple flexion (ankles, knees, hips) + trunk rotated away from throw direction; ball held at outside knee (figure 3.2a)
3. Athlete rotates and explodes up and away, throwing with both hands as far as possible (figure 3.2b)
4. Measure from starting line to first point of ball contact with the floor
5. Collect at least one trial each direction (right-to-left and left-to-right); ≥2 min rest between trials
6. Perform pre- and post-testing comparisons for progress tracking

## Interpreting Results
- **Absolute distance**: track improvement over training blocks
- **Asymmetry index**: (stronger side − weaker side) / stronger side × 100%; asymmetries >15% may signal injury risk or training imbalances
- No normative data available — all comparisons must be within-athlete or within-team [S11]

## Common Mistakes
1. Using an arm-dominant throw rather than a full-body rotation → invalidates rotational power measurement; cue "drive from the legs"
2. Inconsistent foot position → standardize with floor markings
3. Not collecting bilateral data → misses asymmetry information, which may be the most important output
4. Heavy ball that limits velocity → 3–4 kg is the tested range; heavier loads shift to strength, not rotational power

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: rotational power is universal — every position benefits from trunk rotation training
- **PG, SG**: rotational power drives crossover drives, euro steps, and direction changes
- **PF, C**: post moves, box-outs, and pivot-based scoring all require rotational trunk power
