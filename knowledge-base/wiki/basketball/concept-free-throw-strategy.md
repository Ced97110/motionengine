# Free Throw Strategy and Teaching Framework

## Summary
Free-throw shooting is among the most strategically important skills in basketball. The unguarded 15-foot shot is the one moment when the entire focus of the game lands on a single player — with no place to hide. Every coach must treat free-throw development as a daily challenge, not an afterthought. Data from the 2010 and 2011 NCAA tournaments shows repeatedly that teams lost close games specifically because of missed free throws [S13, pp.118-121].

The inside-out offensive philosophy — attacking the block first, then kicking out only when the post has no scoring opportunity — is directly tied to maximizing free-throw opportunities. Coach Dean Smith's famous hierarchy was: (1) Score, (2) Get fouled and score, (3) Get a good shot — with a preference for getting fouled while scoring [S13, p.119].

## When to Use
- **Daily practice**: Free-throw routine must be treated as an everyday fundamental at all levels
- **Close game situations**: Coaches must know which players are reliable under pressure, not just in non-pressure situations
- **Inside-out possessions**: Entering the post first increases foul-drawing opportunities before kicking to perimeter shooters
- **Late-game scenarios**: Being a dependable free-throw shooter is one of the most valuable late-game assets a player can provide

## Key Principles
1. **Three requirements** for a player to improve at free throws:
   - Proper shooting mechanics (balance, grip, aim, release, follow-through)
   - Concentration
   - Ability to overcome pressure — the hardest of the three [S13, p.118]
2. **Establish consistency**: A uniform, consistent attitude toward the unguarded free throw must be developed early and reinforced daily
3. **Inside-out strategy**: Get the ball inside first → if no scoring opportunity, pass back outside → this sequence draws more fouls than shooting perimeter jumpers directly [S13, p.119]
4. **Pressure simulation in practice**: The only way to develop pressure-free-throw shooters is to simulate pressure in practice (competitive games, penalty stakes)
5. **Know your roster**: Coaches must identify which players are reliable under pressure vs. which can only hit in non-pressure situations
6. **Historical context**: Dr. James Naismith originally valued the free throw at 3 points (not 2) and set it at 20 feet — evidence that the skill has always been recognized as critical [S13, p.119]

## Data: Free Throws in Postseason Play
From the 2010 and 2011 NCAA Tournaments [S13, Table 5.1]:
- **2011**: Louisville lost by 1 (9 FTs missed, 7-16); Kentucky lost by 1 (8 FTs missed, 4-12); Pittsburgh lost by 1 (6 FTs missed, 12-18)
- **2010**: Texas lost by 1 (13 FTs missed, 20-33); Vanderbilt lost by 1 (12 FTs missed, 17-29); Kentucky lost by 7 (13 FTs missed, 16-29)
- Pattern: every team that lost a close game missed 4-13 free throws

Teams with high free-throw attempt volume and winning records in 2009-2010 include Duke (33-5, 75.9% FT), Butler (32-4, 74.8%), Kansas (33-3, 70.1%), and Kentucky (33-3, 68.0%) [S13, Table 5.2].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating free throws as self-practice only** → coaches must build in structured, supervised FT practice with accountability
2. **Never simulating pressure** → players who only practice uncontested FTs fail in close games
3. **Assuming mechanics are automatic** → mechanics must be rehearsed daily even for experienced shooters
4. **Ignoring the inside-out connection** → teams that skip the post and shoot jumpers first get fewer FT opportunities
5. **Setting numbers too high for poor shooters** → making 5 or 10 in a row is unrealistic for weak FT shooters; adjust the goal accordingly [S13, p.121]
