# Shooting Drill Design Principles

## Summary
Every shooting drill must be game-realistic, time-efficient, and competitive. The design of shooting drills is not about getting shots up — it is about simulating the exact conditions under which those shots will be taken in games. This means correct passer locations, shooter starting points that require cuts, game-speed tempo enforced by the clock, and pre-set standards for both volume and accuracy.

The by-product of well-designed, high-tempo shooting drills is conditioning. When all players are competing at full game speed — shooters, passers, and rebounders — the physical demands of practice naturally exceed the demands of most games. [S6, pp.16-18]

## When to Use
- When designing any shooting drill for practice
- When evaluating whether current shooting drills are transferring to games
- When building a program-wide shooting development system

## Key Principles
1. **Game-location specificity** — study your offense to identify where shots will come from; start shooters at their initial positions and require cuts to the actual shot spots [S6, p.18]
2. **Game-pass specificity** — passers must use the same pass types (chest, bounce, skip, entry) used in the corresponding game action [S6, p.18]
3. **Pre-set quantity standards** — every drill has a specific number of attempts required [S6, p.17]
4. **Pre-set quality standards** — every drill has a specific number of makes required [S6, p.17]
5. **Clock as universal opponent** — scoreboard clock gives every player a visible, common adversary; time limits prevent comfort and slow tempo [S6, p.17]
6. **Stress both quantity AND accuracy** — quantity forces game speed; accuracy prevents sloppy mechanics. "Be quick, but don't be in a hurry." [S6, p.17]
7. **Accelerate all three roles** — not just the shooter; passers and rebounders must operate at full game speed [S6, p.17]
8. **Competition builds game realism** — rewards for winners, light penalties for losers (sprints, push-ups, sit-ups) [S6, p.17]
9. **Shoot free throws after strenuous drills** — players must experience shooting while winded; this is when game free throws actually occur [S6, p.5]
10. **Integrate multiple skills** — every shooting drill should also train passing, rebounding, cutting, coming off screens, catching, and pivoting [S6, p.17]
11. **Dummy defenders add realism** — managers with hands up in front of the shooter simulate contest pressure without disrupting the drill flow [S6, p.18]
12. **Conditioning is a byproduct** — when quantity and quality standards are met at game speed, conditioning occurs naturally [S6, p.18]

## Common Mistakes
1. Stationary shooters waiting for passes → require cuts before every catch
2. Passers standing in unrealistic spots → place passers where they actually pass in games
3. Drills without time limits → players pace themselves; use the scoreboard clock
4. Only tracking makes, not attempts → volume forces pace; track both
5. Shooting drills done at the start of practice, before fatigue → insert shooting immediately after conditioning segments
