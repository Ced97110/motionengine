# Defenses and the Offenses That Break Them

## Summary
A coach-grade catalog of the defensive systems a team will see over a season, paired with the offensive sets and actions that most reliably break each one. Organization is by family: **man-to-man variants**, **zone defenses**, **junk (combination) defenses**, **pressing defenses**, and **special-situation defense**. Each entry documents setup, what the defense takes away, its structural weakness, and the offensive actions that exploit that weakness.

Categorization principle: a defense is grouped by how it *assigns* defenders. Man variants assign a defender to each offensive player and differ only in how on-ball screens, gaps, and post catches are covered. Zones assign defenders to floor areas. Junk defenses blend the two — some players are in zone areas, one or two chase assigned offensive players. Presses are defined by where on the court the pressure starts, not the assignment logic. Special-situation defense is defined by the game state (inbounds, late clock, scramble) rather than alignment.

Use this page as a scouting and game-plan reference. When preparing for an opponent, identify the defenses they deploy, then pull the listed counters and build your practice script from them.

## How to Read an Entry
Every entry has six sections:
1. **Name and aliases** — common terminology
2. **Setup** — alignment and primary responsibilities
3. **Strengths** — what the defense takes away
4. **Weaknesses** — structural vulnerabilities the offense can target
5. **Offenses that break it** — specific sets, actions, or principles, with the reason each works
6. **Citation** — only where the content is structurally verified against the source library

## Part 1 — Man-to-Man Variants

Every man variant starts from the same assignment rule: a defender per offensive player. They differ in how the defense treats on-ball screens, post catches, and gap help.

### 1.1 Standard Man-to-Man
**Aliases**: straight man, pack-nothing man.

**Setup**: Each defender guards an assigned offensive player. On-ball defender applies pressure; off-ball defenders hold ball-you-man vision with help at the nail and ball-side elbow. Post is fronted or three-quartered depending on ball position. [S1, pp.42-43]

**Strengths**: Clear accountability. Takes away easy perimeter catches when ball pressure is maintained. Fronting the low post eliminates interior entry. Closeouts are short because each defender starts near their man.

**Weaknesses**: Screens. Every screen forces a rotation decision (switch, hedge, fight through) and each choice has a counter. Weak-side help is thinned because every defender already has an assignment.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Ball screens with a shooting big** — forces the big's defender to commit to either the ball or the pop; drop coverage concedes the pop, hedge concedes the roll.
- **Stagger and pindown for a shooter** — second screen cleans up the switch; the defender cannot fight over two screens and stay attached.
- **Flex continuity** — the cross-screen/down-screen combination attacks the switch decision on every action; one misstep is a layup.
- **Dribble handoffs (DHO) into a second action** — chained actions stress the defender's ability to recover before the next screen arrives.

**Citation**: [S1, pp.42-43] — Herb Brown, basic rules of defense.

### 1.2 Pack Line (Shrink-and-Pack)
**Aliases**: pack, gap-help man, shrink-and-pack.

**Setup**: Off-ball defenders sag one step inside the three-point line into a "pack," regardless of where the ball is on the perimeter. On-ball defender applies pressure without gambling. Help on straight-line drives is pre-loaded because the gap is already there.

**Strengths**: Suffocates dribble penetration. Eliminates interior catches. Takes away the mid-range. Keeps the paint walled off on drive-and-kick. "Shrink the court and pack the paint." [S1, p.11]

**Weaknesses**: Concedes the perimeter catch. Closeouts are long. A skilled catch-and-shoot attack punishes the sag.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Five-out spacing with quick ball reversal** — forces the pack to close out across long distances; the second pass is usually open.
- **Skip passes (corner to weak-side wing)** — the pack's weak-side help is the furthest from the ball; a skip beats the closeout.
- **Flare screens off ball reversal** — the defender sagging to the pack is late on the closeout and cannot navigate a flare on top.
- **Gut cuts from the weak-side wing through the nail** — the nail defender is sagging deep; a backcut through that gap is open.
- **DHO into pick-and-pop** — the big's defender is already in the pack; a pop to the three line catches the defender retreating.

**Citation**: [S1, p.11, p.14, p.216] — Herb Brown documents "shrink and pack" as a philosophy-level alternative to pressure-extension ("shrink the court and pack the paint"; "half-court shrink, packed zone"). The modern "pack line" is the structured form of this philosophy; specific system-level rules (where exactly the pack line sits, on-ball technique) are not prescribed in the source library.

### 1.3 Switch-Everything
**Aliases**: switch all, one-through-five switch.

**Setup**: Any screen is switched, regardless of mismatch. Defenders must be close in size and agility. Pre-switch communication happens before contact. After the switch, the new on-ball defender does not concede the drive. Late in games, "switch out on ALL picks — put in a lineup that can switch readily." [S7, p.267]

**Strengths**: Kills the screener's advantage. Forces the offense into isolations. Simplifies rotations — no help decisions needed on screens. Best when equal-sized players are in the PnR. [S7, pp.257-260]

**Weaknesses**: Creates mismatches. A slipped screen produces a 4-on-3 short because the switch has not completed. Post-ups after a switch put a guard on a big — Del Harris's big-player rule: "X4 traps down for X3 being posted." [S7, pp.257-260]

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Slip screens** — the screener's defender is preparing to switch; a slip before the screen creates a live cut with no help.
- **Re-screen into post-up** — after the first switch creates a size mismatch, a second screen plants the big on the smaller defender near the block.
- **Ghost screen into pull-up** — the screener fakes the screen and pops; if the defenders call the switch anyway, the original ball-handler has a clean driving lane.
- **Horns double-drag with one shooter, one roller** — the second screen catches the defense mid-switch; the roller is unguarded for a split second.
- **Quick elevator or staggered screens for a shooter** — switching defenders collide; the shooter slips between them.

**Citation**: [S7, pp.257-260] — Del Harris, switch coverage and mismatch-trap-down rule; [S13, pp.210, 216] — switch as one of five PnR coverage options and the Dallas-vs-elite-offense example.

### 1.4 ICE / Force-Baseline (Overplay-Down-to-Baseline)
**Aliases**: blue, down, push, force-down, overplay-down-to-baseline.

**Setup**: On side pick-and-rolls, the on-ball defender positions on the top side (high side) of the ball-handler to force the ball toward the baseline, away from the screen. The screener's defender positions 2-3 steps below the picker in the direction the ball must be pushed. Weak-side defenders rotate to tag the roller and cover the corner. [S7, pp.257-260, Figures 16.4-16.7]

**Strengths**: Takes away the middle, which is the most dangerous pick-and-roll action. Keeps the ball on one side — easier to load weak-side help. Forces the ball-handler into a contested baseline or a pickup. Most effective on wing and corner PnR. [S7, p.258]

**Weaknesses**: Concedes the corner three on rotation. The baseline-side corner is the help defender's man — a kick to that corner is open if the tag is late. If the on-ball defender loses contact, the dribbler splits the trap. [S7, p.258]

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Reject the screen** — if the on-ball defender over-commits to the top side, the ball-handler goes the opposite direction, into the screen's original side, with no help.
- **Screen-the-screener (STS)** — a second screener pins the dropping big, opening the roll to the rim.
- **Spain pick-and-roll** (PnR with a back-screen on the drop defender) — the back-screener erases the low tagger; the roll is open.
- **Weak-side 45-cut to the dunker spot** — as the baseline help rotates to tag the roll, the back-cut from the weak wing attacks the vacated area.
- **Corner lift into flare** — the help defender is committed low; a lift from the corner creates a catch-and-shoot on the wing.
- **Split the trap** — if the ball defender loses body contact, a dribble split goes between X1 and X5. [S7, p.258]

**Citation**: [S7, pp.257-260, Figures 16.4-16.7] — Del Harris, Overplay-Down-to-Baseline coverage. Also [S7, p.259, Figure 16.9] — push-to-baseline-trap in isolation defense.

### 1.5 Drop Coverage (Ball Screen)
**Aliases**: soft drop, deep drop, soft coverage.

**Setup**: On any pick-and-roll, the screener's defender retreats into the paint below the level of the screen. The on-ball defender fights over the top and funnels the ball-handler into the drop defender. The rim is protected; the mid-range pull-up is conceded. In Del Harris's terms: "X1 drops back even with O5, avoiding the screen and playing soft to keep O1 in front — allows X2/X3 to stay home and reduces three-point and draw-and-kick opportunities." [S13, pp.212-213]

**Strengths**: Eliminates the roll to the rim. Keeps the big near the basket for rebounds. Simple assignments; low communication load. Reduces three-point and kick-out opportunities. [S13, pp.212-213]

**Weaknesses**: Concedes the mid-range pull-up and the pick-and-pop. A shooting big punishes the drop every possession.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Pick-and-pop with a stretch big** — the drop defender cannot close out; the pop is a clean three.
- **Pull-up mid-range from a skilled guard** — the drop defender is too deep to contest; the on-ball defender is behind the screen.
- **Double-drag screen** — the second screen frees the ball-handler for a rhythm three over the drop.
- **Short-roll into 4-on-3** — roller catches at the elbow; the remaining three defenders cannot cover four offensive players behind him.
- **Snake dribble** — the ball-handler re-attacks the screen with a change of direction; the drop defender is out of position.

**Citation**: [S13, pp.212-213] — soft/drop coverage on middle PnR; [S1, pp.127-128] — "drop" listed among interlocking PnR techniques.

### 1.6 Hedge and Recover (Contact Show)
**Aliases**: hedge, soft show, flat hedge, contact show, help-and-recover.

**Setup**: The screener's defender steps out to the level of the screen, contacts the screener while jumping out to stop the dribbler, holds for 0-3 steps, then recovers to the screener. The on-ball defender fights over and re-attaches. In Del Harris's vocabulary: **hard help** jumps at 90 degrees to prevent the corner; **soft help** covers the turn at 45 degrees, zone-like over ball-handler and foul lane box. [S7, pp.255-256]

**Strengths**: Stops the pull-up three. Prevents the ball-handler from turning the corner. Buys time for the on-ball defender to recover. Stops ball reversal when the screener's defender "contact shows and steps out." [S1, pp.127-128]

**Weaknesses**: The screener's defender is out of position for a split second; the roll lane is open until recovery. Ball reversals during the hedge expose the weak side. Soft help concedes the jump shot over the pick or the roll. [S7, p.256]

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Horns flare** — as the big hedges on the PnR, the weak-side big flares for a shooter; the hedge has emptied the ball-side top and the flare is unguarded.
- **Slip the screen** — screener slips before setting; rolls into the paint before the hedge can form.
- **Re-screen** — after the first hedge is absorbed, the screener re-screens on the recovery; the second screen catches the big out of position.
- **Quick ball-reversal after the hedge** — skip to weak side while the big is recovering; no rim protection on the kick.
- **Empty-corner PnR** — clears the corner tagger; the roll lane has no second line of help.

**Citation**: [S7, pp.255-256] — Del Harris, help-and-recover (hard/soft); [S13, p.210] — hedge and hard-hedge as two of five PnR technique options; [S1, pp.127-128, 236] — Herb Brown, contact show.

### 1.7 Blitz / Trap the Pick-and-Roll
**Aliases**: hard show, double, trap on the ball screen, trap coverage.

**Setup**: On-ball defender and screener's defender double-team the ball-handler at the screen. The other three defenders rotate in an X-rotation: X4 rotates to cover the rolling 5 as he sees the ball released; X3 provides interim help; X5 rotates opposite the pass into the paint and picks up the open man. [S7, pp.257-258, Figure 16.1]

**Strengths**: Eliminates the ball-handler's shot and drive. Forces a pass under duress. Works when the ball-handler is the offense's engine. Most aggressive version: screener's defender traps BEFORE the pick is set, forcing early decisions. [S7, p.258]

**Weaknesses**: Creates a 4-on-3 behind the trap. The pass out of the trap attacks three defenders covering four; the skip pass is a direct score.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Short roll** — screener rolls to the elbow, not the rim; catches in the middle with a 4-on-3 read: skip to weak-side three, lob, or dump to low help's man.
- **Spain screen into short roll** — back-screen erases the low tagger; the short-roll big has no rotation to contest the pass.
- **Invert the screen** — put the ball-handler at the 4 or 5 spot and have a wing set the ball screen; the trap creates worse mismatches on the kick.
- **Reject the screen** — the ball-handler refuses the screen, splitting the blitz before it forms.
- **Horns with two shooters high** — both big defenders in the trap; the two kick-out options are unguarded three-point shots.
- **Never abandon a trap mid-possession** — the counter the defense gives up is itself: "abandoning a trap halfway is worse than not trapping at all." [S13, p.210]

**Citation**: [S7, pp.257-258, Figure 16.1] — Del Harris, trap coverage; [S1, pp.127-128, p.234] — Herb Brown, "blitz the pick and roll" glossary entry and PnR figures 8.33-8.34; [S13, p.210] — trap as one of five PnR technique options.

### 1.8 No-Middle
**Aliases**: force-sideline, weak (in some systems).

**Setup**: On-ball pressure angled to force the dribbler toward the sideline on every catch. All help is stacked on the middle. The sideline acts as a sixth defender.

**Strengths**: Takes away the paint. Shrinks the offense into the smaller half of the court. Makes help rotations shorter.

**Weaknesses**: Concedes the baseline drive and the corner three. If the sideline force breaks down once, the on-ball defender is trailing with no middle help loaded for the opposite direction.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Baseline drives from the wing** — if the on-ball defender's stance is too aggressive toward the middle, a hard first step baseline beats them.
- **Skip passes to the weak-side corner** — the help is loaded middle; the weak corner is the farthest help rotation.
- **Ball reversal into weak-side PnR** — the defense is shaded to the original ball side; a quick swing creates a clean screen on the new strong side before the defense re-loads.
- **Dribble-at handoffs from the wing to the corner** — the on-ball defender has been forcing sideline; a handoff back toward the middle catches them out of stance.

**Citation**: No structural citation in the source library. "Channel and influence ball direction" is a general principle in [S1, p.11], but no source prescribes a full-team no-middle system end-to-end. **Citation omitted.**

## Part 2 — Zone Defenses

Zones assign defenders to areas of the floor, not to players. Every zone has seams (where two defenders' areas meet) and dead spots (the corner, the short corner, the high post) that the offense can exploit with patient ball and player movement.

### 2.1 2-3 Zone
**Aliases**: 2-3.

**Setup**: Two guards at the top of the key, two forwards at the elbows extended to the wings, one center under the basket. Top two defend the two guards and the high post. Wing defenders cover wings and corners. Center covers the low post and both short corners. Common as the fall-back zone after a full-court press: "full-court press with 3 man-to-man perimeter defenders + 2 tallest players zoning down floor — all five fall back into a 2-3 zone." [S1, p.178]

**Strengths**: Protects the paint. Defends the post without a size mismatch. Controls defensive rebounds because the frontline is in rebound position. Useful as a press-to-zone retreat. [S1, p.178]

**Weaknesses**: The short corner (baseline between the block and the corner) is a dead spot. The high post (free-throw line) splits all five defenders. The opposite corner is far from help. Most coaches prepare against it, so it is less disruptive than gimmick zones. [S13, p.252]

**Offenses that break it**:
- **High-post flash with a skilled passer** — catches in the soft middle; reads the collapse and hits the short corner or the opposite corner.
- **Overload ball side** — four offensive players on one side creates a 4-on-3; the far guard or the center is forced to rotate and one offensive player is always open.
- **Ball reversal to skip pass** — swing from corner to opposite wing; the weak-side top defender is late on the closeout.
- **Short-corner post-up** — the center must choose between the short corner and the low post; whichever is vacated is a score.
- **1-4 high alignment** — spreads the top of the zone; creates gaps at the elbows for dribble penetration.

**Citation**: [S1, p.178] — 2-3 zone as press-to-zone retreat; [S13, p.252] — 2-3 as the "standard" zone most offenses prepare against; countering principles (overload, ball reversal, seams, high-post flash) verified in [S13, pp.252-257].

### 2.2 3-2 Zone
**Aliases**: 3-2.

**Setup**: Three defenders across the top (point and two wings), two defenders below (block and short corner). Point defends the top of the key; wings cover wings and corners; baseline two cover the post and weak-side help.

**Strengths**: Strong on the perimeter; three defenders across the arc take away the three-point shot. Quick closeouts to the wings.

**Weaknesses**: Vulnerable inside. Only two defenders in the paint. The high post and the low post are both catchable. Most offenses prepare for it. [S13, p.252]

**Offenses that break it**:
- **High-post entry** — a skilled 5 or 4 at the free-throw line catches against a single defender; can shoot, pass, or drive.
- **Low-post duck-in** — the back-line two defenders cannot cover both blocks simultaneously.
- **Drive and kick from the wing** — one dribble past the wing defender collapses the back line; a kick to the weak wing is open.
- **Ball screen at the top of the key** — the point defender is isolated; a screen forces a rotation the 3-2 is not built for.
- **Dribble drag across the top** — stretches the three perimeter defenders horizontally; cracks open the lane to the high post.

**Citation**: [S13, p.252] — 3-2 grouped with 2-3 as "standard" prepared-against zones. No structural 3-2 system is documented in the source library; counter principles are general zone-offense principles from [S13, pp.252-257]. System-level citation omitted.

---

### 2.3 1-3-1 Half-Court Zone
**Aliases**: 1-3-1.

**Setup**: One point defender at the top, three across the middle (two wings and a middle), one baseline runner underneath. Point traps the ball at the wing with the wing defender. Baseline runner slides corner to corner. "Particularly effective for trapping corners and denying high-post entry." [S1, p.184]

**Strengths**: Traps the wings and the corners aggressively. Keeps the offense on the perimeter. Eliminates layups when the middle defender stays home. Denies high-post entry. [S1, p.184]

**Weaknesses**: The short corner on the weak side is the deadest spot on the floor. The baseline runner cannot be in both corners. The high post at the free-throw line splits the middle defender's coverage.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **LA guard-to-forward set** — misdirection: ball starts one side, reverses to the other; the baseline runner is caught shifting. Includes a lob to the 5 posting behind the line. [S13, pp.253-254]
- **Overload with a post at the short corner** — on the weak side the baseline runner cannot arrive in time; a skip pass creates a short-corner catch.
- **Flash to the high post** — splits the wing and middle defenders; the flasher reads the rotation and hits the weakest help.
- **Skip pass from corner to opposite wing** — the weak-wing defender has been tilted toward the middle; the skip clears a closeout rhythm three.
- **Post at the nail + baseline cut** — the middle defender is committed to the nail catch; the baseline cutter scores on the dump-down.

**Citation**: [S1, p.184] — 1-3-1 half-court zone after missed shots; [S13, pp.253-254, Figures 9.20-9.21] — LA guard-to-forward misdirection set. See also [[play-versus-1-3-1]].

### 2.4 1-2-2 Zone
**Aliases**: 1-2-2, jug zone.

**Setup**: One point defender, two wings, two baseline defenders. Similar to a 3-2 but the point is a single defender and the wings are deeper.

**Strengths**: Strong along the arc. Good for perimeter closeouts. Protects against shooters.

**Weaknesses**: Weak at the high post and weak at the low block. Only two defenders below the free-throw line.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **High-post entry and pitch** — the point defender is alone at the top; a flash to the high post puts a passer in the soft spot with four scorers around him.
- **Overload the baseline** — two shooters and a post on one side; two defenders cannot cover three.
- **Corner skip and crack-down screen** — the weak-side baseline defender is cracked down on by a cutter; the skip arrives into a clean catch.
- **Ball screen at the point** — the point defender has no peer; a ball screen with a pop creates a 2-on-1 on the wing.

**Citation**: No structural citation in the source library. **Citation omitted.**

---

### 2.5 2-1-2 Zone
**Aliases**: 2-1-2.

**Setup**: Two guards up top, one center in the free-throw area, two forwards on the baseline. The center is the rover at the high post and the low post. Herb Brown's specific rule for overloads: "when opponents overload one side: best defensive forward covers the weak side and is instructed to go hard to the wing to challenge outside shots on ball reversal or skip passes." [S1, p.184]

**Strengths**: Very strong high post. Controls dribble penetration through the lane. Good rebounding alignment. Handles overloads with disciplined weak-side forward closeout. [S1, p.184]

**Weaknesses**: Wings are exposed. The short corner is weak. The rover is over-taxed on any ball screen.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Wing isolation with a shooter** — the top guard is at the nail; the wing is alone against the forward.
- **Corner post-up + kick** — the forward has to choose between the corner shooter and the block post.
- **Ball screen at the top** — forces the rover to help; his baseline teammates are late.
- **Flash across the lane to the opposite block** — the rover commits to the ball-side post; the weak-side cut is unguarded.
- **Rapid ball reversal before the weak-side forward can close out** — beats the one defender forced to cover the far wing on skips.

**Citation**: [S1, p.184] — 2-1-2 half-court zone after missed shots with Herb Brown's overload rule.

### 2.6 Match-Up Zone
**Aliases**: match-up, matchup, zone with man rules.

**Setup**: Defenders align in zone spots (2-3, 1-3-1, or 3-2) but pick up the nearest offensive player in their area and play man principles on that player until a pass or a cut transfers the assignment. In Del Harris's system this shows up as the "matchbox-and-one variant: the four box defenders use matchup zone rules." [S7, p.265]

**Strengths**: Combines zone gap-help with man-to-man ball pressure. Takes away the easy zone-offense reads because defenders closeout like a man defense.

**Weaknesses**: Transfers of assignment on cuts create brief gaps. Any cut that crosses two zones forces a communication call — if missed, the cutter is open.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Screens across zones (cross-screens, flare screens between areas)** — force defenders to switch assignment mid-action; any hesitation creates an open look.
- **Cutter chains** — a sequence of cuts that re-assigns defenders faster than they can communicate; the third cutter is usually unattached.
- **Ball screens with a shooter and a roller in different zones** — the coverage rule for the zone clashes with the man-switch rule for the screen.
- **Overload with a player in the seam between two areas** — the assignment is ambiguous; a catch in the seam creates a 2-on-1.

**Citation**: [S7, p.265] — Del Harris, matchup zone rules within the Matchbox-and-One variant. The source library confirms matchup principles as a defensive tool but does not document a full-team match-up zone as a standalone system.

---

### 2.7 Amoeba
**Aliases**: amoeba, crawl.

**Setup**: A half-court trapping zone that changes shape based on the ball's position — often starting as a 1-2-2 and collapsing into a 1-3-1 trap in the corners. Aggressive deflections, active hands, constant rotation.

**Strengths**: Creates turnovers. Disrupts offensive tempo. Forces inexperienced teams into bad shots. Looks different on every possession.

**Weaknesses**: Breakable by skilled passing. Short corner and high post are both vulnerable when the defense rotates into a trap.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Quick ball reversal before the trap forms** — amoeba requires two or three passes to set its trap; a first-pass reversal beats it.
- **Flash to the high post** — the flasher catches with four offensive players around him; reads the rotation and finds the gap.
- **Skip passes to the weak-side short corner** — the furthest rotation for the defense; a patient skip is an open three or a catch on the move.
- **Dribble split the trap** — aggressive traps concede one dribble of space; a ball-handler who splits creates a 4-on-3.

**Citation**: No structural citation in the source library. **Citation omitted.**

## Part 3 — Junk Defenses (Combination)

Junk defenses blend zone and man. One or two defenders chase specific offensive players; the rest play a small zone. They are tactical; used by underdogs or against star-driven offenses. [S7, pp.265-266]

### 3.1 Box-and-1
**Aliases**: box-and-one, 4-and-chaser.

**Setup**: Four defenders in a box (two at the elbows, two at the blocks) playing a small zone. One chaser plays man-to-man on the offense's best scorer and denies the ball everywhere. Matchbox-and-One variant: the four box defenders use matchup zone rules while one chases. [S7, p.265]

**Strengths**: Takes the star out of the game. Forces non-scorers to beat the defense.

**Weaknesses**: Only four defenders guarding four areas. Open zones on the perimeter. The chaser is isolated — if the star sets a screen for a teammate, the chaser chases into a screen every time.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **LA offense modified with the star as a screener** — the star (O2) does not try to free himself; instead, screens zone defenders and occupies two defenders at once. O1-O3-O5 form a passing triangle to find the open zones. [S13, pp.254-255]
- **Have the star set a pindown for a shooter** — the chaser follows the star into the screen; the shooter is open.
- **Star in the corner with ball-reversal away** — the chaser is locked on; a four-man motion on the opposite side has a 4-on-3.
- **Star flashes to the high post; rest of the offense overloads weak side** — the chaser is following the star into the paint; the weak side has a 4-on-3.

**Citation**: [S13, pp.254-255, Figures 9.22-9.23]; [S7, p.265]. See also [[play-versus-box-and-one]].

---

### 3.2 Triangle-and-2
**Aliases**: triangle-and-two, 3-and-2.

**Setup**: Three defenders in a triangle zone (one at the free-throw area, two on the blocks). Two defenders play man-to-man on the two most dangerous perimeter scorers. [S7, p.265]

**Strengths**: Denies two star shooters the ball. Effective against teams with two-perimeter offense.

**Weaknesses**: Invites the non-chased three players (usually the 3, 4, 5) to shoot. The baseline two in tandem cannot cover three spots. Corners, wings, and top of the key are open for whichever player occupies them.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Put the non-guarded three in the open zones and move the ball** — O3, O4, O5 occupy baseline corners, wings, top of key. O1 and O2 dive to the blocks to screen baseline defenders or cross-screen. [S13, pp.255-256]
- **O1 and O2 cross-screen the two chasers** — creates separation for the guards to catch-and-pass, using the chasers' denial against them.
- **2-on-1 advantage on the baseline** — when the triangle's X4 and X5 slide, attack the sliding defender with a 2-on-1 from the corner.
- **Offensive rebounding with the guards crashing** — O1 and O2 at the blocks are in prime rebounding position.

**Citation**: [S13, pp.255-256]; [S7, p.265]. See also [[concept-attacking-disruptive-defenses]].

---

### 3.3 Diamond-and-1
**Aliases**: diamond-and-one.

**Setup**: Four defenders in a diamond (one at the top, two at the elbows, one under the basket). One chaser plays man-to-man on the star.

**Strengths**: Better perimeter coverage than the box-and-1. The top defender covers the top of the key and stretches to the wings.

**Weaknesses**: The elbow defenders are the only middle help. The corners are dead — only the baseline runner covers both.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Overload a corner with the star as a screener** — the chaser is pulled to the corner; a four-man motion on the other side has a 4-on-3.
- **High-low with the two elbow screens** — force the two elbow defenders to commit; a high-low dump catches the baseline runner alone.
- **Star flashes to the free-throw line; guard cuts to the basket** — the top defender commits to the flash; the weak-side cut is unguarded.
- **Skip pass to weak-side corner** — the baseline runner cannot be in both corners.

**Citation**: No structural citation in the source library. [S7, pp.265-266] documents box-and-1 and triangle-and-2 as junk variants but does not separately describe diamond-and-1. **Citation omitted.**

## Part 4 — Pressing Defenses

Presses are defined by where on the floor pressure starts: full-court, three-quarter, half-court. They trade interior rim protection for turnovers and tempo. [S1, pp.176-186]

### 4.1 1-2-1-1 Full-Court (Diamond Press)
**Aliases**: diamond press, 1-2-1-1 full.

**Setup**: Tallest defender pressures the inbounder. Two wings deny the middle and the far side, channeling to the sideline. A middle defender denies the high-post outlet. A safety protects the basket. Trap the sideline after the catch. [S1, pp.182-183]

**Strengths**: High turnover rate. Disrupts inbounds rhythm. Surprises the offense after a made free throw.

**Weaknesses**: If beaten once, it's a 4-on-3 to the rim. The middle pass is the death blow.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Middle-release man at the free-throw line extended** — catches the pass over the wings; immediate pitch to a sprinter creates a numbers advantage.
- **Inbounder steps into the court as a receiver** — the tallest defender has retreated; the inbounder becomes an outlet.
- **Dribble attack the sideline trap before it forms** — beat the wing defender off the dribble before the tallest defender can close.
- **Pass-and-sprint down the sideline** — outlet catch at the free-throw line extended, then sprint ahead of the trap; the safety is 1-on-3.

**Citation**: [S1, pp.182-183]. See also [[diamond-zone-press]].

---

### 4.2 2-2-1 Full-Court
**Aliases**: 2-2-1, soft press.

**Setup**: Two defenders at the free-throw line extended, two at half-court, one safety. No initial pressure on the inbounder. Trap after the ball crosses the first line. [S1, p.184] In Del Harris's usage: "a 2-2-1 zone press run at three-quarter court can slow a game — be selective about trapping; fall back into half-court man or zone after one trap." [S7, p.255]

**Strengths**: Low-risk. Slows the ball up the floor. Forces the offense to use clock. Can be flipped into a half-court zone seamlessly. [S1, p.184; S7, p.255]

**Weaknesses**: Gives the offense a free inbound. The inbounder's long pass over the first line breaks the press entirely.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Long pass over the first line to a streaking wing** — if the inbounder has a clean look, a long baseball pass skips the first line entirely.
- **Ball-reversal to the middle before the trap** — the first-line defenders commit one direction; a quick reverse creates a middle attack.
- **Dribble the seam between the two front defenders** — one dribble past the first line creates a 4-on-3.

**Citation**: [S1, p.184]; [S7, p.255].

---

### 4.3 1-3-1 Full-Court Press
**Aliases**: 1-3-1 full.

**Setup**: Point defender on the inbounder. Three defenders across the free-throw line extended (two wings, one middle). One safety. Trap on the first sideline catch.

**Strengths**: Strong middle denial. Forces sideline catches. Traps are aggressive with the point and wing.

**Weaknesses**: The far corner inbounds pass is open. If the middle defender is beaten, the safety is isolated.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Far corner inbounds (banana cut)** — the wing defender is too close to the sideline-near side; a banana-cut to the opposite corner creates a clean catch. [See [[concept-press-break-banana-cut]]]
- **Quick reversal after the trap** — the trap has committed two defenders; a pass to the middle and a skip to the opposite sideline creates a 3-on-2.
- **Trailer at the mid-court** — offensive trailer catches behind the first three; releases into a 4-on-2 vs. the safety.

**Citation**: [S1, pp.176-184] — full-court press philosophy and the change-press-after-every-timeout principle. The 1-3-1 full-court variant itself is not documented as a distinct system in the source library; it is catalogued here under the broader press family with Herb Brown's press principles.

---

### 4.4 Run-and-Jump (Full-Court)
**Aliases**: run-and-jump, switch-trap man press, college-era full-court press.

**Setup**: Man-to-man pressure full court. The nearest off-ball defender to the dribbler rotates and "jumps" the ball-handler; the original defender releases to the abandoned man. Best used when the helper is close. In Herb Brown's numbering system this is the "college-era full-court press" used when forwards score: "Constant run-and-jump — force the dribbler into a trap. If the trap is beaten and the ball is passed, the player who guarded the dribbler rotates in the direction where the trapper came from and picks up the free man there." [S1, p.183]

**Strengths**: Disguises like a straight man press but creates a trap without committing two defenders full-time. Forces hurried, spontaneous decisions. [S1, p.177]

**Weaknesses**: A lob pass over the jumper is a direct attack on the abandoned man. Requires elite communication — any miscommunication is a layup.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Lob over the jumper to the released man** — if the dribbler sees the jump coming, a high pass over the jumper finds the released man sprinting open.
- **Dribble handoff to beat the jump** — the ball-handler pitches before the jump connects; the receiver attacks the now-rotating defense.
- **Pull-up in the seam** — if the jumper is a step late, a pull-up jump shot is open before the jump arrives.

**Citation**: [S1, p.177, p.183]. See also [[run-and-jump-defense]].

---

### 4.5 Half-Court Trap (1-3-1 Trap or 1-2-1-1 Half)
**Aliases**: half-court 1-3-1, 1-2-1-1 half, St. Joseph's press.

**Setup**: Zone trap set at half-court or the top of the key. In Herb Brown's St. Joseph's system: fake a three-quarter-court man-to-man press, drop quickly into the front court; when the ball crosses mid-court, attack from a **3-1-1 half-court zone trap**. Middle defender keeps the ball out of the high post and channels to the corner; the baseline defender and nearest wing trap the corner; middle defender fronts the low post; two high defenders retreat to the nail and elbow. [S1, pp.183-185]

**Strengths**: Slows offensive tempo. Forces the offense to beat the trap with one pass. Generates turnovers against teams with weak ball-handling. Psychological advantage: "players love the uniqueness; opponents are baffled by the quick drop and zone attack." [S1, p.185]

**Weaknesses**: The high-post flash breaks it. Skip passes to the weak-side short corner break it. A quick ball reversal before the trap forms breaks it.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **High-post flash with a passer** — catches in the soft middle between the wing trap and the baseline runner; reads the rotation and hits the weakest point.
- **Skip pass corner-to-corner** — the baseline runner cannot be in both corners.
- **Dribble split between the trappers** — any seam in the trap gives the ball-handler one dribble of space — enough to create a 4-on-3.
- **Ball-reversal with a shooter trailing into a flare** — by the time the defense resets the trap, a flare screen on the recovery creates a catch-and-shoot.

**Citation**: [S1, pp.183-185] — Herb Brown's St. Joseph's Press (3-1-1 half-court zone trap). See also [[zone-press-combinations]] and [[st-josephs-press]].

---

## Part 5 — Special-Situation Defense

Defense by game state, not by alignment. These are short situations that deserve their own scouting.

### 5.1 BOB (Baseline Out-of-Bounds) Defense
**Setup**: The defense faces the inbounder under their own basket. Choices: man with switches, zone (typically a tight 2-3 close to the basket), or a hybrid with one chaser on the star. Unconventional option: double-team the inbounds passer with center and power forward on the ball side; weak-side forward covers the baseline; guards drop under the basket for high-pass interceptions. [S1, pp.184-185]

**Strengths of the man BOB**: Takes away direct cuts to the rim.
**Strengths of the zone BOB**: Takes away the quick screen action that most BOB sets rely on.

**Weaknesses**: Screens on the inbounder's defender create catches for direct shots. Stacked sets (box, stack, 4-low) are designed to create miscommunication on switches.

**Offenses that break a man BOB**:
- **Stack sets with an elevator screen** — two screeners for a shooter coming off a stack; the switch defender is caught between two bodies.
- **Box set with a back-screen for a lob** — back-screen on the target's defender; the lob is live before the switch can form.
- **Hammer action on the first BOB pass** — as the ball is inbounded, a ball-side screen sets up a skip-to-corner-screen-for-a-shooter sequence.

**Offenses that break a zone BOB**:
- **Overload one side and flash a screener to the ball** — the zone cannot cover three on one side.
- **Corner-to-corner skip from the inbounder** — the zone cannot shift in time if the inbounder makes a long pass.
- **High-post flash from the inbound** — catches with all five defenders facing the inbounder; the flasher has options everywhere.

**Citation**: [S1, pp.184-185] — Herb Brown's under-basket OOB surprise (tight 2-3 + double-team inbounds passer). See also [[concept-oob-defense-del-harris]] and [[concept-out-of-bounds-defense]].

---

### 5.2 SLOB (Sideline Out-of-Bounds) Defense
**Setup**: Defense faces the inbounder on the sideline. Choices mirror BOB: man with switches, zone, or hybrid.

**Strengths**: The sideline acts as a sixth defender. The inbounder has a 5-second constraint and a reduced passing angle.

**Weaknesses**: A quick-hitting action away from the ball (a back-screen for a lob on the weak-side block) creates a direct shot before the defense can recover from the ball-side set.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Ball-side screen + weak-side back-screen for a lob** — attention pulled to the ball; the weak side creates the score.
- **Slip the first screen** — the defense is preparing to switch; a slip creates a live cutter before the switch forms.
- **Step-up screen for the inbounder** — after the first pass, the inbounder gets a ball screen returning; the 5-second reset has not re-armed.

**Citation**: SLOB defense is drilled in Del Harris's Seven/Nine-Possession Game: "front-court SLOB vs. man-to-man" — [S7, pp.268-269]. See also [[concept-oob-defense-del-harris]] and [[play-diamond-slob]].

---

### 5.3 Late-Clock Defense
**Setup**: Under 10 seconds on the shot clock or game clock. Assumes the offense will force a shot. Switch everything to avoid any rotation breakdown. Contest every shot. No fouls except by design. Del Harris's specific rule: when the opponent needs a three to tie, "all 5 defenders play on the top side of their assignments; do NOT drop back to help on penetration; switch out on ALL picks — put in a lineup that can switch readily." [S7, pp.266-269; Figures 16.15-16.16]

**Strengths**: Removes help-defense mistakes. The shot clock does the defending.

**Weaknesses**: Switches create mismatches. An isolation on a mismatch is the offense's go-to action.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Screen for the shot-creator, then isolation** — force the switch, then attack the mismatch in the final 5 seconds.
- **Horns double-drag into pull-up** — first screen creates the initial switch; second screen creates the rhythm three.
- **Stagger for a shooter with a flip screen at the end** — the switch is called on the first action; the final flip catches the switch defender flat-footed.
- **Quick 2, then 3** — Harris's documented trailing-team pattern: "get quick 2 first, then go for the three." [S7, p.267]

**Citation**: [S7, pp.266-269, Figures 16.15-16.16] — Del Harris, late-game defensive strategy, three-point-line defense, Five-Man Arc Formation, fouling rules by deficit.

---

### 5.4 Scramble / Post-Rotation Recovery
**Setup**: After a broken defensive rotation (help left a shooter, double didn't recover, switch was missed). The defense is scrambling — nearest defender closes out, the rest find men by ball pressure. In Mike Fratello's vocabulary this is the "full rotation" state: "If penetration cannot be stopped by a stunt, the next-closest defender fully rotates off his offensive player and traps the ball. The next defender rotates to cover the open man. For example: 1 penetrates to middle → X2 traps, X3 rotates to 2, X1 rotates to 3." [S7, p.239]

**Strengths**: Can save a possession if the offense hesitates.

**Weaknesses**: Any decisive next pass attacks an open man.

**Offenses that break it**:
- **Shot-fake and swing** — the closeout defender is overextended; a shot-fake into a swing pass creates a new open shooter one pass away.
- **Drive and kick** — the scrambling defense cannot tag and recover simultaneously; a drive creates a chain of late closeouts.
- **Two more passes before the shot** — scrambles are won by offenses that refuse the first contested shot. Every additional pass exponentially increases the mismatch.

**Citation**: [S7, pp.237-240] — Mike Fratello, Help-and-Recover and Full Rotation coverage. See also [[weak-side-help-defense]] and [[weak-side-defense-rotation]].
