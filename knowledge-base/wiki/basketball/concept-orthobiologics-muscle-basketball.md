# Orthobiologics for Muscle Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Muscle injuries are among the most common injuries in professional basketball. In a 9-year prospective study of 59 male professional European basketball players, muscle injuries accounted for **21.2% of all injuries** — higher incidence than ankle sprains — with a return-to-play time of 7.6 ± 7.1 days [S10, p.596].

Muscle tissue regeneration is primarily limited by **scar tissue (fibrosis)** formation rather than the rate of muscle regeneration itself. Orthobiologics aim to reduce fibrosis, promote regeneration, and enable earlier safe return to play. However, clinical evidence is mixed and heterogeneous, and specific PRP formulations matter significantly [S10, pp.596-597].

## When to Use
- **Significant muscle fiber disruption confirmed on imaging** (MRI/ultrasound) — not for minor strains
- Hematoma/seroma present: evacuate first, then inject platelet-poor fraction (PRGF F1) into injury site
- Repeat injections at minimum 1-week intervals based on ultrasound imaging and symptoms
- **Surgical scenarios**: Complete/extensive musculo-tendinous junction (MTJ) avulsion, chronic symptomatic injuries, symptomatic nerve involvement — consider PPP fraction infiltration into and around the repair site

## Key Principles

### Classification of Muscle Injuries
1. **Intrinsic injuries**: Occur at the myotendinous junction during eccentric contraction — muscle fiber tearing (most common sports mechanism) [S10, p.596].
2. **Extrinsic injuries**: Occur from contusion — direct impact mechanism [S10, p.596].
3. **Conservative management** (standard): RICE protocol (rest, ice, compression, elevation), physiotherapy, NSAIDs, and time [S10, p.596].

### Biologic Considerations
4. **PRP stimulates myoblast PROLIFERATION but NOT differentiation** — differentiation is required to produce actual muscle tissue; proliferation alone is insufficient for regeneration [S10, p.596].
5. **Detrimental growth factors in PRP**: Myostatin and TGF-β1 (present in platelets) have been shown to be detrimental to muscle regeneration [S10, p.596].
6. **Key finding**: Platelet-poor plasma (PPP) and LP-PRP with platelets REMOVED (via second spin) → induces myoblast cells into the **differentiation** pathway (promotes regeneration). Standard PRP without second spin → induces **proliferation** pathway only [S10, p.596].
7. **Anti-fibrotic combination**: PRP + oral antifibrotic agent (Losartan) showed increased muscle regeneration and decreased fibrosis in preclinical mouse model [S10, p.596].

### Clinical Evidence — General Muscle Injuries
8. **Wright-Carpenter et al.**: Autologous conditioned serum (ACS) — significantly shorter recovery time in professional sportsmen with various muscle strains [S10, p.596].
9. **Sanchez et al. (21 professional soccer players)**: PRP group required half the time to resume normal training vs. matched historical controls [S10, p.596].
10. **Rossi et al. (RCT)**: Rehabilitation alone vs. rehabilitation + PRP for hamstrings/quadriceps/gastrocnemius injuries — PRP group: significantly earlier full recovery (21 days vs. 2 days) and significantly lower pain scores [S10, p.596].

### Clinical Evidence — Hamstring Injuries Specifically
11. **Hamid et al. (RCT, n=28)**: PRP injection + rehabilitation vs. rehabilitation alone for hamstrings — PRP group returned in **26.7 days** vs. **42.5 days** in rehabilitation-alone group [S10, p.597].
12. **Bezuglov et al. (n=40 soccer players)**: Similar positive results for PRP in hamstrings injuries [S10, p.597].
13. **CONTRADICTING — Reurink et al. (double-blind RCT, n=80)**: Two intramuscular PRP injections vs. isotonic saline for acute hamstrings tears — **NO benefit** for intramuscular PRP injections [S10, p.597].
14. **Multiple other studies** (Zanon, Rettig, Hamilton, Guillodo): No benefit for intramuscular PRP injections for acute hamstrings tears [S10, p.597].

### Authors' Clinical Protocol
15. Use orthobiologics only when **true and significant muscle fiber disruption is confirmed on imaging** [S10, p.597].
16. If hematoma/seroma present: **Evacuate under ultrasound guidance first**, then inject platelet-poor fraction (PRGF F1 — reduced TGF-β1 concentration, less pro-fibrotic) into injury site and adjacent healthy muscle [S10, p.597].
17. Prefer **platelet-poor fraction** over platelet-rich fraction to minimize pro-fibrotic TGF-β1 [S10, p.597].
18. **Monitor with repeated ultrasound/MRI** to follow healing and detect excessive fibrosis (which predisposes to re-injury).
19. Repeat injections at minimum **1-week intervals** based on imaging and symptoms.

## Current Evidence Conclusion
- Current literature does NOT provide sufficient evidence to support routine PRP for muscle injuries [S10, p.597].
- Laboratory studies suggest PPP or LP-PRP with platelets removed (second-spin preparation) may hold the most promise for muscle regeneration.
- Further high-quality clinical trials necessary.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using PRP for all muscle strains regardless of severity** → reserve for confirmed significant fiber disruption on imaging [S10, p.597].
2. **Injecting into an unevacuated hematoma** → evacuate under ultrasound first, then inject [S10, p.597].
3. **Using platelet-RICH fraction** → increased TGF-β1 is pro-fibrotic; platelet-POOR fraction is preferred for muscle injuries [S10, p.597].
4. **Ignoring the conflicting hamstring evidence** → Reurink et al. and multiple other RCTs show no benefit from intramuscular PRP for acute hamstring tears; clinical judgment required [S10, p.597].
