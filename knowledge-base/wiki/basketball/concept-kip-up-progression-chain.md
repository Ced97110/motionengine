# Kip-Up Progression Chain

## Summary
The kip-up is a floor-to-standing explosive movement beloved by martial artists, gymnasts, and athletes worldwide. Rather than a single all-or-nothing skill, it is best understood as a **family of techniques** arranged in a progressive chain. The chain runs from the beginner-accessible Bridge Kip (Step 4) through to the elite-level No-Hands Kip-Up (Master Step), with each step building the specific hip power, leg speed, and total-body coordination needed for the next.

For basketball athletes, mastery of the kip-up chain develops explosive waist (hip/lower-back) power, reactive agility, and the ability to accelerate from a prone or seated position — qualities that transfer directly to scrambling for loose balls, recovering from contact, and generating first-step explosiveness. "Master this advanced drill, kid, and your total-body speed and agility will start bursting off the charts." [S9, p.8]

## When to Use
- As a power/agility supplemental exercise in the off-season or pre-season
- After a strength base has been established (see [[strength-before-power-periodization]])
- As a warm-up or finishing exercise in an explosives session
- When training athletes to recover quickly from falls or off-balance positions
- As a total-body agility challenge for advanced players

## Key Principles
1. **Treat it as a family, not a binary.** Every athlete can enter the chain at some step; there is no all-or-nothing.
2. **The tuck is the launching pad.** In every step, rolling onto the upper-back and squeezing into a tight ball with bent knees close to the head generates the optimal spring position.
3. **Leg arc is the engine.** "Unravel" by exploding legs in an arc UP then DOWN — this momentum drives the whole movement.
4. **Simultaneous palm push.** As the legs arc down, the palms (or arms) push simultaneously to amplify lift.
5. **Forward torso throw.** As feet strike, keep driving the torso and hands forward — center of gravity must project forward.
6. **Land on the balls of the feet** in a squat, head and hands forward for stability, then stand.
7. **Less arm = more hip demand.** Each step in the chain systematically reduces available arm contribution, forcing the hips/waist to do more work.
8. **Wider stance = easier landing.** Regression tip: a wider foot landing makes the squat catch easier for beginners.

## The Ten Steps

### Step 4: Bridge Kip (Beginner)
Goal: generate enough lower-body power to lift head and upper-back off the floor — no need to flip forward yet. Hands in bridge position (by ears, fingers pointing to feet). Kick legs in arc; head/upper-back lifts. Hold the bridge position briefly, then lower and repeat. [S9, p.2]

### Step 5: Butt Kip (Beginner+)
Same start as Step 4, but push hard enough to lift palms off the floor. Throw torso and hands forward (like an explosive sit-up). Goal: land on butt AND feet with torso upright — not yet fully standing. [S9, p.4]

### Step 6: Half Kip (Intermediate)
Fuse the leg-arc (Step 4) and torso-throw (Step 5) more explosively. As feet touch down, continue throwing torso and hands forward. Land on both feet, even if only for a fraction of a second, before tipping back to butt. Roll back and repeat. [S9, p.6]

### Step 7: Kip-Up — Master Step of the chain (Intermediate)
The classic kip-up. Land on balls of both feet in a squatting position. Keep head and hands forward until stable, then stand. "Impossible without an explosive waist (hips and lower back), super-fast legs and the total-body agility of a panther." [S9, p.8]

### Step 8: Straight-Leg Kip-Up (Advanced)
Begin with legs STRAIGHT (not bent). Roll back with straight legs, knees close to head. Explode hips to straighten at the hips as palms push. At take-off, bend legs and whip them underneath. Much harder — eliminates the thigh/glute contribution, forcing hip/waist to do all the work. [S9, p.10]

### Step 9: Wushu Kip-Up (Advanced)
Arms placed at right-angles from the body (not in bridge position). Push through backs of arms, not palms. Forces significantly less arm power — trains hips to generate the bulk of the momentum. Transitional step toward hands-free kip-up. Regression: begin with bridge-position arms but push through knuckles/backs of hands, then progressively straighten arms. [S9, p.12]

### Master Step: No-Hands Kip-Up (Elite)
Arms never touch the floor. Keep arms to sides throughout. All momentum generated by the waist and legs. "If there is a more impressive — or explosive — way to power up off the floor, then humans haven't invented it yet!" Power is generated by the waist and legs, not the neck. Avoid if neck problems exist. [S9, p.14]

## Progressions (Universal)
- **Easier:** Start from sitting position (uses rolling momentum)
- **Harder:** Start lying FLAT on the floor (no rolling momentum) — works for all steps in the chain [S9, p.11]
- **Harder still:** Keep feet together on landing (requires more forward momentum)
- **Elite:** Straight-leg style + no-hands + start from flat

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating it as all-or-nothing** → Enter the chain at the correct step and build progressively
2. **Insufficient tuck** → Squeeze knees tighter to head before springing out; gives a better position to generate power
3. **Kicking without pushing** → Leg arc and palm push must be simultaneous, not sequential
4. **Torso stays back** → Actively throw the torso and hands FORWARD as feet land; this is what carries center of gravity over the feet
5. **Landing with toes pointing up** → Land on balls of feet in a squat, head and hands forward for balance
6. **Too narrow a stance initially** → Beginner regression: use wider stance to make the catch easier
