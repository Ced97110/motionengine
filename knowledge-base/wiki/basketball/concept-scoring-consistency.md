# Scoring Consistency and Win Rate

## Summary
A team's scoring *consistency* — how much its game-by-game scores vary around its average — is a second major determinant of win rate beyond average point differential. This is captured by the standard deviation of a team's points scored and points allowed distributions. For good teams (positive average point differential), being more consistent produces more wins. For bad teams (negative average differential), inconsistency paradoxically helps — it gives them a higher-than-expected chance of exceeding a quality opponent's score on any given night.

This principle has direct tactical implications: teams that play a disciplined, structured offense and defense reduce scoring variance and better convert their talent advantage into wins.

## When to Use
- When designing offensive and defensive systems for talent-rich teams
- When evaluating whether a team's style of play is translating talent into wins efficiently
- When analyzing why a talented team is underperforming its record expectations
- When scouting opponents: inconsistent teams are more dangerous as underdogs than their average record suggests

## Key Principles
1. **Inconsistency = movement toward .500**: Whether a team is good or bad, greater scoring variance pulls its win percentage toward .500 [S16, p.121]
2. **Good team + high variance = wasted talent**: A 106-ppg team that sometimes scores 80 loses games to inferior opponents; its talent isn't being consistently converted into wins [S16, p.121]
3. **Bad team + high variance = unexpected wins**: A 90-ppg team with a 15-point standard deviation will occasionally put up 115 points and shock a superior team — this is "lottery basketball" [S16, p.121]
4. **Ultimate consistency thought experiment**: A team scoring exactly 106 every game against one allowing exactly 103 wins 100% — perfect consistency eliminates all luck [S16, p.121]
5. **Standard deviation is a measurable statistic**: Track game-by-game scoring distributions to diagnose whether a team's record reflects its talent [S16, p.121]

## Tactical Sources of Scoring Inconsistency
- **Iso-heavy offense**: Relies on individual brilliance that varies night-to-night
- **Three-point heavy offense**: High variance by nature (3PT% fluctuates more than 2PT%)
- **Lack of interior scoring**: No "floor" for scoring; poor shooting nights produce very low totals
- **Young/inexperienced roster**: Decision-making variability inflates scoring variance
- **Poor free throw shooting**: Leaves points on the board variably depending on foul frequency

## Reducing Variance (Consistency Strategies)
- **Motion offense with multiple scoring options**: No single point of failure; if primary scorer is cold, secondary options remain
- **Inside-out attack**: Post play and paint touches establish a scoring floor
- **Free throw generation**: FTs are high-percentage, consistent scoring; teams that draw fouls regularly stabilize scoring
- **Ball movement offense**: Teams relying on passed-for shots rather than created shots tend to have more predictable scoring
- **Defensive consistency**: Limiting opponents to consistent (low) scores prevents the variance that can cause upset losses

## Common Mistakes
1. **Evaluating team quality only by average point differential** → A team with great averages but high variance is less reliable than its differential suggests
2. **Dismissing inconsistent bad teams as easy wins** → A bad team with high variance is more dangerous than a consistent bad team; don't be overconfident
3. **Building an offense around peak performances** → Systems that produce great nights but also terrible nights are not optimal for winning-percentage maximization
