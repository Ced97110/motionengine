# Reaction Time Training

## Summary
Reaction time is the interval between a stimulus (visual, audible, or tactile) and the initiation of a movement response. In basketball, superior reaction time determines whether a defender recovers on a drive, whether a guard can pick off a pass, and whether a player beats the defense down the floor. Reaction time is trainable — it improves through consistent exposure to varied stimuli under physical load.

A key insight from sports science: **different parts of the eye process stimuli at different speeds**. Cones (center of the eye / central vision) register information faster than rods (outside portion / peripheral vision). However, practicing reactions to peripheral stimuli improves peripheral reaction time, and vice versa — both must be trained. This principle is directly relevant to basketball where court sense requires processing stimuli from across the floor while executing footwork tasks. [S8, pp.216-222]

## When to Use
- As a component of every SAQ (Speed, Agility, Quickness) training session
- During the body of a training session after warm-up is complete
- In the late weeks of a mesocycle (weeks 4–6) when complex combined drills are introduced
- As a sport-specific conditioning tool in-season (use basketball-specific reaction cues)

## Key Principles
1. **Dual-task design**: Combine a physical movement pattern (ladder footwork, shuffle, roll) with a simultaneous cognitive task (counting fingers, odd/even calls) — this is how real game reactions occur
2. **Stimulus variety**: Alternate visual and auditory commands — basketball requires both (seeing a cutter AND hearing a screen call)
3. **Central + peripheral training**: Use coach semicircles and multiple coach positions to force peripheral vision processing
4. **Sport-specific cues**: Embed basketball-specific tasks at the end of reaction drills (e.g., catch a pass and shoot after completing a ladder)
5. **Progressive loading**: Master the movement pattern first (weeks 1–3), then add the reaction layer (weeks 4–6)
6. **Minimize amortization**: The goal is to reduce the gap between stimulus recognition and motor response — "see it, move it"
7. **Positive stimulus environment**: Reaction training requires focus; positive coaching reinforcement maintains attention quality

## Stimulus Types
- **Visual**: Coach holds up fingers; athlete calls out the number while completing the drill
- **Auditory**: Coach calls "left," "right," "sprint," "jump," etc.
- **Odd/Even Filter**: Coach shows a number; athlete must only respond if it's odd (or even) — adds a cognitive processing layer
- **Multiple sources**: Two or three coaches around a semicircle flashing different signals simultaneously

## Peripheral Vision Science
- Objects seen by **cones** (center of the eye) register faster than objects seen by **rods** (periphery) [S8, p.216]
- Practicing reactions to **peripheral** stimuli improves peripheral reaction speed
- Practicing reactions to **central** stimuli improves central reaction speed
- Both must be trained for full basketball court awareness

## Common Mistakes
1. **Looking at feet during ladder drills** → keep eyes up on the coach at all times; develop tactile feel for the ladder
2. **Slowing down to react** → the reaction and the movement must happen simultaneously, not sequentially
3. **Skipping the dual-task progression** → don't add the reaction cue before the footwork pattern is mastered
4. **Using only one stimulus type** → vary visual and auditory cues to build true game-applicable reaction
5. **Neglecting peripheral vision** → always incorporate some drills where the coach is not directly in front of the athlete
