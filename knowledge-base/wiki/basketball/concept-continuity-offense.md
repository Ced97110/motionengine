# Continuity Offense (High-Post Offense)

## Summary
The Continuity offense, also called the high-post offense, was developed at Continuity University in 1967 by Pete Carril and later implemented at the pro level by Eddie the iconic scorer. It is built on five elements — cutting, passing, dribbling, screening, and shooting — and requires all five players to work simultaneously. The offense spreads the floor with a high formation, generates open shots through backdoor cuts and screens, and tires defenses over the course of a game through constant, purposeful off-ball movement. The offense's unpredictability is itself a weapon. [S7, pp.111-116]

## When to Use
- Against man-to-man defense at any level
- When a team's personnel includes versatile, unselfish players who can read and pass
- When facing a more athletic opponent — patience and spacing neutralize athleticism
- When a team cannot win in isolation or fast-break basketball
- Late-game situations requiring high-percentage shots within the flow of movement

## Key Principles
1. **Patience, poise, and unselfishness are the prerequisites** — the offense only works when players subordinate individual statistics to team shot quality.
2. **All five players are constant threats** — no player stands; all are in motion reading defensive reactions and finding the best attack angle.
3. **Read the defender to determine the cut** — if the defender is denying: cut backdoor. If the defender is playing loose: cut high to receive. "The defender dictates the direction."
4. **Proper spacing is non-negotiable** — keep players above the free-throw line extended (except during cuts to the basket); spacing creates one-on-one situations and driving/drifting actions.
5. **Shoot within your range and within the flow** — only take shots you know you can make; shots should come with little or no defensive pressure and within the shooter's high-percentage range.
6. **Purposeful dribbling** — have a purpose before putting the ball on the floor. Short dribbles create better passing angles. Too much dribbling freezes teammates.
7. **Never pre-decide** — react to the defense in real time; the offense is designed to exploit whatever the defense gives up. [S7, pp.111-114]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (1):** Primary ball handler; entry initiator; reads wing density and chooses pass, dribble clear, or dribble weave entry; creates moment-of-truth situations.
- **SG (2):** Off guard; must be able to handle the ball and make decisions; exchanges with wings; cuts and backdoor reads.
- **SF (3) / SF (4):** Wings; primary entry receivers; cut backdoor when denied; post down low when 1 dribble-clears toward them.
- **C/PF (5):** High-post player — must be able to pass, dribble, and shoot like a "big point guard." Flashes to free-throw area; sets mid-screens on the ball; initiates two-man games from the elbow.

## Initial Set

### Formation
2-3 alignment: two guards (1 and 2) at the guard spots, two wings (3 and 4) at the wing positions, and one high post (5) near the free-throw line/elbow. The set can evolve to:
- **3-out / 2-in:** Three perimeter players, two at low post [S7, p.115]
- **4-out / 1-in:** Four perimeter players, one at low post [S7, p.115]
- No formation is permanent; positions constantly shift based on defensive reads.

## Entry Options

### Pass Entries
**Pass to the wing (simplest):** 1 passes to 3 (wing on his side); 1 then cuts same side or crosses to the other side. Most common starting action when wings are not over-guarded. [S7, p.115]

**Pass to high post (guards cut or exchange):** When both wings are overplayed and the reverse is denied, 1 passes to 5 at the free-throw area. 1 and 2 cut to low-post positions on their respective sides; wings (3 and 4) replace the guards at the guard spots. 1 and 2 can exchange positions with the wings after the high-post entry. [S7, p.115]

### Dribble Entries
**Dribble clear:** When no teammate is free, 1 dribbles toward 3; the wing reads this as a signal to clear out — either cut backdoor to the basket or post down low. [S7, p.115]

**Dribble weave:** 1 dribbles toward 3 and makes a short kickoff pass (or hand-off pass) to 3 if the defense plays loose; 2 and 4 can exchange positions simultaneously. [S7, p.116]

**Reverse dribble:** 1 dribbles one direction, makes a reverse dribble, and passes to the wing (4) who has exchanged positions with 2. 1 can also pass to 5 who has popped to the free-throw lane. [S7, p.116]

### Screen Entries
**Midscreen on the ball:** 5 sets a midscreen on 1's defender. 2 (overplayed) cuts into the lane to the opposite corner; 3 and 4 come high; 1 passes to 3 coming high. [S7, p.116]

**Midscreen on the ball + reverse dribble:** 1 receives the midscreen from 5 but 3 is still overplayed; 1 makes a reverse dribble and passes to 2 coming off a down screen set by 4. [S7, p.116]

## Rules (Pete Carril's Framework)
- Loosely guarded player: come toward the ball for a pass or direct handoff.
- Overplayed player: cut backdoor immediately — move away from pressure, never fight it.
- Ball handler passes backdoor: use a **one-hand bounce pass** off the dribble — quicker and harder for the defense to read.
- Spacing: all five players maintain proper floor spacing — drives and drifts only work with adequate spacing.
- Read, don't pre-decide: players react to defensive movement in the moment. [S7, pp.113-114]

## Variations
### Guard Squeeze Action
When 3 can't receive the ball on a backdoor cut: 1 screens on 2 ("guard squeeze"); 2 cuts to 3 feet in front of 5; 5 passes to 2. After the screen, 1 rolls to the free-throw area for a possible pass from 5. [S7, p.109]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stopping a cut in midstream** → the passer may have committed to the pass; always finish the cut even if the pass doesn't come.
2. **All four off-ball players cutting simultaneously** → creates a lane traffic jam; no one is open. Read teammates and stagger cuts.
3. **Excessive dribbling** → freezes teammates and kills the motion offense; dribble with purpose only.
4. **Indecisiveness in shooting** → committing to a shot is essential; hesitation hands the defense time to recover. "The worst thing in shooting is indecisiveness."
5. **5 not being versatile enough at the high post** → the high-post player must pass, dribble, AND shoot to function as the offense's hub; a purely physical post player cannot fill this role.
