# Countermovement Jump (CMJ) as a Fatigue Monitoring Tool

## Summary
The countermovement jump (CMJ) and drop jump (DJ) are the two primary jump protocols used to monitor neuromuscular fatigue in high-performance basketball. Over 54% of practitioners in high-performance sport use vertical jump testing on a daily, weekly, or monthly basis to monitor fatigue. The CMJ is particularly valuable in basketball because neuromuscular function underpins jumping, change-of-direction ability, and overall court performance. [S12, pp.9-10]

While jump height is the most commonly reported CMJ variable, it may not be the most sensitive indicator of fatigue. The **Flight Time to Contraction Time (FT:CT) ratio** and other kinetic variables derived from force plates or linear position transducers provide greater resolution into the mechanisms of neuromuscular fatigue than height alone.

## When to Use
- Daily or pre-/post-practice to detect session-to-session neuromuscular recovery status
- Pre-game on game days to confirm player readiness
- After congested game schedules (back-to-backs) to verify recovery before return to high-intensity training
- Weekly as part of a broader athlete monitoring system

## Key Principles
1. **CMJ vs. DJ measure different SSC qualities** — The CMJ assesses slow SSC (contact time >250 ms); the DJ assesses fast SSC (contact time <250 ms); they are not interchangeable and cannot be compared directly [S12, p.10]
2. **FT:CT ratio is more sensitive than jump height** — Flight Time to Contraction Time ratio detects neuromuscular fatigue in female basketball athletes; height alone shows conflicting results across studies [S12, p.10]
3. **Average of multiple CMJs beats single maximum** — Using average height across multiple jumps is more sensitive to fatigue and supercompensation than maximum height alone [S12, p.9]
4. **DJ reactive strength index lacks basketball sensitivity** — RSI (flight time/contact time) from a 40 cm DJ was not sensitive to s-RPE training load changes during an elite Australian basketball season [S12, p.10]
5. **Kinetic variables add insight** — Eccentric and concentric phase variables from force plates provide greater insight into SSC integrity and loading strategies than jump height alone
6. **Equipment selection affects feasibility** — Force plates offer the most data but lowest portability; contact mats and linear position transducers offer practical field-based alternatives

## Protocols

### CMJ Protocol (Standard)
1. Player stands on force plate or contact mat, hands on hips
2. Self-selected depth counter-movement followed by maximal vertical jump
3. Record jump height AND flight time, contact time (if equipment permits)
4. Perform 3–5 trials; record average (not maximum)
5. Compare to individual baseline using z-scores (z = [score − individual mean] / individual SD)

### Drop Jump Protocol (Fast SSC Assessment)
1. Player steps off a 40 cm box
2. Minimal ground contact time with immediate maximal jump
3. Record contact time, flight time, and RSI (flight time / contact time)
4. Note: RSI from DJ not sensitive to basketball training load changes — use CMJ instead for load monitoring [S12, p.10]

## Coaching Points
- "Use average jump height, not peak" — a single best effort masks fatigue signals
- "FT:CT is your early warning system" — a drop in ratio before height changes signals early neuromuscular fatigue
- "Compare to the individual, not the team" — a 3% drop in FT:CT is significant for one player and normal noise for another
- "CMJ beats DJ for load monitoring in basketball" — the slow SSC matches the predominant jumping mechanics in the sport

## Common Mistakes
1. **Tracking only maximum CMJ height** → Use average of 3–5 trials and add FT:CT ratio for greater sensitivity
2. **Using the same threshold for all players** → Establish individual baselines and apply z-scores; team averages mask individual fatigue signals
3. **Substituting DJ for CMJ as a primary monitoring tool** → DJ RSI has not shown sensitivity to basketball training load; CMJ FT:CT has
4. **Monitoring infrequently** → Weekly minimum; daily monitoring during congested schedules provides more actionable data
5. **Ignoring kinetic variables** → If force plate data is available, analyze eccentric/concentric phase contributions, not just height
