# Zone Press and Combination Defenses

## Summary
Effective defensive teams change their look throughout the game to delay offensive recognition and disrupt offensive flow. Herb Brown's approach pairs full-court presses with different half-court zone defenses, rotating based on who scored and what the game situation demands. The key idea: once you have a sound man-to-man foundation, innovation — combining presses and zones, changing after every timeout, and using personnel-keyed triggers — becomes your competitive edge. [S1, pp.175-186]

> "Once you have established a sound fundamental man-to-man defensive philosophy with your team, innovation is the key to great defense." — Herb Brown [S1, p.180]

## When to Use
- When you want to disrupt the opponent's offensive rhythm and recognition
- When a lead in the fourth quarter makes tempo control more important than risk-taking
- Against teams that depend heavily on one ball-handler or scorer
- After establishing a man-to-man look — use zone/press combinations as a surprise
- After every timeout (regardless of which team called it) — always change your half-court defense

## The Three Named Presses (Krinsky System)

### Diamond Zone Press (Center scores)
- **Formation**: 1-2-1-1 full-court zone press
- **Trigger**: Used whenever the center scores (field goal or free throw)
- **Purpose**: Pressure the inbounds pass, bat the ball out, channel to sideline, trap the dribbler
- **Based on**: John Wooden's High-Post full-court zone press
- See: [[diamond-zone-press]] for full execution detail [S1, pp.182-183]

### college-era full-court press (Forwards score)
- **Formation**: Man-to-man full court with run-and-jump tactics
- **Trigger**: Used whenever a forward scores (field goal or free throw)
- **Execution**: Constant run-and-jump — force the dribbler into a trap. If the trap is beaten and the ball is passed, the player who guarded the dribbler rotates in the **direction where the trapper came from** and picks up the free man there
- **Based on**: the originating head coach's UNC press system [S1, p.183]

### St. Joseph's Press (Guards score)
- **Formation**: Fake a three-quarter-court man-to-man press, drop quickly into the front court; when the ball crosses mid-court, attack from a **3-1-1 half-court zone trap**
- **Trigger**: Used whenever a guard scores (field goal or free throw)
- **Key role — middle defender**: Keep the ball out of the high post. Channel to the corner. The baseline defender and nearest wing trap the corner. Middle defender fronts the low post. Two high defenders retreat to free-throw line and weak-side block (nail and elbow).
- **Effect**: Psychological advantage — players love the uniqueness; opponents are baffled by the quick drop and zone attack
- **Based on**: Dr. Jack Ramsey's St. Joseph's teams [S1, pp.183-185]

## Half-Court Zone Defenses After Missed Shots
1. **Straight man-to-man defense**
2. **Run-and-jump at every backcourt opportunity** — aggressive scrambling whenever the ball is in the backcourt
3. **2-1-2 zone defense** — when opponents overload one side: best defensive forward covers the weak side and is instructed to go hard to the wing to challenge outside shots on ball reversal or skip passes
4. **1-3-1 zone defense** — particularly effective for trapping corners and denying high-post entry [S1, p.184]

## Changing Defenses as a Tactical Tool
- Change defenses after **every timeout** (regardless of who called it) — never let the opponent adjust to a consistent look
- Change after **end of quarters/halves**, **dead balls**, and **free throws**
- Vary between: full-court man → half-court zone; zone press → man-to-man; and any combination that fits your personnel
- Use the **element of surprise** as a constant factor — an aggressive pressure defense that the opponent hasn't prepared for generates easy offensive baskets [S1, pp.176-178]

## Zone Defense in the Fourth Quarter
- Some coaches zone when holding a lead to: take away easy baskets, shorten possessions, and avoid foul trouble
- Counter-argument: teams playing zone want to push the pace, so your defenders may struggle to match up in transition
- Solution option: full-court press with 3 man-to-man perimeter defenders + 2 tallest players zoning down floor → all five fall back into a 2-3 zone [S1, p.178]

## Under-Basket Inbounds Surprise
- Set up in a **tight 2-3 close to the basket** on under-basket OOB
- Unconventional option: **double-team the inbounds passer** with center + power forward on the ball side
- Weak-side forward covers the baseline away from ball
- Both guards drop all the way under the basket — look to intercept high passes or bat them down court for easy baskets
- Most effective when the opponent's inbounds passer is poor under pressure [S1, pp.184-185]

## Key Principles
1. **Man-to-man fundamentals first** — teach 1v1, 2v2, 3v3 before any combination defense
2. **Disguise delays recognition** — the later the offense recognizes your defense, the bigger your advantage
3. **Zone is not a hiding place** — every player must be defensively sound; zone does not mask weak individual defenders
4. **Walk through positioning before use** — before a game where you plan to use a specific zone, walk your team through all ball locations slowly in practice [S1, p.185]
5. **Personnel determines the press** — the defense you employ depends on which players you have available and their ability to adjust

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using zone to hide weak defenders** → Correct: zone defense requires everyone to be sound; each player must be able to close out, challenge shots, and move with the ball.
2. **No half-court change after timeouts** → Correct: always change your half-court defense after a timeout — the opponent just drew up a play against what they've been seeing.
3. **Predictable press triggers** → Correct: vary your press triggers; don't always diamond after free throws — mix in face-guard or the college program press to disrupt scouting.
