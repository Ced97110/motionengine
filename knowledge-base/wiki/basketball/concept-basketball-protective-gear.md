# Basketball Protective Gear: Evidence-Based Recommendations

## Summary
Protective gear in basketball — ankle braces, foot orthoses, mouth guards, and compression clothing — is widely used but inconsistently supported by research. The strongest evidence supports hinged ankle braces for female basketball players and mouth guard use for preventing dental injuries. Compression garments show some promise for anaerobic performance and knee injury prevention, but further studies are needed. Foot orthoses alter lower-limb biomechanics without meaningfully changing jumping performance. NBA rules prohibit dangerous hard-substance equipment and require padding on all hard braces. [S10, pp.952-954]

## When to Use
- Selecting prophylactic gear for players with injury history
- Advising female athletes on ankle brace selection
- Making recommendations during preseason equipment screening
- Evaluating compression garment use for performance or recovery

## Key Principles

### Ankle Braces
1. **Hinged ankle brace (Active T2)** is biomechanically superior to lace-up brace (ASO) for prophylactic ankle support in female basketball players — it significantly restricts peak ankle inversion, reduces ankle/knee compressive forces, and reduces knee anterior shear forces [S10, p.952]
2. A 16-week prophylactic ankle brace intervention led to significantly improved dynamic postural control and single-limb functional tests in basketball players [S10, p.952]
3. Wearing an ASO brace unilaterally on the dominant side reduces ankle inversion without posing risk to the unbraced leg — any braced condition shows benefit [S10, pp.952-953]
4. **"The hinged ankle brace may be a better choice of prophylactic ankle support for female basketball players from a biomechanical perspective."** [S10, p.953]

### Foot Orthoses
- Foot orthoses affected lower limb biomechanics at take-off but did **not** improve jumping performance (CMJ or standing broad jump) in basketball players with or without flat feet [S10, p.953]
- Foot orthoses remain useful for managing flat feet biomechanically but should not be expected to enhance performance

### Mouth Guards
- Mouth guards are effective, inexpensive, easy to use, and proven to prevent sports-related dental injuries — yet vastly underutilized in basketball [S10, p.953]
- 98% of coaches believe mouth guards prevent dental injuries, but only 47% suggest their use [S10, p.953]
- Even among players who had already sustained a dental injury, only 6.3% used a mouth guard [S10, p.953]
- Most common reasons for non-use: discomfort (37.7%), difficulty breathing (7.3%), difficulty talking (6.4%), and not being required (65.3% of high school athletes) [S10, p.953]
- **Coaching obligation**: Coaches should actively advocate for mouth guard use to prevent preventable dental injuries

### Compression Clothing
- Lower body compression garments enhanced mean power output, anaerobic capacity, and total work in NCAA Division I basketball players (Wingate Anaerobic Test), with lower perceived exertion [S10, p.953]
- No effect on peak power, anaerobic power, or fatigue index [S10, p.953]
- Compression leggings significantly reduced hip abduction range of motion during drop vertical jumps — potentially reducing knee valgus and aiding knee injury prevention — but further research is needed [S10, p.953]
- **Coaching implication**: Lower body compression garments may provide modest anaerobic performance benefits and could support knee injury prevention; they are safe to use and worth considering for high-volume players

### NBA Rules on Equipment
- Players may not wear jewelry [S10, p.950]
- Officials must not permit dangerous equipment; all hard substances (casts, splints, guards, braces) must be padded/foam covered with no exposed sharp edges [S10, p.950]
- Equipment designed to artificially increase height, reach, or advantage is prohibited [S10, p.950]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Use mouth guards — advocate with coaches and institutions for mandatory use; wear compression garments for anaerobic performance support and potential knee protection
- **Guards (PG/SG)**: Ankle brace use especially important given high lateral movement demands; hinged brace preferred for history of ankle sprain
- **Bigs (PF/C)**: Compression garments may support anaerobic capacity during post-play exertion; hard braces (casts, knee braces) must comply with NBA padding rules

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not requiring mouth guards** → Coaches should actively promote mouth guard use; dental injuries are preventable and painful [S10, p.953]
2. **Choosing lace-up over hinged brace for women** → Evidence favors the hinged ankle brace for female basketball players from a biomechanical standpoint [S10, p.952]
3. **Expecting foot orthoses to improve jumping** → Orthoses alter biomechanics but do not improve jump performance in non-flat-foot athletes [S10, p.953]
4. **Wearing hard braces without padding** → NBA rules require all hard-substance equipment to be padded — unpadded braces/casts are illegal and dangerous [S10, p.950]
