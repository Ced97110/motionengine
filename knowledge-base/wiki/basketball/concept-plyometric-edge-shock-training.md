# The Plyometric Edge — Shock Training for Explosive Power

## Summary
Plyometrics (originally called "shock training" by its creator Yuri Verkhoshansky) refers specifically to exercises where muscles are *forcibly stretched under load* a fraction of a second before they contract concentrically. This pre-stretch activates the myotatic (stretch) reflex and the stretch-shortening cycle, causing muscles to fire with greater power than they could produce from a static starting position. Many athletes confuse general explosive training with true plyometrics — the key distinction is the presence of an eccentric pre-load immediately before the concentric effort [S9, pp.300-302].

For basketball, plyometric training is the bridge between strength gains in the gym and explosive on-court performance — vertical jump, first step off a cut, and second-jump ability.

## When to Use
- Off-season and pre-season power development
- Immediately following a strength base phase (strength-before-power periodization)
- When athletes need to improve second-jump ability, first-step quickness, or contact finishing
- As a pre-potentiation method before high-skill explosive work

## Key Principles
1. **True plyometrics requires an eccentric pre-load immediately before the concentric effort** — a jump from a dead stand is explosive training but NOT plyometric; only the pre-loaded version activates the stretch reflex [S9, p.300]
2. **The depth jump is the foundational plyometric** — stepping off an elevated surface causes rapid forced stretching on landing, followed by an immediate explosive rebound jump [S9, p.300]
3. **Continuous reps unlock plyometric benefit in simpler exercises** — the first rep of a standing jump series is not plyometric, but landing from rep 1 and immediately launching rep 2 IS, because the landing provides the eccentric pre-load [S9, pp.301-302]
4. **No pause between reps on jumps and pushups** — eliminate the pause to access rebound/elastic forces; treat the landing as a loading spring, not a resting point [S9, p.302]
5. **"Shock training" works via the myotatic reflex** — the nervous system perceives the forced stretch as a threat and fires back with extra protective force [S9, p.300]
6. **Verkhoshansky's original name was shock training** — coined as "plyometrics" by American Fred Wilt; both terms describe the same phenomenon [S9, p.300]

## Two Methods for Applying Plyometric Principles

### Method 1: Depth Jump / Box Drop (True Shock Training)
- **For jumps:** Step off a box (12-30 inches), absorb landing with minimal knee bend, immediately explode back upward — or onto another box, or with a maximum vertical jump
- **For pushups:** Begin with hands on two boxes at shoulder width, drop off the boxes into a pushup position, immediately explode back up onto the boxes
- **Intensity note:** This is for strong athletes with robust joints only [S9, pp.300-301]

### Method 2: Continuous Machine-Gun Reps (Accessible Shock Training)
- Perform all reps in a set without pausing at the bottom or top
- The landing/bottom position from each rep pre-loads the muscles for the next rep
- "Hit your first rep as hard as possible, then the second you land, use the rebound and elastic forces in your muscles to immediately bounce back up" [S9, p.302]
- Applies to both jumps and explosive pushups
- **Exception:** Flips and kip-ups should have a mental reset between reps for safety [S9, p.302]

## Relationship to Stretch-Shortening Cycle
The mechanism underlying plyometric training is the stretch-shortening cycle (SSC):
- **Eccentric phase:** Muscle stretches under load, storing elastic energy in tendons and activating muscle spindles
- **Amortization phase:** The instant between eccentric and concentric — must be minimized
- **Concentric phase:** Elastic energy + reflex activation amplify force output beyond what voluntary contraction alone can achieve

See also: [[stretch-shortening-cycle-plyometrics]] for the detailed physiological explanation.

## Basketball Applications
- **Vertical jump:** Depth jumps develop the reactive strength needed for second jumps in rebounding
- **Rim runs/tip-ins:** SSC training builds the fast amortization needed to go from landing to immediate re-jump
- **First step:** The explosive transition from loaded defensive stance to driving step uses SSC mechanics
- **Power layups:** Gathering a power dribble into a two-foot jump uses the same eccentric pre-load principle

## Common Mistakes
1. **Calling all jump training "plyometrics"** → True plyometrics requires the pre-loaded eccentric; static jump training is valuable but different
2. **Pausing between jump reps** → Eliminates the stretch-reflex benefit; "machine gun" continuous reps are essential for true shock training effect
3. **Starting plyometrics without a strength base** → Verkhoshansky's method requires structural resilience; follow strength-before-power periodization
4. **Using boxes too high too soon** → Landing forces scale with box height; progress conservatively
5. **Applying plyometric rebound principles to flip/kip-up reps** → These skill movements require mental reset between reps for safety and quality
