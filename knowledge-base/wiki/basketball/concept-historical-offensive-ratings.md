# Historical Offensive Ratings — History's Greatest NBA Offenses

## Summary
Appendix 2 of *Basketball on Paper* provides complete individual floor percentages, offensive ratings, points produced per game, and percentage of team possessions used for all players on 25 of the greatest NBA offenses in history (selected by standard deviations above average from Chapter 3). This database allows era-neutral comparison of player contributions on dominant offensive teams [S16, pp.350-358].

The data reveals consistent patterns in how elite offenses are constructed: a small number of high-usage, high-efficiency players drive most of the production, supported by role players with high floor percentages but low usage rates.

## When to Use
- Benchmarking current players against the greatest in history on a possession-normalized basis
- Understanding the usage-efficiency tradeoff at elite levels
- Identifying which types of offensive roles generate the highest offensive ratings
- Teaching the concept that raw scoring average does not equal offensive efficiency

## Key Principles

### 1. The Usage-Efficiency Tradeoff
The greatest offensive players in history maintained high efficiency while absorbing large percentages of team possessions. This is extremely rare [S16, pp.350-358]:
- Michael Jordan (1991 Bulls): Floor% 0.61, Off. Rtg 126, **31% of team possessions**
- Magic Johnson (1987 Lakers): Floor% 0.603, Off. Rtg 123.9, **29% of team possessions**
- Larry Bird (1988 Celtics): Floor% 0.57, Off. Rtg 122, **29% of team possessions**

### 2. Role Players Show Higher Floor% Than Stars
Because role players only take high-percentage shots, their floor percentages often exceed stars' [S16, pp.350-358]:
- Horace Grant (1992 Bulls): Floor% **0.66**, Off. Rtg **132** (16% of possessions)
- James Donaldson (1987 Mavs): Floor% **0.65**, Off. Rtg **132** (11% of possessions)
- Steve Kerr (1996 Bulls): Floor% **0.58**, Off. Rtg **141** (12% of possessions)
- A.C. Green (1987 Lakers): Floor% **0.601**, Off. Rtg **121.6** (15% of possessions)

### 4. What an Offensive Rating of 100 Means
An offensive rating of exactly 100 means a player produces exactly one point per possession they use — the historical league average baseline. Ratings above 110 are good; above 120 is elite; above 130 is exceptional for any usage level.

### 5. Low-Rated Players on Great Teams
Every great team has low-rated players. Dickey Simpkins (1996 Bulls: 0.51/102) and Jud Buechler (0.50/113) were far below Jordan's level but provided role value. The team system elevated them to acceptable efficiency [S16, p.352].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Selecting only high-Off.Rtg players without checking % possessions** → Steve Kerr's 141 Rtg on 12% possessions is not comparable to Jordan's 124 on 31%
2. **Ignoring floor% in favor of scoring average** → Kevin McHale's 0.62/126 is more impressive than a scorer with 0.52/108
3. **Not accounting for era** → Different eras had different average team offensive ratings; the standard-deviation method corrects for this
4. **Treating low Off.Rtg as a failure** → Some low-rated players are asked to do difficult, low-probability tasks (primary ball-handlers for bad teams)
