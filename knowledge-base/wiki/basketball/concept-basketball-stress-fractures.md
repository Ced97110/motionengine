# Basketball Stress Fractures

## Summary
Stress injuries are common in professional basketball players, particularly in the foot. In a study of 75 NBA players, 76 lower extremity bony stress injuries were found, with over half (55%) involving the foot and most occurring during the regular season [S10, p.207]. The 5th metatarsal stress fracture is the most commonly reported stress injury (18.4%), with devastating return-to-play implications — 42% of affected NBA players were unable to return to play despite 100% surgical management [S10, p.207].

## When to Use
- Evaluating any basketball player with foot pain that worsens during the season
- Differentiating stress fracture from lateral ankle sprain (they present similarly)
- Counseling players and families about the severity of 5th metatarsal injuries
- Designing load management programs to prevent stress injuries during long seasons

## Key Principles

### NBA Stress Injury Distribution
Types of bony stress injuries in NBA athletes (2005–2015):
- 5th metatarsal stress fracture: 18.4% (most common)
- Other foot stress fractures: 14.5%
- Foot stress reaction: also common
- Tibial stress fracture and reaction: also common
- Fibula, ankle, navicular, patella, calcaneus sesamoid, tarsal: less common [S10, p.206-207]

### 5th Metatarsal Stress Fracture
1. **Most serious** — 100% surgical management with open reduction internal fixation [S10, p.207]
2. **Return-to-play**: 42% of players unable to return to play despite surgery [S10, p.207]
3. **Mechanism**: Peroneus brevis avulsion from base of 5th metatarsal — same force that ruptures SPR; also from inversion loads when jumping/landing [S10, p.207]
4. **Differential diagnosis**: Presents similarly to lateral ankle sprain — must palpate base of 5th metatarsal specifically [S10, p.207]

### Mechanisms Contributing to Foot Stress Injuries
1. Strong contraction of peroneal muscles during dorsiflexion → tears SPR → avulsion from fibula
2. Inversion stress on landing → stress on base of 5th metatarsal
3. Repetitive jumping and landing loads (70–250 jumps per game) accumulate over a long season [S10, p.193]

### Other Foot Stress Pathologies
- Avulsion fractures of navicular: present with symptoms similar to low-ankle sprains [S10, p.207]
- Anterolateral calcaneus fractures: foot abducted and plantar flexed [S10, p.207]
- Peroneal tendon subluxation: SPR disruption allows peroneal tendons to sublux anteriorly [S10, p.207]
- Osteochondral lesions of talar dome: can occur concomitantly with lateral ankle sprains [S10, p.207]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating 5th MT stress fracture as a lateral ankle sprain** → Always palpate base of 5th metatarsal; use Ottawa rules to determine if X-ray is needed
2. **Underestimating severity** → 42% of NBA players with 5th MT stress fractures cannot return to play — this is a career-altering injury
3. **Delaying surgical management** → Conservative management is not appropriate for 5th MT stress fractures in professional basketball players; surgery should not be deferred
4. **Missing talar dome lesions** → Osteochondral lesions of the talar dome are radiographically occult; consider MRI when recovery from ankle sprain is delayed
