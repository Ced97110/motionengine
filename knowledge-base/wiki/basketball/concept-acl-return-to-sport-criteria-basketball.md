# ACL Return-to-Sport Criteria for Basketball Players

## Summary
Deciding when a basketball player is ready to return to sport (RTS) after a knee injury is one of the most difficult decisions in sports medicine. There is currently no gold-standard RTS criteria. The decision should be collaborative — involving the treating clinician, medical team, coaches, and the athlete — and must be based on objective, individualized criteria rather than time from surgery alone. Using a battery of RTS criteria (rather than a single test) has been shown to reduce re-injury risk after ACL reconstruction by up to 84% (Grindem et al.). [S10, pp.717-718]

## When to Use
- End of late rehabilitation phase before clearing a player for full team participation
- After any knee injury, but especially after ACL reconstruction
- When evaluating re-injury risk in young athletes (highest risk group)

## Key Principles
1. Time from surgery is a necessary but insufficient criterion — objective functional criteria are required [S10, p.717]
2. Criteria must be individualized to the athlete using the **StARRT Framework** (Strategic Assessment of Risk and Risk Tolerance) [S10, p.717]
3. A battery of criteria — not a single test — is recommended to reduce future re-injury risk
4. RTS testing may be valuable in fatigued AND non-fatigued states — deficits may only appear under cardiovascular or cognitive fatigue [S10, p.718]
5. Return to play ≠ return to performance; return to performance requires meeting pre-injury performance indicators
6. Secondary prevention (preventing recurrence) must continue after return to play

## The StARRT Framework
Clinicians should apply the StARRT framework (Shrier) to guide individualized RTS criteria:
1. **Tissue health assessment:** demographics, signs, symptoms, injury/medical history — evaluate tissue readiness
2. **Activity risk assessment:** type of sport, position, competitive level, ability to protect the injury, psychological readiness — assess tissue stress
3. **Risk tolerance and modifiers:** time in season, external pressures, conflicts of interest, litigation risk, athlete pressure [S10, p.717]

## Return to Sport Criteria — Common in the Literature
Criteria supported by the strongest evidence [S10, p.717]:
- ≥90% quadriceps strength limb symmetry index (isometric or isokinetic)
- ≥90% on single-legged hop tests (single hop, triple hop, crossover hop for distance, and 6-m timed hop)
- ≥90% score on the Knee Outcomes Survey—Activities of Daily Living
- ≥90% global rating of perceived knee function (0–100%, 100% = pre-injury)
- ≥90% score on the ACL Return to Sport after Injury (ACL-RSI) Questionnaire

## Other Potential RTS Criteria
- Quadriceps strength equal to or greater than pre-injury
- ≥90% pre-injury strength in hamstrings, gluteus medius, and adductors
- Achieved maximum pre-injury running speed (straight and during play)
- Pre-injury or better on the YoYo Intermittent Recovery Test (valid/reliable for basketball) [S10, p.717]
- Pre-injury or better scores on basketball-movement tests: 10 m acceleration, 505 test, jump height
- Pre-injury or better score on a reliable movement assessment
- Multiple sessions of high-speed running completed
- On-court progressions completed with increasing contact and increasing number of players
- At least one (ideally multiple) full training/practice session with the team
- Built up chronic workload sufficient to avoid a large acute-to-chronic spike upon RTS [S10, p.717]

## Basketball-Specific RTS Tests
- **YoYo Intermittent Recovery Test:** valid and reliable for estimating VO₂max and comparing to pre-injury data [S10, p.717]
- **10 m acceleration and 505 test:** assess acceleration, deceleration, and change of direction tolerance [S10, p.717]
- **Jump height:** assesses knee tolerance to high forces
- **Dribble deficit:** sprint test performed with and without a basketball; time difference = dribble deficit; measures how much dribbling impairs speed, indicating dual-task readiness [S10, p.718]

## Return to Play vs. Return to Performance
- **Return to training** = sports participation resumed
- **Return to play** = begins playing games (not necessarily at pre-injury level)
- **Return to performance** = achieves key performance indicators equal to or greater than pre-injury

**Physical performance indicators:** accelerations/decelerations per minute, total distance, distance above a certain speed
**Basketball performance indicators:** points, rebounds, assists, blocks per game [S10, p.718]

## Secondary Prevention After Return
- ACL re-injury incidence ipsilateral or contralateral = up to 30%, with younger and female athletes at highest risk [S10, p.718]
- Modifiable risk factors for re-injury: biomechanical risk factors AND workload
- Effective neuromuscular prevention programs include: **11+ / FIFA 11+**, **Knäkontroll**, **Sportsmetrics** (demonstrates benefit for women) [S10, p.718]
- Common elements of effective prevention programs:
  - ≥20 min per prevention session
  - ≥30 min total volume per week
  - Exercise components involving **strengthening AND plyometrics** [S10, p.718]

## Common Mistakes
1. Clearing a player based on time alone — correction: apply the full RTS criteria battery
2. Testing only in non-fatigued states — correction: assess also under cardiovascular and cognitive fatigue
3. Declaring rehabilitation complete at return to play — correction: continue secondary prevention program indefinitely
4. Using group criteria without individualization — correction: apply StARRT framework to the specific athlete's context
