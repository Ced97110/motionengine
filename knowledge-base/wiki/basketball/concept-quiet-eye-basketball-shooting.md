# Quiet Eye and Gaze Behavior in Basketball Shooting

## Summary
Elite basketball shooters differ from near-elite shooters primarily in their **gaze behavior** — specifically, in the duration and timing of the **Quiet Eye (QE) period**. QE is the final stable fixation on the target before initiating the shooting movement. Longer QE duration correlates with higher shooting percentage, and anxiety directly reduces QE duration, which explains why pressure situations cause shooting performance to decline. Understanding and training gaze behavior offers a concrete, measurable pathway to improving shooting under pressure [S10, pp.984-986].

## When to Use
- Diagnosing why a player's shooting percentage drops in late-game pressure situations
- Designing free-throw pre-shot routines
- Coaching players through shooting slumps caused by anxiety
- Differentiating expert from near-expert shooters during player evaluation

## Key Principles
1. **Gaze behavior determines shooting accuracy**: Expert FT shooters (78% avg) maintain a significantly longer fixation on the target combined with an earlier fixation offset compared to near-experts (56% avg) [S10, p.985]
2. **Quiet Eye period is the critical variable**: QE = the final fixation from onset to the first observable movement of the hands into the shooting action (preshot). Longer QE = higher shooting percentage [S10, p.985-986]
3. **Anxiety shortens QE**: Under pressure, players look at multiple visual targets for very short durations rather than maintaining a single sustained fixation — the brain's stimulus-driven attentional system becomes overactive [S10, p.986]
4. **External focus outperforms internal focus**: Focusing attention on an external cue (the basket) rather than internal mechanics prevents athletes from focusing on internal distractors, negative thoughts, or skill mechanics under pressure [S10, p.986]
5. **The brain does not have one "screen"**: Sensory inputs (visual, tactile) are processed in multiple brain areas and integrated over time — mistiming of this integration during anxiety disrupts performance [S10, p.984]
6. **Perceived behavioral control = self-efficacy**: Loss of perceived control under pressure reduces information-processing capacity, increases physical fatigue perception, and triggers energy-saving mental strategies ("save it for the 4th quarter") [S10, p.985]

## Mechanisms of Quiet Eye
QE enhances performance through four mechanisms:
1. **Visuomotor accuracy**: The motor system is more accurate when the gaze system provides timely target information. Holding QE on the basket gives the arm the information it needs [S10, p.986]
2. **Visual memory buffer**: Maintaining a steady gaze stores target location effectively in working memory, reducing distraction from other visual sources [S10, p.986]
3. **Movement pre-programming**: Movement parameters (direction, distance, force) and timing/coordination of the arms are fine-tuned during this pre-shot cognitive programming window [S10, p.986]
4. **Psychomotor quiescence**: QE produces a general calming of the psychomotor system, reflected in increased neural efficiency [S10, p.986]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All shooters**: Develop a consistent pre-shot routine that locks gaze on a specific target point (center of the basket/back of the rim) and holds it until the shooting motion begins
- **Free throw shooters**: The QE on the front of the rim should be the last action before the hands initiate the shooting motion
- **Coaches**: Teach shooters to identify their fixation point and practice maintaining it under progressively more pressure-inducing conditions

## Perception, Attention, and Gaze in Defense
The same multi-sensory integration challenge applies defensively: a man-to-man defender must simultaneously integrate visual inputs (ball location, opponent position) and tactile inputs (body contact to detect cuts) across multiple brain areas in real time. The timing of this integration determines the defender's locus of control in each game situation [S10, p.984-985].

## Variations
### Anxiety Manipulation Training
Expose shooters to progressively higher-pressure conditions (audience, consequence, score scenarios) while coaching them to maintain QE discipline — building tolerance to pressure and protecting gaze behavior under stress [S10, p.986].

### Pre-Shot Routine Design
A structured pre-shot routine anchors the athlete's attention and provides the "external cue" described by Singer's five-step pre-performance routine: fixate the external target (basket) to prevent focus on internal distractors or mechanics [S10, p.986].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Changing the fixation point under pressure** → shifting attention between multiple visual targets destroys QE and shooting accuracy; coach players to commit to a single fixation point in their routine
2. **Internal focus during shooting** → thinking about mechanics ("bend my knees," "elbow in") under pressure activates the wrong attentional system → use external cue focus ("find the front of the rim")
3. **Ignoring gaze behavior in shooting diagnosis** → coaches often correct mechanics without addressing where the player is actually looking — fixing gaze can be more impactful than mechanical changes
