# Animal Agility Drills

## Objective
Develop lateral chain strength, total-body coordination, trunk stability, and multi-plane agility through asymmetrical animal-movement patterns that conventional linear exercises cannot replicate.

## Setup
- 1 or more players
- Open floor space (gymnasium, outdoor court, or any flat surface)
- Pull-up bar or jungle gym for chimp swing, bat flips, and monkey turns (optional)
- No other equipment needed

## Background
Mimicking animal movements to build athletic fitness has roots in ancient kung fu forms and has been practiced for thousands of years (evidenced by Paleolithic cave paintings). Conventional exercises (jumps, push-ups, pull-ups) are predominantly **linear** — the body moves up and down. Animal drills are **asymmetrical** — one limb moves before the other — exposing the body to lateral and torque forces that develop the small stabilizer muscles criss-crossing the trunk and hip/shoulder girdles, increasing coordination, efficiency, and injury resistance [S9].

**Rule of thumb**: Keep springy. These are not endurance exercises. Go until warm, not exhausted. Each drill can be performed forwards, backwards, or sideways for different stimulus.

## Execution

### 1. BEAR WALK
- Get on all fours with straight arms and slightly bent legs.
- Keep the butt high, spine relatively flat (not excessively rounded).
- Walk forward on palms and feet — a great total-body warmer for arms, abs, chest, hamstrings, glutes, and spine.
- Beginner-friendly. Can be done by any athlete.

### 2. PANTHER STALK (Spider-Man Crawl)
- Get low on all fours, butt down toward the ground.
- "Stalk" forward, touching the **knee to the elbow on the same side** (ipsilateral) — this is the key that activates the lateral chain.
- Keep entire body as low as possible without touching the floor except with palms and toes.
- **Crocodile variation**: body completely parallel to the floor, army-crawl style with knee-to-elbow contact.

### 3. CROCODILE WALK
- Full-body prone crawl with entire body as close to the floor as possible.
- Only palms and toes contact the ground.
- Forward movement in the lowest possible body position — excellent core and hip flexor challenge.

### 4. DOG SPRINT
- Full-speed all-fours running — let nature dictate the form.
- Hands lead, legs follow; extend the body through the legs and repeat.
- Leading with one side is natural and acceptable.
- World record for 100m on all fours: 16.87 seconds (Kenichi Ito) [S9].

### 5. LOCUST JUMPS
- Start in a push-up position with legs together.
- Bend the arms until the chest is a few inches from the floor.
- Hop forward in short jumps, keeping only palms and toes in contact with the floor.
- Attempt to cross the room this way. Great total-body warmer rooted in Indian yoga tradition (B.K.S. Iyengar, *Light on Yoga*).

### 6. CRAB WALK
- Sit on the floor, place hands behind you, and push hips up (reverse table position — ass down, face up).
- Walk forwards, backwards, sideways, or diagonally.
- Targets back muscles, scapular stabilizers, and triceps; trunk muscles work to keep the body elevated.
- **Advanced variation**: Walk in a full **bridge hold** (hips as high as possible, full spine extension).

### 7. DUCK WALK
- Squat as deeply as possible and walk while maintaining the lowest squat position achievable.
- Builds tension-flexibility in hips, thighs, knees, and ankles.
- A bipedal movement (two limbs only), unlike the crawls above.
- Advanced athletes can move quickly in this position.

### 8. KANGAROO HOP
- From the deep duck-walk squat position, jump from spot to spot rather than walking.
- Stay springy: remain on the balls of the feet.
- **Harder stretch variation**: Drop onto the heels between jumps.
- **Advanced variation**: Arms held behind the head (tests balance); hands can be placed on the floor between jumps for beginners.

### 9. CHIMP SWING
- Hang from a horizontal bar (any pull-up bar or jungle gym).
- Swing backwards, forwards, side-to-side, diagonally, or in circles.
- Works upper-body muscles through ranges that upright exercises never access.
- Entry-level arboreal locomotion movement.

### 10. BAT FLIPS
- Hang from a bar with an overhand grip.
- Bring the knees up slightly for momentum.
- Use that momentum to quickly **flip the hands over** (reversing the grip).
- Return to original grip and repeat.
- Builds explosive grip strength, forearms, elbows, shoulders, and abs.

### 11. MONKEY TURNS
- Hang from a bar with mixed grip (one hand overhand, one underhand).
- Release the overhand hand while turning the body 180° to face the opposite direction.
- Re-grip with the previously-overhand hand now in underhand grip.
- Requires single-hand hanging for part of the movement — advanced grip and shoulder strength required.
- Can be performed on monkey bars/jungle gym for freestyle locomotion.

## Coaching Points
- Focus on movement quality and range of motion, not speed or rep count
- "Keep springy" — these are agility/mobility tools, not conditioning exercises. Stop before fatigue degrades form
- Each drill can be performed in all three directions (forward, backward, sideways) — each feels completely different and targets different muscles
- Bear walk and duck walk are appropriate entry points for beginners; locust jumps, bat flips, and monkey turns are advanced
- Animal drills are excellent **warm-up or cool-down** choices, or can be inserted mid-workout as variety breaks [S9]

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Bear walk, crab walk, duck walk — all low-intensity, floor-based, beginner-accessible.
2. **Intermediate**: Panther stalk, crocodile walk, kangaroo hop, locust jumps — require more total-body coordination and strength.
3. **Advanced**: Dog sprint (speed), bat flips and monkey turns (require grip strength and single-arm hang capacity), bridge-hold crab walk.
