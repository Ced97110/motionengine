# Hard Cut to Paint

## Summary
A hard cut to the paint is an intentional, game-speed cut from a wing or slot to the restricted area, aimed at collapsing the defense, creating a finish at the rim, or opening a dump-off. The cutter sells the cut with change of pace, takes a decisive two-step acceleration, and finishes through contact if the look is there. It is distinct from a drift or relocation — the cutter intends to be a scoring threat on arrival.

## When to Use
- When the defender ball-watches or sags off the cutter
- Off a skip pass or ball-reversal, to punish late rotation
- As a baseline-runner action to stretch help defenders
- As a backdoor read when the defender denies the pass

## Key Principles
1. Set the defender up first — one deliberate jab or hesitation before the cut commits.
2. Accelerate on the second step. The cut is not a jog; the defender must not recover.
3. Straight-line path to the rim unless the floor forces a curl. Avoid curving.
4. Hands ready, eyes on the ball — target-catch with two hands at hip height.
5. If the help rotates, the cutter becomes the passer — look for the corner shooter or the opposite post.

## Player Responsibilities
- **Cutter**: set-up step, acceleration, finish or dump-off read
- **Passer**: early read on the cut; two-hand bounce or direct pass timed to the second acceleration step
- **Off-ball spacers**: do NOT clog — if the cutter is committed, stay at the arc

## Common Mistakes
- Cutter drifts instead of accelerating — defender recovers unchallenged
- Cutter telegraphs the cut by staring at the rim before moving
- Passer waits for the cutter to arrive at the rim before delivering — the window closes
