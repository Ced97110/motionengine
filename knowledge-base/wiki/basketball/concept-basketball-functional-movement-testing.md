# Basketball Functional Movement Testing (Lab-Based)

## Summary
Lab-based functional movement testing provides objective, quantifiable data on how an elite basketball player moves during sport-specific tasks. While clinical movement screens identify whether dysfunction exists, lab-based 3D motion analysis reveals *why* — uncovering joint angles, moments, torques, and asymmetries that 2D screens miss. The gold-standard tests for basketball players integrate lateral, vertical, and single-leg demands that directly simulate shooting, rebounding, defending, and cutting. Findings from these tests feed into a 3-phase correction and biomechanical enhancement program that progresses from isolated interventions to full basketball-specific integration. [S10, pp.650-655]

## When to Use
- Pre-season baseline screening for all players (not just those with injury history)
- Return-to-play decision-making after injury
- Identifying athletes at elevated ACL or ankle injury risk
- Designing individualized corrective exercise programs
- Tracking biomechanical adaptation over a season

## Key Principles
1. Testing reveals **how** an athlete moves; additional range of motion, strength, and injury history data reveals **why** — both are required before prescribing corrective interventions.
2. Two-dimensional analysis adequately identifies sagittal and frontal plane kinematics; 3D analysis adds transverse plane motion (hip/knee rotations) and joint moment calculations that are critical for identifying dynamic lower extremity valgus.
3. The **distribution of sagittal plane moments** is particularly informative — identifying whether a player is hip-dominant (gluteus maximus) or knee-dominant (quadriceps) during loaded movements.
4. A player with poor movement test results AND a player with adequate movement test results should both progress through functional movement training and basketball-specific integration phases — all athletes benefit.
5. The greater the loading demands placed on a player, the more critical it is that they demonstrate optimal functional movement quality with minimal compensation.
6. Testing results must not be directly equated with injury prediction; however, **specific interventions following testing have a profound impact on injury prevention**.

## The Four Lab-Based Tests

### 1. Drop Vertical Jump (DVJ)
- **Gold standard** for assessing jump landing biomechanics.
- **Setup**: Athlete stands on a 30-cm box with feet spaced 35 cm apart.
- **Execution**: Fall forward off the box (no stepping or jumping), land with both feet on separate force platforms, immediately perform a maximal vertical jump.
- **Variables of interest**: Peak knee and hip flexion angles and moments, sagittal plane angular excursions, peak knee abduction angles and moments, distribution of sagittal plane moments.
- **Clinical value**: Prospectively identifies individuals at higher risk of primary AND secondary ACL injury.
- [S10, p.650]

### 2. Single-Leg Countermovement Jump
- **Best test** for eliciting asymmetrical lower extremity function.
- **Setup**: Athlete balances on one limb on a force platform; a physical target is set at the height of their maximum double-leg CMJ.
- **Execution**: Perform a maximal vertical jump while reaching with the opposite upper extremity for the overhead target.
- **Variables of interest**: Accurate jump height via center-of-mass vertical displacement; distribution of sagittal plane joint torques; side-to-side asymmetries.
- [S10, p.650]

### 3. Single-Leg Lateral Hop
- **Most basketball-specific** test — integrates three key basketball demands simultaneously:
  1. Lateral demands (defending)
  2. Vertical demands (rebounding, defending, shooting)
  3. Single-leg landings (rebounding, cutting)
- **Setup**: Athlete stands one-half of their height away from the side of a force plate.
- **Execution**: Balance on the outside leg → jump laterally → land on the other leg → immediately perform a maximal vertical jump reaching with the hand opposite the jumping leg.
- **Clinical value**: Best differentiates basketball player mechanics from other sports; elicits high knee abduction moments and ankle inversion velocity — both identified as predictors of future knee and ankle injury.
- [S10, pp.650-651]

### 4. 10-Second Single-Leg Hop for Height
- Athlete jumps as quickly as possible for maximum height on one leg for 10 seconds.
- **Variables of interest**: Side-to-side asymmetries (easy to identify due to unilateral nature); degree of height degradation over time (less = better); movement quality changes — athletes often transition from hip-dominant (max height) to knee- or ankle-dominant (quickness) strategy as they fatigue.
- [S10, p.651]

## Movement Correction / Biomechanical Enhancement: 3-Phase Philosophy

Upon completing all tests, a complete picture of the athlete's biomechanical function is assembled. The program then transitions into 3 corrective phases:

### Phase 1 — Isolated Variable Interventions
| Corrective Category | Intervention Objective | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Joint mobility | Restore ROM | Posterior-to-anterior hip mobilization |
| Muscle extensibility | Lengthen tight tissue | Anterior hip stretch |
| Muscle strength | Activate weak muscles | Hip extension (prone) |
| Muscle co-activation | Improve neuromuscular timing | Hip extension + transverse abdominis stabilization (standing) |

### Phase 2 — Functional Movement Training
- Addresses issues found during movement testing in multi-joint, functional patterns.
- Examples:
  1. **Squat series** (mini band at knees): Squat → Squat to calf raise → Squat to hop
  2. **Slider to Back** (lightly resisted): single-leg slider posterior chain loading
  3. **Step-up drive** (resisted): single-leg drive mimicking first-step mechanics
- Targets: Functional joint mobility, functional muscle extensibility, functional strength/stability, neuromuscular coordination.

### Phase 3 — Basketball-Specific Integration
- Train on the court, with a ball, moving against an opponent — to stimulate the environment and unanticipated nature of the game.
- Examples:
  1. **Lunge drive to basket** (resisted): mimics first-step drive to the rim
  2. **Drive against player** (with ball): live opponent adds unpredictability
- Targets: Motor control, power, technical skill/awareness, sport vision.

> **Key insight**: "It is essential to train the athlete on the court, with a ball in their hands and moving against an opponent to stimulate environment and unanticipated nature of the game to obtain optimal carryover into game performance." [S10, p.652]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stopping at Phase 1 or 2** → Always progress to basketball-specific (Phase 3) integration for true carryover to performance.
2. **Testing only injured or symptomatic players** → All athletes benefit from functional movement training and basketball-specific integration, regardless of test results.
3. **Equating test results with injury prediction** → Testing identifies intervention targets; it is the *intervention* that reduces injury risk, not the testing alone.
4. **Ignoring the 'why'** → A poor movement test result could stem from tightness, weakness, or poor motor control (learned compensation) — each requires a different intervention.
5. **Neglecting loading history** → The greater the loading demands on a player, the more critical optimal movement quality becomes.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Participate in baseline testing at the start of pre-season; engage in Phase 1-3 programming as indicated by individual test results.
- **Bigs (PF, C)**: Higher body weight + rebounding jump volume = greater importance of managing dynamic valgus and hip-dominant landing patterns.
- **Guards (PG, SG, SF)**: High cutting and lateral demand means single-leg lateral hop results are particularly predictive of ankle and knee risk.
