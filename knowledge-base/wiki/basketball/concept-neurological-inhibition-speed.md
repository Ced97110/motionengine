# Neurological Inhibition and Speed

## Summary
The human body is physically capable of much higher levels of athletic performance than athletes can normally consciously access. The reason is neurological inhibition: the nervous system deliberately places subconscious "blocks" on movement speed and force output to prevent the body from damaging itself or burning out. These blocks are adaptive — they exist for good reason — but they also cap athletic performance well below true physiological potential.

Paul Wade, drawing on Loren Christensen's *Speed Training* (1996), argues that the mind can directly manipulate these subconscious blocks [S9]. Simply watching super-fast movements, thinking about moving quickly, or adopting a speed-based identity and vocabulary convinces the nervous system that fast movement is safe — and the blocks are lifted. Christensen himself reported that at least half of his personal speed improvement during his research came not from physical training but from sustained mental focus on speed [S9, p.7].

For basketball coaches, this is a powerful and underused training tool: mental rehearsal, positive speed-based cuing, watching elite fast players, and removing negative self-talk ("I'm too slow") all have measurable physiological effects on movement speed.

## When to Use
- Before explosive training sessions — "prime" the nervous system with speed-focused visualization
- During skill instruction — cue players with "fast and loose" rather than "slow it down"
- When a player is performing below their physical ability — investigate self-limiting beliefs as a factor
- In film sessions — watch elite fast players (especially at game speed or slightly accelerated) to calibrate the nervous system's sense of "normal" movement speed

## Key Principles
1. **The body can go faster than the mind allows** — neurological blocks, not just muscle fiber, are the primary limiter of athletic speed in trained athletes
2. **Mental focus on speed creates physical speed** — sustained thinking about fast movement convinces the nervous system it is safe, lifting inhibitory blocks
3. **Watching fast movement is training** — observing elite sprinters, guards, or explosive athletes activates the same neurological calibration as physical practice
4. **Language matters** — banish "I'm too slow" and replace with "I can go faster"; internal vocabulary shapes neural expectations
5. **"Fast and loose" is the cue** — relaxed, rapid movement (not tense, forced movement) is the neurological target; tension is the enemy of speed
6. **Volume of fast reps beats slow perfect reps** — the more times the nervous system experiences high-speed movement safely, the more it recalibrates its upper limit upward

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Adopt a speed-based identity and practice pre-training visualization of explosive, fast movement
- **PG**: First-step quickness is highly neurological — mental training has outsized return on investment for ball handlers
- **All**: In film review, pay attention to the fastest moments of elite players as calibration, not just scheme

## Variations
### Accelerated Video Visualization
Watch film of elite fast players (e.g., pro guards in open court) slightly sped up. The subconscious nervous system does not clearly distinguish real from observed movement and recalibrates accordingly.

### Pre-Practice Speed Priming
Spend 2-3 minutes before explosive training sessions in a quiet space imagining yourself moving with maximum speed and fluidity. Use the words "fast" and "loose" as anchors.

### Positive Cuing Protocol
Replace all negative speed language in practice: "I'm too slow" → "I can go faster"; "Slow down" → "Relax and accelerate"; "You're not quick enough" → "Faster feet!"

## Common Mistakes
1. Treating speed as purely a physical quality → neurological training is equally important
2. Using tense, straining movement cues → tension = inhibition = slower movement
3. Watching slow or incorrect movement in film review → this recalibrates the nervous system downward
4. Negative self-talk about speed → neurologically reinforces the inhibitory blocks you are trying to lift
