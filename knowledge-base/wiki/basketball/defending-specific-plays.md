# Defending Specific Plays — Herb Brown's Complete System

## Summary
Chapter 9 of *Let's Talk Defense* provides Herb Brown's granular defensive responses to virtually every common offensive maneuver. The unifying principle across all situations is **pressure the ball to make passing difficult**. This is the first and non-negotiable rule for defending every specific play in this chapter.

Brown's system relies on keeping the isolated player one-on-one in front of help defenders, using nail/plug/elbow/box weak-side help, and employing the pro Shadow and Tilt format or Mike Woodson's elbow-and-boxes help defense principles when needed. [S1, pp.130-146]

## When to Use
- Any time the opponent runs identifiable set actions (flex, High-Post, horns, etc.)
- Post-up and isolation possessions
- When scouting reveals a team's preferred off-ball screening actions

## Key Principles
1. **Pressure the ball** — making passing difficult is the starting point for every defensive maneuver
2. **Keep isolated player in front of help** — play one-on-one but force toward the nail/elbow/box defenders
3. **Body up to screen recipients** — force them high, force them uphill, take away the preferred catch spot
4. **Don't chase the back picker** — screener's defender must not overrun and screen their own teammate
5. **Stay attached** — defenders must maintain contact with their man through every screening action
6. **Plug/nail/elbow/box defenders drawn to ball** — weak-side help must be active and positioned properly
7. **Force uphill** — in virtually every situation, forcing offensive players uphill toward help is the preferred direction

## Player Responsibilities

See individual maneuver breakdowns below. The general weak-side help structure:
- **Nail/Plug man**: covers the middle lane, stops drives and high-low action
- **Elbow defenders**: strong-side and weak-side coverage above the lane
- **Box defenders**: weak-side block area coverage
- **Ball pressure**: the on-ball defender must be aggressive enough to make every entry pass contested

## Variations

### Cross Screen Defense
Body up to the player receiving the screen — force him from **low to high** to prevent a low-block catch. Stay attached to prevent a low-post mismatch. On big-on-big horizontal cross screens: body up and force uphill. Option: screener's defender bumps the recipient while recipient's defender goes under and beats him to a spot above the low block. On running cross screens (screener gets a running start): may need to zone up, talk, and help the screener's defender over. [S1, pp.130-131]

### Flex Cut Defense
Body up, ride, and **force the cutter low and out of bounds** to remove him from the play. Stay attached to the screener to prevent a post-up seal after the cut. On big-man flex cut off another big: aggressively switch. Can also "butt screen" — screen the offensive player before he reaches position so he cannot cut inside across your face. Force him backdoor or make him change his path. Ball defender: pressure the passer, do not allow an uncontested entry pass. [S1, pp.131-132]

### High-Post Vertical Post Cut Defense
Defend the cutter based on whether he is a low-post threat:
- **Low-post threat**: body up, ride the cutter over the top of the screen
- **Non-threat**: go below the screen (shortcut) to take away his baseline single/double screen options on the other side
"We don't want to give offensive players a choice." [S1, p.132]

### Utah Diagonal Up Screen from Baseline
Screener's defender calls out the screen. Recipient's defender bodies up, rides, and forces opponent over the screen and away from the basket. Screener's defender: do NOT chase the back picker — get too close and you screen your own teammate. Position to bump, step out, and hold up the cutter. [S1, p.132]

### Hawk Cut Defense
Same as High-Post cut principles — depends on low-post threat level. The defender guarding the dribbler must force his man uphill so the ball cannot be entered to the cutter posting up after the cut. [S1, p.133]

### Step-Up or Open-Court Back Screen Defense
Trap the dribbler using the screen OR aggressively switch and force dribbler back uphill away from basket. Switch all three defenders involved in step-up back pick and flare — especially in late-game or short-clock situations. [S1, p.133]

### Diagonal Back Screen Defense
Body up, force cutter to take a high path. Screener's defender bumps to stand up or hold up the screen recipient. Do NOT chase the back picker and screen your own teammate. [S1, p.133]

### Jam or Flare Screen Defense
Close the distance and body up to the player receiving the screen. Screener's defender helps by: (a) screening the screener, or (b) faking at the flaring player to hold him up. Screener's defender must also watch for the screener slipping and diving to the basket. [S1, pp.133-134]

### Turnouts and Curls Defense
Hold the screener up. Defender on the cutter locks and trails, gets on outside shoulder and hip (with big man's help), and **makes him curl the screen**. Screener's defender must contact show. Once ball is passed to the curling player, the passer's defender drops immediately to the nail to stop penetration.

**Gap option**: defender on screen recipient shoots the gap; screener's defender stands screener up then steps back to let the defender go through in front. Can also squeeze the screener or give the cutter only one option before he moves. [S1, pp.134-135]

### Wide Diagonal Down Screen Defense
Stand up the screener and shortcut to make the receiver fade, OR topside the screen recipient and force him baseline. [S1, p.136]

### Zipper or Straight Vertical Down Screen Defense
Stand up the screener, stay attached, deny and force the receiver away — or let him catch and force toward the near sideline to stop ball reversal. Key principle: **never play with your back to the ball** — always be moving toward the ball to slip the screen. "Your mind-set should be to never permit yourself to be screened, and to slip inside the screener if possible." [S1, p.136]

### Isolation Defense
Force the isolated offensive player **uphill toward help** whenever possible. Options:
- Force to the middle (toward plug/nail, elbow, box defenders)
- Force to the near sideline (with nearest big brought over as help — pro Shadow or Tilt tactic)
Other three defenders occupy elbows and boxes. [S1, pp.136-137]

### Post-Up Defense
**Front all post-ups** when the ball is at the free-throw line or below. Sit on the knees of the posted player to root him off the post and protect against the lock-and-lob. Three-quarter the post player when ball is above the free-throw line. Must have **great ball pressure** whenever fronting the post. [S1, p.137]

### Staggered Screens Defense (Horizontal, Vertical, or Diagonal)
Stand up the screeners, bump/body the cutters, force cutters over the screens if possible. Trail on the first screen, go through or shortcut the second. If screener's defender is beaten, have the screen recipient's defender call a switch up the line. [S1, p.137]

### Weak-Side Cuts Toward the Ball
Defenders on weak side must be **drawn to and stay level with the ball**. Keep opponents away from body — don't let them close distance or cut across your face. Maintain contact, keep cutter in front, or body up and ride high. Never turn head away from ball — ball-you-man principle. If you lose sight of your man, open up and retreat to the goal (ball will draw you to your man). [S1, pp.138-139]

### High-Low Action Defense
Pressure the passer at the high post — play him as if the dribble is used (treat him like a driver). Post defender must front and sit on the knees of the low-post player with hands up. Must stay connected. [S1, p.139]

### Fades Defense
When offensive player fades a turnout or down screen — defender may shoot the gap instead of locking and trailing. Screener's defender may have to switch out to pick up the shooter if his teammate is screened. [S1, p.139]

### Pick and Pop Defense
Try to stay attached when screener picks and pops for a shot. Easier to stay connected if you can stand up the screener. May squeeze the screener and let the dribbler's defender go under the screen. [S1, p.139]

### Feed the Post Cuts Defense
When defending a cutter who has passed to the post: get level with the ball, **butt to the baseline**, keep the cutter in front. This enables digging on the post or forcing the cutter to the middle where weak-side help is waiting. Can also take man to the goal and snap back to double the post. [S1, p.139]

### Brush Screen Defense
Cutter's or screener's defender must slow down to provide help in case the ball carrier's defender is screened. Weak-side plug/nail, elbow, and box defenders must see the ball, be drawn to it, and be in position to help. [S1, p.140]

### Lob Defense
Stay attached and/or keep opponents in front. Prevent lock-and-lob by remaining connected and guarding on the **inside** instead of three-quartering. On-ball pressure is imperative. If you lose sight of your man: snap back and retreat to goal with hands up in passing lanes, OR open up and retreat without losing sight of the ball. On-ball pressure and weak-side defensive help are always necessary. [S1, p.140]

### Horns Staggered Double Screen Defense
Defender on the cutter can: (a) play topside to force cutter low below the screens, or (b) go over the first screen and through on the second — provided screener's defenders step out and slow the cutter. [S1, pp.140-141]

### Dive Defense
Once you lose sight of your man (who cuts to basket when dribbler drives at you): either **snap back and retreat to the goal** OR open up, make yourself wide, and retreat with hands up. [S1, p.141]

### Loop Defense
Lock and trail — get on the outside shoulder and hip of the offensive player — OR play to the inside and turn/force the cutter to the corner or sideline away from the screen. [S1, p.141]

### Big-to-Big Diagonal Screen Defense
Body up and force the screen recipient from **low to high**. Alternatively, switch defenders of equal size. [S1, p.141]

### Rip or Blind (Back) Screen Defense
Talk and call out the screen. Do NOT chase the back picker. Body up and ride the cutter high and away from the basket when the screen is anticipated. [S1, p.141]

### Reverse PnR (Small Sets Screen for Tall Player)
Jump out and aggressively switch — send dribbler back uphill. Alternative: trap aggressively when players come together. [S1, p.142]

### Bonnie or L Cut Defense
When the cutter cuts high: get below and in front of him and try to pick up the offensive foul as he reverses and cuts to the basket. Get inside and deny on the right-angle cut to the basket. [S1, p.142]

### Blast Cut Defense
Jump to the ball once a wing pass is made (to eliminate inside cuts leading to easy baskets). Then: take away an option and play inside to force cutter to ball-side corner, OR get on outside shoulder and force cutter away from ball. If he posts up, front him. Once cutter goes away, take him to the rim or weak-side foul lane and look to provide weak-side help. [S1, p.143]

### Corner-Filled Situations (Wing/Side PnR)
Communicate and force the dribbler sideline where help exists — can possibly trap the ball. [S1, p.143]

### Pass and Screen Away Defense
On a horizontal pass and screen away: switch the back man with the man nearest the ball (back man calls the switch), OR body up and ride the man over the screen. [S1, p.143]

### Dribble Handoff Defense
Go through or inside your teammate whose man passes the ball, OR aggressively trap or switch the handoff and send the dribbler back to his defender. May overplay the receiver to force him backdoor. [S1, p.143]

### Get Action (Pass and Follow for Return Handoff) Defense
Jump switch and trap, OR step back to allow the defender whose opponent receives the pass to go through. Do NOT get screened. [S1, pp.143-144]

### Fake Cross Screen and Pop/Bump Back Defense
Remain attached and stay with the screener while bodying up and forcing the screen recipient up the lane. [S1, p.144]

### Baseline Cross Screen Defense
Normally switch big-to-big. When smaller player screens for bigger: stay connected, body up, force from **low to high**. Apply maximum on-ball pressure to make entry pass difficult. [S1, p.145]

### Pistol Action Defense (Multiple High Point-of-Ball Screens)
Switch whenever possible — especially in short/late-clock situations. If can't switch the second screen: body up and ride the screener over the second screen with tough on-ball pressure. [S1, p.145]

### Get Hand-Back and Flare Defense
Switch (same as Pistol action) OR body up and fight over the jam/flare screen. May trap the hand-back if anticipated. [S1, p.145]

### Misdirection Action Defense
Screener's defender calls out the screen and bodies the cutter to slow him. Cutter's defender forces his man from high to low. [S1, p.145]

### Double-Doubles Defense
Lock and trail while screener's defenders screen the screeners — enabling the lock-and-trail defenders to stay attached. Stand screeners up as far from the cutter as possible. May switch the second screen in each double or shoot the gap. On-ball pressure is a must. [S1, p.146]

### Elbow Pass and Dive Defense
Off-ball defenders must be drawn to the ball on every dribble or pass. Body up and beat weak-side cutters to their spots, or deny and force them high or backdoor. Defender on the elbow ball handler pressures as if the dribble is used (treat him like a driver). [S1, p.146]

### Pick the Picker Defense
Stand up screeners and lock/trail or shoot the gap on the picker receiving a screen. May switch the perimeter man on the weak side with the screener's defender if corner/wing is occupied. [S1, p.146]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Losing sight of the ball while guarding a weak-side cutter** → stay level with the ball, use peripheral vision, ball-you-man principle
2. **Chasing the back picker** → screener's defender must never overrun and pick off their own teammate
3. **Failing to body up on screen recipients** → soft defense allows easy low-block catches and cuts to the goal
4. **On-ball defender failing to pressure** → every play in this system requires maximum ball pressure to succeed
5. **Post defender not fronting when ball is at or below FT line** → three-quartering gives the lock-and-lob opportunity
6. **Weak-side defenders not being drawn to the ball** → level with ball must be the default, not sagging too deep
