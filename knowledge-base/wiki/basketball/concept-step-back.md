# Step Back

## Summary
The step back is an advanced shot-creation move used when an offensive player has beaten their primary defender with a drive but is confronted by a second help defender. By planting the inside (pivot) foot and pushing off it to step backward and away, the offensive player creates the space necessary to shoot comfortably on balance. It is one of the most advanced techniques in a scorer's arsenal and must be built from the ground up with correct footwork before adding a shot.

There are two primary variations: the **weak-side step back** (easier, learned first) and the **strong-side step back** (more complex, requires squaring to target). An equally important counter is the **step-back, rise-up-and-go**, which exploits defenders who recover to contest the step-back shot. [S5, pp.108-111]

## When to Use
- After beating your primary defender and encountering a help defender in the lane
- When driving into the paint and a second defender takes away the pull-up or layup
- To create long-range three-point opportunities by using a step back from further out
- When the defense is loaded to prevent drives and a quick step back opens a midrange or three-point shot

## Key Principles
1. **Always push off the inside pivot foot** — not the outside foot; using the outside foot merely pulls the body backward slowly, giving the defender time to recover [S5, p.108]
2. **Land on balance in shooter's stance** — shooting foot slightly in front, toe pointing to target, knees bent, shoulders in front of feet, head in front of shoulders [S5, p.109]
3. **Shoulders must be in front of feet on landing** — if not, weight goes backward and the shot falls short [S5, p.109]
4. **Stay low** — being upright causes drift in the direction of the dribble, resulting in a fading, off-balance shot [S5, p.109]
5. **Weak side before strong side** — master the easier weak-side step back first; progress to the strong-side only after the weak-side is comfortable [S5, p.108]

## Variations

### Weak-Side Step Back
The shooter steps back at an angle to their **weak side** (right-handed players step back left; left-handed step back right).

**Execution (right-handed shooter going right to left):**
1. Drive hard and low at the defender
2. Plant the **right (strong-side/shooting) foot** between the defender's two feet
3. Push back off that foot, hopping or stepping back with the back foot to create space
4. Land on balance in shooter's stance

Because the strong-side foot is planted, the shooter is already in shot line — **no drift or squaring is required** to align with the basket. This is what makes it easier than the strong-side version. [S5, pp.108-109]

### Strong-Side Step Back
The shooter drives with the shooting hand to the strong side and steps back at an angle to the **strong side**.

**Execution (right-handed shooter going left to right):**
1. Dribble with the shooting (right) hand, driving to the strong side
2. Plant the **left (nonshooting/inside) foot**
3. Hop or step back to create space
4. **Square or drift toward the direction you are going** to re-establish shot line with the basket
5. Land on balance in shooter's stance and rise to shoot

The path of the strong-side step back **looks like a checkmark** rather than a straight step back. Staying low is critical — being upright causes uncontrolled drift. [S5, p.109]

### Step Back, Rise Up, and Go (Counter)
When a defender recovers and closes out on the step back:
1. Execute the step back — defender reacts and closes the space
2. **Rise the head and shoulders** as if about to shoot — defender commits to the contest
3. **Lower the head and shoulders** and **blow past the defender** for a pull-up jump shot or layup at the rim

**Crossover variation**: After a weak-side step back (dribbling with the weak hand), if the defender closes on the dribble hand, immediately **cross over** and drive in the other direction before getting into the pull-up. [S5, p.111]

## Drilling Progression
1. **Master footwork first** — no shot; just practice the push-off and landing mechanics
2. **Top of key, live dribble — Weak side**: attack the paint with weak hand, plant shooting foot, step back to midrange, shoot
3. **Top of key, live dribble — Strong side**: attack with strong hand, plant nonshooting foot, square, step back to midrange, shoot
4. **Extend to three-point range** once midrange is consistent
5. **On-the-move catch routine**: From either wing, spin out toward baseline or middle; catch on inside pivot foot; incorporate step back (4 combinations per wing: driving weak and strong on both baseline and middle spin-outs)
6. **Combine with other moves**: e.g., catch on move → stub toe → shot fake → show-and-go → crossover → strong-side step back [S5, pp.110-111]
7. **Step-back-rise-up-and-go**: Drill the counter from both weak-side and strong-side step backs, first off live dribble at top of key, then on the move from either wing

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stepping back with the outside foot** — creates little space and is slow → always push off the inside pivot foot [S5, p.108]
2. **Being too upright on the strong-side step back** → uncontrolled drift; shot falls off-balance and fading → stay low throughout [S5, p.109]
3. **Shoulders behind the feet on landing** → weight transfers backward; shot falls short → ensure shoulders are in front of feet at landing [S5, p.109]
4. **Not squaring on the strong-side step back** → misaligned shot line → consciously drift/square to the basket during the step back [S5, p.109]
5. **Skipping the footwork phase** → sloppy mechanics at game speed → always isolate footwork before adding the shot
