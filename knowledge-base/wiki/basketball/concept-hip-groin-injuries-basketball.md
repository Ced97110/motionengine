# Hip and Groin Injuries in Basketball — Overview

## Summary
The hip and groin of basketball players are subjected to high biomechanical loads from jumping, pivoting, cutting, and direction-change demands. This leads to a significant burden of both soft tissue and intra-articular injuries. The majority of hip injuries are soft tissue strains and contusions managed conservatively, but intra-articular pathologies — particularly FAIS — have been increasingly recognized and may have been underestimated for years [S10, p.328].

Basketball-specific movement patterns (pivoting, flexion with rotation, abrupt direction changes) create a unique injury profile. Hip injuries in the NBA account for a meaningful proportion of time-loss injuries, and retired NBA athletes are at significantly higher risk for hip and groin pain and total hip arthroplasty compared with the general population [S10, p.328].

## When to Use
- Any basketball player presenting with hip, groin, or inguinal pain
- Distinguishing between soft tissue (adductor, hip flexor, CMI) and intra-articular (FAIS, labral tear, dysplasia) sources
- Designing injury prevention programs addressing the hip/pelvis complex
- Return-to-sport decision making after hip injury or surgery

## Key Principles

### Injury Taxonomy
1. **Soft tissue injuries (most common)**: adductor strains, hip flexor strains, contusions — managed primarily conservatively with rehabilitation protocols and activity modification [S10, p.328]
2. **Core muscle injury (CMI)**: sports hernia / athletic pubalgia — injury to abdominal wall fascial attachments at pubic symphysis; triggered by abdominal hyperextension + hip abduction during cutting [S10, p.317]
3. **Intra-articular injuries**: FAIS (most common), labral tears, cartilage injuries, acetabular dysplasia — account for up to 10% of all hip/groin injuries in elite football and a growing proportion in basketball [S10, p.318]
4. **Peri-articular impingement**: AIIS/subspine (hip flexion >90°) and ischiofemoral impingement (hip extension/adduction/ER) — manage conservatively initially [S10, p.320]

### Basketball-Specific Risk Profile
5. **Pivoting sport risk**: basketball players involved in constant pivoting, flexion, and rotation are at increased risk for FAIS, labral tears, and cartilage injury [S10, pp.320, 328]
6. **Adolescent development risk**: high-intensity sport around adolescence may cause physeal stress contributing to CAM morphology development — higher FAIS incidence in high-level athletes vs. age-matched non-athletes [S10, p.320]
7. **Long-term OA risk**: >36% of retired NBA athletes report current hip/groin pain; 14.7% have undergone total hip arthroplasty; retired NBA athletes are more likely than the general population to require arthroplasty [S10, p.328]

### Diagnosis Framework
8. **History**: acute vs. overuse, contact vs. non-contact, pain characterization, radiation, childhood hip disorders, steroid/alcohol use (avascular necrosis risk), OA [S10, pp.320-321]
9. **Examination**: gait assessment, seated and supine ROM, Thomas test, adductor squeeze test, FADDIR test, FABER test, Trendelenburg test, single-leg functional tasks [S10, pp.321-323]
10. **Imaging**: X-ray (AP pelvis + lateral) → MRI (soft tissue/labrum) → CT (bony morphology, 3D reconstruction for CAM/version) → ultrasound-guided diagnostic injection [S10, pp.324-325]

### Treatment Philosophy
11. **Conservative first**: NSAIDs, injections, physical therapy — most soft tissue injuries resolve; extra-articular pathology responds better to conservative care than intra-articular [S10, p.326]
12. **Surgery for recalcitrant cases**: high-level athletes with intra-articular pathology (FAIS, labral tears) benefit more from operative management; hip arthroscopy is the procedure of choice [S10, pp.326-327]
13. **RTS criterion-based**: leg symmetry in hip strength and one-leg jump performance, pain-free complex sport-specific tasks, psychological readiness [S10, p.327]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Under-diagnosing intra-articular pathology** → Prevalence of FAIS in basketball has grown; high index of suspicion required for any persistent deep groin pain with hip flexion/rotation
2. **Attributing all groin pain to adductors** → Systematic workup including FADDIR/FABER tests and imaging is essential
3. **Missing long-term OA risk** → Basketball programs should proactively manage CAM morphology and FAIS to reduce career-long hip arthroplasty rates
4. **Ignoring adolescent athlete hip development** → Physical load management in youth players may reduce CAM morphology development and lifetime FAIS risk
