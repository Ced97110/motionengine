# Setting Screens

## Summary
Screening is one of the most teachable and impactful fundamentals in basketball. A well-set screen creates an open shot or a layup; a poorly set screen results in a moving screen violation, a recovered defender, or a missed scoring opportunity. Coaches frequently overlook screening technique in favor of drilling the cutter, but the screener's execution is equally important to the play's success.

The principles below apply across all screen types: down screens, cross-screens, flare screens, back screens, and on-ball screens.

## When to Use
- Any half-court offensive possession involving off-ball or on-ball screens
- SLOB and ATO plays built around box formations and screen action
- Motion offense where players screen away to create off-ball movement

## Key Principles
1. **Hunt the defender, not the space.** The screener must locate and move to the defender being screened — not just set a screen in open space. "Don't screen space!" [S4, p.96]
2. **Establish a legal, stationary position.** Set your feet before the cutter arrives. Moving into the defender's path at the last second is a moving screen. [S4, p.77]
3. **Hold the screen until the cutter clears.** Releasing early allows the defender to recover. The screener is still doing their job as long as they hold position. [S4, p.96]
4. **Set screens at the correct angle.** Angle determines whether the defender can slip under the screen or must go over it. Incorrect angle negates the screen entirely. [S4, p.94]
5. **Time the screen correctly.** Setting a screen too early telegraphs the play to the defense. Too late and the cutter is already past. [S4, pp.82, 96]
6. **Create separation from your own defender.** On ball screens especially, exploding out quickly to set the screen prevents the screener's defender from hedging on the ball-handler. [S4, p.94]
7. **After screening, read and react.** Roll to the rim, pop to the perimeter, seal the defender in the post, or flash to the ball — depending on how the defense plays it.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: When used as a secondary screener (as in double screens), must time arrival to coincide with the cutter running off. [S4, p.79]
- **SG**: Often a wing screener in SLOB plays; must set physical back screens or cross-screens.
- **SF**: Can serve as both screener and cutter depending on the play; must be disciplined in holding position.
- **PF**: Primary screener in most SLOB and half-court plays; responsible for down screens, back screens, and on-ball screens.
- **C**: Must set the most physical screens; on-ball screens should be set with proper angle to force the defender to go over, not under.

## Variations
### Down Screen
Screener comes from above and screens below for a cutter going up toward the ball. Common in SLOB plays and motion offense.

### Cross-Screen
Screener moves horizontally across the lane to screen for a post player or cutter moving across the key. Used in Triangle SLOB and similar plays.

### Back Screen
Screener sets a screen on a defender's back, typically near the basket, to free a cutter for a lob or layup. See Loop Fly SLOB.

### Flare Screen
Screener sets a screen away from the ball to free a cutter moving toward the perimeter (flaring out). Used in Diamond SLOB.

### On-Ball Screen (Ball Screen)
Screener sets a screen directly on the ball-handler's defender. Must be set at an angle that forces the defender over the top. See Prowl SLOB and Zipper Curl counter.

### Double Screen
Two players set adjacent screens for one cutter. The cutter reads which side to use. [S4, pp.78-79]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Screening open space instead of the defender** → correction: physically locate the defender before setting the screen. [S4, p.96]
2. **Moving into the screen (moving screen violation)** → correction: establish both feet and stop completely before the cutter uses the screen. [S4, p.77]
3. **Releasing the screen too early** → correction: hold position until the cutter is completely clear. [S4, p.96]
4. **Wrong angle on ball screens** → correction: coach must diagram the angle for each specific play and drill it repeatedly. [S4, p.94]
5. **Setting the screen too early (telegraphing the play)** → correction: time the screen to the cutter's movement — set it as they're about to use it. [S4, pp.82, 79]
