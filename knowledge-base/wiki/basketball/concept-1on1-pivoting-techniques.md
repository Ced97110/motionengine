# 1-on-1 Pivoting Techniques: The Four-Phase Ball-Handler System

## Summary
When a ball handler kills his dribble in the frontcourt under defensive pressure, he must use a systematic series of pivot moves to outflank the defender and create a passing lane. This four-phase system — step-out, rip-through, swing-around, and repeat/safety valve — gives offensive players a repeatable framework for defeating on-ball defenders without committing a traveling violation or losing the ball. [S6, pp.124–131]

The foundational rule: **the inside foot (the foot closer to the basket) is always the pivot foot.** The outside foot is the free foot that steps, attacks, and creates passing angles.

## When to Use
- After killing the dribble in the frontcourt while under ball pressure
- Whenever a dribbler stops and needs to pass to a teammate being denied
- Against trapping or aggressive on-ball defenders
- To teach all ball handlers systematic ball protection and passing mechanics

## Key Principles

### Identifying Pivot and Free Feet
- **Right side of court:** inside foot = left foot (pivot foot); outside foot = right foot (free foot)
- **Left side of court:** inside foot = right foot (pivot foot); outside foot = left foot (free foot)
- After a two-foot jump stop, the player chooses which foot is pivot/free based on court position [S6, p.124]

### Ball Protection Position
- Ball is always placed beside the knee of the free (outside) leg — the knee acts as a shield between the defender and the ball [S6, pp.124–125]
- Coaching cue: *"Protect the ball beside the knee."*

## The Four Phases

### Phase 1: Step-Out Move
1. From the frontcourt jump stop, identify pivot foot (inside) and free foot (outside).
2. Step laterally with the free foot — *due east or due west, never north or south* — attempting to place the free foot outside the defender's near foot. [S6, p.125]
3. Ball remains protected beside the free knee throughout the step.
4. If the free foot beats the defender's foot: look to pass over or under the defender's outstretched arm. Use "fake high and go low" or "fake low and go high." [S6, pp.124–125]
5. Key cue: *"Step due east or due west — not north or south."* Do not over-extend; stay balanced.
6. If successful, the pass is made and the phase series ends.

### Phase 2: Rip-Through Move
1. Triggered when Phase 1 (step-out) is defended — the defender's foot matches or beats the passer's free foot.
2. With the ball protected beside the free knee, **rip the ball low and hard across the shoe tops** using a front pivot on the inside foot, swinging the free leg across in the same direction as the ball. [S6, pp.125–126]
3. The ball should be kept just inches above the floor — most defenders cannot get their hands that low. [S6, p.126]
4. The free foot lands laterally outside the defender's opposite foot, and the ball is repositioned beside the free knee on the new side.
5. Passer now has back partially toward the defender; must **check that the receiver is open** before passing. Pass with a cupped hand. [S6, p.126]
6. Key cue: *"Rip low — inches off the floor."*

### Phase 3: Swing-Around Move
1. Triggered when Phase 2 (rip-through) is also defended — the defender again matches the ball handler's move.
2. The ball handler executes a **second front pivot**, this time swinging back toward the original attacking side (away from the current pressure). [S6, pp.127–128]
3. Same coaching points apply: step due east, protect the ball beside the free knee, don't over-extend.
4. The passer now has his back fully turned to the original defender.
5. If the free foot now beats the defender's foot on this side, fake high-go low or fake low-go high and make the pass. [S6, p.128]
6. If still unsuccessful, repeat phases or find a safety valve receiver farther from the basket. [S6, p.128]

### Phase 4: Safety Valve / Repeat
- If all three phases are defended, find a teammate with less defensive pressure — a safety valve receiver farther from the initial target. [S6, p.128]
- Alternatively, repeat the step-out/rip-through sequence from the new position.

## Left-Side Equivalents
- On the left side of the court, the outside foot is the left foot (free foot); the same three phases apply but in mirror-image directions. [S6, pp.128–129]
- Phase 1: step due west with the left foot; protect ball on the left knee.
- Phase 2: rip left, front pivot off right pivot foot.
- Phase 3: swing back east. [S6, pp.128–129]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Primary practitioner — ball handlers must master all three phases bilaterally to break pressure.
- **SG/SF**: Apply when receiving and re-attacking denial defense after catching perimeter passes.
- **PF/C**: Apply in post-entry and high-post catch situations when fronted or trapped.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stepping north or south (toward/away from defender)** → gives defender recovery room; always step laterally. Coaching cue: *"Step due east or due west."*
2. **Over-extending the step-out** → player loses balance and cannot execute the rip-through counter; keep a controlled, not maximal, lateral step.
3. **Ripping the ball too high** → defenders can deflect it; ball must be dragged mere inches off the floor.
4. **Not checking the receiver before passing** → with back turned, passer may throw into a defender; always verify the receiver is open. [S6, p.126]
5. **Losing ball protection** → ball drifts away from the free knee; always return the ball to the knee shield position between phases.

## Teaching Cues (Summary)
- *"Step due east or due west — not north or south."*
- *"Protect the ball beside the knee."*
- *"If your foot beats his foot, look to pass."*
- *"Fake high and go low" / "Fake low and go high."*
- *"Rip low — inches off the floor."*
