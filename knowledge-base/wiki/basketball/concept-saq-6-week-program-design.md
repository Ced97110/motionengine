# SAQ 6-Week Program Design Framework

## Summary
*Training for Speed, Agility, and Quickness* (S8) provides sport-specific 6-week sample programs that demonstrate how to sequence drills across a training block. The framework organizes weekly drill selections by **need category** (Speed, Agility, Quickness, or combinations), with progressive complexity across the 6 weeks.

The Soccer/Lacrosse sample program (p.234) illustrates the key principle: weeks 1-3 target individual qualities in isolation (e.g., speed drills separate from quickness drills), while weeks 4-6 combine multiple qualities in the same drill (e.g., Four-Point Pop-Up to 20-Yard Shuttle trains speed+agility+quickness together). This progression mirrors the General Adaptation Syndrome — establish quality movement first, then add complexity and load.

## When to Use
- **Off-season training blocks**: 6-week SAQ programs build the athletic foundation before sport-specific skill work intensifies
- **Preseason**: Final 6 weeks before competition should combine all qualities and add reaction time elements
- **In-season maintenance**: Reduce volume (1 drill per quality per week) but maintain all five quality categories
- **Position-specific camps**: Customize by selecting drills from quality categories most relevant to each position

## Key Principles
1. **Identify needs first** — Categorize training goals as Speed, Agility, Quickness, or combinations (Speed+Agility, Agility+Quickness) before selecting any drills [S8, p.234]
2. **Start isolated, end combined** — Weeks 1-3 target single qualities; weeks 4-6 use drills that combine 2-3 qualities simultaneously
3. **Progress drill level across weeks** — Week 1 uses the Basic or lower-Intermediate version; subsequent weeks advance to higher-numbered drill variations
4. **2 drills per quality per week** — The sample programs show approximately 2 drill selections per need category per week — enough stimulus without volume overload
5. **Use drill numbers for precision** — The Drill Finder (pp.241-251) allows coaches to find the exact drill by name, number, level, and equipment in seconds
6. **Customizable template** — The blank template (p.235) allows coaches to insert their own goal statement and drill selections using the same 6-week/2-phase structure

## Program Structure

### Phase 1: Weeks 1-3 (Isolated Quality Development)
- **Row 1 (Speed need)**: 2 speed drills per week, progressing in intensity/complexity
- **Row 2 (Agility need)**: 2 agility drills per week, different patterns each week
- **Row 3 (Quickness need)**: 2 quickness drills per week, increasing explosiveness

### Phase 2: Weeks 4-6 (Combined Quality Development)
- **Row 1 (Speed+Agility+Quickness)**: Combined drills or back-to-back sequences
- **Row 2 (Speed+Quickness)**: Transition acceleration combined with explosive jumps
- **Row 3 (Agility+Quickness)**: Cutting + first-step drills; ladder + reaction patterns

## Basketball-Specific Adaptation
For a basketball 6-week program, coaches should:
1. **Weeks 1-2**: A-skips, B-skips, single-leg balance, vertical jump, ladder speed run
2. **Weeks 3-4**: Z-pattern run, lateral skaters, lateral shuffle pass (medicine ball), hop-scotch drill
3. **Weeks 5-6**: Four-point pop-up to sprint, half-ladder skill to sport skill (dribbling/shooting), sprint and cut on command, partner blind tosses

## Common Mistakes
1. **No goal statement** → The template starts with "Goals:" for a reason; without defined athletic needs, drill selection is random
2. **Same drills every week** → The program must progress week-over-week; repeating identical drills produces adaptation then stagnation
3. **Too many drills per session** → 4-6 drills total per session is appropriate; the sample programs show 2 per need category, not 10
4. **Skipping phase 2 combinations** → Weeks 4-6 combined drills are where sport transfer happens; do not stay in isolated quality training all 6 weeks
