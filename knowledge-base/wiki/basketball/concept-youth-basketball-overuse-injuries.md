# Youth Basketball Overuse Injuries

## Summary
Overload issues in young basketball players may be even more problematic than in adult players. Adolescents are at risk for unique injuries such as apophysitis (growth plate inflammation at tendon attachment sites) that adults do not face. Patellar tendinopathy — arguably the most common overuse injury in basketball — appears to begin as a degenerative process as early as adolescence. If these injuries go unrecognized or untreated, they can lead to chronic problems. Burnout is another significant concern in this age group.

Load monitoring, setting training limits, ensuring adequate recovery, and seeking appropriate medical attention are the four pillars of injury prevention for young basketball players. [S10, p.923]

## When to Use
- Designing training programs for players aged 13–18
- Evaluating adolescent players with chronic knee pain
- Implementing load management protocols for youth teams
- Justifying sport specialization delays and multi-sport participation

## Key Principles
1. **Apophysitis is unique to youth athletes.** Growth plate injuries at the patellar tendon insertion (Osgood-Schlatter) or heel (Sever's disease) do not occur in skeletally mature athletes — early recognition is critical.
2. **Patellar tendinopathy starts in adolescence.** Mersmann et al. found locally disorganized collagen fascicles (high tendon strain, low peak spatial frequency) in the patellar tendons of elite 14–15-year-old basketball players — structural abnormalities begin before adulthood [S10, p.923].
3. **Resistance training improves explosive strength safely in youth.** Santos showed a 10-week resistance training program improved vertical jump and medicine ball throw performance in 14–15-year-old players vs. controls [S10, p.923].
4. **Periodized plyometrics produce large effect-size gains.** Asadi demonstrated 8 weeks of periodized plyometric training (117–183 jumps/session, progressive volume) in 16 young players improved: vertical jump (ES=2.8), broad jump (ES=2.4), agility T-test (ES=2.2), Illinois agility test (ES=1.4), maximal strength (ES=1.8), and 60-m sprint (ES=1.6) [S10, p.923].
5. **Burnout is a real risk.** Early specialization and excessive year-round load contribute to psychological burnout — a concern that is separate from physical overuse injury.
6. **Monitor load proactively.** The four key protective measures: load monitoring, setting training limits, ensuring recovery, and timely medical attention.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Communicate pain honestly to coaches and medical staff; do not play through growth plate or tendon pain
- **Coaches/trainers**: Track session volume (jumps/session, sprint volume), respect periodization, use progressive overload principles

## Variations
### Apophysitis Management
Reduce loading of the affected apophysis, use relative rest, modify activity rather than complete cessation. Return to sport is guided by pain resolution and clinical evaluation.

### Patellar Tendinopathy Early Intervention
Eccentric loading protocols (e.g., decline squats) introduced progressively once acute pain subsides; imaging (ultrasound) can quantify tendon structure changes.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Ignoring tendon pain as 'growing pains'** → Patellar tendinopathy requires structured load management; chronic pain in adolescents warrants imaging.
2. **Excessive jump volume without periodization** → Plyometric training must be periodized (not constant high volume); use progressive session load increases (e.g., 117→183 jumps over 8 weeks).
3. **Year-round single-sport specialization** → Increases overuse injury risk; multi-sport participation reduces it per AMSSM position statement.
4. **No resistance training out of fear of injury** → Evidence supports resistance training for 14–15-year-olds as safe and beneficial for explosive performance.
