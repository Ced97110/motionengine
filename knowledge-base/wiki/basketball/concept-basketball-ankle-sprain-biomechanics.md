# Basketball Ankle Sprain Biomechanics, Risk Factors, and Prevention

## Summary
Ankle sprains are the most common injury in basketball, occurring at 1.49 (men) and 1.21 (women) injuries per 1000 athletic exposures — the highest ankle sprain rate of any sport. A history of ankle sprain makes a player five times more likely to suffer a subsequent sprain. Despite typically not requiring surgery, ankle sprains demand significant rehabilitation time, with 30–33% resulting in more than 7 days lost from sport [S10, p.41].

Understanding the precise biomechanical mechanism — sudden inversion >70° in low plantarflexion (10°–35°) with or without internal rotation — enables coaches and medical staff to design targeted prevention programs. Neuromuscular training has been shown to reduce ankle sprain risk by up to 35% [S10, p.41].

## When to Use
- Designing ankle prehabilitation programs for all basketball players
- Prioritizing prevention resources for centers (highest positional risk)
- Selecting appropriate external support (bracing/taping) for high-risk players
- Guiding post-sprain rehabilitation toward full neuromuscular recovery before return to play

## Key Principles
1. **Lateral ligament complex is most at risk** — Lateral sprains account for 80–83% of all ankle sprains; deltoid (6–7%) and syndesmotic (7%) make up the remainder [S10, p.41].
2. **Mechanism: rapid inversion >70° in low plantarflexion** — Anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL) and calcaneofibular ligament (CFL) are most strained; presence of internal rotation determines which ligament is primarily affected [S10, p.41].
3. **Syndesmotic injury mechanism differs** — Foot/ankle external rotation with a dorsiflexed ankle and pronated foot; syndesmotic and deltoid injuries commonly co-occur [S10, p.41].
4. **Most occur in the key during rebounding** — Contact with another player's foot is the most common cause, with centers at highest positional risk [S10, p.41].
5. **High recurrence rate demands comprehensive rehabilitation** — Players with prior sprain are 5× more likely to re-injure; return to play without full neuromuscular recovery is the primary risk factor for recurrence [S10, p.41].
6. **Neuromuscular risk factors are modifiable** — Decreased slow eccentric ankle inversion strength, increased fast concentric plantarflexion strength, and slower peroneus brevis reaction time all increase risk — all are trainable [S10, p.41].
7. **Gluteus maximus and hip extensor strength protects the ankle** — Hip extensor strength influences lower extremity alignment and helps mitigate ankle sprain risk — train the full kinetic chain [S10, p.41].
8. **Neuromuscular training prevents sprains** — Seven players must participate in one neuromuscular training session to prevent one ankle sprain; programs reduce risk by up to 35% by improving single-limb balance and postural sway [S10, p.41].
9. **Bracing and taping reduce recurrence risk** — External support is an evidence-based prevention tool, particularly for players with prior sprain history [S10, p.41].

## Player Responsibilities
- **C (Center)**: Highest risk position due to rebounding proximity to the basket — prioritize neuromuscular training and external support.
- **All positions**: Proprioception and single-leg balance training is a universal priority given the 5× recurrence rate.

## Variations
### High BMI Risk
A higher body mass index is associated with greater lateral ankle sprain risk — weight management and lower extremity strength training are particularly important for heavier players [S10, p.41].

### Postural Sway as a Predictive Marker
High levels of postural sway (in all directions) predict future ankle sprain — single-leg balance assessment should be part of pre-participation screening [S10, p.41].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Returning to play before neuromuscular control is restored** → Single-limb balance and postural stability must be fully recovered, not just pain-free status — the 5× recurrence rate results from premature return.
2. **Neglecting hip and glute strength in ankle rehabilitation** → Hip extensor strength contributes to ankle protection through kinetic chain alignment — rehabilitation must extend beyond the ankle joint.
3. **Omitting neuromuscular training from injury prevention programs** → Up to 35% risk reduction is achievable through neuromuscular training — this is the highest-value ankle prevention intervention.
