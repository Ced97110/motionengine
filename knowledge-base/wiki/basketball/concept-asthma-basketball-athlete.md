# Exercise-Induced Bronchoconstriction (EIB) and Asthma in Basketball

## Summary
Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB) — also called exercise-induced bronchospasm — is a common condition in basketball athletes both with and without a diagnosis of clinical asthma. It is characterized by transient airway narrowing triggered by exercise. Basketball and track are the two most frequent activities recorded at the time of fatal asthmatic events in a 7-year study through the Temple Sports Asthma Research Program. This makes EIB management critical knowledge for basketball coaches and medical staff.

EIB is classified as:
- **EIB with asthma (EIBA)**: exercise-induced bronchoconstriction with clinical asthma symptoms
- **EIB without asthma (EIBwA)**: acute bronchoconstriction without underlying asthmatic airway changes

## When to Use
- Pre-participation evaluation for any athlete reporting wheeze, cough, dyspnea, or chest tightness with exercise
- When designing warm-up protocols for athletes with known EIB
- When prescribing medications for EIB — WADA compliance required
- When a player shares an arena with ice hockey (cold, dry air + ice resurfacing pollutants increase EIB risk)

## Key Principles

### Prevalence
- General population EIB: 5–20%
- Athletes with current asthma diagnosis (EIBA): up to 90%
- General athlete population: 23–55% [S10, p.913]
- **Basketball and track** are the two most frequent sports at time of fatal asthmatic event

### Etiology
- Airway cooling and dehydration → augmented airway-lining fluid osmolarity
- Mediators released from airway inflammatory cells → smooth-muscle contraction and airway edema
- Mechanical/osmotic stress over time → airway remodeling
- **Environmental risk**: cold, dry air; pollutants (ice resurfacing machines); shared ice hockey/basketball facilities especially risky

### Clinical Presentation
- Wheeze, cough, dyspnea, chest tightness, fatigue with exercise
- Symptoms worse **5–30 min after initiating exercise**
- Usually resolve **30–60 min after stopping exercise**
- Differential includes: exercise-induced laryngeal obstruction (EILO), cardiovascular causes, anxiety

### Diagnosis Algorithm (Fig. 71.2)
1. Symptoms of dyspnea, cough, wheezing, chest tightness with exercise
2. Bronchial Provocation Test (BPT) — preferred methods: field exercise test, inhaled mannitol, or eucapnic voluntary hyperpnoea (EVH)
3. **Positive BPT**: drop in FEV1 >10–15% with associated dyspnea = diagnostic for EIB → confirm EIBA or EIBwA
4. **Negative BPT with high suspicion**: repeat test with more intense exposure; consider EILO, cardiovascular, or other diagnoses

**Important**: Patient self-report alone is NOT accurate for EIB diagnosis; a trial of B-adrenergic agonist is NOT acceptable for TUE eligibility in national/international competition — formal BPT is required.

### Management Framework

**Step 1: Assess severity** — Emergency action plan for moderate-to-severe exacerbation; transport to hospital

**Step 2: Non-pharmacologic measures**
- **Warm-up**: 15-minute warm-up creates a 1–3 hour refractory period of relatively normal airway reactivity [S10, p.914]
- **Environmental controls**: Keep air warm, moist, and free of pollutants
- **Research areas** (not yet evidence-based): fish oil and vitamins to limit hyperreactivity

**Step 3: Pharmacological stepwise approach**
1. **Inhaled short-acting B2-agonist (SABA)** taken 20–30 min before activity — most common first-line treatment; effectiveness decreases with frequent use
2. **Inhaled glucocorticoid** — when daily medication is required for management
3. **Leukotriene-receptor antagonists** — maintenance treatment option
4. **Inhaled long-acting beta-agonist** — for uncontrolled symptoms (indicates likely uncontrolled underlying asthma)

### WADA Compliance
- **All B2-agonists are prohibited** in competition except:
  - Salbutamol (in limited doses)
  - Formoterol (in limited doses)
  - Salmeterol (in limited doses)
- Must comply with relevant section of international standard for therapeutic use exemption (TUE)
- Each player with known EIBwA or EIBA should have an **individualized action plan** and appropriate **rescue medications readily available**

## Common Mistakes
1. **Diagnosing EIB on symptoms alone** → formal bronchial provocation testing is required for accurate diagnosis and TUE eligibility.
2. **Using a B-adrenergic agonist trial as diagnostic proof for TUE** → this is NOT accepted for national/international competition TUE applications.
3. **Skipping warm-up for EIB athletes** → a proper warm-up creates a critical 1–3 hour refractory period; "jumping cold" dramatically increases EIB risk.
4. **Prescribing prohibited B2-agonists without TUE** → only salbutamol, formoterol, and salmeterol in limited doses are permitted; all others are banned.
5. **Not differentiating EIB from EILO or cardiovascular causes** → all can present with dyspnea during exercise; correct diagnosis determines correct treatment.
