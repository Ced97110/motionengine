# Wrist and Hand Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Wrist and hand injuries are frequent in basketball due to ball contact, falls, and physical play. Many injuries are initially misdiagnosed as simple sprains, underscoring the need for high clinical suspicion and appropriate imaging. Treatment is personalized based on timing within the season, the affected extremity, the athlete's ability to perform with a splint, and competitive level. Professional basketball athletes are often offered surgical treatment because it ensures earlier return to competition. [S10, Ch.26]

## When to Use
- Any player presenting with wrist or hand pain, swelling, or deformity after a collision, fall, or ball impact
- Evaluating return-to-play timelines for hand/wrist injuries
- Differentiating sprains from fractures or ligament disruptions

## Key Principles
1. **High suspicion is required** — many wrist/hand injuries are misdiagnosed as sprains; specific imaging (X-ray, CT, MRI arthrogram, arthroscopy) is frequently necessary
2. **Professional athletes generally benefit from surgical treatment** — ensures faster healing, earlier restoration of motion, and shorter absence from sport
3. **Return-to-play depends on** whether the player can control the ball with a protective splint, position, and surgical vs. conservative management
4. **CT follow-up every 6 weeks** is recommended after scaphoid fixation until complete recovery
5. **Treatment should be individualized** based on season timing, affected extremity, and athlete's future expectations

## Injury Types and Management

### Scaphoid Fractures
- **Gold standard for professionals**: immediate headless screw fixation via percutaneous dorsal approach [S10, p.296]
- Surgery indicated for: all proximal pole fractures, displaced fractures (>1 mm), combined injuries, neglected fractures >1 month
- Conservative treatment (~4 months RTP) vs. surgery (~2 months RTP) for acute non-displaced waist fractures
- **Rehab**: gentle ROM when pain-free (4 weeks), forearm strengthening with wrist curls when stable, weight-bearing after minimum 6 weeks
- **Return criteria**: ≥50% bone bridging on CT, ≥80% normal grip strength, pain-free, full joint mobility

### Hook of Hamate Fractures
- Cause ulnar-sided palmar pain; frequently misdiagnosed as sprain — often present as non-unions [S10, p.296]
- Mechanism: direct hit or fall
- Treatment: short-arm cast 3–4 weeks; if symptoms persist, excision of the ununited hook
- Risk of tendon dysfunction/rupture and ulnar nerve dysfunction with symptomatic non-union → consider early excision

### Metacarpal Fractures
- Account for 18% of fractures below the elbow in the general population [S10, p.296]
- Classified by location (neck, shaft, base) and morphology (transverse, oblique, spiral)
- **Surgical indications** (neck): rotational deformity or angulation >15–20° (index/long), >30–40° (ring), >70° (small); shaft: >10° (index/long), >30° (ring/small)
- Each 2 mm of shortening decreases extensor lag by 7°
- Surgical options: CRPP (neck), intramedullary pinning or plate (shaft), CRPP or ORIF (base/Bennett/Rolando)
- **RTP**: non-displaced fractures 4–8 weeks; protected play for shaft fractures may start at week 3
- Bennett fracture: intra-articular thumb base fracture — treated with CRPP or ORIF, goal articular step-off <1–2 mm

### Triangular Fibrocartilage Complex (TFCC) Injuries
- TFCC absorbs 18–20% of total wrist force; injuries cause ulnar-sided pain and audible/palpable click on forearm rotation [S10, p.298]
- **Mechanism**: wrist extension and pronation under axial load
- **Diagnosis**: MRI arthrogram (highest sensitivity/specificity); wrist arthroscopy is gold standard
- **Palmer Classification**: Traumatic (IA–ID), Degenerative (IIA–IIE)
- **Treatment**:
  - Central type IA: arthroscopic debridement → splint 1 week → ROM; RTP 4–5 weeks
  - Peripheral IB/IC/ID: 2–6 weeks immobilization, then 6 weeks ROM/strengthening; competitive play 8–12 weeks post-surgery
  - Minimal instability: corticosteroid injection to complete season; repair after season is established practice in professional basketball

### Scapholunate Ligament Disruption
- Occurs with wrist extension, ulnar deviation, carpal supination; frequently misdiagnosed as sprains [S10, p.299]
- **Diagnosis**: PA and lateral radiographs (cortical ring sign, SL interval >3 mm, SL angle >70°); MRI arthrogram; diagnostic arthroscopy (gold standard for professionals)
- **Geissler Arthroscopic Classification**: Grade I (hemorrhage, no gap) → Grade IV (gross instability, 2.7-mm arthroscope passes through gap)
- **Treatment** depends on stability, injury duration, and degenerative changes:
  - Pre-dynamic (dorsal SL intact): arthroscopic debridement, thermal shrinkage, k-wire
  - Dynamic/recoverable <3 weeks: dorsal capsulodesis or ligamentoplasty
  - Chronic >3–6 weeks: SL ligament reconstruction (Brunelli, modified Brunelli/Garcia-Elias, Elsaftawy, Weiss, or Ross techniques)
  - Conservative (immobilization 4–6 weeks + PT): reserved for non-professional athletes with stable minor tears

### ECU Tendinitis and Subluxation
- Common cause of ulnar-sided wrist pain; caused by single forceful wrist flexion/ulnar deviation/supination [S10, p.301]
- Diagnosis: physical evaluation + US or MRI
- **Conservative treatment**: cold therapy, corticosteroid injection in tendon sheath, pulsed ultrasound, massage
- Traumatic subluxation/dislocation: conservative first; surgical reconstruction if subluxation persists

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating all wrist/hand injuries as sprains** → order X-ray for any point tenderness or deformity; MRI/CT for suspected ligament or scaphoid injuries
2. **Delaying treatment of displaced fractures** → increases healing time and risk of non-union; surgical fixation achieves faster recovery
3. **Removing metacarpal plate too late** → plate left after fracture healing causes extensor tendon damage/rupture; remove immediately after healing
4. **Returning to play before adequate grip strength** → criterion for scaphoid: ≥80% of normal grip strength before return
