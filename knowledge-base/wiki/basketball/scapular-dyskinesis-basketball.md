# Scapular Dyskinesis in Basketball

## Summary
Scapular dyskinesis refers to abnormal static or dynamic positioning and movement of the scapula (shoulder blade) on the thorax. The shoulder complex consists of three joints (sternoclavicular, acromioclavicular, and glenohumeral) and the scapulothoracic articulation. For pain-free and powerful shoulder motion, the scapula and glenohumeral joint must work together in a coordinated pattern called scapulohumeral rhythm [S2, pp.148-150].

Basketball players are at particular risk because repetitive shooting and overhead motions cause tightness in the pectoralis and latissimus dorsi muscles, loss of internal glenohumeral rotation, and gradual loss of thoracic spine mobility. Right-handed shooters specifically tend to lose thoracic rotation toward the left. These imbalances result in the scapula being pulled away from the thorax (winged or tilted position), disrupting the normal rhythm and increasing injury risk [S2, pp.148-149].

## When to Use
- When a player presents with shoulder pain, especially during shooting or overhead passing
- As part of pre-season screening for players with heavy shooting volume
- When a player demonstrates visible scapular winging during shoulder elevation
- As injury prevention for post players performing frequent overhead activity

## Key Principles
1. **Scapulohumeral rhythm is essential**: The scapula must move in a precise 2:1 ratio with the humerus during shoulder elevation (120° humeral + 60° scapular = 180° total) [S2, p.148]
2. **Three phases of normal rhythm**:
   - Phase 1 (0-30°): Scapula setting phase — minimal scapular movement
   - Phase 2 (30-90°): Scapula rotates; 2:1 humerus-to-scapula ratio
   - Phase 3 (90-180°): Scapula rotates and upwardly elevates; 2:1 ratio continues [S2, p.148]
3. **The lower trapezius is the most critical stabilizer**: Maintains scapular control during shoulder elevation; prone to weakness due to dominance of the upper trapezius [S2, p.149]
4. **Key force couples**: Upper and lower trapezius; rhomboids and serratus anterior — must fire in coordinated patterns for proper scapular control [S2, p.149]
5. **Restore flexibility first**: Tightness in pectoralis, latissimus dorsi, and posterior capsule must be addressed before strengthening
6. **Thoracic spine mobility**: Basketball players lose thoracic extension and rotation toward the nondominant side; restoring this normalizes scapular positioning [S2, pp.148-149]
7. **Strengthen in correct sequence**: Scapular stabilizers must fire BEFORE rotator cuff muscles in the correct firing pattern [S2, p.150]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions, especially shooters**: High-volume shooters must maintain thoracic mobility and pectoralis/lat flexibility. Right-handed shooters should specifically train left thoracic rotation.
- **PF/C**: Overhead posting and rebounding create chronic overhead stress; scapular stability work is essential.

## Normal vs. Abnormal Scapular Positioning
- **Normal**: Each scapula appears identical in height and distance (2-3 inches / 5-7.5 cm) off the thoracic spine; appears flat on the thorax
- **Abnormal (dominant arm)**: Measures more than 3 inches off the spine; appears lower; exhibits a winged (tilted) position [S2, p.149]

## Rehabilitation Sequence

### Shoulder Stretching
- Posterior capsule stretch
- Latissimus dorsi stretch
- Pectoralis stretch
- Sleeper stretch

### Shoulder Range of Motion
- Thoracic spine mobility

### Isotonic Strengthening
- YTW
- Side-lying shoulder external rotation
- Cheerleader
- Overhead press

### Plyometric Strengthening
- Overhead medicine ball toss [S2, p.150]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Strengthening before restoring flexibility** → Tight pectoralis and latissimus dorsi will pull the scapula back into abnormal positioning despite strengthening efforts
2. **Only strengthening the upper trapezius** → The lower trapezius is the critical stabilizer; it is chronically under-trained due to upper trap dominance
3. **Ignoring thoracic spine mobility** → Restricted thoracic rotation directly limits total shoulder arc of motion and scapular positioning
4. **Training rotator cuff before scapular stabilizers** → The firing order matters: scapular stabilizers first, then rotator cuff [S2, p.150]
5. **Treating only the dominant arm** → In most athletes, the dominant arm has the abnormal scapula position and requires specific attention
