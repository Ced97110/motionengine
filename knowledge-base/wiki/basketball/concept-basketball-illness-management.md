# Basketball Illness Management — Viral, Concussion, and Cardiac

## Summary
Basketball players face the same general illnesses as the broader population, plus sport-specific risks from intense aerobic demand, travel, contact, and high-volume schedules. The team physician must rapidly triage illness severity to determine safe participation. Key illness categories include viral upper respiratory infections, infectious mononucleosis, concussion, and cardiac conditions — the last of which represents the leading cause of sudden death in young athletes during sport [S10, pp.138-142].

## When to Use
- Any time a player reports illness symptoms before practice or game
- Sideline or post-game evaluation following a head impact
- Pre-participation screening to detect hidden cardiac conditions
- Advising players on travel, sleep, and lifestyle management

## Key Principles

### Illness Participation Framework: Neck Up / Neck Down
- **"Neck up" symptoms only** (sneezing, sore throat, rhinorrhea, sinus pressure, mild headache) → safe to continue playing with symptomatic management
- **"Neck down" symptoms** (fever, chills, sweats, myalgias, significant fatigue/malaise, lymphadenopathy, deep cough, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) → hold from basketball activities until resolved [S10, p.139]

### Viral Illness
- Most common: viral upper respiratory infection (URI) / "common cold" — mild, self-limited, 3–5 days
- Classic URI = "neck up" features → continued participation with symptomatic management
- Influenza: common, easily diagnosed via rapid testing; typically "neck down" features → medical hold
- **Infectious mononucleosis (EBV)**: 3–6 week course of fevers, significant fatigue, exudative pharyngitis, posterior cervical lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly → **hold from ALL activity for a minimum of 3 weeks** from onset of illness due to splenic injury risk; additional recovery weeks may be needed [S10, p.139]

### Concussion
**Common mechanisms in basketball**: Inadvertent elbow contact, hitting the floor on charge/block collision, being undercut during rebound/jump

**Symptoms**: Acute-onset headache, nausea ± vomiting, dizziness; also confusion, fatigue, light/sound sensitivity, poor concentration, disrupted sleep

**Critical rule**: No athlete returns to sport on the day of injury — no exceptions [S10, p.139]

**Management**:
- Rely on consensus guidelines (5th International Conference on Concussion in Sport, Berlin 2016)
- Low-intensity exercise before complete symptom resolution is now supported if it does not exacerbate symptoms
- Once symptom-free: standardized assessment of neurocognitive tests (SCAT5, ImPACT, or equivalent), modified BESS (balance), and VOMS (oculo-vestibular) testing
- Return to basketball only when all parameters are normal or at athlete-specific baselines

**NCAA Return-to-Play Stepwise Progression** [S10, pp.139-140]:
1. Active targeted treatment (progressive aerobic exertion, vestibular therapies, vision therapies)
2. Return-to-Play Steps:
   a. Light aerobic exercise (walking, swimming, stationary bike) — no resistance training
   b. Sport-specific activity with no head impact
   c. Non-contact sport drills + progressive resistance training
   d. Unrestricted training
   e. Return to competition (cleared by team physician/AT)
3. Return-to-Learn: stepwise program with multidisciplinary team including physicians, coaches, psychologists, academic advisors

### Cardiac Concerns
**Basketball players have elevated risk of sudden cardiac death** relative to many other athletes, likely due to extended high-level aerobic demands [S10, p.140]

**Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)**:
- Most common cause of sport-associated sudden cardiac death in the USA (~50% of cases)
- Abnormal LV/septal wall thickening → pathologic outflow obstruction or arrhythmia
- Incidence ~1:500 in general population; highly variable penetrance
- **Physical exam**: Harsh systolic murmur loudest at lower left sternal border, increases with Valsalva or squat-to-stand, does not radiate to carotids; NOTE: some HCM patients have no murmur
- **EKG**: May show left axis deviation or LV hypertrophy
- **Definitive**: Echocardiogram or cardiac MRI
- **Challenge**: HCM may develop slowly over years, often after initial screening; sudden death is a common presenting feature [S10, p.140]
- Male athletes of African descent have a **higher risk** of certain cardiac anomalies causing sudden cardiac death [S10, p.140]

**Marfan's Syndrome**:
- Genetic connective tissue disorder affecting heart, eyes, blood vessels, and bones; associated with tall stature → screen taller basketball players
- Key concern: aortic root widening → rupture risk
- Players with Marfan's may potentially play with close cardiology monitoring for progressive aortic root dilation [S10, p.140]
- Diagnostic criteria: Revised Ghent criteria (systemic score ≥7 points indicates systemic involvement) [S10, p.141]

**Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy (ARVC)**:
- Most common cause of sudden cardiac death in athletes in Italy
- Results from desmosome unlinking → focal myocardial weakness + fibrofatty deposition
- Generally **not compatible** with high-level basketball participation [S10, p.141]

**Other cardiac conditions to consider**:
- Anomalous coronary arteries, myocarditis, various cardiomyopathies
- Congenital long QT syndrome, Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome
- May present as exercise intolerance, chest pain, inappropriate shortness of breath, syncope, or sudden death

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Report symptoms early, do not hide illness; understand the neck-up/neck-down framework and advocate for proper assessment

## Common Mistakes
1. **Returning from mono too early** → Minimum 3 weeks from onset regardless of how the player feels — spleen rupture risk is real
2. **Same-day concussion return** → Never; this is an absolute rule regardless of symptom resolution on the sideline
3. **Skipping cardiac screening** → Pre-participation EKG and echo are strongly recommended for basketball players despite ongoing debate in sports medicine
4. **Missing HCM** → It develops slowly; a normal baseline screen does not guarantee the player is permanently clear; rescan when new symptoms appear
