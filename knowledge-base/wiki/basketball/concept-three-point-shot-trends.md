# Three-Point Shot: Trends and Strategic Use

## Summary
The three-point shot has been one of the most transformative forces in basketball since its college introduction in 1987. From just 9.2 attempts per game in 1987, NCAA Division I teams steadily increased to 18-19 attempts per game by the late 2000s — maintaining a remarkably stable field-goal percentage of approximately 34% throughout, except for the early years [S13, pp.122-123].

Coaches who initially refused to use the three-pointer (notably Terry Holland and Denny Crum) eventually changed their philosophy as the shot became structurally embedded in offensive systems. Understanding the three-pointer's role — and drilling it at the five key spots — is now a non-negotiable component of any offense [S13, p.122].

## When to Use
- **Fast-break push**: Teams can push sidelines seeking quick three-point shots before the defense sets
- **Corner/wing spot-ups**: After inside-out ball movement or dribble penetration (draw-and-kick)
- **Off staggered double screens**: Forwards and guards curling or fading off double screens can receive three-point shots
- **Transition**: Long shots = long rebounds; fast-break teams believe their offensive rebound rate is higher in transition than in half-court sets
- **Pressure release**: When the post is doubled, the spot-up three is the designed kick-out

## Key Principles
1. **Five key three-point practice spots**: Both corners, both wings, and the top of the key — these are the primary shooting locations to drill
2. **Stable percentage**: NCAA Division I three-point FG% has stayed near 34% for 25+ years — volume matters more than dramatic percentage improvement for most teams
3. **Long shots = long rebounds**: Teams running fast-break offenses prioritize transition three-point opportunities because long rebounds favor teams already in motion
4. **Big men must adapt**: The three-point emphasis forces centers and power forwards to extend their defensive coverage to the three-point line — no longer can bigs retreat to the paint
5. **Balance with inside-out**: Three-point shooting is most effective when paired with an inside-out attack that creates corner kick-outs and open spot-ups (Dean Smith's hierarchy still applies) [S13, p.119]
6. **Record pace**: In 2010, Cornell set Sweet 16 records of 42.9% from three, 9.5 makes, and 22 attempts per game [S13, Table 5.5]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Ignoring the three-point shot entirely** → puts teams at a mathematical disadvantage; even Terry Holland and Denny Crum changed their minds
2. **Shooting undisciplined threes** → volume without good looks lowers FG% and produces long missed-shot rebounds that fuel opponent fast breaks
3. **Not practicing from all five spots** → players who only practice wing threes are ineffective from corners or off the top
4. **Neglecting big men's perimeter coverage** → as offenses push the three-pointer, defenses must follow — centers who can't cover the three-point line become liabilities
5. **Treating three-pointers as a substitute for interior attacks** → the inside-out philosophy remains the highest-efficiency offensive approach [S13, p.119]
