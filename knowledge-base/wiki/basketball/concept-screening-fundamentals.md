# Screening Fundamentals

## Summary
A screen is a **leverage transfer**: the screener briefly absorbs the screened player's defender, buying a half-second of space and time for the ball handler or receiver. Every rule on this page — the 90° angle, the screener's target pivot, the read progressions — is optimizing one variable: how long the defense stays a step behind. [S7, pp.51-54]

## When to Use
- When a defender has better positioning than your player can beat 1v1 inside the shot-clock time budget.
- On-ball and off-ball, throughout half-court offense.
- Against any defense — man-to-man most directly; zones require angle adjustments.
- Not useful when your player already has a clean lane or shot — a screen there adds bodies, not leverage.

## Key Principles
Screens are geometry optimizations against **defender time-to-recover**. The four rules below each extend this one idea.

1. **Read the defense before, during, and after the screen.** A screen is never a fixed action — it's a live read on which lane the defender concedes. [S7, p.51]
2. **Angle is the most important variable.** Present the screen at a **90° angle** to the defender's path — this maximizes the distance they must cover to stay attached. [S7, p.52]
3. **The screener must always offer a passing option** — roll to the rim, pop out, or cut opposite the screened player. A screener without a release is a wasted body. [S7, p.54]
4. **The screener generally moves opposite the screened player.** Baseline cut → screener high. High cut → screener low. Opposite directions force the defense into two simultaneous decisions. [S7, p.54]

Coaching cue: *"Your body is a wall for one second. Make the defender pay to get around it."*

## Player Responsibilities

### Screener Rules
- **See the ball.** Time the screen to the handler's move, not to a fixed count.
- **Solid stance.** Wide base, low center of gravity, at the angle the ball handler needs.
- **After contact: pivot and face the ball.** A screener with their back to the ball can't catch. [S7, p.52]
- **Read both defenders** — the screened player's and your own. Your next move depends on whichever one commits first.

### Screen Receiver Rules
- **See the screener.** Don't move until the screen is set; cutting early is a foul or a wasted action.
- **Use the screen's angle to your advantage** — the angle was set to free a specific lane. Take that lane unless the defense closes it.
- **Read the defender's commitment.** Has your defender been slowed? Has the screener's defender switched? Different answers → different cuts.
- **Don't cut at full speed.** Change of pace beats full speed because it lets the defender commit first. *"Walk him into the screen, then explode."* [S7, p.52]

### Passer Rules (When the Ball Handler Is Picked Up)
- **See the full court** — the screener is rarely the only option, but often the best one.
- **Read the defensive reaction.** Who helped? Who stayed? Pass to the defender's abandoned zone.
- **Crisp pass away from the defender** — lob if the passing lane is closed. [S7, p.53]

## Reading the Defense — Screened Player's Reads

Each read below decodes one pattern: **where the defender has committed their weight**. Exploit the zone they just conceded.

### Curl
- **Trigger**: Defender trails — they've conceded the path *around* the screen.
- **Action**: Circle tightly around the screener (brush the shoulder) and cut to the rim. The tight cut closes the defender's recovery window. [S7, p.53]

### Curl and Slip
- **Trigger**: Curl is on, and the screener's defender leaves to help — they've conceded the rim.
- **Action**: Screener slips to the rim immediately. The help was a mistake; punish it. [S7, p.53]

### Bump and Pop
- **Trigger**: Defender goes *over* the screen to beat the curl — they've conceded the *space behind* the screen.
- **Action**: "Bump" off the screener's back or hip, pop away a step, receive the pass in the vacated space. [S7, p.54]

### Bump and Slip
- **Trigger**: After the pop, the screener's defender hedges out to slow the ball — they've conceded the rim.
- **Action**: Screener slips behind the hedge and cuts to the basket. [S7, p.54]

## Screen Plays Categories
Four categories of screens: [S7, p.54]
1. **Off-the-ball screens** — back screen, down screen, flare screen.
2. **On-the-ball screens** — pick-and-roll (PnR).
3. **High-post rub cuts.**
4. **Special screens.**

## Common Mistakes
Each mechanical failure below is really a read-or-intent failure underneath.

1. Screen set too early or late → *screener isn't watching the ball*; treating the action as fixed rather than timed to the handler's move.
2. Screen angle isn't 90° to the defender → *screener didn't picture the defender's recovery path*; geometry failure.
3. Receiver cuts full-speed off the screen without reading → *ignoring defender commitment*; burns the read window.
4. Screener doesn't pivot to face the ball → *treats screening as a terminal action, not a setup*; misses the slip or pop pass.
5. Receiver moves before the screen is fully set → *treats the screen as a handoff, not a barrier*; fouls or loses the advantage.
6. Ball handler doesn't look for the screener as a secondary option → *reading only the primary cutter*; misses the curl-slip and bump-slip passes. [S7, pp.52-54]
