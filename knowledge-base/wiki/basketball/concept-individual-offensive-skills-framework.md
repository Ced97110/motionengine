# Individual Offensive Skills Framework

## Summary
Chapter 4 of *Winning Basketball Fundamentals* presents Lee Rose's framework for developing complete individual offensive players. Rose argues that scoring is a by-product of executing sound fundamentals — ballhandling, passing, screening, shooting — combined with court balance, proper spacing, and sound decision-making. The framework begins with improving trainable athletic tools (speed, quickness, jumping) and builds upward through fundamental skills to the all-important component of basketball decision-making [S13, pp.65-66].

A key coaching insight: coaches must be flexible about positions and roles. Players without a complete skill set can still contribute if they have one elite quality (e.g., shooting, screening, playmaking, rebounding). The stereotypical position fit often does not apply below the professional level.

## When to Use
- When assessing player roles and position assignments
- When designing individual player development plans
- When deciding how to use limited practice time across skill categories
- When identifying which athletic qualities a player needs to develop

## Key Principles
1. **Scoring is a by-product of fundamentals** — Teams that insist on player and ball movement create defensive breakdowns that produce excellent scoring opportunities without isolation plays.
2. **Complete game development is required** — Ballhandling, passing, screening, and shooting are the four core skill categories every player must develop.
3. **Court balance and spacing** — Players must learn to balance the court and use proper spacing to make themselves and their teammates harder to guard.
4. **Decision-making is the capstone quality** — Good decision-making is described as "that all-important intuitive quality" built on top of athletic tools and fundamental skills.
5. **Athletic qualities ARE trainable** — Speed, quickness, and jumping ability can all be improved through proper training techniques despite common coaching myths to the contrary [S13, p.66].
6. **Role players have value** — A big who screens and passes, an athletic rebounder with limited shooting, and a small playmaking guard all have specific team niches; coaches must be creative in finding uses for incomplete skill sets.
7. **Hard work drives improvement** — "The status quo is rare in basketball, because players usually improve or fall back in each practice. No magic is involved." [S13, p.65]

## Athletic Tools Overview

### Speed
- Muscular power largely determines an athlete's speed
- Two pathways to speed improvement: **increase stride frequency** or **increase stride length**
- Both require overload training beyond the body's normal capacity
- Overload speed program components: warm-ups, strength, power, endurance, flexibility, and speed techniques
- Teams with speed can push the ball on every possession offensively and press/overplay defensively [S13, p.66]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Prioritize decision-making and playmaking; a small guard with limited rebounding/post defense but excellent playmaking decision-making is ideal on a pressing team.
- **SG/SF**: Shooting ability is always in demand regardless of size; perimeter players should invest in screening, spacing, and passing skills alongside shooting.
- **PF/C**: Even without speed, a big player with good instincts, screening ability, and passing skills has team value; athletic rebounders with speed complement team defense even with limited scoring.

## Four Core Skill Categories
1. **Ballhandling** — Protecting the ball, dribble moves, and maintaining poise under pressure
2. **Passing** — Court vision, pass selection, and delivery accuracy
3. **Screening** — Setting legal, effective screens that free teammates
4. **Shooting** — A complete shooting repertoire from multiple court areas

## Common Mistakes
1. **Overemphasizing scoring** → The PRS (see [[concept-performance-rating-system]]) penalizes low-percentage shooting; coaches should reinforce that scoring is a by-product, not the primary goal.
2. **Assuming athletic tools are fixed** → Speed, quickness, and jumping are all improvable; coaches who do not train these qualities leave measurable gains on the table.
3. **Forcing players into stereotypical position molds** → Below the professional level, creative role definition often serves the team better than rigid position assignments.
4. **Neglecting spacing and court balance** → Even skilled individual players become easy to guard without proper spacing discipline.
