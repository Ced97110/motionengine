# Athlete Assessment for SAQ Training

## Summary
Before designing an SAQ program, coaches must conduct a **needs analysis** — a systematic evaluation of the athlete's functional strength, movement mechanics, and sport metabolic demands. This assessment determines which exercises are appropriate and at what intensity, and prevents injury by ensuring prerequisites are met before advancing to higher-intensity SAQ work [S8, pp.7-8].

The three principal assessment domains are: (1) **functional strength**, (2) **movement competencies and techniques**, and (3) **metabolic demands of the sport**.

## When to Use
- At the start of any new SAQ training program
- When an athlete changes level (novice → experienced) or returns from injury
- When designing a new mesocycle to set appropriate intensity and volume
- Before prescribing advanced shock-type plyometrics or reactive drills

## Key Principles

### 1. Functional Strength Assessment
Four methods, matched to athlete level [S8, pp.8-11]:

**Basic Strength (Novice/Intermediate)**
- Athletes should squat 1.5–2× body weight before advanced shock-type (depth/drop jump) plyometrics [S8, p.8]
- Single-leg squat and lunge (forward, lateral, backward) are practical prerequisites for unilateral agility work
- Evaluating these movements reveals movement competency and basic strength baseline

**Reactive Resources (Novice/Intermediate)**
- Compare squat jump height to drop jump height from 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, (56) cm
- Novice: drop jump 20-25% *below* squat jump → large reactive resources → SSC deficit → focus on drop jumps, vertical jumps, countermovement jumps
- Advanced: drop jump 20-25% *above* squat jump → small reactive resources → focus on hypertrophy and neural adaptations (maximal strength) [S8, pp.8-9]

**Strength Deficit (Advanced)**
- Difference between absolute (eccentric) strength and maximum (concentric) strength
- Large deficit (up to 45%) → emphasize explosive exercises to improve neuromuscular activation
- Small deficit (~5%) → emphasize hypertrophic methods followed by maximal heavy efforts [S8, p.9]
- Eccentric strength approximated by measuring max load lowerable under control for 3-5 sec during squat; evaluate each leg independently

**Speed-Strength (Advanced)**
- Use normative tables (Tables 2.1-2.4 in source) to benchmark athlete performance
- Explosive power tests: 30-m dash, 5/10 double- and single-leg bounds, backward overhead weight throw [S8, p.9]
- Do NOT generalize norms across positions or sports (e.g., a WR is not a sprinter)

### 2. Movement Competencies
**Sprinting**: Three variants — acceleration, transition, maximum velocity. Assess body position (look direction of travel), arm action (aggressive hand/knee hammering), leg action (explosive, minimize ground support time) [S8, p.12]

**Agility**: Directional changes initiated by turning head to new target FIRST, then footwork (open or crossover step). Arm action critical during acceleration phase of directional change. [S8, p.12]

**Deceleration** (progressive evaluation) [S8, p.11]:
1. Athlete decelerates from "second gear" (half speed), stopping in 3-4 steps
2. Progress to "third gear" (3/4 speed), braking in 5-6 steps
3. Progress to "fourth gear" (full speed), braking in 7-8 steps

### 3. Metabolic Demands
- Assess whether the sport requires short 10-second energy bursts or sustained minutes of motion
- This determines training conditioning specificity ("special endurance") [S8, p.13]
- Basketball: primarily alactic/lactic anaerobic — short explosive bursts with partial recovery

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping needs analysis** → prescribing drills at inappropriate intensity risks injury and wasted adaptation [S8, p.7]
2. **Generalizing norms across sports/positions** → an interior lineman's scores should not match a sprinter's; misdirected training results [S8, p.9]
3. **Initiating deceleration at full speed** → must progress through half → three-quarter → full speed across multiple sessions [S8, p.11]
4. **Confusing reactive resources with reaction time** — reactive strength (SSC ability) is a physical quality; reaction time is a perceptual-cognitive quality [S8, p.8]
5. **Beginning agility turns with hips/shoulders** → always initiate with eyes and head, then footwork [S8, p.12]
