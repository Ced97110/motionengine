# Rip Step

## Summary
The rip step is an offensive ball-protection and attack move used when a player catches or holds the ball on the perimeter while a defender closes out or applies pressure. The player "rips" the ball low — swinging it from one side of the body to the other in a low arc — to protect it from the defender's reach and simultaneously initiate a drive. The move creates a driving lane and keeps the ball in a protected position as the player attacks off the dribble.

The rip step is the foundation of several advanced dribble-drive finishing sequences including the stride stop jump shot, the step-through layup/floater, the big hop two-foot finish, and the hop-back three. Mastering all four finishing options off the rip step makes a player nearly impossible to guard one-on-one.

## When to Use
- When a defender is closing out hard and reaching for the ball
- After catching a pass on the wing, elbow, or top of the key with a defender in your space
- When a shot fake has drawn the defender off balance
- As an entry into a dribble-drive sequence from a triple-threat position

## Key Principles
1. Keep the ball low and tight to the body during the rip — do not swing it wide where it can be poked away
2. Initiate the rip with the lead (pivot) foot already set — the non-pivot foot then steps through as the rip begins
3. The direction of the rip (left or right) determines which shoulder leads into the defender
4. Take 1 or 2 dribbles to create separation before the finishing action — both must be practiced equally
5. Practice going both left (L) and right (R) from every position so the move is equally effective in both directions
6. Immediately transition from the rip into the chosen finish: stride stop, step-through, big hop, or hop back

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Primary user of rip step — initiates dribble-drive offense, attacks closeouts, creates for self and others
- **SG**: Uses rip step on wing catches off screens or hand-offs to attack two-guard mismatches
- **SF**: Wing attacks off rip step to create driving lanes or mid-post scoring
- **PF**: Uses rip step in mid-post and elbow situations to attack slower bigs who close out

## Variations
### Rip Step + Stride Stop (Jump Shot)
After the rip and 1–2 dribbles, the player executes a stride stop (two-count stop with the inside foot landing first, then shooting foot) and shoots a pull-up jump shot. Practiced both left (L) and right (R) sides. [S3]

### Rip Step + Step-Through (Layup/Floater)
After the rip and 1–2 dribbles and a stride stop, the player reads the defender and counters with a step-through underneath or past the defender for a layup or floater instead of shooting. [S3]

### Rip Step + Big Hop (Two-Foot Finish)
After the rip and 1–2 dribbles, the player gathers and executes a "big hop" — jumping off one foot but landing on two feet simultaneously — to finish at the rim with balance and contact resistance. [S3]

### Rip Step + Hop Back (Step-Back Three or Mid-Range)
After the rip and 1–2 dribbles, the player executes a "hop back" — hopping backward off both feet to create separation — and rises into a three-point shot or mid-range jumper. [S3]

### Rip Step + Hop Back to Roll Out
A counter to the hop-back: after the hop back, the player "rolls out" with a dribble to reset or attack from a new angle if the defender over-pursues. [S3]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Ripping the ball too high** → keep the ball below the waist level during the rip to prevent steals
2. **Going only one direction** → defenders will overplay the strong side; practice L and R equally each workout
3. **Taking more than 2 dribbles before finishing** → gives defense time to recover; be decisive and attack immediately
4. **Skipping the finish variation** → practicing only the stride stop makes the player one-dimensional; all four finishes must be trained
5. **Losing balance on the big hop** → land with two feet simultaneously, knees bent, weight centered
