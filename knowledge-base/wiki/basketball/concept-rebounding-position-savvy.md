# Rebounding Position Savvy (Primary Rebounding Rules)

## Summary
Rebounding is not random. The ball obeys predictable physical laws, and players who understand these laws can move to the correct spot *before* the ball arrives rather than reacting after it caroms. This "savvy" gives shorter players a consistent edge over taller, less-knowledgeable opponents.

Coaches should teach these rules explicitly and test them in competitive drills (see [[drill-count-slide-pivot-savvy]]).

## When to Use
- Teach these rules before any competitive rebounding drill
- Reinforce them every time a player misses a primary rebound by going to the wrong spot
- Film analysis can verify whether players are using savvy positioning or simply reacting

## Key Principles

### Rule 1: Arc Height → Rebound Distance
- **High arc shot** = short rebound, closer to the basket. The ball travels high and comes straight down.
- **Flat/low arc shot** = long rebound, further from the basket. The ball skips off the rim with more horizontal energy.
- *Coaching cue*: "High arc = short bounce. Flat arc = long bounce."

### Rule 2: Shot Distance → Rebound Distance
- **Longer shots** produce **longer rebounds**. A 3-point shot rebounds further from the basket than an 8-foot shot.
- Players must gauge both shot distance and arc simultaneously to find the primary spot.

### Rule 3: Shot Angle → Rebound Direction
- The ball caroms at approximately the **mirror angle** on the opposite side of the basket.
- Shots from the **left corner** → rebound toward the right corner.
- Shots from a **45-degree angle** → rebound at ~45 degrees on the other side.
- Shots from **straight on (area D)** → rebound back toward the same area.
- *Coaching cue*: "Opposite angle, opposite side."

### Rule 4: Ball Liveliness
- A livelier ball (e.g., Wilson Jet vs. Spalding 100) rebounds further and higher. Check the ball before the game.
- A ball with more air caroms further and higher than a softer ball. Check inflation before play.

### Developing a Count System
- Both offensive and defensive rebounders should develop a **mental count** after a shot.
- For long-distance shots: *"one thousand one, one thousand two"* gives the ball time to travel.
- Over time, players develop an innate timing mechanism and no longer need to count consciously.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Internalize all four rules and apply them in real time during games.
- **Perimeter defenders (PG/SG)**: Use slide-step + front pivot when boxing out from the perimeter. Front pivot allows longer observation of the assignment before turning to the ball.
- **Post players (PF/C)**: Use reverse pivot (immediate boxout) when near the basket — the carom arrives quickly and a reverse pivot lets you see the ball sooner. You will lose sight of your assignment, but the attacker cannot move far in the brief time it takes the ball to arrive.
- **Offensive rebounders (all)**: Use jab-step-and-go or jab-step-and-roll with the swim technique to get even with or in front of the defender.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Going under the basket on corner shots** → Correction: angle shots produce opposite-side caroms; move to the correct angle, not directly under.
2. **Jumping too early on flat shots** → Correction: flat shots travel further; count briefly before exploding.
3. **Ignoring the count system** → Correction: practice counting after each shot in drills until the timing becomes automatic.
4. **Not checking ball inflation** → Correction: pre-game routine should always include a ball inflation check.
