# Triangle Offense: Passing Principles

## Summary
"Passing is underrated these days, but it's the key to executing the triangle offense." Phil Jackson and Tex Winter identify seven specific passing principles that players must internalize to run the triangle effectively. These are not general coaching clichés — they are precise, actionable rules that govern when, where, and how to pass in a system that relies on ball and player movement rather than isolation or called plays. [S7, p.93]

## When to Use
- All passing situations within the triangle offense
- Applicable as universal passing principles for any offensive system

## Key Principles
1. **Pass when the defender is three feet from the ball or closer.** The closer the defender covers the offensive player, the less time the defender has to react. "This principle of good passing is violated more than any other."
2. **See the passing lanes and the receiver, but don't stare at the target.** Use peripheral vision to identify the receiver without telegraphing the pass. Never make a blind pass.
3. **Use fakes with a purpose, without overfaking.** Purposeful fakes move the defender based on their reaction. The passer must stay poised and attuned to all passing lanes while executing fakes.
4. **Eliminate all unnecessary movements.** Use quick wrist and finger action, shortening the passing motion as much as possible.
5. **Pass quickly to an open teammate.** The receiver typically has no more than three seconds to make a play after catching. "For every second under three seconds it takes to execute the play, the better basketball player he becomes. For every second over three seconds, the poorer he becomes." Quick ball movement forces defensive adjustment and creates passing lanes.
6. **Pass the ball to a teammate's open side.** The passer must see not only his own defender but also the receiver's defender. The receiver helps by providing a good target and sealing the defender away from the passing lane.
7. **Anticipate receiving the ball and think ahead.** The best players are already planning their next pass before they receive the ball. "If players don't have this ability, you need to build an offensive pattern that ensures the quick movement of the ball to keep the defense busy."

## Player Responsibilities
- **All players:** Master these seven rules; they apply to every player in every possession, regardless of position.
- **Ball handler:** Prioritize the 3-feet rule (principle 1); resist staring at the target (principle 2).
- **Receivers:** Provide a good target on the open side; use a seal-off to keep the defender from the passing lane (principle 6).

## Common Mistakes
1. **Passing to a covered side** → Always pass to the receiver's open side; read the receiver's defender, not just your own.
2. **Telegraphing passes by staring** → Use peripheral vision; the defense will cheat to the obvious target if you look directly at them.
3. **Holding the ball after catching** → Three seconds or less is the standard; every second over three makes the player and the offense less effective.
4. **Overfaking** → Fakes must have a specific purpose based on the defender's reaction; random fakes waste time and tip off the passer's intent.
