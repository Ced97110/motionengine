# Shared Mental Models in Basketball Team Decision-Making

## Summary
The apparent "mind-reading" quality of great offenses — where players move fluidly and in perfect synchronization without calling every action — is explained by shared mental models (SMM). A shared mental model is a psychological state in which each team member has a common understanding of the situation and how teammates will perform, and all members draw from the same knowledge base. [S10, pp.1001-1002]

Individual expert performance depends on advanced knowledge structures developed through years of practice, allowing the brain to transform small information bits into larger, meaningful patterns that can be rapidly recognized and retrieved. At the team level, this individual expertise must be multiplied across all five players simultaneously — making team coordination orders of magnitude more complex, and making shared mental models the key explanatory variable. [S10, p.1001]

## When to Use (Coaching Application)
- When building offensive and defensive systems that require all five players to react simultaneously
- When designing training sessions that develop interpersonal synergies, not just individual skills
- When explaining to players why team practice is non-substitutable, even for skilled individuals
- During timeouts and film sessions to update shared knowledge based on game developments
- When on-boarding new players who must rapidly acquire the team's knowledge base

## Key Principles
1. **Expert teams are not just teams of experts.** Individual expertise does not automatically produce team coordination — group-level processes require group training. [S10, p.1001]
2. **Shared knowledge must be established before competition.** Practice sessions must place players in prototypical game situations that develop the interpersonal synergies and situational probability knowledge required for game performance. [S10, p.1001]
3. **Shared knowledge must be updated during competition.** Basketball's dynamic nature means the pre-game shared knowledge quickly becomes outdated; teams must continuously update their shared understanding. [S10, p.1002]
4. **Two update mechanisms exist: incidental and deliberate.** [S10, p.1002]
5. **Communication is the primary vehicle for shared knowledge.** Verbal (shouting) and non-verbal (pointing, hand signs, gestures) communication during active play is how shared knowledge is updated in real-time. [S10, p.1002]
6. **Cohesion, chemistry, communication, and coordination are the four team-level outcomes** of well-developed shared mental models. [S10, p.1003]

## The Four Levels of Planning and Decision-Making (Hayes-Roth & Hayes-Roth)
For coaching staff to communicate effectively and build shared mental models, training must operate across four abstraction levels: [S10, p.1002]

1. **Intention (Most General)** — the desired outcome of the team (e.g., "win by 10 points"); establishes the motivational frame
2. **Scheme** — the team's general behavior pattern (e.g., "up-tempo fast break offense"); establishes the style of play
3. **Strategy** — the specific procedures the team maintains during the game (e.g., "double-team defense in the post"); the tactical principles
4. **Tactic (Most Specific)** — the specific operations for individual situations (e.g., "execute this specific end-game play"); real-time execution

When coaching staff communicate all four levels effectively, each player knows:
- (a) When to use each play and which signal indicates them
- (b) What action each involved player must execute
- (c) How their actions coordinate with teammate actions

## How Shared Knowledge Is Updated During Games

### Incidental Updates
One or two players begin adapting their strategy in response to game realities; teammates observe these changes, infer the strategy has shifted, and align accordingly without explicit instruction. Example: players start defending man-to-man after recognizing the zone defense is ineffective against the opponent. [S10, p.1002]

### Deliberate Updates
Explicit communication via:
- **Verbal** — shouting, calling plays
- **Non-verbal** — pointing, predetermined hand signs from the coach
- **Timeout whiteboards** — drawing new plays and explaining new playing styles
- **Active play signals** — predetermined signs from coaches during live action
[S10, p.1002]

## Individual vs. Team Knowledge Structures

### Individual Level
Athletes construct mental models through practice and experience, encoding sport-specific cues into short-term memory and storing them in retrievable long-term memory (Long-Term Working Memory / LTWM). Expert athletes recognize and recall structured information at a higher rate than novices — they see "chunks" of play where novices see individual events. [S10, p.1001]

### Team Level
Group training — beyond individual practice — creates shared situational probability knowledge. Teams develop a collective understanding of what each member will do in any given situation, eliminating the need for explicit communication in the moment. [S10, pp.1001-1002]

## Coaching Implications
1. **Design practice around prototypical game situations** — not drills that isolate individual skills, but scenarios that require five players to develop interpersonal synergies simultaneously [S10, p.1001]
2. **Explicitly teach at all four knowledge levels** — players need Intention + Scheme + Strategy + Tactic to execute as a coordinated unit [S10, p.1002]
3. **Prioritize communication skill development** — the quality of shared knowledge depends on how well strategies are communicated and internalized [S10, p.1002]
4. **Build update protocols for in-game adjustments** — teams need predetermined signaling systems so that deliberate updates can occur without timeouts [S10, p.1002]
5. **Measure team-level processes** — technology for assessing individual gaze behaviors exists; team-level processes (shared mental models) are harder to measure but equally important [S10, p.1003]

## Four Psychological Skills for Mastery (Chapter Summary)
The complete set of perceptual-cognitive skills basketball players must develop: [S10, p.1002]
1. **Prolong the quiet-eye period** during free throws
2. **Control emotions and thoughts** (be in the zone) to increase chances of a hot streak
3. **Perceive pressure situations as challenging** (not threatening) to achieve clutch performance
4. **Share knowledge among teammates** to perform as an expert team, not as five individuals

## Common Mistakes
1. **Training only individual skills.** No amount of individual excellence substitutes for the shared situational knowledge built only in group training.
2. **Communicating only at the Tactic level.** Players who only know "run this play" without understanding the Scheme and Strategy behind it cannot adapt when the play breaks down.
3. **Treating shared knowledge as static.** The pre-game plan is a starting point; teams that cannot update knowledge dynamically during competition are locked into an obsolete model.
4. **Underinvesting in psychological training.** Most teams address psychological skills only when problems arise — a systematic psychological training program (analogous to S&C periodization) is needed from youth through professional levels. [S10, p.1002]
