# Quiet Eye for Basketball Free Throw Shooting

## Summary
The quiet eye (QE) is defined as the final fixation before movement initiation — the duration of time a basketball player gazes at the rim (or their fixation target) before initiating the throwing movement. Numerous studies have demonstrated that longer QE periods are associated with superior shooting performance. [S10, p.996]

The free throw is unique in basketball: the shooter stands 4.6 m (15 ft.) from the basket with no defensive pressure and a stopped clock. Yet some elite players struggle to exceed 50% conversion. Sport psychology research explains this paradox — without physical constraints, psychological and attentional factors (internal anxiety, self-doubt, crowd distraction) dominate and disrupt the motor program necessary for consistent shooting. The QE period is the visible window into whether attentional control is maintained. [S10, pp.995-996]

## When to Use (Coaching Application)
- When diagnosing inconsistent free throw shooting in otherwise technically sound players
- When designing pre-performance routines for free throws
- When a player shows accuracy drops in high-pressure game situations (playoffs, late-game)
- As part of a systematic mental skills training program using quiet eye protocols

## Key Principles
1. **Expert shooters fixate longer and on fewer locations.** Expert players (>75% FT season average) fixated for 1058 ms vs. 773 ms for near-experts (<65%). Both fixation duration and number of fixation points distinguish experts. [S10, p.996]
2. **QE duration predicts shot outcome within an individual.** Expert hits: 972 ms mean QE; expert misses: 806 ms; near-expert hits/misses: both under 400 ms. [S10, p.996]
3. **During the QE period, the motor program is being loaded.** Task-relevant information is processed and motor programs retrieved and coordinated during this fixation. When it is interrupted or too short, execution deteriorates. [S10, pp.996-997]
4. **Visual control continues through and after the shot.** Shooting is not purely preprogrammed — visual information is acquired and processed before, during, and after movement execution ("online vision"). The timing of visual acquisition depends on individual shooting style. [S10, p.997]
5. **QE training improves performance.** Training protocols teaching athletes to hold a steady fixation on a preferred target before and during shooting have been shown to enhance athletic performance. [S10, p.997]
6. **Shooting style affects QE timing.** High-style shooters (target always visible until ball release) fixated ~780 ms; low-style shooters (target occluded by hands during final elbow extension) fixated >1 s (1213 ms). [S10, p.997]

## Eye Tracker Technology
Modern eye-trackers resemble regular glasses and incorporate at least two high-frequency cameras:
- **Anterior camera**: records the player's point of view
- **Eye camera**: records pupil movement
- Software identifies **fixations** (location, duration, order), **saccades** (rapid gaze-location changes), and **eye blinks**

Tobii Glasses 2 is one example used in basketball research. [S10, p.996]

## Expert vs. Near-Expert Gaze Patterns
| Metric | Expert (>75% FT) | Near-Expert (<65% FT) |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Total fixation duration | 1058 ms | 773 ms |
| QE — hits | 972 ms | <400 ms |
| QE — misses | 806 ms | <400 ms |
| Fixation locations | 1 (rim's front) | Multiple points around rim |

*Source: Vickers [6], cited in [S10, p.996]*

## QE Training Protocol — Three-Step Free Throw Method
Research-supported steps to improve free throw accuracy through QE training: [S10, p.997]

1. **Assume the stance** — ensure gaze is directed on a preferred spot and bounce the ball as many times as the player is accustomed to
2. **Lock in the fixation** — hold the ball in the shooting stance and direct eyes on a **single preferred location for about 1.5 seconds**; regardless of whether it is the front rim, back rim, or backboard, lock eyes on that direction without moving them
3. **Shoot and maintain** — shoot and maintain the fixation on the preferred location for around **half a second after the ball is released**

With practice, this protocol becomes automatic and integrates into the natural free throw process. [S10, p.997]

## Pre-Performance Routine Integration
The QE protocol is most effective when embedded within a complete pre-performance routine for free throws: [S10, p.1001]
1. Receive the ball from referee
2. Position yourself at your comfortable spot on the line
3. Take two deep breaths
4. Bounce the ball three times
5. Take one more breath; imagine the ball leaving your hand on a perfect arch, moving smoothly through the net with a "swoosh" sound
6. Put the ball at eye level
7. **Look at the basket for 1.5 s** ← QE step
8. Shoot the ball

## Variations
### High-Style Shooters
Target remains visible throughout the shot; fixation approximately 780 ms. QE training can focus on maintaining the single-point fixation without the shooter deviating to check hand position during the release.

### Low-Style Shooters
Target is occluded by hands during final elbow extension; fixation exceeds 1 s (1213 ms). These shooters also maintain fixation on the rim even after the ball enters their field of view — they "trust" the motor program more explicitly.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Multiple fixation points → cue to refocus.** A player looking at multiple rim points before shooting is likely anxious; coach them to identify and lock onto one preferred target.
2. **Releasing gaze too early.** Dropping the gaze before or immediately at ball release shortens QE and disrupts the motor program. Hold fixation through the release.
3. **Skipping the pre-performance routine under pressure.** Anxious players rush and truncate their routine; the routine must be practiced under simulated pressure until automatic.
4. **Ignoring individual shooting style.** High-style and low-style shooters have different QE timing needs — a single prescription does not fit all players.
