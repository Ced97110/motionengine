# Creating Your Own Shot

## Summary
Creating your own shot is the ability to generate an open scoring opportunity against a contested defender using fakes, footwork, and decisive dribble moves — within the flow of the offense, not as selfish isolation play. It is the highest level of individual offensive skill and is built on top of stationary shooting, catch-and-shoot, and shooting on the move.

The key distinction is that shot creation must happen with purpose and within an offensive structure. Dribbling in place, fancy ball-handling for its own sake, and forcing shots are explicitly not what shot creation means. Great scorers combine reading the defense with efficient movement — north to south (toward the basket), not east to west — and use a specific toolkit of fakes and footwork to create space. [S5, pp.91-93]

## When to Use
- Shot clock winding down (3 seconds or less) with no good catch-and-shoot option available
- Defender closes out hard and leaves a driving lane
- Defender is in a compromised position after coming over a screen
- Late-game isolation situations
- Whenever a player reads that their defender is out of position

## Key Principles
1. **Create within the structure of the offense** — shot creation is not selfish one-on-one play; it happens in the flow of the team offense [S5, p.91]
2. **North to south, not east to west** — dribble attacks should move directly toward the basket; lateral dribbling wastes possessions [S5, p.93]
3. **Dribble with a purpose** — push the ball out in front to gain distance, cover ground, and get into a pull-up with the correct footwork [S5, p.93]
4. **Think shot first** — always be in triple-threat position (prepared to shoot, pass, or drive); this keeps you low, balanced, and ready to react [S5, p.96]
5. **Protect the ball with the arm bar** — the inside arm (closest to the defender) is kept bent in an L-shape with a closed fist to shield the dribble [S5, pp.93-94]
6. **Read the defense before committing** — do not predetermine direction; go where the defender gives you the opening [S5, p.99]
7. **Stay low throughout fakes and drives** — straightening up after a fake allows the defender to recover; knees bent = explosive first step available [S5, p.96]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG/SF**: Primary users of shot creation off the dribble; must master strong-side and weak-side show-and-go, jab step, and pull-up bank/jump shot
- **PF/C**: Use shot creation tools primarily in post turnaround and mid-post situations; arm bar and jump stop are especially relevant

## Variations
### Shot Creation Off Screens
Moving without the ball, setting and coming off screens reduces the amount of individual shot creation required. The more effective a player is off-ball, the less they need to rely on creating off the dribble. (Detailed separately in Chapter 9.) [S5, p.91]

### Shot Creation Under Pressure (End of Clock)
With 3 seconds or less, rely on rehearsed moves drilled thousands of times — jab step, shot fake, or show-and-go — executed from the triple-threat position without hesitation. [S5, p.92]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Dribbling in place (east-west movement)** → Attack north-to-south; convert contested perimeter shots into open midrange pull-ups [S5, p.93]
2. **Predetermining direction before reading the defense** → Wait until the defender commits, then go opposite; going early leads to offensive fouls [S5, p.99]
3. **Straightening up on fakes** → Remain in athletic stance throughout; straightening up requires reloading before the drive and gives the defender time to recover [S5, p.96]
4. **Fancy ball-handling without a scoring purpose** → Every dribble should have an intent; reduce dribbles and increase direct attacking moves [S5, p.91]
5. **Only practicing stationary shooting** → Game shooting requires creating space and shooting off fakes and the dribble; both must be drilled regularly [S5, p.92]
