# Basketball Preseason Resistance Training Programming

## Summary
The preseason mesocycle bridges physical qualities built in the off-season with the demands of the basketball season. It is the final development window before in-season maintenance begins. The primary focus shifts from maximal strength to movement velocity, reactive strength, and sport-specific explosiveness — converting the strength and power developed in previous mesocycles into SSC-based reactive ability. The phase lasts **4–6 weeks** at all competitive levels and must be integrated with increasing basketball workloads (practices, scrimmages) without creating destructive fatigue. [S11, Ch.11]

A study by Balabinis and colleagues found that concurrent training (resistance + basketball conditioning) can significantly increase strength, power, and aerobic endurance in the preseason if the program is properly structured. Key: separate resistance training from conditioning by at least several hours; resistance training before conditioning if strength/power gains are the primary goal. [S11, Ch.11]

## When to Use
- Immediately following the final off-season mesocycle (power phase)
- As basketball practices, scrimmages, and conditioning begin ramping up
- 4–6 weeks before the first regular-season game
- When the athlete needs to convert maximal strength and power into reactive, sport-specific explosiveness

## Key Principles
1. **Velocity is the primary target.** Every repetition should be executed with intent to move as fast as possible — quality over quantity.
2. **Reduce volume from off-season levels.** Increased basketball workload demands a concurrent reduction in lifting volume to prevent overtraining.
3. **Maintain the force-velocity curve.** Programs should train across the full curve: from maximal strength to speed-strength — not just one zone.
4. **Prioritize multijoint foundational movements.** Squats, deadlifts, unilateral lower-body, and upper-body exercises in all three planes remain the training backbone.
5. **Emphasize Olympic lifts and derivatives.** Explosive movements (hang cleans, snatches, jump squats) develop the reactive strength needed for the season.
6. **Structure: 1–3 primary + 2–4 assistance exercises.** Primary exercises drive power development; assistance exercises maintain hypertrophy, structural balance, and durability.
7. **Assistance volume:** 1–3 sets × 8–12 reps for hypertrophy and muscular endurance maintenance.
8. **Session scheduling:** Resistance training before practice (high intensity, low volume) or after demanding practice (higher volume, lower intensity).

## Advanced Training Methods

### Cluster Training
Splits traditional sets into multiple **intrasets** with brief (15–45 s) rest between groupings of 1–3 reps. Allows greater power output, velocity, and technique quality throughout the set because phosphocreatine and ATP are partially restored between intrasets. After completing all intrasets (a "cluster set"), take a 3–4 minute full recovery before the next cluster set. [S11, Ch.11]

**Three cluster variations:**
- **Standard:** Same load throughout all intrasets
- **Undulating:** Load increases then decreases (pyramid) — exploits PAP between intrasets
- **Ascending:** Load increases progressively across intrasets

*Reserved for athletes with resistance training experience and appropriate strength levels.*

### Postactivation Potentiation (PAP)
Performing a near-maximal or maximal exercise before a more explosive movement creates a potentiating effect by:
- Phosphorylating myosin regulatory light chains
- Increasing calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum
- Enhancing recruitment of Type II motor units

**Result:** Improved power output in the subsequent explosive exercise (e.g., near-maximal squat → countermovement jump improvement). [S11, Ch.11]

**PAP recovery guidelines:**
- Stronger athletes: 5–7 min rest, single-set maximal training
- Weaker athletes: >8 min rest, multiple sets of submaximal training
- General recommendation: experiment with 2–12 min rest windows (Gołaś)
- Shallower squat depth produces a greater PAP effect regardless of strength level (Seitz & Haff)
- Note: Isometric pre-activation has a negative effect on PAP
- First exercise load: >80% 1RM to provide sufficient potentiation

### Complex Training
All heavy sets are completed first, followed by lighter power/plyometric exercises. Leverages PAP from the heavy work. Better for older, more skilled athletes. [S11, Ch.11]

### Contrast Training
Alternates heavy and light exercises every set (e.g., back squat → countermovement jump). Movements should be biomechanically similar. Both complex and contrast methods exploit PAP but can become metabolic conditioning circuits if rest is insufficient — avoid this. [S11, Ch.11]

### French Contrast Method
Four-exercise sequence alternating between heavy and light loads:
1. Near-maximal resistance exercise (e.g., heavy back squat)
2. Plyometric exercise (e.g., box jump)
3. Velocity-based resistance exercise (e.g., barbell jump squat @ 30–50% 1RM)
4. Reactive (short ground contact time) plyometric (e.g., band-assisted repeated CMJ)

Hernandez-Preciado protocol: isometric partial squat (85% 1RM, 3-s hold) → 3 drop jumps → 3 dynamic squats (50% 1RM) → 3 hurdle jumps (50 cm), 20 s rest between exercises → significant CMJ height improvement. [S11, Ch.11]

*Reserved for athletes with advanced training experience, high fast-twitch fiber distribution, and lower power-to-strength ratios.*

### Accentuated Eccentric Loading
Holding dumbbells (≈20% body mass) during drop jumps, then releasing them before the unloaded vertical jump. Produces eccentric overload enhancing neuromuscular activation. Bridgeman et al.: 1 set of 5 loaded drop jumps was more effective for CMJ improvement than unloaded drop jumps. Habitual use improves strength, power, reactive strength, and sprinting performance. [S11, Ch.11]

### Variable Resistance Training (Chains/Bands)
Chains or elastic bands deliver dynamic loading throughout the range of motion:
- At the **bottom** (weakest position): load is lowest (chains rest on floor) → more manageable for direction change
- At the **top** (strongest position): load is highest (chains fully lifted) → muscles can deliver maximum force

**Benefits over traditional resistance training:**
- Greater improvements in strength, velocity, and power (Rivière et al.)
- Greater constant acceleration through the concentric phase
- Reduces deceleration at the end of the concentric phase
- Higher initial loads increase motor unit recruitment
- Greater elastic energy storage during eccentric phase
- Useful **in-season** as well: lower mechanical stress at weakest position limits muscle damage and soreness

**Sample contrast pairings:** Barbell lunges + bodyweight split squats; Barbell bench press + medicine ball chest passes; RDLs + resisted horizontal broad jumps. [S11, Ch.11]

### Velocity-Based Training (VBT)
Assigns target movement velocities rather than fixed %1RM loads to elicit specific training adaptations. Provides real-time feedback on athlete readiness and autoregulates intensity. [S11, Ch.11]

**Force-velocity training zones (Figure 11.1):**
| Zone | Velocity Target |
|---|---|
| Absolute strength | 0.15–0.35 m/s |
| Circa max strength | 0.35–0.45 m/s |
| Accelerative strength | 0.45–0.75 m/s |
| Strength-speed | 0.75–1.0 m/s |
| Speed-strength | 1.0–1.5 m/s |
| Starting strength | >1.5 m/s |

Progression through preseason: early phase → higher loads, lower velocities (accelerative strength); late preseason → lower loads, higher velocities (speed-strength, starting strength).

**Olympic lift peak velocities (Table 11.1):**
| Exercise | Peak Velocity |
|---|---|
| Snatch from floor | 1.52–1.67 m/s |
| Snatch power pull | 1.81 m/s |
| Snatch power shrug | 1.45 m/s |
| Hang snatch | 1.96 m/s |
| Power clean | 1.2–1.32 m/s |
| Hang clean | 1.3–1.4 m/s |
| Power shrug | 1.15 m/s |

For Olympic lifts: record **peak** velocity (not average); train 2–5 sets; never to failure; always explosive intent. [S11, Ch.11]

## Level-Specific Guidelines

### High School
- Training age: low to moderate; technique focus is paramount
- Duration: 4–6 weeks, **1–2 total-body sessions/week**, 30–45 min/session
- Use linear or undulating progressions; establish movement patterns first
- Undulate heavy/medium/light loads to manage total workload
- Multi-sport athletes: may need gradual reintroduction to resistance training after fall sport competitive season — modify frequency, load, intensity, or volume
- Complex/contrast not appropriate until strength base is established; use traditional S&P instead with plyometrics programmed FIRST in the session [S11, Ch.11]

### College
- Training age varies widely (biological age ≠ training age)
- Duration: 4–6 weeks, **1–2 total-body sessions/week** minimum, 30–45 min/session
- Group athletes by training age or proficiency level for programming
- Must coordinate with academic schedule (NCAA rules on weekly training hours)
- Sessions must be timely, efficient, and flexible — know which exercises are non-negotiable
- Communication with academic advisors and basketball coaching staff essential [S11, Ch.11]

### Professional
- Biological age range: late teens to early 40s (NBA average ≈ 28)
- Duration: 4–6 weeks, **2–4 sessions/week**, 30–45 min/session
- Total body focus; complement basketball skill development
- Rookies: may stay at team facility — more control over physiological readiness
- Veterans: assess current readiness on return to training camp; modify volume/intensity based on individual assessments
- Travel management: contact opposing team coaches and hotels in advance for facility access
- Programming is more flexible (individual or small-group sessions throughout the day)
- Phase covers training camp through first regular-season game [S11, Ch.11]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Excessive volume during preseason** → fatigue accumulates, reducing basketball performance and increasing overuse injury risk; reduce volume, maintain intensity
2. **Applying PAP/complex/contrast to inexperienced athletes** → they lack the strength to elicit PAP and may be injured under heavy loads; use traditional S&P instead
3. **Insufficient rest between PAP pairings** → complex/contrast sets devolve into metabolic conditioning circuits with a different (wrong) training effect
4. **Training Olympic lifts to failure or with average velocity** → Olympic lifts must be explosive and recorded at peak velocity; never grind through them
5. **Ignoring multi-sport athletes' different baseline** → fall sport athletes arrive at preseason with different readiness; blanket programming is inappropriate
6. **Not coordinating with coaching staff** → unexpected practice schedule changes will disrupt resistance training; communication is essential
