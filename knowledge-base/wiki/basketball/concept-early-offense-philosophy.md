# Early Offense Philosophy

## Summary
Early offense refers to structured offensive actions designed to attack before the defense has a chance to fully organize. Unlike pure transition basketball (the fast break), early offense is semi-structured: it flows seamlessly from a made basket or free throw into a half-court set, using the momentum of transition to create advantages the defense cannot yet erase.

The goal is to exploit a fundamental defensive disadvantage — the first few seconds after a change of possession, when defenders are still communicating, rotating, and setting up their schemes (including double-teams). By initiating a post-up, screen, or pick-and-roll action while the defense is still organizing, the offense gains a half-step advantage that a fully-set defense would not allow [S14, p.1].

## When to Use
- Immediately after a made basket (most common trigger)
- After a made free throw
- When the opposing defense is known to use post-double-teams — early action gets the ball inside before the double can be organized
- When a team has a dominant post player who is easier to feed in the first 3–4 seconds of a possession than after defenders have positioned themselves
- As a complement to a half-court offense — early offense attempts the advantageous action first; if unavailable, the team flows into its set half-court system

## Key Principles
1. **Push the ball immediately** — the point guard's first priority is to advance the ball as quickly as possible without sacrificing control.
2. **First big man down fills the low post** — whichever post player gets to the offensive end first claims the ball-side low block, creating an immediate post threat.
3. **Second big man fills the high post** — the trailing big holds at the elbow or top-of-key area, creating a high-low two-man game and providing a passing hub.
4. **Guards fill the wings and corners** — perimeter players run to designated spots to create spacing, passing lanes, and potential kick-out opportunities.
5. **Read, don't force** — each early-offense action has multiple options. The ball handler reads the defense and chooses: direct post feed, high-low pass, ball reversal, or a secondary screen action.
6. **Flow into half-court if nothing is available** — early offense is not a gamble; if the early action is unavailable, the team resets into its primary half-court offense without losing time or structure.
7. **Run both sides** — every early-offense action should be executable on either side of the floor; the positions and roles of the wing players and post players are interchangeable.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Push the ball up the floor; initiate the early action (wing pass, dribble entry, or drive at a wing/post); read and distribute.
- **SG/SF**: Sprint the wings; fill ball-side wing or weak-side wing depending on system; serve as kickout targets and potential secondary scorers.
- **PF/C**: Sprint hard to offensive end; first big claims ball-side low post; second big fills high post near elbow/top of key. Roles are interchangeable.

## Variations
### High-Low System (San Antonio Spurs — Gregg Popovich)
Designed for two interchangeable post players (David Robinson and Tim Duncan), this system creates a continuous high-low threat. The first big down posts low, the second holds high. Ball reversal from high post to weak-side guard triggers the low post to re-seal and attack the front of the rim. See [[play-san-antonio-early-offense-corner-fill]] [S14, pp.1–7].

### Single Post System
Teams with one dominant post player use early offense to feed that player before a double-team can be organized, with perimeter players spacing to create passing lanes out of the post trap.

### Pick-and-Roll Early Action
After the initial post-feed is unavailable, a post player steps up to set a corner or high ball screen for the point guard, creating a pick-and-roll out of the early offense structure. See [[play-san-antonio-corner-fill-pnr]] [S14, p.4].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Slowing down after crossing half-court** → The PG must push pace until the defense is clearly organized; slowing down allows defenders to recover.
2. **Post players jogging to the low block** → Post players must sprint; the first-step advantage lasts only 2–3 seconds.
3. **Forcing the early action** → If the defense is already set, flow into the half-court offense rather than forcing a low-percentage early play.
4. **Poor spacing by perimeter players** → Wings who stop in the lane or crowd the post eliminate passing lanes and invite defenders to help.
5. **Ignoring the secondary read** → Early offense always has multiple options; reading only the first pass and failing to scan to the second pass (high-low, ball reversal) wastes the structure.
