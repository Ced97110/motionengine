# Osteoarthritis in Basketball Players

## Summary
Osteoarthritis (OA) is an irreversible, painful degenerative joint condition common in active basketball players. Initially presenting as nonspecific knee pain, stiffness, and periodic swelling, its symptoms are frequently misattributed to normal basketball wear-and-tear. Through early recognition, risk modification, and systematic nonsurgical treatment, players can manage symptoms and slow disease progression while continuing to compete. Surgical options ranging from osteotomy to joint replacement are reserved for cases where conservative management fails or the athlete retires [S10, Ch.42].

## When to Use (Clinical Decision Points)
- Player reports knee or hip pain, stiffness, or periodic swelling that worsens with activity
- MRI or X-ray reveals cartilage lesion, osteochondral injury, or radiographic OA changes
- Previous meniscectomy, ACL surgery, or ankle trauma — all increase long-term OA risk
- Younger athlete with identifiable biomechanical issue (e.g., knee malalignment) amenable to surgical correction
- Retired athlete who has exhausted nonsurgical options and is a candidate for arthroplasty

## Key Principles
1. **Early recognition matters** — initial OA symptoms (diffuse knee pain, stiffness, swelling) are often dismissed as basketball soreness; systematic screening improves outcomes [S10, p.526].
2. **Modify risk factors first** — address BMI, training load, footwear, and bracing before escalating treatment.
3. **Nonsurgical modalities are primary** — exercise, footwear modification, bracing, NSAIDs, intra-articular corticosteroids, and viscosupplementation (hyaluronic acid) form the core treatment ladder.
4. **Ortho-biologics are emerging** — platelet-rich plasma (PRP) and bone marrow aspirate concentrate (BMAC) show promise but evidence is still developing [S10, p.526].
5. **Osteochondral lesions in young athletes** — microfracture allows early return to play but risks early OA onset; osteochondral allografts/autografts allow return to high-level basketball in up to 80% of cases [S10, p.526].
6. **Unicompartmental/patellofemoral arthroplasty** — an option for severe single-compartment OA in active players; return to high-level competition is generally discouraged due to polyethylene wear and loosening concerns, though recreational return is reported [S10, p.526].
7. **Total arthroplasty for retired athletes** — total knee or hip arthroplasty becomes the definitive solution once competitive demands are removed.

## Nonsurgical Modalities
- **Exercise therapy** — systematic review evidence supports exercise for lower limb OA [S10, p.529 ref 41]
- **Footwear and bracing** — offloading braces for medial compartment OA [S10, p.529 ref 40]
- **Intra-articular corticosteroids** — short-term pain relief; long-term safety is acceptable with appropriate frequency [S10, p.529 ref 42]
- **Viscosupplementation (HA)** — intra-articular hyaluronic acid injections; meta-analyses show therapeutic trajectory benefit [S10, p.529 refs 43–49]
- **PRP and ortho-biologics** — growing evidence base; leukocyte concentration and protocol optimization remain under investigation [S10, p.529 ref 51]

## Surgical Options by Indication
| Situation | Surgical Option |
|---|---|
| Osteochondral lesion (focal, young player) | Microfracture, OCA/OAT graft |
| Malalignment (varus/valgus knee) | Osteotomy |
| Severe single-compartment OA (active player) | Unicompartmental or patellofemoral arthroplasty |
| Severe multi-compartment OA (retired player) | Total knee or hip arthroplasty |

[S10, pp.526–527]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Attributing early OA to normal soreness** → Use systematic screening; investigate persistent diffuse knee pain with imaging.
2. **Jumping to surgery without exhausting conservative care** → Follow the algorithm: exhaust nonsurgical before considering arthroplasty.
3. **Microfracture without long-term counseling** → Players must understand the risk of accelerated OA progression post-microfracture [S10, p.526].
4. **Encouraging high-level return after unicompartmental replacement** → Component wear and loosening risk are real; counsel players appropriately [S10, p.526].
