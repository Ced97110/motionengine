# Reaction Time Training

## Summary
Reaction time is the interval between a stimulus (visual, audible, or tactile) and the initiation of a physical response. In basketball, faster reaction time translates directly to better defense, faster transition breaks, and superior anticipation of opponent movements. Training reaction time requires athletes to process stimuli while under physical stress — not in isolation — so that game-speed reactions become automatic.

The key principle is **dual-task loading**: force athletes to execute complex movement patterns (agility ladder drills, shuffles, rolls) while simultaneously processing external cues from a coach. This trains the central nervous system (CNS) to prioritize and process information faster under fatigue and movement. [S8, pp.214-222]

## When to Use
- During the quickness and agility block of any practice session
- As a warm-up progression once basic footwork patterns are mastered
- In the second half of a 6-week mesocycle when drill combinations are introduced
- With defenders to improve help-side recognition and close-out reads
- With ball handlers to improve decision-making speed under pressure

## Key Principles
1. **Dual-task loading**: Perform a movement pattern while reacting to an external cue — this forces the CNS to process two inputs simultaneously [S8, p.216]
2. **Visual vs. peripheral**: Cones (central vision nerves) process faster than rods (peripheral vision nerves). Train both — reacting to peripheral stimuli improves central reaction time and vice versa [S8, pp.216-222]
3. **Ready position first**: All reaction drills begin from an athletic ready position — weight on balls of feet, slight knee bend, hands active
4. **Progressive complexity**: Master the movement pattern before adding the reaction component. A sloppily executed complex drill reinforces poor movement patterns [S8, p.224]
5. **Sport-specific cues**: Use basketball-specific commands (defensive slide left, close out, sprint to help) rather than generic movements whenever possible
6. **CNS priming**: Rapid-fire foot taps between commands keep the CNS primed for explosive response — do not allow athletes to stand flat-footed between cues

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Learn each footwork pattern to automaticity before adding reaction cues
- **PG/SG**: Focus on forward-facing reaction drills that simulate reading defense and offense simultaneously
- **SF/PF/C**: Include ground-level reaction drills (rolls to reaction) simulating loose ball and rebounding scenarios

### Peripheral Vision Complexity
Coach works a semicircle at the end of the ladder; two or three coaches flash numbers from different positions to force athletes to track stimuli across the full visual field [S8, pp.216-222]

## Common Mistakes
1. Starting the reaction drill before the movement pattern is automatic → slow dual-task processing reinforces nothing. Master footwork first.
2. Looking at feet during ladder drills → kills peripheral vision development. "Trust your feet, see the coach."
3. Stopping between commands → CNS resets, losing the priming effect. Keep rapid foot taps between commands.
4. Generic cues instead of sport-specific ones → missed transfer opportunity. Use "close out!" not just "go!"
