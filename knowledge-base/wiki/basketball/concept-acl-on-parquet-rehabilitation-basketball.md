# On-Parquet Rehabilitation for Basketball ACL Recovery

## Summary
On-parquet (on-court) rehabilitation is the critical final phase of ACL reconstruction recovery for basketball players. It bridges the gap between controlled clinical movement retraining and the full demands of basketball practice and match play. The program consists of five progressive stages of increasing movement intensity, complexity, and sport-specificity. It constitutes four pillars: (1) restoring movement quality, (2) physical conditioning, (3) restoring sport-specific skills, and (4) progressively developing chronic training load. [S10, pp.731-734]

## When to Use
- After completing the targeted NMT program and meeting Stage 1 entry criteria
- Final phase before return to team training after ACLR
- Applicable to any basketball player after serious knee injury requiring late-stage sport-specific retraining

## Key Principles
1. Current rehabilitation programs typically fail to restore movement quality before RTS due to insufficient volume, intensity, and sport-specificity — on-parquet training directly addresses this gap [S10, p.732]
2. Three dimensions of progression across all five stages:
   - **Movement Intensity:** progress from jogging to maximal sprint/change-of-direction intensity
   - **Movement Specificity/Complexity:** progress from planned → reactive movements, from isolated → multi-player contact situations
   - **Movement Volume:** gradually replicate the workload demands of practice to build chronic load and avoid an acute-to-chronic spike upon RTS [S10, pp.732-733]
3. Reactive movements produce worse knee biomechanics and higher knee loads than planned movements — this is why planned movements must be mastered before reactive work
4. A laterally planted foot during cutting increases knee abduction moment (KAM) — necessary for performance but a known injury risk; optimal technique must be reinforced
5. The functional recovery of a basketball player following ACLR ends with on-parquet rehabilitation

## The Five-Stage On-Parquet Rehabilitation Program

### Stage 1: Linear Movement Training
**Entry Criteria:**
- No pain or swelling
- No subjective instability
- No positive laxity tests
- Symmetrical range of motion
- Knee flexor and extensor LSI >80%
- Ability to run at 8 km/h for 10 minutes with normalized running mechanics
- Sufficient movement quality during foundation movements

**Goal:** Linear movement training

**Exercises:**
- Linear running (forward and lateral)
- Foundation movement tasks (squatting, lunging, athletic walks)
- Deceleration tasks in pre-planned situations of differing velocities
- Mobility drills [S10, p.733]

---

### Stage 2: Multidirectional Movement Training
**Entry Criteria:**
- No pain or swelling
- Satisfactory progression through Stage 1

**Goal:** Multidirectional movement training

**Exercises:**
- Increased speeds of movements from Stage 1
- Multidirectional pre-planned coordination drills (cutting drills at increasing angles, bended running, figure-of-eight, accelerations/decelerations) [S10, p.733]

---

### Stage 3: Basketball Technical and Reactive Movement Training
**Entry Criteria:**
- No pain or swelling
- Knee flexor and extensor LSI >90%
- Optimal movement quality during pre-planned sport-type tasks

**Goal:** Basketball technical and reactive movement training

**Exercises:**
- Maximum speed pre-planned linear and multidirectional movement drills (change-of-direction drills, peak running speed exposure, ladder drills)
- Reactive movement retraining — high speed multidirectional pre-planned speed, acceleration, and deceleration training (closed tasks)
- Movement practice under external focus with technical-based drills [S10, p.733]

---

### Stage 4: Basketball-Specific Movement and Skill Restoration
**Entry Criteria:**
- No pain or swelling
- Satisfactory progression through Stage 3

**Goal:** Basketball-specific movement and skill restoration

**Exercises:**
- Continued pre-planned and reactive movement training — high-speed multidirectional pre-planned and reactive movements
- Movement in basketball-specific situations
- Closed basketball-specific fitness drills (e.g., Stage 3 basketball movement drills for conditioning)
- Repeated sprint running
- Reactive movement training with perturbations (ropes, Swiss ball, agility circuit with ropes/Swiss balls, player contact)
- Technical drills with pressure and contact to force the player off-balance [S10, p.734]

---

### Stage 5: Training Simulation / Re-Conditioning
**Entry Criteria:**
- No pain or swelling
- Satisfactory progression through Stage 4

**Goal:** Training simulation and re-conditioning

**Exercises:**
- Basketball-specific movement training
- Basketball-specific plus speed and agility training in pre-planned and reactive tasks
- All tasks with and without fatigue [S10, p.734]

## Three Dimensions of On-Parquet Progression

### Movement Intensity
- Basketball involves high-intensity actions — accelerations, decelerations, jumps, changes of direction at rapid intensity [S10, p.732]
- Knee loading increases as approach velocity increases during change-of-direction tasks
- Must progressively arrive at maximal movement intensity before returning to team
- "It is essential to arrive at maximal movement intensity, prior to return to the team." [S10, p.732]

### Movement Specificity / Complexity
- Progress from planned → reactive movements
- Gradually increase decision-making options (challenge neurocognition)
- Gradually increase player contact and number of players [S10, p.732]
- Reactive movements are associated with worse knee biomechanics and higher knee loads than planned movements

### Movement Volume
- Expose athlete to workload demands comparable to training to build chronic load
- Avoid a spike in workload upon RTS — acute-to-chronic ratio must stay in safe range [S10, p.733]
- The final periods of on-parquet rehabilitation should replicate the workload demands of training

## Common Mistakes
1. Skipping reactive movement phases — correction: athletes must demonstrate good movement quality in planned tasks before progressing to reactive
2. Returning to team before reaching maximal movement intensity in on-parquet program — correction: Stage 5 must include fatigue conditions before clearing
3. Insufficient volume in on-parquet sessions relative to practice demands — correction: replicate actual practice workload in final stages
4. Ignoring player contact and perturbation work — correction: Stages 4–5 must include ropes, Swiss ball challenges, and player contact
