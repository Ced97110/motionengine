# Calling the Ball

## Summary
Calling for the ball is a communication discipline that extends far beyond simply shouting for the basketball. Great shooters signal their readiness through three channels simultaneously: voice, eye contact, and hand targets. When executed properly, calling for the ball helps the shooter catch in rhythm — already prepared to shoot — and encourages the passer to deliver a quality pass to the right location.

Coaches must enforce this habit deliberately; players who are reluctant to call for the ball should be told that teammates simply will not pass to them unless they communicate clearly and confidently.

## When to Use
- Every time a shooter is open and prepared to receive the ball
- Especially critical when moving at speed (e.g., transition jump shots, coming off screens)
- Applied in all catch-and-shoot situations: midrange, three-point, off screens

## Key Principles
1. **Demand the ball verbally.** Be loud and insistent: "Ball, ball, ball, ball, ball!" A quiet call is no call. In a packed gymnasium, only you will know you're open if you whisper.
2. **Make eye contact with the passer before the catch.** Lock in to your teammate before the ball arrives. Don't let the defender or crowd distract you. Eye contact signals readiness to the passer.
3. **Show hand targets.** Palm faces the passer like a catcher's mitt facing the pitcher. This naturally gets the shooting arm into the L-shaped position, keeping the player on shot line and increasing make probability. More makes = more passes received.
4. **Calling creates rhythm.** When you alert the passer and show a target, both passer and shooter are synchronized — the result is a catch already in the shot pocket, flowing directly into the shooting motion.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Calling for the ball is a universal habit. Shooters at every position must internalize this before any shooting drill.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Calling quietly** → Teammates won't know you're open; demand the ball loudly.
2. **Looking away or letting the defender disrupt your focus** → Breaks eye contact with passer; keep your eyes locked on the passer until the catch.
3. **Not showing hand targets** → Pass arrives in the wrong position, breaking shot pocket alignment; always show the palm as a target.
4. **Expecting the pass without communicating** → Players who don't call don't receive; enforce "no call, no pass" as a team rule.
