# Power, Speed, and Agility — Foundational Definitions

## Summary
Paul Wade's *Explosive Calisthenics* builds its entire training philosophy on three precisely defined athletic qualities: **power**, **functional speed**, and **agility**. These definitions differ meaningfully from casual usage and provide a coherent framework for designing explosive bodyweight training. The central insight is that moving your own bodyweight quickly represents the "golden mean" of athletic power — neither too light (isolated limb drills) nor too heavy (maximal barbell lifts).

For basketball players, all three qualities are directly transferable: power drives jumping and contact; functional speed determines first-step quickness and transition speed; agility governs the direction changes, cuts, and spatial awareness that define on-court excellence.

## When to Use
- When designing athletic development programs for basketball players
- When evaluating whether a drill trains power, speed, or agility (or some combination)
- When explaining to players *why* explosive bodyweight work is superior to slow heavy lifting for court performance
- When differentiating between simple plyometric training and true agility development

## Key Principles

### Power = Strength × Speed
1. **Power is not synonymous with strength.** Strength at its purest requires zero speed (holding a load in a fixed position). Power requires movement — both a load and velocity. [S9, p.17]
2. **The formula is multiplicative:** increase either strength or speed and power increases. [S9, p.17]
3. **Light loads produce high speed but low power** (a fast punch moves a light arm). Heavy loads produce high strength but low speed. Bodyweight is the ideal middle ground. [S9, pp.18-19]
4. **The practical implication:** Moving your body's own weight quickly — as an acrobat or parkour athlete does — is the optimal training stimulus for athletic power. [S9, p.19]

### Functional Speed = Whole-Body Short-Distance Quickness
5. **Speed is not endurance.** Covering long distances quickly is primarily aerobic endurance. True functional speed is the ability to displace the *entire body* over a short distance very rapidly — a single explosive action. [S9, p.19]
6. **Isolated limb speed is insufficient** for sport. Flipping coins or catching dropped rulers builds finger/hand speed but has no transfer to athletic situations where the whole body must move. [S9, pp.19-20]
7. **Whole-body speed overlaps with power** — because moving the body's mass quickly requires force production, speed and power are inseparable qualities in practice. [S9, p.20]

### Agility = Complex Power (Rapid, Coordinated Direction Change)
8. **Power ≠ Agility.** An athlete can have enormous vertical leap (simple power in one direction) but be incapable of a back flip (which requires agility — power in multiple rapidly changing directions). [S9, p.20]
9. **Agility requires nervous system training**, not just muscular power. The nervous system must learn to coordinate multiple muscle groups firing in rapid succession through changing vectors. [S9, p.20]
10. **Traditional plyometrics develop simple power** (one direction), not agility. Agility training requires multi-directional, multi-velocity movement patterns. [S9, p.20]
11. **Agility = complex power:** think of it as power that must be redirected mid-movement, demanding coordination and kinesthetic awareness beyond brute force. [S9, p.20]

## Player Responsibilities
All positions benefit equally from developing these three qualities, though emphasis varies:
- **PG**: Functional speed (first step) and agility (change of direction) are primary
- **SG/SF**: All three qualities — power (athleticism, contact finishing), speed (transition), agility (off-ball movement)
- **PF/C**: Power (rebounding contact, post play) and agility (footwork, back-to-basket moves)

## Variations
### Simple Power
Explosive movement in a single direction — vertical jump, broad jump, single-plane sprint. Trained by plyometric progressions and power jumps. [S9]

### Complex Power (Agility)
Explosive movement requiring redirection — cutting, back flip, kip-up, spinning moves. Requires both power base AND nervous system coordination training. [S9]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Confusing strength with power** → Correct by asking "is there movement?" No movement = strength only. Add velocity to turn strength into power.
2. **Training only isolated limb speed** (e.g., reaction drills, finger speed) → Correct by using whole-body explosive drills that displace the entire body rapidly.
3. **Assuming power training = agility training** → Correct by including multi-directional, multi-velocity patterns that demand nervous system coordination, not just single-plane plyometrics.
4. **Using only heavy external loads for power** → Correct by using bodyweight movements at maximal velocity as the primary power development tool.
