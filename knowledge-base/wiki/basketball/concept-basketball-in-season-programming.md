# In-Season Resistance Training Programming for Basketball

## Summary
In-season resistance training is often underutilized or abandoned by basketball programs, but it represents the **longest mesocycle of the year** — 16-20 weeks for high school, up to 24 weeks for college, and up to 30 weeks for professional athletes including playoffs. Abandoning the weight room during this period guarantees regression of the physical qualities built during the off-season and preseason.

The primary goal of in-season resistance training is to **bridge the weight room and the court**: continuing to develop power qualities needed for basketball while maintaining the strength base established during the off-season. Athletes should train 1-2 total body sessions per week (30-45 minutes each), with sessions organized around the game schedule, travel, and recovery needs of each individual athlete [S11, Ch.12].

## When to Use
- Throughout the entire competitive season (from first game through playoffs)
- Adjusted based on athlete's playing time, travel schedule, and fatigue state
- Post-game sessions prioritize assistance/endurance exercises; nongame sessions allow power/strength emphasis
- Different protocols for starters, bench players, injured athletes, and redshirt players

## Key Principles
1. **Continue training — don't stop**: Resistance training during the competitive season prevents regression and builds on physical attributes; movement technique also improves with continued practice [S11, p.X]
2. **Individualize by playing time**: Starters (high game minutes), moderate-minute players, and DNP/injured athletes each require different volume and intensity prescriptions within the same training session
3. **Prioritize ground-based, multijoint movements**: Primary exercises should use movement patterns and speeds that occur in basketball (squat jumps, Olympic lift variations, loaded squats, hex bar deadlifts)
4. **Include multiplanar assistance work**: Basketball is a multiplanar sport — program lunges, step-ups, and pressing in sagittal, frontal, and transverse planes
5. **Protect the shoulder joint**: Scapular stabilizers and rotator cuff exercises must be included as non-fatiguing assistance work paired during rest periods
6. **Manipulate parameters, not exercises**: In-season variation should come from manipulating tempo, range of motion, intensity, velocity, pairings, and equipment — NOT by introducing completely new exercises (familiarity reduces soreness)
7. **Session duration 30-45 minutes**: Include proper warm-up and cool-down; sessions longer than 45 minutes compromise recovery for the next game

## Level-Specific Guidelines

### High School
- **Season length**: 16-20 weeks
- **Frequency**: 1-2 sessions/week
- **Session length**: 30-45 minutes including warm-up/cool-down
- **Focus**: Fundamental movement patterns with technical proficiency; less emphasis on intensity for beginners
- **Scheduling constraint**: Must work around classes, practices, games, extracurriculars, and off-school days

### College
- **Season length**: 20-24 weeks
- **Frequency**: Minimum 1-2 total body sessions/week
- **Session length**: 30-45 minutes
- **Scheduling constraint**: NCAA regulations limit days off and travel; 4-5 day training splits from off-season become impossible — full coordination with academic calendar required
- **Training groups**: Beginners (developmental), intermediate, trained — differentiated by training age and experience

### Professional
- **Season length**: 24-30 weeks including playoffs
- **Frequency**: Minimum 1-2 total body sessions/week
- **Session length**: 30-45 minutes
- **Unique challenges**: 3-4 games/week including back-to-backs; road travel limits weight room access; NBA schedule redesign (games typically every other day) creates some predictability
- **After-game sessions**: Focus on assistance exercises and strength-endurance (lunges, RDLs, hamstring curls) when athletes are already fatigued

## Exercise Selection Hierarchy

### Primary Exercises (Performance-Focused)
These are the core exercises that drive continued physical development and court performance:
- Barbell squat jumps (quick contraction, maximal power output intent)
- Loaded bilateral and unilateral squat variations
- Traditional and hex bar deadlifts
- Olympic lifting variations (clean, snatch, jerk derivatives)
- "Most exercises should reflect the sport and be specific to its demands" [S11, Ch.12]

### Assistance Exercises (Durability-Focused)
These address structural balance, individual weaknesses, and joint health:
- **Horizontal pulling**: Dumbbell rows, inverted rows
- **Vertical pulling**: Lat pulldowns, pull-ups, chin-ups
- **Posterior chain**: Romanian deadlifts (bilateral and unilateral), good mornings, reverse hip extensions, posterior bridging
- **Multiplanar lower body**: Forward/side/reverse lunges, step-ups with dumbbell or barbell in all three planes
- **Upper body multiplanar pressing**: Shoulder pressing in various directions (sagittal, frontal, transverse)
- **Joint support**: Scapular stabilizer and rotator cuff exercises paired during rest periods (non-fatiguing to primary lifts)

## Frequency Management by Game Schedule

### High-Minute Athletes (After a Game)
- Sessions immediately post-game or next day: focus on assistance exercises
- Emphasize strength-endurance under higher volume
- Exercises: lunging, RDLs, hamstring curls
- Power/maximal strength exercises: only on non-game days

### Low/No-Minute Athletes
- Can train at higher intensities more consistently
- May complete same volume at higher absolute loads
- Example: 5×2 back squat at 85.5% ("heavy" relative intensity) vs high-minute starter at 69% ("light" relative intensity)

### Scheduling Principle
- Adjust workload and intensity when sessions fall at non-optimal times (only available slot)
- Ensure adequate recovery before next competition is the non-negotiable priority
