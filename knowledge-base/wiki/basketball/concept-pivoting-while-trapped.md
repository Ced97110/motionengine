# Pivoting While Being Trapped

## Summary
When a ball handler is caught in a double-team trap, the same three-phase pivoting system used against a single defender (step-out, rip-through, swing-around) applies — with one critical modification. Because there are now two defenders, if both flanks are successfully defended, the ball handler must **split the trap** by driving through the gap between the two trappers rather than outflanking either one. [S6, pp.133–140]

Successfully passing out of a trap creates an immediate 4-on-3 advantage for the offense, which should produce a fast-break scoring opportunity. [S6, p.139]

## When to Use
- Whenever the ball handler is caught in a full-court or half-court double-team
- After a killed dribble when two defenders converge on the ball
- In press-break situations when the ball is trapped in the backcourt or near a sideline

## Key Principles

### General Rules (Same as 1-on-1)
1. Inside foot = pivot foot; outside foot = free foot.
2. Ball protected beside the free knee at all times.
3. Step east or west — not north or south — when passing.
4. Fake high-go low or fake low-go high before passing.
5. Coaching cues: *"Step east or west — not north or south."* / *"Protect the ball beside the knee."* / *"If your foot beats his foot, look to pass."* [S6, p.136]

### Phase 1: Step-Out vs. Trap
1. Attack the trapping defender's outside foot that is on the **same side as the free foot**. [S6, p.133]
2. Step directly to the free foot's side (due east or west), ball beside the free knee.
3. If the free foot beats the trapper's outside foot: pass over or under the trapper's outstretched hand. [S6, pp.133–136]

### Phase 2: Rip-Through vs. Trap
1. If Phase 1 is defended (X2 matches the step), front pivot and rip the ball low and hard across the shoe tops toward the **other trapper's outside foot** (X1's outside foot). [S6, pp.136–137]
2. The free leg follows the ball path — same technique as 1-on-1 rip-through, but now directed at the second trapper's shoulder/foot.
3. Ball remains mere inches off the floor — most defenders cannot get their hands that low to steal it. [S6, p.137]
4. If the free foot beats X1's outside foot: pass over or under X1's hand (fake high-go low or fake low-go high). [S6, p.137]
<!-- DIAGRAM: Diagram 2-17 — step-out is defended; ball handler rips through to attack X1's outside foot. [S6, p.137] -->

### Phase 3: Swing-Around vs. Trap — Split the Trap
This is the critical modification from the single-defender swing-around:

1. If both X1 and X2 defended their outside flanks, both trappers have spread themselves laterally — creating a **gap between them**. [S6, pp.137–139]
2. Execute the second front pivot (swing-around), but instead of going all the way back to the original flank, **the step with the free foot goes only halfway — directly into the space between the two trappers**. [S6, p.139]
3. Ball handler pulls the ball in tight to the upper chest, **chins the ball**, and lowers his head slightly as he steps through. [S6, p.139]
4. Both elbows extend outward, protecting the ball from the reaching hands of both defenders. [S6, p.139]
5. Eyes stay up to avoid committing an offensive charge (do not bowl into the trappers). [S6, p.139]
6. Do not over-extend or lean — remain balanced and in control to avoid a travel call. [S6, p.139]
7. Once through the trap, raise the head and immediately find an open teammate.
8. This move is like a fullback receiving a handoff and driving into the defensive line. [S6, p.139]

## Why Splitting Works
If both defenders spread to defend their outside flanks, the middle is weakened. The swing-around step exploits this by attacking the gap rather than either flank. With two defenders on the ball, only three defenders remain to guard four off-ball offensive players — someone is always open once the pass is made. [S6, p.139]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Must recognize the trap early and execute all three phases smoothly and without panic.
- **All ball handlers**: Must train all three phases bilaterally (both feet as pivot foot).
- **Off-ball teammates**: Must break aggressively toward the ball to be available pass receivers after the trap is split; coaches should emphasize *meet every pass*. [S6, p.142]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Panicking and forcing a lob over both defenders** → easy interception; use the systematic phases instead.
2. **Over-extending the split-the-trap step** → travel call or loss of balance; keep the step controlled, only as far as the gap between trappers.
3. **Ball not chinned/protected when splitting** → both defenders can swipe at it; pull ball to upper chest with elbows out.
4. **Eyes down when splitting** → may commit an offensive charge on a trapper; keep eyes up throughout.
5. **Attacking the same flank on the swing-around** → goes back to where both defenders are strongest; step only halfway back to find the gap.

## Teaching Cues
- *"Step east or west — not north or south."*
- *"Protect the ball beside the knee."*
- *"Chin the ball — elbows out — eyes up."*
- *"Split the gap — don't go all the way back."*
- *"Once through, head up — find the open man."*
