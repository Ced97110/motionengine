# Team Defensive Stunts

## Summary
Team defensive stunts are a second-layer pressure system applied on top of a sound man-to-man foundation. They serve two purposes: (1) force turnovers when trailing to get back in the game, and (2) disrupt the rhythm of an opponent executing their offense efficiently. The four core stunts — run-and-jump, run-and-trap, trap the dribble, and trap the pass — are progressive in complexity and each can be run from either an even (2-guard) or odd (1-guard) front formation.

These stunts must be installed **only after man-to-man fundamentals are sound**. Applied prematurely, they create defensive breakdowns rather than turnovers. Applied correctly, they create confusion, force poor decisions, and compel opponents to slow down [S15, p.323].

## When to Use
- When **trailing** and needing a quick turnover to get back in the game
- When an opponent is **executing their offense too efficiently** and needs a rhythm disruption
- **Late game** — time and score dictate whether safety or all-out interception is appropriate
- As a possession-to-possession **surprise weapon** — mixing stunts so the offense cannot predict which is coming

## Key Principles
1. **Prerequisites first**: Man-to-man team defense must be fundamentally sound before stunts are introduced. "You want to put in these defensive stunts only after your man-to-man team defense is fundamentally sound." [S15, p.323]
2. **Channeling enables everything**: All trapping stunts depend on the primary ball defender channeling the dribbler in a predetermined direction. The direction change is the trigger.
3. **Trappers hold; teammates cover lanes**: The two trappers keep the trap active as long as the dribble is alive. The remaining three defenders cover passing lanes.
4. **Safety vs. all-out interception**: Safety leaves one safety defender (nearest basket); all-out denies all three nearest receivers and leaves the farthest player open. Time and score dictate the call.
5. **The habit pass**: When a defender arrives unexpectedly on the ball-handler, the "habit" pass goes to the first open-looking receiver — often player 2 on the wing. Teammates must anticipate and intercept it.
6. **Hedge-and-recover as a fake**: The defender makes a run-and-jump motion but stops short and recovers. After facing true stunts, ball-handlers frequently pick up the dribble even on the fake — creating a 5-second turnover.
7. **Dribble used = maximum pressure**: When the ball-handler picks up the dribble, all five defenders get in passing lanes immediately. A 5-second count is a free turnover.

## The Four Stunts

### Stunt 1: Run-and-Jump
- X1 overplays the dribbler past half-court, forcing a direction change (spin or crossover)
- X2 **runs and jumps** the dribbler emerging from the change, taking control of the ball
- X1 rotates to cover X2's assignment
- X4 (help side) covers both 2 and 4 in a flat triangle
- *Variations*: two-player, three-player (X4 steals the habit pass), hedge-and-recover

### Stunt 2: Run-and-Trap
- Same initiation as run-and-jump, but X2 **stays** with X1 and traps as long as the dribble is alive
- X3, X4, and X5 cover passing lanes using safety or all-out interception

### Stunt 3: Trap the Dribble
- X1 channels the dribbler to midcourt; the direction change **signals** X3 to race for the trap
- X3 stays in the passing lane to player 3 while trapping (forces lob or bounce passes — the slowest passes)
- X2 and X5 play in the gaps; X4 is safety

### Stunt 4: Trap the Pass
- X3 **deliberately opens** the wing passing lane to lure a pass from player 1
- Once the pass is made, X1 and X3 immediately trap the receiver
- Same safety/all-out interception coverage applies

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (X1)**: Primary ball defender — channels, overplays, either jumps or traps; rotates to cover wing on run-and-jump
- **SG (X2)**: Secondary defender — executes the jump or trap; covers safety on rotation
- **SF (X3)**: Wing denial or deliberate lane-opening; becomes secondary trapper in trap-the-pass
- **PF (X4)**: Help-side safety; covers the basket; reads lob passes for interception opportunities
- **C (X5)**: Interior gap defender; covers passing lanes; denies assigned player before and after trap activation

## Variations

### Odd vs. Even Front
All four stunts can be run from a **2-guard front (even/2-1-2)** or a **1-guard front (odd/1-3-1)**. Coaches should drill from both so players understand that stunts work against any offensive formation.

### All-Out vs. Safety Interception
- **Safety**: Two gap defenders shade toward passer's eyes; one safety defender guards basket; can run all game
- **All-out**: Three nearest receivers denied tightly; farthest player left open; used selectively based on time and score

## Common Mistakes
1. **Activating stunts before fundamentals are sound** → Creates breakdowns; build man-to-man first
2. **X2 arriving too early on run-and-jump** → Ball-handler hasn't committed to direction; X2 gets beat
3. **Trappers splitting the trap (letting dribbler through)** → Arms must be active and feet wide; no gaps through the middle
4. **X3 leaving the passing lane while trapping** → Ball-handler makes the easy escape pass; X3 must stay in the lane while running
5. **Defenders not recognizing "dribble used"** → Must get in passing lanes immediately when the dribble is picked up
