# Shared Decision-Making in Sports Cardiology

## Summary
The traditional binary yes/no approach to sports eligibility decisions for athletes with cardiovascular conditions has been widely criticized for failing to account for individual nuance. A major trend in sports cardiology is the shift toward **shared decision-making (SDM)** — a patient-centered model where sports cardiologists and other providers fully inform athletes of risks and engage them in decisions that account for their life goals and values [S10, p.886].

SDM plays a central role in the ESC sports participation recommendations and is gaining acceptance in the AHA/ACC community as well. However, SDM is not absolute — some conditions (like ARVC) warrant uniform restriction regardless of athlete preference.

## When to Use
- An athlete has a cardiovascular condition that places them in a "gray zone" for eligibility
- AHA/ACC guidelines recommend restriction but the athlete or family requests individualized evaluation
- Return-to-play decisions after cardiac events where risk data is incomplete
- Athletes with channelopathies, ICDs, or conditions where observational safety data exists

## Key Principles
1. **Sports cardiologists must fully inform athletes** of all known and unknown risks before any decision [S10, p.886]
2. **Athlete life goals and identity matter** — sports participation is often fundamental to personal identity, unlike most other medical decisions; disqualification carries real psychological and financial costs
3. **The clinician's highest priority remains athlete health and wellness** — regardless of athlete preference, financial pressure, or institutional considerations
4. **Risks are often unknown or unquantifiable** — limited safety data for most cardiovascular conditions during sports participation makes the discussion less concrete than typical medical counseling [S10, p.886]
5. **SDM is not absolute** — for certain conditions (e.g., ARVC), sports cardiologists uniformly agree that high-intensity competitive sports are contraindicated regardless of athlete preference [S10, p.886]
6. **Medical interventions can change the risk calculus** — successful WPW ablation, optimal LQTS management, or aortic surgery changes eligibility decisions; the potential to mitigate risk through intervention is a key decision variable

## Practical Challenges to SDM in Basketball

| Challenge | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Unknown/unquantifiable risks | Limited safety data for specific conditions + competitive basketball demand |
| Athlete identity and dreams | Longstanding goals tied to sport make objectivity difficult |
| External financial pressures | Professional contracts, scholarships, draft positions create potential conflicts |
| Youth risk-taking behavior | Young athletes may not accurately appreciate long-term risk implications |
| Third-party stakeholders | Schools, teams, and leagues may prioritize institutional safety or legal considerations |
| Disqualification consequences | Psychological distress, loss of scholarships, loss of professional income [S10, p.886] |

## The Limits of SDM
- **ARVC**: Uniformly restricted from high-intensity competitive sports — SDM does not change this recommendation
- **Marfan Syndrome**: Basketball is contraindicated at any aortic size — not a matter for SDM
- **Acute myocarditis**: Strong recommendation against sports regardless of athlete preference
- **Definite HCM**: AHA/ACC advises restriction from most competitive sports regardless of symptoms or perceived risk
- **Outcome data** for optimally managed LQTS and CPVT is very limited for cardiomyopathies — cautious approach is prudent until more safety data emerges [S10, p.886]

## Role of Coaches and Athletic Trainers
Coaches and athletic trainers **must be informed** of an athlete's cardiac condition (with appropriate consent) so that:
- Emergency action plans can be established and rehearsed
- Workload modifications can be implemented
- AED is available at all practices and competitions
- Signs of decompensation can be recognized and acted upon promptly [S10, p.887]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Making eligibility decisions without cardiologist or disease-specialist input** → correction: always refer to a sports cardiologist for formal risk stratification
2. **Allowing financial or institutional pressure to override medical judgment** → correction: clinician's primary duty is athlete health; document all discussions and decisions
3. **Failing to communicate the athlete's condition to the coaching staff** → correction: establish a communication plan (with athlete consent) so team personnel can support emergency response
4. **Not revisiting decisions annually** — conditions change → correction: annual evaluation is mandatory for all athletes with cardiovascular conditions competing in sports
