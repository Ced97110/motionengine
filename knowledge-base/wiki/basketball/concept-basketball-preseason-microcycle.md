# Basketball Preseason Microcycle — Reactive Strength and Velocity (Weeks 1-6)

## Summary
The preseason Microcycle 1 (Tables 11.2 and 11.3 from S11) prescribes a 4-day training split for basketball athletes during weeks 1-6 of the preseason phase, with a primary emphasis on **reactive strength and velocity**. Guards and bigs follow nearly identical structural templates but differ in key exercise selections and velocity targets — reflecting the position-specific demands of the sport.

Each day follows a **complex pairing (PAP — Post-Activation Potentiation) structure**: a heavy primary lift is paired with an explosive plyometric or medicine ball throw that benefits from the neurological priming effect. Days 1 and 2 focus on pushing (lower and upper body respectively), while Days 3 and 4 focus on pulling, with Olympic lift variations as the primary exercise.

## When to Use
- First 6 weeks of preseason, after off-season strength base has been established
- When transitioning from maximal strength emphasis to power/reactive strength emphasis
- For athletes classified as intermediate or trained (college/pro level)
- Advanced complex (3a/3b exercises) added only for college or pro athletes

## Key Principles
1. **PAP complex pairing**: Heavy primary lift (1a) is immediately followed by an explosive plyometric or reactive movement (1b) to potentiate power output — rest periods increase set by set (1 min → 3 min) to allow full PAP effect
2. **Velocity targets drive load selection**: Back squat targets average velocity of 0.8–1.0 m/s; Olympic lifts (split snatch for guards, power/hang clean for bigs) target peak velocity of 1.2–1.9+ m/s
3. **Variable resistance in later sets**: Sets 4–5 of squats and bench press use 76%–85% with variable resistance (bands/chains) at a 1:X:X tempo to address the force-velocity curve through the sticking point
4. **Explosive tempo (X:X:X)** is used for all plyometric and Olympic lift work — maximum concentric intent
5. **Short rest for accessories (<30 s)**: Tri-set structure (2a/2b/2c) with <30 second rest between exercises keeps density high for assistance work
6. **Position differentiation**: Guards use split snatch (Days 3); Bigs use power/hang clean (Day 3) — the clean has slower peak velocity requirements (1.3 m/s vs 1.9 m/s for snatch) reflecting different power expression demands

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG (Guards)**: Higher velocity emphasis (split snatch ≥1.9 m/s); med ball reactive chest pass; walking lunge; side rotational V-up
- **PF/C (Bigs)**: Slightly higher load tolerance on Olympic lifts (power clean ≥1.3 m/s, up to 65% load); overhead med ball wall throw single-leg; step-up; deadbug on stability ball; landmine squat to rotational press
- **All positions**: Same structural template, PAP pairings, and assistance tri-set format

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not monitoring bar velocity** → Use a velocity tracking device to ensure target velocity zones are hit; if velocity drops below threshold, reduce load
2. **Insufficient rest before PAP plyometric** → The PAP effect requires ~30-60 seconds rest after the heavy set; rushing undermines the potentiation benefit
3. **Applying advanced complex to beginners** → The 3a/3b exercises are labeled "college or pro athlete" only; high school athletes should stop after 2c
4. **Chains swaying during variable resistance sets** → Some chain must remain on the floor at the starting position; if chains sway, risk increases significantly
5. **Ignoring tempo prescriptions** → The 3:1:1 tempo on squats and bench press builds controlled eccentrics critical for injury prevention; X:X:X means truly maximal intent

## Variations
### High School Modification
Omit the advanced complex (3a/3b) entirely. Reduce Olympic lift load to 25-45% max and prioritize technique over velocity. Target 30-45 minute total session time.

### In-Season Modification
Reduce to 2 days/week (Day 1 + Day 4, or Day 2 + Day 3), cut assistance volume to 2 sets, and rely on RPE/velocity-based autoregulation rather than fixed percentages.
