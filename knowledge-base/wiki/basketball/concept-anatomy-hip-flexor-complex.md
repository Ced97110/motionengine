# Hip Flexor Complex

## Summary
The hip flexor complex drives every cutting, driving, and first-step action in basketball — raising the thigh toward the torso or bending the torso toward the thigh. The primary muscle is the **iliopsoas** (psoas major + iliacus); secondary contributors are the **rectus femoris**, **sartorius**, and **tensor fasciae latae (TFL)**. When this region is tight or weak, baseline cuts, defensive slides, and driving-step acceleration all degrade. [S2]

The `rectus femoris` is named verbatim in Motion's drill corpus as a secondary muscle trained by the hip thrust: "quadriceps (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis, vastus intermedius)" [S2, pp.18-19].

## When to Use
This anatomy matters to coaching decisions in every situation involving:
- Cutting hard from a stationary position — baseline cuts, flash cuts, backdoor cuts
- First-step acceleration off a triple-threat or catch
- Transition-offense sprinting, especially the initial strides
- Recovery footwork when a defender has been beaten and is resetting
- Post-play footwork — sealing, up-and-under moves, drop-step mechanics

## Key Principles
1. **Hip flexion is a chain, not a single muscle** — the iliopsoas does the primary work; rectus femoris, sartorius, and TFL contribute at specific joint angles. Training one without the others creates imbalance. [S2]
2. **Mobility before strength** — a tight iliopsoas limits stride length and baseline-cut depth before strength ever becomes the limiter. Mobility work precedes strength work for this region.
3. **Reciprocal with the glutes** — chronically tight hip flexors inhibit gluteal activation; training hip flexor mobility unlocks the posterior chain. See [[exercise-hip-thrust]] for the reciprocal glute loading. [S2, pp.18-19]
4. **Concentric and eccentric loading both matter** — hip flexors work concentrically on acceleration and eccentrically on deceleration (stopping into a jump shot). Under-training the eccentric side is a common strain source.
5. **Female-athlete considerations** — S2 emphasizes elevated ACL-injury risk tied to lower-body imbalance in female athletes; hip flexor balance is one contributing factor. [S2]

## Player Responsibilities
Hip flexors load across all positions; emphasis differs by role:
- **PG**: highest total volume — every possession demands first-step and change-of-direction work. Monitor for cumulative tightness mid-season.
- **SG**: similar to PG, with added baseline-cut volume from coming off screens.
- **SF**: cutting, slashing, transition sprints.
- **PF**: post-seal footwork and rebounding — hip flexors extend and stabilize against contact.
- **C**: post-up drop-steps and up-and-under footwork — loaded under contact; vulnerable to strain if undertrained.

## Variations
### Iliopsoas (psoas major + iliacus)
Primary hip flexor. The psoas major is the only muscle that directly connects the lumbar spine to the femur. Tightness here restricts lumbar extension. [S2]

### Rectus Femoris
The only quadriceps muscle that crosses both the hip and the knee. Named in Motion's hip thrust exercise as a secondary muscle trained [S2, pp.18-19].

### Sartorius
The longest muscle in the body. Runs diagonally from the pelvis to the medial knee. Contributes to hip flexion, hip external rotation, and knee flexion. [S2]

### Tensor Fasciae Latae (TFL)
Lateral hip flexor that connects into the iliotibial band. TFL overuse is a common source of lateral knee pain via IT band friction. [S2]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Flexion-strength only** (leg raises, sit-ups) without mobility work → tight, short, weak muscles prone to strain.
2. **Skipping warm-up of this region** → cold hip flexors on explosive cuts or drives are a frequent soft-tissue strain source.
3. **Passive stretching without eccentric loading** → leaves the tissue vulnerable on deceleration.
4. **Iliopsoas-only training** (leg raises) → neglects the sartorius/TFL lateral-stabilizer role, creating lateral knee risk.
