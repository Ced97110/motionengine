# Soft Landing Mechanics

## Summary
Soft landing mechanics are the joint-stacking and sequenced-absorption patterns players use to dissipate vertical impact after a jump, rebound, or shot. A good landing distributes force across the ankle, knee, and hip in that order, with the trunk upright and the knees tracking over the second and third toes. A hard landing — flat-foot, knees valgus, trunk-forward — transfers load disproportionately into the ACL, patellar tendon, and lumbar spine. Every possession with a vertical component is a training rep for landing mechanics, whether coached or not.

## When to Use
- All jumping actions: shot release, rebound, block, lob catch, pull-up, alley-oop finish
- Closeout decelerations that end in a vertical contest
- Post-play rebound scrums with unexpected jump re-set

## Key Principles
1. Land on the balls of the feet first, then heel — never flat-foot from height.
2. Knees track over the second and third toes. Any valgus collapse (knees caving inward) is a red flag — stop the action.
3. Hips break first, then knees, then ankles — the hip absorbs the primary load.
4. Trunk stays upright. A forward-trunk landing shifts load forward through the patellar tendon.
5. Land with intent to re-jump or react — a "soft but loaded" position, not a collapse.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: same fundamentals; frequency and height vary by role

## Common Mistakes
- Flat-foot landing — impact spikes through the heel into the low back
- Knees collapse inward on landing — ACL and MCL stress; deceleration cue (drill-squat-landing-mechanics) corrects
- Trunk leans forward on landing — quad-dominant load shifts
