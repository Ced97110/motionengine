# Biomechanical Perspective on ACL Rehabilitation in Basketball

## Summary
ACL injuries are among the most common serious knee conditions in basketball. After ACL reconstruction (ACLR), approximately one-third of athletes fail to return to play, and 21–35% suffer a second ACL injury. The core argument of this framework is that movement quality restoration — not just strength recovery — is the critical factor in reducing secondary ACL injury risk. Rehabilitation must progress from isolated neuromuscular training (addressing strength imbalances) to targeted neuromuscular training (functional movement retraining) to sport-specific on-parquet retraining. [S10, pp.723-724]

## When to Use
- All basketball players following ACLR, especially in late-stage rehabilitation
- When planning a targeted neuromuscular training (NMT) program
- When assessing movement quality readiness before on-parquet rehabilitation
- Applicable to movement screening for primary ACL prevention in high-risk players

## Key Principles
1. Movement dysfunction after ACLR is a known, modifiable risk factor for secondary ACL injury [S10, p.723]
2. RTP rates and secondary injury risk can be improved by targeting specific modifiable factors — especially movement quality
3. Basketball players likely require more specialized NMT programs than other sports due to the extreme height, athleticism, and movement demands [S10, p.723]
4. Most rehabilitation programs fail to fully restore movement quality before RTS because of insufficient volume, lower intensities, and lack of sport specificity in final stages [S10, p.723]
5. The goal is to support RTP at lower risk of reinjury — balancing two primary outcomes that exist in tension: return to performance AND prevention of secondary ACL injury
6. **The main goal of a targeted NMT program after ACLR is the reduction of the second ACL injury rate through the modification of biomechanical factors** [S10, p.724]

## ACL Injury Mechanism in Basketball
Based on Krosshaug et al. (39 cases of ACL injuries in basketball players) [S10, p.724-725]:
- Most common mechanism: **landing after a jump** while attacking and in ball possession
- Second common mechanism: **cutting while in ball possession**, often triggered by indirect contact with an opponent
- **72% of injuries were non-contact** in nature — but in many cases player-to-player interaction (indirect contact) played an important role

## Four Neuromuscular Imbalance Patterns (Hewett et al.)
These patterns observed at ACL injury should be addressed in both primary prevention and post-ACLR rehabilitation [S10, p.727]:

| Neuromuscular Imbalance | Observed Movement | NMT Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| **Ligament dominance** (excessive knee adduction, frontal plane) | Valgus appearance on the frontal plane | Train for proper technique; functional strengthening of hip abductors and external rotators; frontal plane feedback |
| **Quadriceps dominance** (low knee flexion at landing, stiff landing) | Early knee flexion during load acceptance | Plyometric training to increase knee flexion at landing; lateral plane feedback; promote soft landing |
| **Leg dominance** (asymmetrical landing, favoring one limb) | All body weight on one limb | Train side-to-side symmetry with quantitative feedback (e.g., vGRF graph) |
| **Trunk dominance** (trunk/pelvic imbalances, inability to control CoM) | Ipsilateral trunk tilt | Advanced core stability and functional core strengthening program |

## Movement Deficits in Basketball Players After ACLR
Four key movement quality deficits to assess [S10, pp.728-731]:

### 1. Dynamic Knee Valgus (Coronal/Transverse Plane)
- Greater dynamic knee valgus during jumping = increased ACL tension and prospective secondary injury risk
- Results from combination of hip/femoral and knee/tibial kinematics
- Treatment: neurophysiological progression, frontal plane biofeedback exercises, corrective hip abductor/external rotator strengthening — minimum 10–12 sessions [S10, pp.728-729]

### 2. Core Control Deficits (Pelvis Drops, Hip Hikes, Trunk Tilt)
- Ipsilateral trunk tilt increases knee abduction moment — a factor in ACL injuries
- Common in single-leg tasks like cutting and lateral shuffles
- Treatment: advanced core stability program progressing from non-weight-bearing to functional/sport-specific exercises — always challenge trunk stability in the frontal plane [S10, p.729]

### 3. Shock Absorption Deficits (Stiff Landing)
- Stiff/loud landings with small knee flexion angles = increased peak vertical ground reaction force (pvGRF)
- Reduced knee flexion angle is directly correlated with increased pvGRF
- Quadriceps strength deficits are often associated with higher pvGRF and increased trunk flexion
- Treatment: address quadriceps strength deficit first; then plyometric program with biofeedback (pvGRF graph) to increase knee flexion and promote soft landings [S10, pp.729-730]

### 4. Internally Rotated Cutting Pattern (Basketball-Specific)
- An "internally rotated cutting pattern" is commonly seen after ACLR in basketball during sidestep/change-of-direction tasks [S10, pp.730-731]
- Features (evaluated via 2D video):
  - Internally rotated foot progression angle
  - Internally rotated hip appearance
  - Dynamic knee valgus (knee falling medially)
  - Contralateral pelvic drop
  - Variable trunk position
  - Generally reduced knee flexion angles
- Associated with high **knee abduction moments (KAM)**
- **Recommended cutting technique** (Dempsey et al.) to reduce KAM:
  1. Close placement of stance foot relative to coronal plane midline of pelvis
  2. Neutral foot alignment
  3. Upright torso posture
  4. Torso facing the direction of travel [S10, p.731]

## Criteria to Enter Targeted NMT Program
Before starting the targeted NMT program, the player must achieve [S10, p.727-728]:
1. Limb Symmetry Index (LSI) >80% on isokinetic test for knee extensor AND flexor strength
2. Good movement quality (no excessive dynamic knee valgus, altered motor strategy, or trunk/pelvis deviations) during single-leg squat at 60° knee flexion
3. Ability to jog on a treadmill for at least 10 minutes at 8 km/h with good running gait

> "It is a clinical mistake to commence this targeted NMT program with a strength deficit greater than 20%." [S10, p.728]

## Motor Re-Learning Approach
- Motor learning follows three stages: cognitive → associative → automatic [S10, p.731]
- Apply feedback techniques (instantaneous and delayed visual feedback on frontal and sagittal plane) to increase athlete awareness of body position
- Progress from **planned movements** → **reactive movements** (reactive movements produce worse knee biomechanics and higher knee loads than planned movements) [S10, p.732]
- Gradually increase decision-making options, neurocognitive challenge ("brain training"), player contact, and number of players

## Common Mistakes
1. Starting NMT program too early (strength deficit >20%) — correction: meet the three entry criteria first
2. Only using time-based criteria for return to sport — correction: apply full criteria battery
3. Ignoring the internally rotated cutting pattern specific to basketball — correction: perform 2D video analysis of change-of-direction tasks
4. Failing to progress to sport-specific movement intensity before return to team — correction: arrive at maximal movement intensity on-parquet before RTS
