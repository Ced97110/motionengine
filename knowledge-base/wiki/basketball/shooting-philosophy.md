# Shooting Philosophy

## Summary
the shooting specialist's shooting philosophy is the mental and habitual foundation upon which all technical shooting skill is built. It holds that **shooters are made, not born** — every great shooter is the product of deliberate practice, patient development, correct mechanics, and an unwavering positive mindset. Technical adjustments are the easy part; the hard part is the mental commitment to embrace change, measure progress, and persist through the long arc of development.

The philosophy is distilled into eight actionable principles that apply equally to players learning to shoot for the first time and to veterans refining an already-high-level game. The common thread is that improvement is always possible — "nobody has the perfect shot, and until you can make every shot, there is room for improvement" [S5, p.10].

## When to Use
- As a framework for designing individual shooting workouts
- When onboarding a new player to a shooting program
- When a player's confidence or consistency is slipping
- When evaluating whether a player's practice habits are producing results
- As a cultural touchstone for a team's offensive development philosophy

## Key Principles
1. **Have the desire to improve.** Will, desire, drive, discipline, and patience are prerequisites. "Perfect practice makes perfect." Embrace change rather than defending poor habits. [S5, p.10]
2. **Chart your shots.** Keep a notebook. Record makes and attempts from every spot. Seeing concrete improvement creates confidence. "Confidence leads to success and success leads to confidence." [S5, p.11]
3. **Set goals.** Specific, measurable targets (e.g., "make 5 in a row before moving to the next spot"; graduate to "25 in a row from the college line") maintain motivation and provide a sense of accomplishment when achieved. [S5, p.12]
4. **Take baby steps toward improvement.** Start close to the basket and work outward. Young players should use lower baskets and smaller balls. "Babies crawl before they walk and walk before they run." Never sacrifice form for range. [S5, p.13]
5. **Don't rush development.** Becoming a great shooter requires hundreds of thousands — possibly millions — of correctly executed shots over years. Learn to use misses as diagnostic data (short? long? left? right?) rather than sources of frustration. [S5, p.14]
6. **Visualize successful shots.** Picture every shot swishing — not just going in, but swishing. "Mediocre shooters try to make shots, but great shooters want to swish shots." Visualization builds the mental template that mechanics follow. [S5, p.15]
7. **Strive for consistency.** Develop a set practice routine with a warm-up starting close to the basket. Work all areas of the court, including weak spots. Mix shot types (layups, midrange, threes, free throws, off the catch, off the bounce). [S5, p.16]
8. **Keep it fun and maintain a positive attitude.** Challenge yourself daily with goals and competitions against yourself. Change reps and time constraints to keep drills fresh. "Make shots, not excuses." [S5, pp.17-18]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Every player at every position benefits equally from this philosophy. Shooting is a universal skill — the object is always the same: put the ball in the basket.

## Variations
### Youth Application
For young players, modify the environment: use lower baskets, smaller balls, and shorten the distance dramatically before worrying about three-point range. Correct muscle memory formed early is far more valuable than early range development with poor form. [S5, p.13]

### Advanced Player Application
For players already at a high level, the philosophy demands raising the standard: the goal is not "making" shots but "swishing" shots. Track makes-in-a-row rather than simple percentages to sharpen focus and demand precision. [S5, p.12]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Heading straight to the three-point line** → Start close to the basket. Master unguarded short shots before extending range. [S5, p.13]
2. **Practicing only comfortable spots** → Identify weak zones from shot charts and spend extra time there. Weaknesses become strengths with targeted work. [S5, p.16]
3. **Abandoning mechanical adjustments too soon** → Adjusted form won't feel right immediately. "New kicks may not feel right at first, but once you break them in, they become comfortable." Allow adequate time for new habits to take hold. [S5, p.10]
4. **Practicing without charting** → Without data, you can't see improvement and you can't identify problems. Always record makes and attempts. [S5, p.11]
5. **Reacting emotionally to misses** → Every great shooter misses thousands of shots. Use misses as diagnostic information, not as reasons for frustration. [S5, p.14]
