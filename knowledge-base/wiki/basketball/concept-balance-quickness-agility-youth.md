# Balance, Quickness, and Agility — Youth Basketball Foundation

## Summary
Proper balance is the physical foundation for all basketball movement — it enables explosive, quick movement that is the top priority for a great one-on-one player. The book distinguishes two types of balance appropriate to different court situations. From this foundation, quickness (playing on the toes) and agility (controlled body movement in any direction) are developed. [S15, p.3]

## When to Use
- **Speed balance**: When running full court, sprinting in transition, where speed is more important than control
- **Control balance**: In one-on-one situations, defensive slides, post play — any situation where precise footwork matters more than raw speed

## Key Principles
1. **Speed balance**: Erect stance, head directly over torso, slight forward lean, slight knee bend, long stride, land on toes
2. **Control balance**: Shorter steps, greater knee bend (no more than 135-degree angle), head over torso, torso slightly bent with straight back (not humped), toes "grab" at the floor, foot lands heel to toe
3. **Quickness** requires playing on the toes until ready to stop — players then land heels first and come up on toes
4. **Concentration and intensity facilitate quickness** — the mental component of quick movement is as important as the physical
5. **Agility** is control of the body under all conditions — players must be able to place their body in any position under control as they advance in competition
6. **Toes grab the floor** — the consistent cue across all balance types; feet should glide, neither dragging (friction slows) nor hopping (vertical energy wastes time) [S15, p.15]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Develop both types of balance as they apply to different game situations; guards prioritize speed balance for transition; post players prioritize control balance for low-post footwork

## Variations
### Defensive Slide Balance
Lower legs perpendicular to floor, upper legs at 135-degree angle, torso straight and perpendicular, hands out palms up, feet gliding just off the floor. [S15, p.11]

### Dribbling Balance
Dribbling uses only the upper finger pads (not the palm). Wrist follows an up-and-down motion as if waving good-bye. Eyes up, not watching the ball. [S15, p.9]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Humped back in control stance** → correction: keep torso straight, bent slightly, back straight not rounded
2. **Raising feet too high in defensive slides** → correction: glide feet just off the floor, toes grab at the surface [S15, p.15]
3. **Dragging feet** → correction: friction slows movement; feet must clear the floor slightly [S15, p.15]
4. **Hopping** → correction: hop wastes vertical energy; stay low and glide
5. **Letting arms drop in defensive slide** → correction: hands out, palms upward at all times [S15, p.11]
6. **Palm-dribbling** → correction: only finger pads contact the ball during dribble, pass, or shot [S15, p.9]
