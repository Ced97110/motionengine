# Five Key Principles of Explosive Training

## Summary
Paul Wade's system for developing explosive power, functional speed, and agility is built on five interlocking principles. Together they define not only *what* to train but *how* to train it. These principles emerge from the argument that modern gym training (isolation work on machines, slow bodybuilding movements) actively conditions athletes to move more slowly, while traditional total-body calisthenics build genuine, integrated athleticism [S9, pp.4-12].

The five principles are: (1) Use Bodyweight, (2) Go Spartan, (3) Apply Total-Body Training, (4) Focus on a Small Group of Exercises, and (5) Be Progressive.

## When to Use
- When designing off-season athletic development programs for basketball players
- When evaluating the quality of a player's strength/conditioning program
- When introducing players to explosive bodyweight training for the first time

## Key Principles

### 1. Use Bodyweight
Bodyweight methods are the superior method for developing all three explosive qualities simultaneously. They are the only approach that develops power (body mass moved quickly), functional speed (whole body in motion), and agility (direction change at velocity) together. External weights and machines, when performed at slow controlled speeds with isolated muscles, de-condition the nervous system from integrated rapid movement [S9, pp.4-5].

### 2. Go Spartan (Minimal Equipment, Train Solo)
The most effective explosive training requires nothing but a floor, a wall, and a horizontal bar for hanging. Modern equipment (foam mats, rubber bands, blocks) creates an illusion of safety while getting in the way of real training. Solo training develops discipline, focus, and self-reliance. No drill in the system requires a spotter or training partner [S9, pp.7-8].
- **Coaching cue**: "Find something to hang from, and you have everything you need."

### 3. Apply Total-Body Training
In sport, you never need just a quick arm or a quick foot — you need a fast *body*. A boxing punch involves legs, waist, torso, shoulders, and arms. Speed in one isolated limb does not transfer to sport performance. The best explosive exercises work as many muscles as possible simultaneously, building *integrated power* — the gestalt property where the body as a whole exceeds the sum of its parts [S9, pp.8-9].
- **Coaching cue**: "You need to work as many muscles as possible to get the biggest bang for your buck."

### 4. Focus on a Small Group of Exercises
Because total-body exercises cover the full system, you don't need many of them. Spreading training across dozens of exercises leads to overkill and loss of focus. Wade recommends six movement families (the Explosive Six) that together cover all explosive bases. Fewer than six misses key speed-power skills; more than six risks diluting focus and reducing mastery [S9, pp.9-10].

### 5. Be Progressive
Explosive movements must be organized into progressions — series of gradually harder variations of the same movement type. Progress is measured not by moving to harder steps but by how deeply the athlete has mastered their current step. **Speed and power are built by working with steps you can do well** — not by racing to harder versions. Moving up a step only *demonstrates* what has already been built [S9, pp.10-12, 18].
- **Coaching cue**: "You become a better athlete by taking the time to practice the progressions you *can* do well."

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using slow isolation exercises expecting transfer to court quickness** → De-conditions the nervous system from rapid integrated movement. Switch to explosive whole-body movements.
2. **Skipping steps to reach harder progressions** → Rushing progressions leads to failure and missed development. Master current steps before advancing.
3. **Adding too many exercises** → More exercises dilutes mastery and focus. Stick to 6 movement families maximum.
4. **Relying on equipment as a safety crutch** → Foam mats and spotters create an illusion of safety. Progressive mastery of the movement itself is the only real safety system.
5. **Training body parts in isolation for speed** → Specialized isolated speed work (e.g., hand-speed drills) has no carryover to whole-body sport movement.
