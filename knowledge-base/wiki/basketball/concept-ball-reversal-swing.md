# Ball Reversal (Swing the Ball)

## Summary
Ball reversal — swinging the ball from one side of the floor to the other — is a core principle of the head coach's attacking offense. When an 80% efficiency shot (a layup) is not available and the defense catches up, the offense must get the ball to the weak side in any way possible: through the post, via pick-and-roll, or by passing up the chain.

When the ball is swung, the defense must rotate and adjust to a new, quickly developing situation. This rotation opens passing lanes, drives, and catch-and-shoot opportunities that did not exist on the original side. The shooting percentage typically goes up on the weak side for exactly this reason. [S7, pp.77-79]

## When to Use
- When no direct layup opportunity develops after 1-2 seconds
- When the ball handler's side is overloaded by defensive help
- When the weak-side defender has overcommitted to trap/help
- After a PnR that does not produce a scoring opportunity

## Key Principles
1. **Do not keep the ball on the same side.** If nothing positive develops, the team must swing the ball — there are no exceptions.
2. **Swing in any possible way.** Post up, PnR, isolation kickout, or simple pass chain — all are valid methods to reverse the ball.
3. **The weak side has higher shooting percentage** because the defense must rotate, creating openings.
4. **Receiver must act immediately.** The player receiving the swing pass must "show and go" — attack before the defense can recover. Passive catches negate the advantage gained by the reversal.
5. **Zipper action.** A specific chain for swinging: 2 cuts around 4, freezes his defender, pops out, receives from 1, passes to 5 who has screened for 3 and popped back; 3 then curls around a double pick set by 1 and 4 to get the ball to the other side.

## Execution
1. Ball is on the right side; no 80% shot available.
2. 1 looks to reverse via: (a) direct pass over the top, (b) post entry to 5 who passes weak-side, or (c) PnR with 5 who pops and swings.
3. On zipper action: 2 cuts around 4 → receives from 1 → passes to popped-back 5 → 3 curls around the double pick by 1 and 4 to receive on the left side.
4. Receiver on the left side catches and immediately attacks: shoot, drive, or swing again if the defense has recovered.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Reversing passively and holding** → The whole point of the reversal is to attack; the receiver must act immediately.
2. **Staying on one side too long** → Allows the defense to set, collapse, and trap effectively; the ball must move before the defense can organize.
3. **Screener hugging the trap after the PnR** → Screener must separate to become a viable swing-pass option on the weak side.
