# The Explosive Six Movement Families

## Summary
The "Explosive Six" are the six movement families at the core of Paul Wade's explosive calisthenics system. Each family trains the entire body and develops all three explosive qualities — power, functional speed, and agility — in an integrated way. Together, they represent the most complete bodyweight athleticism development system available [S9, pp.9-10, 15].

For basketball players, these six families map directly onto on-court demands: vertical jump (jumps), finishing through contact (power pushups), change of direction (kip-ups, rotations), and bar pullover strength for rebounding position (explosive bar work).

## When to Use
- As the structural framework for an off-season athletic development program
- When basketball players need to develop explosiveness without gym equipment
- As a summer conditioning program for players ages 14+
- As a supplement to strength training in-season to maintain explosiveness

## Key Principles
1. Start with jumps and power pushups first — they are conditioning/power movements that prepare joints for the skill-based movements that follow [S9, p.15].
2. Kip-ups, front flips, back flips, and muscle-ups are SKILL exercises — approach them only after achieving at least Step 5 in the jumps and pushup chains [S9, p.15].
3. Each family addresses a unique movement dimension — removing any one leaves an athletic gap [S9, p.9].
4. All six require only a floor, wall, and hanging bar [S9, p.7].
5. Working one family hard adds explosive power to the entire body — all six together produces elite total-body athleticism [S9, p.15].

## The Six Families

### 1. Jumps
**Purpose**: Teach posture, explosive vertical leaping, tucking skills, and safe landing mechanics.
**Basketball application**: Vertical jump for rebounds and shot contests; explosive first step.
**Master Step**: Suicide Jump [S9, p.19].

### 2. Power Pushups
**Purpose**: Condition arms and shoulders for high-velocity movements; develop upper-limb reflexes and horizontal power.
**Basketball application**: Explosive passing, pushing through screens, finishing through contact.
**Master Step**: Superman Pushup (flying Superman) [S9, p.19].

### 3. Kip-Ups
**Purpose**: Build high levels of speed and power in the waist/core; develop basic agility through rapid direction change from the ground to upright.
**Basketball application**: Getting up off the floor quickly; core explosion for layup contact; hip drive for finishing.
**Master Step**: No-Hands Kip-Up [S9, p.19].

### 4. Forward Rotations (Front Flips)
**Purpose**: Teach the body to revolve forward at optimal speed; develop rotational agility and spatial awareness.
**Basketball application**: Body control in traffic; catching yourself after a drive; spatial awareness during air time.
**Master Step**: Front Flip [S9, p.19].

### 5. Backward Rotations (Back Flips)
**Purpose**: Teach the body to revolve backward at optimal speed; develop the highest level of agility (direction change 180°+ in mid-air).
**Basketball application**: Advanced spatial awareness; body control; reactive landing mechanics.
**Master Step**: Back Flip [S9, p.19].

### 6. Explosive Bar Work (Up-and-Over / Muscle-Up)
**Purpose**: Teach the body to pull itself upward and over with maximum power and skill; develop upper-body pulling explosiveness and the "kipping" agility movement.
**Basketball application**: Aggressive rebounding pull-down; tip-dunk; explosive transitions from hang to above the rim.
**Master Step**: Muscle-Up [S9, p.19].

## Learning Order
```
Phase 1 (Conditioning): Jumps → Power Pushups
                           ↓ reach Step 5 in both
Phase 2 (Skill):       Kip-Ups, Forward Rotations, Backward Rotations, Explosive Bar Work
                           (can be trained simultaneously once Phase 1 is established)
```
[S9, p.15]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping Phase 1 and jumping to flips** → Joint injury risk is high without the conditioning base. Achieve Step 5 in jumps and pushups first [S9, p.14].
2. **Trying to learn a Master Step without progression** → The back flip attempted without the backward roll → kick-over → monkey flip → handspring progression chain will fail [S9, p.11].
3. **Treating flips as gymnastic skills, not athletic progressions** → These are progressive calisthenics, not gymnastics. No spotter needed; master each step solo [S9, p.7].
4. **Adding too many additional movements beyond the six** → Dilutes focus and development. Six is the ideal number for total-body coverage without overkill [S9, p.10].

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Master Steps 1-3 in Jumps and Power Pushups chains.
2. **Intermediate**: Steps 4-6 in all six families simultaneously.
3. **Advanced**: Steps 7-10 (Master Steps) in each chain, with Going Beyond super-advanced variations.
