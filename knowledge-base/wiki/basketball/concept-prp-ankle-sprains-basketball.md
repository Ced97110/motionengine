# PRP for Ankle Sprains in Basketball

## Summary
Ankle sprains are the most common injury in basketball across all age groups, genders, and levels of play. Given their frequency and the significant impact on player availability, accelerating recovery from ankle sprains has direct team performance implications. Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injections have been investigated as a tool to enhance ligament healing and shorten return-to-play (RTP) timelines [S10, pp.594-595].

Current evidence distinguishes between **acute lateral ankle sprains** (no demonstrated benefit from PRP) and **high ankle sprains / syndesmosis injuries** (LP-PRP shows promising benefit — shorter RTP time and less residual pain in elite athletes).

## When to Use
- **High ankle sprains (syndesmosis injuries)** in elite athletes where faster RTP is critical: LP-PRP injection to the antero-inferior tibiofibular ligament (AITFL) at initial presentation + repeat injection 7 days later, combined with rehabilitation
- **NOT indicated** for routine acute lateral ankle sprains (Grade I-II) — RCT evidence shows no benefit over placebo

## Key Principles

### Acute Lateral Ankle Sprains
1. **Rowden et al. (RCT, n=37)**: Ultrasound-guided LR-PRP injection vs. normal saline for acute ankle sprains — NO statistical difference in VAS pain or Lower Extremity Functional Scale (LEFS) at day 0, 3, and 8 [S10, p.594].
2. Small RCT (n=21, Grade II lateral ankle sprain): Single PRP injection to ATFL + rigid immobilization showed better pain reduction and functional scores at 8 weeks vs. immobilization alone, but outcomes were SIMILAR at 24 weeks [S10, p.594].
3. **Conclusion**: Current evidence has NOT shown PRP to be efficacious in acute ankle sprains [S10, p.595].

### High Ankle Sprains (Syndesmosis)
4. **Laver et al. (RCT, n=16 elite athletes)**: LP-PRP injection to AITFL at presentation + repeat injection at 7 days, combined with rehabilitation, vs. rehabilitation alone:
   - LP-PRP group returned to play in **40.8 days** vs. **59.6 days** in control group (P < 0.006)
   - Only 1 patient had residual pain in PRP group vs. 5 in control group
   - No significant difference in dynamic ultrasound of external rotation at 6 weeks [S10, p.594].
5. **Samra et al. (n=10 Rugby players, single PRP injection to AITFL)**: Significantly shorter RTP time (p=0.048) vs. historical controls; higher agility (p=0.002) and vertical jump (p=0.001), lower fear avoidance in PRP group [S10, p.595].
6. **LP-PRP is preferred** for high ankle sprains — leukocyte-poor formulation reduces local inflammation.

## Current Evidence Conclusion
- LP-PRP injections **may be beneficial** in the management of high ankle sprains to reduce RTP time and decrease incidence of residual pain in elite athletes [S10, p.595].
- Further high-quality evidence is needed to define the role of PRP and cell-based therapies in both ankle and high-ankle sprains.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using PRP for routine lateral ankle sprains** → current RCT evidence shows no benefit; reserve for high ankle sprains where RTP pressure is high [S10, p.595].
2. **Using LR-PRP instead of LP-PRP** → leukocyte-rich PRP increases inflammation; LP-PRP is the preferred formulation for ankle ligament injuries [S10, p.594].
3. **Injecting without ultrasound guidance** → ultrasound guidance ensures accurate delivery to the AITFL [S10, p.594].
