# Femoroacetabular Impingement Syndrome (FAIS) in Basketball

## Summary
Femoroacetabular impingement syndrome (FAIS) is a motion-related hip disorder defined as a triad of symptoms, clinical signs, and imaging findings — established by the "Warwick Agreement" international consensus [S10, p.318]. It represents the most common diagnosis of intra-articular hip pain in athletes and is particularly prevalent in pivoting sports like basketball. FAIS involves abnormal premature contact between the proximal femur and the acetabulum, leading to labral, cartilage, and soft tissue damage over time.

Two primary bony pathomorphologies drive FAIS: **CAM deformity** (femoral-sided — flattening or convexity at the femoral head-neck junction) and **PINCER deformity** (acetabular-sided — global or focal overcoverage of the femoral head). Both can co-exist as "mixed-type FAIS." Importantly, CAM or PINCER morphology alone without symptoms and clinical signs does not constitute a diagnosis of FAIS, as a substantial portion of the general population harbors these morphological features asymptomatically [S10, pp.318-320].

## When to Use (Clinical Recognition)
- Basketball player with sharp, deep groin pain during hip flexion, internal rotation, or adduction
- Painful clicking suggesting labral involvement or internal snapping hip
- Difficulty performing deep squats, sudden stopping/starting, or cutting movements
- Young athlete with history of high-intensity sport from adolescence (CAM morphology risk factor)
- History of childhood hip disorders (Legg–Calvé–Perthes, SCFE, hip dysplasia) increasing FAIS risk

## Key Principles

### Pathogenesis
1. **Basketball-specific risk**: constant pivoting, flexion, and rotation expose the hip joint to abnormal forces; high-intensity sport around adolescence may cause physeal stress contributing to CAM morphology development [S10, pp.320, 328]
2. **CAM mechanism**: loss of concavity at the anterolateral femoral neck prevents smooth sliding under the acetabulum → labrum lifting from shear stresses → chondrolabral junction disruption [S10, p.319]
3. **Compensatory stress transfer**: when hip ROM demand exceeds capacity, stresses shift to lumbar spine, pubic symphysis, sacroiliac joints, and periarticular musculature — causing secondary injuries [S10, p.320]

### Diagnosis
4. **History**: characterize acute vs. overuse onset, pain provocation (flexion, IR, adduction), clicking/snapping, activity limitations, childhood hip disorders [S10, p.321]
5. **Gait assessment**: winking gait (excessive pelvic rotation), Trendelenburg gait (abductor deficiency), antalgic gait, short leg gait — all may indicate hip pathology [S10, p.321]
6. **FADDIR test** (Flexion-Adduction-Internal Rotation / Anterior Impingement Test): highly sensitive but low specificity — positive in most intra-articular hip problems; a negative test makes intra-articular pathology unlikely [S10, p.323]
7. **FABER test** (Patrick's test — Flexion-Abduction-External Rotation): positive if it reproduces the patient's typical groin symptoms; lower sensitivity and specificity than FADDIR [S10, p.323]
8. **Thomas test**: assesses hip flexor contractures contributing to hip pathology [S10, p.323]
9. **Imaging sequence**: X-ray (AP pelvis + lateral) first → MRI (3.0T preferred) for labral/cartilage → MR arthrogram if high-resolution MRI unavailable → CT for bony morphology/version profiling/3D reconstruction [S10, pp.324-325]
10. **Radiographic thresholds**: CAM = alpha angle >55° on Dunn view; PINCER = lateral center edge angle >39°; hip dysplasia = lateral CE angle <20°; borderline dysplasia 20-25°; Tonnis angle >10-14° = dysplasia [S10, pp.324-325]
11. **Diagnostic injection**: intra-articular injection (ultrasound/fluoroscopy-guided) — pain relief supports FAIS diagnosis [S10, p.325]

### Treatment
12. **Conservative first**: NSAIDs for inflammation; local anesthetic injections (bursal/intra-articular/symphysis pubis); physical therapy focused on pain reduction, dynamic stability, strength, and normalizing joint movement [S10, p.326]
13. **Surgery when conservative management fails**: high-level athletes with intra-articular pathology benefit more from operative management [S10, p.326]
14. **Hip arthroscopy procedures**: labral repair (suture anchors), labral debridement (fraying/small tears), acetabuloplasty (PINCER), CAM osteochondroplasty (femoral head-neck junction reshaping), subspine debridement [S10, p.326]
15. **Capsular closure**: routine complete capsular closure following capsulotomy is important to restore natural hip biomechanics and optimize outcomes [S10, p.326]
16. **Post-op rehabilitation**: progressive isolated hip stabilizer exercises (Copenhagen Adduction, sliding hip, band hip flexion) → functional activities → sport-specific tasks; RTS based on hip muscle strength symmetry and one-leg jump performance [S10, p.327]

### RTS Outcomes
- NBA athletes: 85.7% RTP at average 243 days post-arthroscopy (Stubbs et al.) [S10, p.327]
- 81% RTP in 24 NBA players, mean RTS = 175 days; no difference in games played or sport-specific performance scores [S10, p.327]
- Broader athlete population: only 57% returned to preinjury sport at preinjury level; only 29.6% reported optimal sports performance [S10, p.327]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: report persistent deep groin/hip pain with cutting or pivoting that doesn't resolve; early identification dramatically improves outcomes
- **Coaches and medical staff**: monitor for reduced ROM in deep squat and sudden stops; reassess training loads in adolescent players who may be developing CAM morphology

## Variations

### Labral Tears
A horseshoe-shaped fibrocartilaginous structure attached to the acetabular rim that increases socket depth, surface area, and provides a suction seal. Typically torn anterosuperiorly in association with FAI or dysplasia. Vascularized peripherally; contains nerve fibers causing pain. Traumatic injuries can also occur from superphysiologic hip flexion [S10, pp.318-319].

### Acetabular Dysplasia
Developmental disorder present in ~4% of athletes. Undercoverage of the femoral head increases focal contact pressures → labral hypertrophy → labral/cartilage damage. Lateral CE angle <20° is diagnostic. Untreated dysplasia leads to early hip arthrosis [S10, p.319].

### Peri-Articular / Extra-Articular Impingement
- **AIIS / Subspine impingement**: prominent anterior inferior iliac spine contacts the inferior/medial femoral neck with hip flexion >90°; may result from prior rectus femoris avulsion injuries (ages 13-23 most at risk) [S10, p.320]
- **Ischiofemoral impingement**: pain with hip extension, adduction, and external rotation; reduced distance between ischial tuberosity and lesser trochanter injures the quadratus femoris muscle; manage conservatively initially [S10, p.320]

### Hypermobile Athlete
Excessive hip ROM can pinch the labrum between the femoral head and acetabulum. Beighton hypermobility score ≥5-6/9 suggests joint laxity. High proportion of hypermobile athletes also present with borderline hip dysplasia [S10, p.318].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Diagnosing FAIS from imaging alone** → CAM/PINCER morphology is common in asymptomatic athletes; a positive MRI without matching symptoms and clinical signs does not equal FAIS
2. **Treating all hip/groin pain as adductor strain** → FADDIR and FABER tests plus imaging workup are essential to differentiate intra-articular from musculotendinous sources
3. **Premature return to sport after arthroscopy** → RTS must be criterion-based (strength symmetry, one-leg jump performance, pain-free sport-specific tasks), not just time-based
4. **Ignoring psychological barriers** → Self-efficacy, motivation, and fear of re-injury are important RTS factors and must be addressed during rehabilitation [S10, p.327]
5. **Skipping capsular closure** → Incomplete capsular repair after arthroscopy destabilizes the hip and worsens outcomes
