# Perimeter Getting Open

## Summary
Before a player can execute one-on-one moves, they must first get open to receive the ball. Stan Van Gundy outlines a complete system for perimeter players to create separation from their defender, culminating in a catch in triple-threat position. The foundation is timing, deception, and contact — not just speed. [S7, pp.19-21]

## When to Use
- Any time a perimeter player needs to receive the ball against active, denying, or face-guarding defense
- High-ball-screen situations where the off-ball player must relocate
- Any half-court offensive set requiring perimeter ball movement
- When the defender is in a three-quarter or face-to-face position

## Key Principles
1. **Start the move only when the passer is ready** — begin the cut only when your teammate is in position to deliver the pass. Cuts made too early give the defense time to recover.
2. **Take only 2-3 seconds to get open** — the longer it takes, the longer the defense has to adjust.
3. **Move toward the defender with normal steps** — short steps and eyes on the passer signal your intent; use natural-looking movement.
4. **Make contact (bump) with the inside shoulder and forearm** — without pushing, lean on the defender's chest to momentarily freeze them.
5. **Pop out at a different angle** — never move back on the same line; the separation move must come from a new angle to create recovery time for the defender.
6. **Stay low, knees bent** — be ready to attack immediately upon receiving the ball.
7. **Use the far hand as a target** — when popping out, the hand farthest from the defender signals the passer where to deliver the ball.
8. **Catch with two hands and immediately turn feet toward basket** — land in triple-threat position, ready to play one-on-one and see the entire court.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Primary ball handler; still must know how to get open on DHO, off-ball screens, and relocations.
- **SG**: Most common recipient; V-cuts and L-cuts on the wing and in the mid-range zone.
- **SF**: Gets open in both high and low spots; reverse move from mid-post area is common.

## Variations

### V Cut
The most common off-ball cut, usable at any spot on the floor. Named for the V-shaped path it traces. To receive at a high spot: bring the defender low first, then pop out high. To receive at a low spot: bring the defender high, then pop out low.

**Mechanics (right wing example):**
- *First count*: Take 1-2 steps toward the defender, shoulders square to the basket. Plant the inside foot (nearest baseline) between the defender's legs. Lean on the defender's chest with right forearm and shoulder without pushing. Pivot on the outside foot and make a crossover step with the right leg over the defender's forward foot to freeze them.
- *Second count*: Turn on the ball of the inside foot, push off, stride with the outside foot. Make a two-count stop — left foot lands first (pivot foot), then right foot. Call for the ball with the outside hand. On receiving, front pivot to face the basket in triple-threat position. [S7, p.20]

### L Cut
Used when the player has their back to the baseline, starting near mid-to-low post facing midcourt.

**Mechanics:**
- *First count*: Take 1-2 normal steps upward toward midcourt. Plant the inside foot (foot nearest the lane) near or over the outside foot of the defender. Lean with the inside shoulder and forearm on the defender's chest without pushing.
- *Second count*: Shift weight onto the ball of the inside foot, make a lateral stride and hop, land on the floor with a two-count stop, receive the ball, and front pivot to face the basket in triple-threat position. [S7, p.21]

### Reverse
A backward spin move, used when the V-cut or L-cut is not available because the defender is playing too tight.

**Mechanics:**
- *First count*: Take 1-2 steps toward the defender. Plant the outside foot between the defender's legs. Lean on the defender's chest with the left forearm and shoulder without pushing. Pivot on the outside foot and make a reverse, briefly "sitting" on the defender's forward leg to freeze them.
- *Second count*: Take a step forward to create space, receive the ball, front pivot to face the basket in triple-threat position. [S7, p.20]

### V Cut and Reverse
Combines V cut and reverse when the defender remains tight after the initial V-cut attempt. Execute the V-cut first count, then immediately follow with the reverse when the defender denies the pop-out. [S7, p.20]

### L Cut and Reverse
Same combination logic as V Cut and Reverse, but starting from the L-cut position. [S7, p.21]

### Special Move: Circle Around
Used against a nearly face-to-face defender. Bring the defender low by extending both hands overhead. When near the baseline, swing hands down (arms extended, swimming motion) around the defender's inside hip to freeze them momentarily. Then curl completely around the defender and pop out to the same side to receive the ball. Do not push — go around. [S7, p.21]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Taking short steps and watching the passer** → signals the intent to the defender; use normal steps, watch over the defender
2. **Moving on the same line back and forth** → use different angles on the separation move to increase defender recovery time
3. **Getting open too slowly (more than 2-3 sec)** → gives the defense time to recover and adjust rotations
4. **Not bumping the defender** → fails to freeze the defender; the physical contact is the key to creating separation
5. **Catching without turning feet toward basket** → ball catcher must land in triple-threat position immediately; do not receive, then re-orient
6. **Using the near hand as the target** → the hand farthest from the defender signals the passer and protects the reception; near-hand signals are often deflected
