# Live-Ball Workout System

## Summary
The live-ball workout system is a structured individual skill development framework built around combination offensive moves — stride stops, step-throughs, big hops, hop backs, and hop-back counters. Each move is trained bilaterally (left and right hand) at three volume levels — beginner (80 shots), intermediate (120 shots), and advanced (240 shots) — with free-throw clusters embedded between every set of combination shots.

The system is designed so a player or individual workout coach can run a solo session with clear volume targets, percentage benchmarks, and a shot-tracking table to evaluate progress across multiple training sessions. [S3, pp.101–120]

## When to Use
- Individual pre-practice or post-practice skill development workouts (30–60 minutes)
- Off-season individual player development programs
- In-season maintenance for guards, wings, and high-post forwards
- When a player needs to develop a specific live-ball move as a primary weapon
- As a testing/evaluation framework — percentage benchmarks reveal which moves and which hands need the most work

## Key Principles
1. **Volume progression is mandatory** — begin every new move at 80 shots. Only advance to 120 when FG% consistently reaches 60%. Only advance to 240 when 120 is consistent.
2. **Bilateral training is non-negotiable** — every set is performed going both left (L) and right (R); treat the weaker side as a separate skill requiring deliberate attention.
3. **Embedded free throws are part of the workout** — FTs between sets are not optional rest; they build the routine and composure needed to make pressure free throws late in games. Target 80–85% FT rate.
4. **Percentage benchmarks over feel** — the shot tracking table (Date | FGA | FGM | % | FTA | FTM | %) is the objective record; only data confirms readiness to advance.
5. **One move per workout block** — each numbered workout focuses on one finishing move; mixing multiple moves in a single session dilutes the deliberate practice effect.
6. **Combo = dribble + footwork finish** — the "combination" is always a pairing of a dribble action (1 or 2 dribbles) with a specific footwork termination (stride stop, hop, etc.).

## Move Progression Sequence
The workouts are numbered and sequenced in order of ascending complexity:
1. Stride Stop Shot (#4-6 through #4-6) — foundational; pure pull-up from a balanced landing
2. Stride Stop / Step-Through (#4-7 through #4-9) — adds a secondary read (fake → drive)
3. Big Hop (#4-10 through #4-12) — wider landing for more space creation
4. Hop Back (#4-13 through #4-15) — backward space creation for three-point or midrange
5. Hop Back / Roll Out (#4-16 through #4-18) — adds a counter dribble when the defender follows the hop
6. Hop Back / Roll Out / Crossover (#4-19) — adds a crossover to change direction mid-counter

## Volume Standards
| Volume Level | Combo Reps/Set | Sets | Total Combos | FT Reps/Cluster | FT Clusters | FG Goal | FT Goal |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Beginner | 5 | 8 | 40 | 5 | 8 | 60% | 85% |
| Intermediate | 10 | 8 | 80 | 5 | 8 | 60% | 80% |
| Advanced | 20 | 8 | 160 | 10 | 8 | 60% | 85% |

*Note: "Total Shots" figures in the source include both combo shots and FT attempts summed.*

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping volume levels** → the mechanics of a move change at game speed; 240-shot sessions before mastering 80-shot versions produces bad habits
2. **Ignoring FT tracking** → free throw percentage in workouts is a leading indicator of composure; a player who drops below 75% FT in workouts will struggle under game pressure
3. **Not tracking by date** → the shot tracking table is meaningless unless dated; without dates, trend analysis is impossible
4. **Training the strong hand only** → the entire value of (L) and (R) labeling is forcing equal bilateral training — skipping the weaker side is the single biggest missed opportunity in individual skill development
5. **Shooting from incorrect distances** → stride stop and big hop shots should be midrange (12–18 ft); hop backs should land at or behind the three-point line — practicing at wrong distances creates wrong-distance confidence
