# Individual Shooting Workout Design

## Summary
Effective individual shooting workouts are not random shot collections — they follow a structured architecture that balances volume, intensity, accountability, and variety. The workouts in S3 (Chapter 11, Workouts #11-2 through #11-15) reveal a consistent design philosophy: full-speed effort on every rep, free throws woven between field-goal sets, self-rebounding (no walking), scalable volume tiers, and both make-based and attempt-based goal formats.

Coaches should design individual workouts that challenge players to maintain shooting mechanics under fatigue, address both strengths and weaknesses, and build an honest picture of a player's hot zones and cold zones via shot charting.

## When to Use
- Pre-season and in-season individual skill development sessions
- Before or after practice as a structured shooting workout
- Summer development programs when players train independently
- When a player needs to address a specific shooting deficiency (weakest spots)
- When a shooter needs confidence-building reps at their best spots

## Key Principles
1. **Full speed on every rep** — "Do not walk after rebounds." Conditioning and shooting mechanics must coexist; game shots are taken under fatigue.
2. **Alternate field goals and free throws** — Free throw sets (typically 5) between FG sets replicate the physiological demands of a game where FTs follow exertion.
3. **Make-based workouts build accountability** — Requiring a fixed number of makes (10, 20, or 30) regardless of attempts forces sustained engagement; attempt-based workouts allow passive completion.
4. **Train strengths AND weaknesses** — Favorite spot workouts (Workouts #11-10 to #11-12) build confidence and in-game execution; weakest spot workouts (Workouts #11-13 to #11-15) eliminate cold zones that defenders will exploit.
5. **Escalate volume progressively** — Start with lower volumes (Workout #11-10: 30 total makes) before advancing to higher volumes (Workout #11-12: 90 total makes). Only advance when current level is consistently achieved at benchmark percentages.
6. **Self-pass spinouts simulate game catches** — Throwing the ball to oneself and sprinting to catch it creates a dynamic catch-and-shoot scenario rather than static standing practice.
7. **No shots inside 5 feet** — Spot shot workouts are perimeter training; layup/paint shots are addressed elsewhere.
8. **Track everything** — Record FGA, FGM, and percentage per spot and per session. Over time, the data reveals patterns, verifies improvement, and informs future workout selection.

## Workout Architecture Framework

| Format | Structure | Total Shots | Goal Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spot Shots (50) | 5 spot + 5 FT × 5 rounds | 50 | Attempt-based |
| Spot Shots (100) | 20 spot + 5 FT × 5 rounds | 125 | Attempt-based |
| Follow the Leader | 10 reps + 5 FT × 4 sets, 2–20 players | 60 | Attempt-based |
| Favorite Spots Make 10 | Make 10 at 3 spots | 30+ makes | Make-based |
| Favorite Spots Make 20 | Make 20 at 3 spots | 60+ makes | Make-based |
| Favorite Spots Make 30 | Make 30 at 3 spots | 90+ makes | Make-based |
| Weakest Spots Make 10/20/30 | Same as favorite but at weakest zones | 30/60/90+ makes | Make-based |

## Common Mistakes
1. **Walking between reps** → Require a sprint after every rebound; fatigue is an intentional part of the workout design.
2. **Choosing shots inside 5 feet** → Spot shot workouts are perimeter-focused; enforce the minimum distance rule.
3. **Avoiding weak spots** → Players naturally gravitate toward their best spots; coaches must assign or verify that weak spots are being addressed.
4. **No tracking** → Without shot chart data, players cannot objectively measure improvement or identify patterns.
5. **Jumping to high-volume workouts too soon** → A player who hasn't achieved 60% on 50-shot workouts should not be doing 125-shot workouts.
