# WNBA Player Evaluation: Analytics Application

## Summary
Basketball on Paper extends its offensive rating / defensive rating / floor% / stop% framework to the WNBA, demonstrating that the same analytical tools that reveal player value in the NBA work equally well in women's basketball. The WNBA analysis also provides a powerful case study in how **talent can be misperceived in the early days of a new league** — with heavily promoted players (Lobo) underperforming and under-promoted players (Cooper) being easily the league's best.

The framework's most important WNBA finding: Cynthia Cooper, who was largely overlooked in the league's initial marketing push, was by far the best player in the league's first four years — leading the Comets to four straight championships with an offensive rating of 118-119 and a .961 team winning percentage [S16, pp.312-313].

## Key Findings by Player

### Cynthia Cooper — the Greatest WNBA Player (1997-2000)
- Offensive rating: 118 career average
- Team possessions: 28-30%
- Stop%: 0.50
- Defensive rating: 92-97
- Team W-L: 22.8-0.9 (0.961 winning percentage)
- Like George Mikan, she was the dominant force who enabled her team's dynasty. Led 4 straight championships, lost only 3 playoff games. Was 34 when she played her first WNBA game [S16, pp.312-313].

### Lisa Leslie — Marquee Player Who Delivered
- Career: 0.50 floor%, 103 Ortg, 28% team possessions, 0.70 stop%, 88 Drtg
- Team W-L: 29.5-7.7 (0.792)
- Struggled at first but became a genuine superstar with elite defensive metrics (0.70 stop%, 88 Drtg) [S16, p.317]

### Sheryl Swoopes — Legitimate Marquee Player
- Career: 0.51 floor%, 110 Ortg, 27% team possessions, 0.67 stop%, 86 Drtg
- Team W-L: 24.4-2.8 (0.897)
- Two MVPs, two DPOY, four titles — elite on both ends [S16, p.317]

### Yolanda Griffith — Best Individual Winning Percentage
- Career: 0.58 floor%, 116 Ortg, 24% team possessions, 0.71 stop%, 90 Drtg
- Team W-L: 20.0-2.0 (0.910)
- Transformed Sacramento from 8-22 to 19-13 in her first WNBA season [S16, p.315]

### Teresa Weatherspoon — Defensive Specialist
- Career: 0.46 floor%, 98 Ortg, 16% team possessions, 0.59 stop%, 92 Drtg
- Team W-L: 18.0-10.4 (0.633)
- Dominated early WNBA when turnover rates were 25%; her effectiveness declined as league cleaned up ball handling [S16, p.314]

### Rebecca Lobo — Overpromoted, Underperformed
- Career: 0.44 floor%, 91 Ortg, 22% team possessions, 0.60 stop%, 90 Drtg
- Only a 5.7-4.7 career W-L record despite heavy promotional investment
- Numbers validate the perception that she was misvalued — allocated to NY Liberty more for market reasons than merit [S16, p.314]

### Tamika Catchings — Exceptional Rookie (2002)
- 2002 season: 0.51 floor%, 121 Ortg, 27% team possessions, 0.75 stop%, 91 Drtg
- Team W-L: 7.1-0.5 (0.932)
- Described as the league's next star franchise player [S16, p.317]

## Key Principles
1. **Analytical frameworks are gender-neutral** — the same possession-based rating system that works in the NBA works identically in the WNBA.
2. **Marketing ≠ merit** — early leagues promote players for market reasons (geography, narrative) that may not align with actual performance.
3. **Defensive rating advantage** — the early WNBA had very high turnover rates (~25% per possession vs. NBA's ~16%), making defensive specialists disproportionately valuable in the league's first years.
4. **Stop percentage is critical for bigs** — Leslie (0.70) and Griffith (0.71-0.75) had elite stop percentages explaining much of their teams' defensive dominance.
5. **Turnover rates affect optimal personnel** — as the WNBA's turnover rate declined from 25% toward 21%, the relative importance of perimeter defenders who thrive on steals (Weatherspoon) decreased.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Trusting early league promotions** → The WNBA's initial marketing missed its best player (Cooper) entirely.
2. **Confusing narrative with performance** → Lobo's UConn legacy and New York market placement generated All-WNBA votes that her numbers didn't justify.
3. **Ignoring league-context turnover rates** → A defensive specialist's value is partially a function of how sloppy the league's offenses are; skills that dominate in a turnover-prone environment may not translate as conditions improve.
