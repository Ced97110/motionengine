# Screen Types and Reads

## Summary
the source coach (Motion Offense) provides a comprehensive framework for every major screen type used in pro half-court offense, along with specific read-and-react options for both the screened player and the screener. The guiding principle is that execution is entirely *defender-dependent* — players must read the defense and choose the correct option rather than predetermining their cut.

Screens can be a double-edged sword: properly read, they create open shots and layups; poorly executed or pre-decided, they cause turnovers and disrupt offensive flow. [S7, pp.57-71]

## When to Use
- Any half-court offensive possession where the defense is set
- When transition opportunities are unavailable
- When isolating your best scorer is not the primary option
- When building a team-oriented offense that stresses all five defenders

## Key Principles
1. **Read the defender first** — never predetermine the cut; react to what the defense gives you.
2. **Screened player and screener always move opposite each other** — if one curls, the other flares/pops; if one goes high, the other goes low.
3. **Screener always pops or rolls after contact** — a stationary screener is a wasted player.
4. **Keep the dribble alive** — on on-ball screens, the ball handler maintains his dribble while reading the coverage.
5. **Spacing wins** — three non-screening teammates must spot up to force defensive rotations and open kick-out passing lanes.

## Off-Ball Screen Types and Reads

### Back Screen (Pin Down)
Set by one player to free a cutter moving toward the basket.
- **Defender anticipates cut** → screened player cuts directly to the basket; screener pops out.
- **Defender slides over the top** → screened player goes away first, then cuts into the lane; screener pops out.
- **Defender slides under screen** → screened player cuts high over the screen; screener opens up to the ball in the opposite direction.
- **Defender slides over the screen** → screened player comes out low along the baseline; screener opens up high in opposite direction.
- **Defender anticipates (lob threat)** → screened player stops near the basket for a lob pass; screener opens up opposite. [S7, pp.57-58]

### Cross Screen
Two players at the blocks on both sides of the lane. Same read options as back screen, applied laterally across the paint. [S7, p.57]

### Flex Screen
A back screen set *along the baseline* — the foundational action of the flex offense. Reads and options identical to the back screen, executed horizontally at the baseline. [S7, p.58]

### Corner Pin Down Screen
Set by a player at the midpost position for a teammate in the deep corner. Same reads as the back screen/pin down, run at the corner-to-midpost spot. [S7, p.58]

## On-Ball Screen Types and Reads

### Side Screen
Screener fronts or has back to the sideline:
- **Defender trails** → ball handler drives over the top; screener rolls opposite.
- **Defender slides under** → ball handler pulls up for jump shot; screener rolls opposite.
- **Defender anticipates drive** → ball handler drives baseline; screener rolls opposite.
- **Screener's defender shows late** → ball handler splits between both defenders and drives to basket; screener rolls opposite.
- **Double team** → ball handler takes two dribbles away, passes to rolling screener or to the teammate whose defender left to help.
- **Defender forces baseline** → ball handler chooses: (a) drive middle and pull up, (b) pass to rolling screener (5), or (c) pass to a flash-cutter from the help side to the elbow. [S7, pp.58-60]

### High Screen (Midscreen)
Set in the middle of the court outside the three-second lane:
- **Defender trails** → ball handler drives around screen; screener rolls opposite.
- **Defender slides under** → ball handler pulls up for jump shot; screener rolls to the basket.
- **Defender pushes ball handler outside** → ball handler passes to the rolling screener. [S7, pp.60-61]

### Horns Screen
A high/midscreen with a *second* high screener (V-formation). Ball handler can choose to drive off either side of the free-throw lane. All reads are identical to the high screen. Critically: **if the screener rolls to basket, the non-screener pops out; if the screener pops out, the non-screener rolls to the basket.** [S7, p.61]

## High-Post Rub Cuts (3-Player Actions)

### High-Post Screen
Vertical back screen at the corner of the free-throw area (John Wooden's High-Post offense). Ball handler passes to the wing, then rubs off the high-post screen cutting toward the basket. Screener rolls to basket or pops out afterward. Same reads as the back screen, applied at this court location. [S7, p.61]

### Hawk Screen
Diagonal screen at the free-throw circle — popularized by the head coach with the pro team. Used when the ball is at the wing. Ball handler (1) drives to the wing; high post (5) back-screens for 2; 5 then rolls to the basket in the opposite direction of 1's drive. [S7, p.62]

## Special Screens (4–5 Player Actions)

### Screen-the-Screener
Combines a baseline screen and a down screen. Ball handler (1) crosscourt passes to guard (2), then sets a down screen. 3 comes off 4's baseline screen; 4 pops out off 1's down screen. 2 first looks to pass to 3 (baseline cut or post-up), or to 5 (post feed), or to 4 (popping off down screen), or to 1 (rolling off down screen). Runs as a continuity on both sides. [S7, pp.62-63]

### Double Screens
**Parallel Double Screen**: 4 and 5 shoulder-to-shoulder parallel to the free-throw line.
- **Defender trails** → cutter (2) curls and cuts to basket.
- **Defender cheats over** → 2 flares for a jump shot.
- **5's defender hedges/switches** → 5 slips to basket.
- **4's defender helps on 5's slip** → 1 lobs to 4. [S7, p.63]

**Perpendicular Double Screen**: 4 and 5 perpendicular to the baseline.
- **Defender trails** → 2 curls and cuts to basket.
- **Defender goes over** → 2 fades to receive and shoot. [S7, p.64]

**Stagger Double Screen**: Series of down screens (cross baseline screen + down screen) to free shooters. 3 cross-screens for 2; 2 continues off 5's down screen. Ball handler looks to pass to 2 or to slipping screener 5. [S7, pp.64-65]

**Single/Double Screen**: Shooter starts under the basket. 3 and 5 set a perpendicular double screen on one side; 4 sets a single down screen on the opposite side. Shooter (2) chooses which screen to use, creating multiple options. [S7, p.65]

## Reactions to the Defense (Off-Ball Players)

### Hedge and Over
X4 hedges; X1 goes over. Ball handler options:
- Dribble out away from X4's hedge and pass to rolling screener (4).
- Split between X4 and X1 if X4 delayed the hedge; pass to 4, 5 (if X5 helps), 3, or spotted-up 2.
*Key*: keep the dribble alive while going away from the screen; three teammates spot up.

### Hedge and Under
X4 hedges; X1 (screened defender) goes under (usually defending a non-shooter). Ball handler tries to drive inside the lane to create shooting possibilities.

### Trap and Rotate
X1 and X4 trap the ball handler. Offensive players spread on the half-court. Ball handler dribbles out of trap; screener (4) slips the screen early to receive an outlet pass, causing defensive repositioning and creating inside/perimeter openings.

### Push to the Baseline
Defenders force the ball handler baseline. Screener (4) rolls to the basket; nearest perimeter teammate flash-cuts to the free-throw area. Ball handler passes to roller or flash-cutter. [S7, pp.66-67]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Pre-deciding the cut** → correct by drilling the defender-read before the move.
2. **Screener standing still after contact** → every screener must roll, pop, or slip after the screen.
3. **Ball handler dribbling into the screen too early** → maintain dribble and patience until the screen is set.
4. **Poor spacing by non-screeners** → spot up shooters must spread the floor to stress defensive rotations.
5. **Ignoring the slip** → when the screener's defender over-helps, the screener should slip early for a direct layup pass.
