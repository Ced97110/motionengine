# Pace of Play — NBA Historical Analysis

## Summary
Pace is defined as the number of possessions a team uses per game. A key finding of *Basketball on Paper* is that **efficiency drives pace, not the other way around** — teams do not get better by deliberately playing faster or slower. Rather, an efficient offense creates good shots quickly (using more possessions) and an efficient defense takes away easy shots until the shot clock forces action (using fewer possessions) [S16, p.52].

Historically, the greatest offensive teams played slightly *faster* than average (+0.3 possessions/game) while the greatest defensive teams played slightly *slower* than average (-0.5 possessions/game), but these correlations are weak and not prescriptive [S16, p.50].

## When to Use
- Evaluating whether a team's offensive or defensive strategy should emphasize pace
- Understanding why some coaches consistently produce slow-paced teams
- Interpreting offensive and defensive ratings in context of pace differences
- Game planning: recognizing when a team's pace reflects their personnel rather than a deliberate system

## Key Principles
1. **Efficiency defines pace, not vice versa** — "The efficiency of an offense or defense vaguely defines the pace at which a team plays" [S16, p.52]
2. **Quality offense creates shots quickly** → uses more possessions → faster pace
3. **Quality defense takes away easy shots** → forces shot clock pressure → slower pace
4. **Slow pace may reflect a new coach-team relationship** — many of the slowest teams in history had new coaches in their first or second year [S16, p.52]
5. **Some coaches consistently prefer slower pace** — Doug Collins, Mike Fratello, Hubie Brown appear repeatedly on the slowest-team list [S16, p.52]

*Notable: Many slowest teams had new coaches or were in rebuilding years. The 1991 Detroit Pistons won their title at a very slow pace, suggesting that deliberate slowdown CAN work when the personnel matches the system.*

## Coaching Patterns in Slow-Pace Teams
Coaches appearing multiple times on the slowest-team list [S16, p.52]:
- **Doug Collins** — Chicago Bulls (multiple seasons)
- **Mike Fratello** — Atlanta Hawks, Cleveland Cavaliers
- **Hubie Brown** — New York Knicks
- **Tom Nissalke** — Multiple stops
- **Bill Musselman** — Multiple stops

These coaches may genuinely prefer controlling pace, or their personnel may dictate the style.

## Practical Implications for Coaches
- Do not try to manufacture a slow pace artificially; it will likely reduce offensive efficiency
- Do not try to run a fast pace if your players are not conditioned or skilled enough to convert quickly
- Pace is a *diagnostic* tool: if your team is playing much faster or slower than typical, examine why (personnel mismatch? coaching preference? opponent forcing pace?)
- The best offensive teams create good shots quickly — invest in teaching shot creation, not just shot volume

## Common Mistakes
1. **Setting pace as a goal** → pace is an output; setting "we want 90 possessions" as a goal is putting the cart before the horse
2. **Evaluating offense/defense by points per game without adjusting for pace** → two teams can score the same PPG with wildly different offensive efficiency if their paces differ
3. **Assuming slow = defensive** → some of the slowest teams had poor defenses; slow pace does not guarantee defensive success
