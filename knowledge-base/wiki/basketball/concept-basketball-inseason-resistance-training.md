# In-Season Basketball Resistance Training Periodization

## Summary
In-season resistance training for basketball must be carefully structured around the demands of the competitive schedule. Rather than abandoning the weight room, coaches and strength staff should manage volume, intensity, and exercise selection across two in-season phases — Competitive Phase I and Competitive Phase II — to maintain or even increase strength and power from tip-off through the championship game. Quality always supersedes quantity in this phase [S11, pp.1-3].

The fundamental goal is to complement basketball performance rather than compete with it. Sessions must be scheduled strategically around practices and games, and individualized for athletes who are redshirting, injured, or playing limited minutes.

## When to Use
- Throughout the competitive basketball season (conference play, tournaments, playoffs)
- When transitioning from preseason to competitive play
- When managing workload across a dense game schedule (back-to-backs, travel weeks)
- When preparing for playoffs or tournament competition (competitive phase II peak)

## Key Principles

### Competitive Phase I Principles
1. **Quality over quantity** — reduce total volume vs. preseason while maintaining high intensity [S11, p.1]
2. **Cluster method for volume management** — instead of traditional sets, use intrasets (e.g., 5 intrasets × 2 reps = 10 total reps preseason → reduce to 3 intrasets × 2 reps = 6 total reps in-season) with 20-second rest between intrasets [S11, p.1]
3. **Maintain intensity through load or velocity** — clusters reduce fatigue onset while preserving high-quality movement at sport-specific velocities [S11, p.1]
4. **Total body sessions, minimum 2×/week** — sessions strategically placed around basketball activities [S11, p.2]
5. **Individualize goals** — athletes who are redshirting, not dressing, or returning from injury may continue foundational strength emphasis [S11, p.1]

### Competitive Phase II Principles
1. **Shift emphasis to higher velocity** — accelerative strength and speed-strength become primary focus through explosive, transferrable movement patterns [S11, p.2]
2. **Volume remains same or decreases; target velocity gradually increases** — quality maintained by pursuing higher power and velocity outputs [S11, p.2]
3. **Velocity-based training (VBT)** — load dictated by velocity and proper execution, not percentage of 1RM alone [S11, p.2]
4. **PAP complexes for playoff push** — postactivation potentiation (PAP) complexes: heavy loaded squats or accentuated eccentric drop jumps paired with explosive movements improve CMJ and linear acceleration, especially in stronger athletes [S11, p.2]
5. **Pre-game non-fatiguing workouts** — when recovery is the priority, low-volume power/velocity workouts before games may enhance in-game performance through motor unit excitability and synchronization [S11, p.2]
6. **Maintain muscle mass** — assistance exercises in hypertrophic rep ranges (8-15 reps) to prevent muscle loss [S11, p.3]
7. **Multi-planar, bilateral and unilateral assistance work** — posterior chain, push/pull balance, stability, posture, and ROM [S11, p.3]

### For Younger/Lower Training Age Athletes
- Maintaining strength-speed may be more advantageous than shifting to pure speed-strength [S11, p.2]
- Long-term development goals override short-term peaking needs
- Consistent exposure to resistance training movements accelerates future growth [S11, p.2]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Maintain training a minimum of 1-2× per week during competitive phases
- **Guards (PG, SG)**: Emphasis on speed-strength, push/pull balance, core stability (see Table 12.4 and 12.6) [S11]
- **Bigs (PF, C)**: Similar structure with power movements (split jerk, one-arm push press, landmine squat to rotational press) (see Table 12.5 and 12.7) [S11]

## Variations

### Cluster Sets for Volume Management
Cluster sets allow athletes to accumulate high-quality volume by inserting short intra-set rest intervals (15-30 seconds) between small groups of reps (intrasets of 2). This reduces fatigue and allows sport-specific movement velocities to be maintained. Example: Preseason 5 intrasets × 2 reps → In-season 3 intrasets × 2 reps [S11, p.1].

### PAP (Postactivation Potentiation) Complexes
PAP complexes pair a heavy strength exercise with an explosive movement to exploit the post-activation potentiation effect. Examples:
- Accentuated eccentric load drop jumps → countermovement jump
- Heavy loaded back squat → barbell jump squat [S11, p.2]

Best for college and pro athletes in competitive phase II making a playoff push. Rest periods between the heavy and explosive exercise critically affect the PAP response.

### Velocity-Based Training (VBT)
Load is selected to achieve a target movement velocity rather than a fixed percentage of 1RM. During competitive phase II, target velocity gradually increases across sessions while volume remains flat or decreases [S11, p.2].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Eliminating resistance training in-season** → Leads to strength and power losses that impair late-season performance. Even 1-2× per week total body sessions maintain gains [S11, p.3].
2. **Keeping preseason volume during the season** → Causes accumulated fatigue, overtraining, and poor game performance. Reduce volume while maintaining intensity [S11, p.1].
3. **Using PAP with untrained athletes** → PAP complexes require a strength base to work; more appropriate for college/pro athletes [S11, p.2].
4. **Not individualizing programming** → Athletes playing limited minutes, redshirting, or injured need different emphases than starters [S11, p.1].
5. **Neglecting posterior chain and push/pull balance** → Assistance exercises must include posterior chain work and balanced upper body pushing and pulling [S11, p.3].
