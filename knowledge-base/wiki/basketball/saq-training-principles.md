# SAQ Training Principles

## Summary
Speed, agility, and quickness (SAQ) training is designed to increase an athlete's ability to exert maximal force during high-speed movements. It capitalizes on the **stretch-shortening cycle (SSC)** — the combination of eccentric (muscle-lengthening) and concentric (muscle-shortening) actions — to bridge the gap between traditional resistance training and functional sport performance. Benefits include increased muscular power in multiplanar movements, brain-signal efficiency, kinesthetic spatial awareness, motor skills, and reaction time [S8, pp.1-2].

Critically, SAQ training **supplements** rather than replaces conventional weight training. It is practiced in addition to lifting and requires a solid strength foundation before high-intensity SAQ work is introduced. [S8, p.3]

## When to Use
- **Off-season / pre-season**: build SAQ foundation with high volume, low intensity (2 sessions/week for novices)
- **Pre-competition (1-2 months before season)**: shift to high-intensity SAQ (up to 2 days/week, 30-45 min/session)
- **In-season**: reduce volume, maintain intensity; use lower-intensity SAQ as warm-up or movement education
- **Not used** as a substitute for strength training at any phase

## Key Principles
1. **SSC is the foundation** — every SAQ drill exploits the eccentric pre-stretch to amplify concentric force output ("works like a rubber band") [S8, p.2]
2. **SAQ supplements strength training** — always conducted IN ADDITION to weight room work, not instead of it [S8, p.3]
3. **Build a strength base first** — 6 months to 1 year of general conditioning before high-intensity SAQ; athletes should ideally squat 1.5–2× body weight before advanced shock-type plyometrics [S8, pp.3, 8]
4. **Frequency: 2-3×/week** — novices 2×/week separated by 2-3 rest days; experienced/advanced up to 3×/week [S8, pp.3, 6]
5. **Intensity × Volume inverse relationship** — as intensity increases, volume must decrease; early in program = high volume, low intensity; pre-competition = low volume, high intensity [S8, p.4]
6. **Simple → complex progression** — start with basic techniques and low intensity (40-50% max effort); progress to moderate (50-80%) then high (80-100%) [S8, p.4]
7. **Training age > chronological age** — classify athletes by years of training (novice: beginner; experienced: 1-5 years; advanced: national/international) rather than by age [S8, p.4]
8. **Periodize for peak performance** — use macrocycles (full year), mesocycles (weeks-months), and microcycles (~1 week) to schedule training phases [S8, p.5]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: develop sport-specific SAQ qualities through progressive training
- **PG/SG**: prioritize lateral quickness, first-step acceleration, and reaction time
- **SF/PF**: prioritize multi-directional speed, change-of-direction strength, and balance
- **C**: prioritize explosive vertical power, agility in confined spaces, and contact balance

## Variations
### Low-Intensity SAQ (Novice / Warm-Up)
Use SAQ drills as movement education or warm-up. No prior strength conditioning required. Examples: cone shuffles, basic footwork patterns.

### High-Intensity SAQ (Pre-Competition)
Shock-type movements (depth jumps, reactive drills) requiring 1.5-2× BW squat baseline. Sessions: max 2 days/week, 30-45 min. Emphasize game-speed patterns.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Starting high-intensity SAQ without strength base** → build 6+ months of strength training first; risk of injury otherwise [S8, p.3]
2. **Replacing weight training with SAQ** → SAQ must be practiced IN ADDITION to conventional resistance training [S8, p.3]
3. **Too much volume at high intensity** → reduce volume as intensity rises; these inversely influence each other [S8, p.4]
4. **Classifying by age instead of training age** → a 30-year-old beginner is still a novice; a 16-year-old with 5 years of training is experienced [S8, p.4]
5. **Skipping rest days** → novices need 2-3 days between sessions; eccentric loading causes DOMS that requires adaptation time [S8, p.6]
