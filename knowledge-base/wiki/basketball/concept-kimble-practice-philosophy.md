# Kimble's Practice Philosophy and Teaching Methods

## Summary
Coach John Kimble's practice philosophy centers on the principle that **perfect practice makes perfect** — not mere repetition, but high-intensity, game-realistic, attention-to-detail repetition under competitive pressure. The gymnasium is the classroom, and the coach must be the most enthusiastic, prepared, and demanding person in it. Every drill must justify its place in practice by teaching something specific and simulating game conditions. [S6, pp.14-21]

Kimble's philosophy applies to teaching any basketball skill, technique, offense, or defense. The coach's primary role is teacher: conveying the *what*, demonstrating the *how*, and explaining the *why* for every technique taught. Players who understand why they are performing a task will give more effort and perform at a higher level of intensity.

## When to Use
- Season planning and practice design at any level
- When identifying why players are not executing a skill correctly
- When building a culture of discipline and excellence in a program
- When designing progressive drill progressions for any skill

## Key Principles
1. **"Perfect practice makes perfect"** — The standard is not mere repetition but correctly executed repetitions at game-level intensity. [S6, p.16]
2. **Coaches must teach the what, how, and why** — Players must understand *why* they perform tasks, not just that they are to do them. [S6, p.15]
3. **The gymnasium is the classroom** — Demand attention, concentration, and discipline during teaching moments. Encourage questions at appropriate times. [S6, p.18]
4. **Don't assume skill levels** — "It is better to overteach the fundamentals than to make assumptions and overrate the players' talent and skill levels." [S6, p.18]
5. **Short, frequent drill segments** — Run a drill for 4-5 minutes three times in practice rather than 15 minutes straight. This maintains attention and intensity. [S6, p.19]
6. **Everyone must be active** — Design drills so no player stands idle at the end of a line. Inactivity kills intensity. [S6, p.19]
7. **Progressive difficulty** — All breakdown drills must have gradual levels of difficulty that increase as players improve — "lay a strong foundation before amping up the degree of difficulty." [S6, p.18]
8. **Set realistic performance goals** — Goals in drills should mirror the competitive demands of actual games. [S6, p.19]
9. **Track statistics in practice** — Keep stats on drill performance. Reward winners; apply small penalties for losers. Competition in practice transfers to competition in games. [S6, p.19]
10. **Demand quick transitions between drills** — Wasted time between drills destroys the productive practice mindset. Demand fast rotations from all players and staff. [S6, p.19]
11. **Involve assistant coaches** — Include them in practice planning; let them coach when prepared. [S6, p.19]
12. **Game-realistic high-level drills** — Sophisticated drills must always be performed at game speed with competitive pressure, resulting in winners and losers. [S6, p.19]
13. **Coach energy sets the tone** — "If a coach wants his players to be enthusiastic and energetic, he must be the most enthusiastic and energetic person in the gym." [S6, p.19-20]
14. **Accept only excellence** — Don't accept substandard effort from yourself, staff, or players. [S6, p.20]
15. **Positive correction** — Be critical in a positive, constructive way. Praise hustle and effort openly so players know their work is noticed. [S6, p.20]
16. **Teach the rules** — Teams cannot succeed unless game rules are explicitly taught and learned. [S6, p.20]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Every player must meet the same standards of attention, effort, and fundamental execution. No position is exempt from foundational work.

## Variations
### Expectations Framework
Great coach = great teacher = great student. Coaches must stay current with rules, techniques, and styles of play. Set high expectations for players, staff, and self — people tend to reach only the minimum level they expect of themselves. [S6, p.20]

### Practice Plan as Lesson Plan
"Plan your work, and work your plan." The practice plan is a coach's lesson plan. Maintain flexibility for teaching moments but do not allow the plan to be abandoned haphazardly. [S6, p.18]

## Common Mistakes
1. **"Practice makes perfect" mindset** → Mere repetition without attention to quality ingrains errors. The standard must be *perfect* execution.
2. **Overrating players' fundamentals** → Assuming players know basics they haven't mastered leads to breakdowns under pressure.
3. **Long, stagnant drill segments** → Player focus degrades rapidly in drills lasting more than 5 minutes. Shorter, repeated segments are more effective.
4. **Low-energy coaching** → If the coach is flat, the team will be flat. The coach's energy is the thermostat for the gym.
5. **Skipping the "why"** → Players who don't understand why they do something will not execute with conviction under game pressure.
