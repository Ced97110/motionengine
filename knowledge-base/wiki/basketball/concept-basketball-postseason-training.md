# Basketball Postseason Resistance Training

## Summary
The postseason resistance training phase is the shortest but arguably most critical period of the annual plan. It establishes the athlete's physical starting point, roadmap, and recovery foundation before off-season intensification begins. Despite being easy to overlook, this phase determines how well athletes will handle the demands of GPP and strength training that follow [S11, p.4].

For collegiate athletes, the postseason may begin as early as one week after the last game. For professionals, it can be as short as two weeks after an NBA season that may include 100+ games across eight to nine months, making recovery tactics the primary programming focus [S11, p.1-2].

## When to Use
- Immediately following the final game of the season (1–2 weeks post-season start)
- When athletes show signs of accumulated fatigue, soreness, or reduced movement quality
- When the priority is physical restoration over performance enhancement
- As a bridge between the season and off-season intensification

## Key Principles
1. **Recovery first**: The opening 1–2 weeks prioritize minimizing soreness and fatigue before volume is added [S11, p.1]
2. **Fundamental movement retraining**: Return to basic movement patterns to elicit neuromuscular adaptations and restore range of motion [S11, p.2]
3. **Light loads with full ROM**: Training between 8–12 reps at 60–70% 1RM; set volume kept to 3 sets per exercise [S11, p.3]
4. **Technique over load**: Emphasis on perfect execution through full range of motion; total load is secondary [S11, p.3]
5. **Evaluate and plan**: Use this phase to assess the athlete's prior season performance, developmental status, and current needs to build the off-season program [S11, p.4]
6. **Vibration therapy for DOMS**: High-frequency vibration (120 Hz local, 15 min) applied 24–48 hours post-exercise can restore posture and reduce perceived muscle soreness [S11, p.1]
7. **Eccentric caution**: Eccentric-dominant training is NOT recommended early in the postseason when retraining movement patterns — hip kinematics can be altered after eccentric exercise targeting the quads [S11, p.1]
8. **Strength-endurance focus**: Programs consist primarily of fundamental movement patterns with a strength-endurance emphasis [S11, p.4]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Full participation in recovery-focused sessions; emphasis on movement quality over load
- **PF/C (Bigs)**: Additional shoulder press and stability work; deadbug with stability ball; Bulgarian split squat and walking lunge variations (Tables 9.2, 9.4) [S11, p.6-8]
- **PG/SG/SF (Guards)**: Half-kneeling wood chop, single-leg hip thrust, side lunge, and cable patterns (Tables 9.1, 9.3) [S11, p.5-7]

## Variations

### Collegiate Postseason
May begin 1 week after the last game. First 1–2 weeks = recovery. After acclimation, volume can increase quickly — especially for 3rd- and 4th-year athletes. Motor learning and repatterning are a major focus [S11, p.1].

### Professional Postseason
May last only 2–4 weeks. After a full NBA season (100+ games, 8–9 months), recovery and GPP are interwoven. Professional athletes can initiate more complex movements sooner due to training experience. Can incorporate loaded bodyweight movements (push-ups, inverted rows, squats) with a weighted vest [S11, p.2-3].

### Blood Flow Restriction (BFR) Integration
BFR uses pressure cuffs on proximal portions of limbs to maintain arterial inflow while restricting venous outflow, resulting in metabolic acidosis, intracellular swelling, and increased protein synthesis and growth hormone release. Effective at low loads (20–50% 1RM). Protocol: 2–4 sets to failure, rest 30–60 seconds between sets [S11, p.2-3].
- Heavy-load RT (>65% 1RM) remains superior to BFR for pure strength gains, but BFR is useful for hypertrophy and muscular endurance maintenance at low loads [S11, p.3]
- Applicable in rehabilitation settings (early healing phases) or when mechanical stress must be minimized [S11, p.3]
- Only a qualified clinician should recommend and apply BFR [S11, p.3]

- 1b: Inverted row* — 3×12-15 reps, 1:2:1 tempo, <30s rest
- 1c: Reverse hip extension (mule kick) — 3×8-10 reps, 1:2:1 tempo, 60s rest

- 2b: Push-up** — 2×12-15 reps, 2:2:1 tempo, <30s rest
- 2c: Half-kneeling wood chop, high to low — 2×12-15 reps, 1:2:1 tempo, 60s rest

**Day 2 (Total Body):**
- 1a: One-arm cable chest press* — 3×12-15 reps, 1:2:1 tempo, <30s rest
- 1b: Side lunge** — 3×8-10 reps, 1:2:1 tempo, <30s rest
- 1c: Single-leg hip thrust — 3×12-15 reps, 1:2:1 tempo, 60s rest
- 2a: Straight-arm pulldown — 2×12-15 reps, 1:1:2 tempo, <30s rest
- 2b: Single-leg RDL — 2×12-15 reps, 2:1:1 tempo, <30s rest
- 2c: Side plank (stamina focus) — 2×30-60s, isometric hold, 60s rest

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping the postseason entirely** → The postseason is the planning and evaluation phase for the entire off-season; neglecting it undermines all subsequent training [S11, p.4]
2. **Adding heavy eccentric work too soon** → Eccentric training alters hip kinematics when muscles are already fatigued; save it for the strength microcycle [S11, p.1]
3. **Over-relying on BFR** → BFR does not replace heavy-load RT for strength gains; use it as a supplement at 20–50% 1RM [S11, p.3]
4. **Progressing too fast for younger athletes** → Younger, less developed athletes need longer in the GPP phase; do not rush to strength or power work [S11, p.11]
5. **Excessive conditioning volume** → High conditioning volumes compete with strength adaptations; keep aerobic work moderate during resistance-focused phases [S11, p.10]
