# Postactivation Potentiation (PAP) for Basketball

## Summary
Postactivation potentiation (PAP) is the enhancement of neuromuscular performance that follows a near-maximal or maximal load stimulus. Performing a heavy resistance exercise before an explosive movement creates acute neuromuscular alterations that potentiate subsequent power output. This makes PAP-based methods (complex, contrast, and French contrast training) particularly valuable in the preseason phase when the goal is to maximize sport-specific explosiveness. [S11, Ch.11]

**Physiological mechanisms:**
- Phosphorylation of myosin regulatory light chains
- Increased calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum
- Enhanced recruitment of Type II (fast-twitch) motor units

Example: A near-maximal squat immediately before a countermovement jump improves jump height compared to jumping without the heavy squat stimulus.

## When to Use
- Preseason phase — when converting strength to sport-specific power is the priority
- With athletes who have advanced training experience, high fast-twitch fiber distribution, and lower power-to-strength ratios
- **Not appropriate** for young, inexperienced, or weak athletes — they cannot load heavy enough to elicit PAP and may be injured

## Key Principles
1. **First exercise load: >80% 1RM** — insufficient load will not elicit the PAP response
2. **Second exercise load: 30–50% 1RM** (if loaded) OR bodyweight plyometric
3. **Movements must be biomechanically similar** — squat pairs with jump; bench pairs with chest pass; RDL pairs with broad jump
4. **Recovery between exercises is critical** — too little rest = fatigue (not PAP); too much = potentiation fades
5. **Squat depth matters** — shallower depth produces a greater PAP effect regardless of strength level (Seitz & Haff); isometric contractions have a negative effect

## Recovery Guidelines
| Athlete Type | Recommended Rest | Sets |
|---|---|---|
| Stronger athletes | 5–7 minutes | Single-set maximal training |
| Weaker athletes | >8 minutes | Multiple sets of submaximal training |
| General recommendation | 2–12 minutes (experiment) | Per Gołaś |

## PAP Methods

### Complex Training
All heavy sets completed first, followed by all lighter power/plyometric exercises. PAP from the heavy block potentiates the subsequent explosive block. Better suited to older, more skilled athletes. Less-skilled athletes may not use loads heavy enough to elicit PAP and may experience more fatigue than potentiation.

### Contrast Training
Alternates heavy and light exercises each set:
- Set 1: Back squat (heavy) → Countermovement jump (bodyweight)
- Set 2: Back squat (heavy) → Countermovement jump (bodyweight)
- And so on

Biomechanically similar pairing is essential. Both complex and contrast exploit PAP but can devolve into metabolic conditioning if rest is insufficient.

### French Contrast Method
Four-exercise sequence per set alternating heavy and light loads:
1. **Near-maximal resistance exercise** (e.g., heavy back squat, ~85% 1RM)
2. **Plyometric exercise** (e.g., box jump or drop jump)
3. **Velocity-based resistance exercise** (e.g., barbell jump squat, ~30–50% 1RM)
4. **Reactive plyometric** (short ground contact time, e.g., band-assisted repeated CMJ or hurdle jumps)

**Rest between exercises:** 20 seconds (Hernandez-Preciado protocol)
**Rest between rounds:** Full recovery (5–7+ minutes)

Hernandez-Preciado et al. protocol: isometric partial squat (85% 1RM, 3-s hold) → 3 drop jumps → 3 dynamic squats (50% 1RM) → 3 hurdle jumps (50 cm), 20 s between exercises → significant improvement in CMJ height post-testing.

*Reserved for athletes with advanced experience, high fast-twitch fiber distribution, greater strength capabilities, and lower power-to-strength ratios.* [S11, Ch.11]

## Sample Contrast Pairings
| Heavy Exercise | Explosive Pairing |
|---|---|
| Barbell back squat | Box jump or CMJ |
| Barbell jump squat | Band-assisted repeated CMJ |
| Barbell lunge | Bodyweight split squat jump |
| Barbell bench press | Medicine ball chest pass |
| RDL | Resisted horizontal broad jump |

## Adjusted Application for Less-Experienced Athletes
When time is limited and less-experienced athletes must use supersets:
- Pair primary lower-body exercise (front squat) with plyometric (box jump)
- Add hip mobility exercise as active rest (hip flexor stretch)
- Keep volume low; prioritize technique, mobility, and stability
- Provide adequate recovery despite the active rest format
- Higher training age athletes recover faster and can use shorter rest [S11, Ch.11]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Load too light** → no PAP response; becomes a standard superset without the neuromuscular potentiation effect
2. **Insufficient rest between exercises** → becomes metabolic conditioning, not PAP training
3. **Applying to inexperienced athletes** → they cannot load safely at >80% 1RM; fatigue overwhelms any potentiation
4. **Using isometric contractions as the first exercise** → negative effect on PAP (Seitz & Haff)
5. **Full squat depth for PAP stimulus** → shallower depth is more effective for potentiation
