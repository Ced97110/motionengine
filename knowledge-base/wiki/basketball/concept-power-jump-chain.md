# Power Jump Chain

## Summary
The Power Jump Chain is a 10-step progressive bodyweight jump training system from *Explosive Calisthenics* (S9). Each step systematically builds one or more of the five essential jumping skills — launching, tucking, landing, myotatic rebound, and blocking — before advancing to more demanding movements. The chain requires zero equipment and can be performed in any space.

The chain culminates in the Master Step: the *suicide jump*, where the athlete jumps over a bar or broomstick they are holding. Mastery of the full chain produces elite-level jump height, leg speed, and hip flexibility sufficient for tucks and acrobatic movements.

## When to Use
- As the foundational lower-body power development system for basketball athletes
- In pre-season conditioning when equipment access is limited
- As a progressive jump training program for athletes at any level
- As an alternative or supplement to box-jump-based plyometric training

## Key Principles
1. **Bodyweight only** — True power is strength × speed; adding external load reduces movement speed and kills explosive potential [S9, p.6]
2. **Progressive steps** — Begin at Step 1 regardless of fitness level; each step builds prerequisites for the next
3. **Five skills must all be trained** — Jump height alone is insufficient; athletes must develop launching, tucking, landing, myotatic rebound, and blocking
4. **Myotatic rebound** — Never pause between jumps; allow the elastic tendon rebound to propel the next rep immediately
5. **Symmetry on landing** — Always land with hip-led squat mechanics, absorbing force through the full leg and body, not just the feet
6. **Arms matter** — At Step 3 (vertical leap) and beyond, arms are trained to contribute to explosive momentum

## The 10-Step Chain

| Step | Name | Primary Skill Developed |
|------|------|------------------------|
| 1 | Straight Hop | Landing, ankle elasticity |
| 2 | Squat Jump | Launching (hip/knee/leg power) |
| 3 | Vertical Leap | Arm coordination + launching |
| 4 | Block Jump | Blocking (horizontal→vertical momentum) |
| 5 | Butt-Kick Jump | Hip flexor conditioning, leg speed |
| 6 | Slap Tuck Jump | Tucking (waist-level) |
| 7 | Tuck Jump | Tucking (chest-level) |
| 8 | Catch Tuck Jump | Full tuck mastery |
| 9 | Thread Jump | Neurological preparation, hip flexibility |
| 10 (Master) | Suicide Jump | All five skills at elite level |

## The Five Jumping Skills

### 1. Launching
The ability to squat down and explode upward optimally. Keys: body symmetry, lead with hips (as if sitting down, not bowing), total leg and hip power. Developed through high-volume reps on basic jump steps. [S9, p.7]

### 2. Tucking
Pulling knees up into the chest mid-air. Requires strong hip flexors. Essential for all acrobatic movements — so central that gymnasts call flips "tucks." Progressively developed through Steps 5–8. [S9, pp.7-8]

### 3. Landing
Absorbing force symmetrically through the entire leg and body, not just feet/ankles. Hip-led squat bend on landing. Often neglected by coaches who focus only on the "up" phase. Begin with small jumps to condition soft tissues. [S9, p.8]

### 4. Myotatic Rebound
Tendon elasticity and the myotatic reflex (stretch reflex) amplify the next jump if the athlete does not pause between reps. Landing triggers a tendon elastic rebound + neurological muscle contraction that powers the next jump. "Don't pause in between jumps" is the coaching cue. [S9, p.8]

### 5. Blocking
Transforming horizontal running momentum into vertical jump momentum. Achieved by taking a run-up, then planting both feet together and "punching" down through the floor. Essential prerequisite for front flips and advanced acrobatics. [S9, p.9]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping steps** — Every step builds prerequisites; jumping ahead risks injury and poor mechanics
2. **Pausing between reps** — Kills the myotatic rebound and reduces training stimulus
3. **Landing on heels** — Lands must be caught on the balls of the feet with hip-led squat absorption
4. **Relying exclusively on box jumps** — Box jumps are useful but prevent development of free-standing bounding, tucking, and catching skills [S9, p.10]
5. **Neglecting arm mechanics** — Arms become critical at Step 3 and beyond; ignoring them limits jump height
6. **Using external loads** — Barbell squats and deadlifts build strength but not explosive power; bodyweight is the training medium for power [S9, p.6]
