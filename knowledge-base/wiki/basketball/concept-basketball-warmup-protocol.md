# Basketball Warm-Up Protocol — Arnie Kander (pro team)

## Summary
Arnie Kander, Strength and Conditioning Coach/Physical Therapist for the pro team, presents a comprehensive, fully standing warm-up protocol designed specifically for basketball. The warm-up is considered **part of practice, not a separate entity**, and should set the tone for the entire session. All coaches should participate. No floor stretching is used — floor stretches are not sport-specific, are not progressive, and most athletes cannot perform them biomechanically correctly. [S1, pp.217-226]

The protocol has three phases: (1) a **starting position** foundation, (2) an **18-movement basic routine** performed stationary, and (3) a **13-drill running program** progressing from backward jogs to bounding skips and change-of-direction runs.

## When to Use
- At the start of every practice or game
- As a replacement for traditional floor-stretch warm-ups
- As a standalone conditioning/movement-quality session in the off-season

## Key Principles
1. **Sport-specificity:** Every movement must mirror actions that occur during a basketball game.
2. **Progression:** Move from simple → complex; slow/controlled → up-tempo.
3. **Biomechanical alignment:** Work on posture, core strength, and joint alignment throughout.
4. **Running technique:** Include specific running drills that develop awareness of all game movements.
5. **No floor stretches:** They are non-progressive, non-specific, and lead to conversation and lost focus.
6. **Spacing:** Enough room between athletes so movements can be performed correctly.
7. **Total team activity:** All coaches perform the movement portion alongside players.

## Starting Position
The foundation of every movement in the warm-up [S1, p.218]:
- Feet pointed **directly forward**, weight evenly distributed
- Knees **slightly flexed**
- Pelvis in **neutral position** (not too rounded, not too arched)
- Shoulders **held strong**, not rounded forward
- Eyes **straight ahead**
- Goal: lower center of gravity; develop flexibility, strength, endurance, and body awareness to maintain this alignment throughout the warm-up

## Basic Routine — 6 Repetitions of Each Movement

| # | Movement | Key Coaching Cue |
|---|----------|------------------|
| 1 | Arm swings front to back | Maintain posture; don't let shoulders round or low back arch |
| 2 | Arm reaches (overhead, one arm at a time) | Palm faces you; elbow stays in; stop if palm rotates away |
| 3 | Arm reaches across body | Palm up, trunk rotates; opposite elbow pulls back for clearance |
| 4 | Ankle bends (partial squats) | Back upright; knees forward over toes; heels stay down |
| 5 | Ankle bends with one step forward | Weight evenly split; knees track over toes; avoid caving knee |
| 6 | Leg stride | 3–4 ft stride; raise arms to side ×6, then to front ×6; lengthens hip flexors and calves |
| 7 | Cross leg stretch | Cross one leg behind; reach hands back, sides, front; lean forward with straight back; hold 6 sec each |
| 8 | Standing trunk rotation (elbows flexed) | Rotate 60° each side; eyes forward; abs initiate the movement |
| 9 | Standing trunk rotation (elbows straight) | Hand finishes in front of eye; feet stay flat; eyes forward |
| 10 | Golf rotation | Allow feet to pivot, head to move; slow/controlled; use abs and back |
| 11 | Midlevel rotation | Palms up; rotate past midline; feet pivot |
| 12 | High-level rotation | Arc hand up toward ceiling past midline; opposite hand reaches high on each rotation |
| 13 | Running technique (stationary) | Hand in front of same-side eye; opposite elbow high and back; fingers relaxed; knees rhythmically bend |
| 14 | Marching — knees high | Left hand + left foot forward together; thigh perpendicular to ground; knee and foot in line |
| 15 | Marching — knees mid | Feet go directly up behind buttocks; hamstrings work to produce movement |
| 16 | Marching — feet behind | Resembles reverse leg curl; knees together; chest upright; no side-to-side movement |
| 17 | Marching — cross body | Cross knee across body; upper body stays square; stretches lateral hamstrings, gluteals, hip rotators |
| 18 | Marching — upper and lower cross | Right knee + left arm cross; left knee + right arm cross (reciprocating pattern) |

[S1, pp.218-222]

## Running Preparation (in place, 5 movements)
1. Jog in place — absorb shock through ankles, knees, hips, back
2. Jog front to back — stop with alternating legs, knees over feet, back upright
3. Jog with weight shift (narrow, 2–3 ft) — rectangle pattern, change direction on one leg
4. Jog with weight shift (wide, 4–5 ft) — bent knees, level center of gravity, arms out at shoulder height
5. Carioca — feet never cross (one foot lines directly in front of the other); three hand positions: matching legs, out to sides, overhead [S1, pp.222-224]

## Running Program — Progressive, Full or Three-Quarter Court

| # | Drill | Key Coaching Cue |
|---|-------|------------------|
| 1 | Backward running | Body level; hands past hips then under eyes; activates balance receptors |
| 2 | Forward running | Light jog; eyes level; same arm mechanics |
| 3 | Backpedal S-curve | Look over shoulder in direction of curve |
| 4 | Forward S-curve | Head curves with body; body follows head |
| 5 | Backpedal skip | Primarily ankle movement; loosens hips; begins vertical loading |
| 6 | Forward skip | Knees bend slightly; rhythmic arm swing |
| 7 | Backpedal high-knee skip | Knee to 90°, heel to buttocks; no bounding; core strength required |
| 8 | Forward high-knee skip | Thigh parallel to ground; no bounding |
| 9 | V-cuts | 4–5 diagonal steps; plant outside foot; head/shoulders turn before hips |
| 10 | Bounding skips | 6–8 inches off ground; alternate legs; running or finishing arm motion |
| 11 | Change of direction | Forward to FT line → backpedal to half-court → forward to far FT line → backpedal to baseline |
| 12 | Change of stride length | Jog → longer strides → jog; foot lands under knee, not in front |
| 13 | Change of intensity | Jog → 70% → 80% → 90% speed; calves and hamstrings progressively activated |

[S1, pp.224-226]

## Common Mistakes
1. Using floor stretches → not sport-specific, leads to player disengagement
2. Skipping the starting position check → players develop poor posture habits that carry into practice
3. Letting ankles stiffen without intervention → players compensate by leaning forward from hips, losing balance and strength [S1, p.219]
4. Overstriding in running drills → foot landing in front of the knee brakes momentum and increases injury risk
5. Caving the knee inward during ankle bends → compensation that leads to tendonitis and joint irritation [S1, p.219]
