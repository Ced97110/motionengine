# Basketball Needs Analysis & Competition Modeling

## Summary
Before designing any speed, agility, or quickness training program, coaches must conduct a needs analysis that matches training demands to the actual demands of competition. The starting point for SAQ training is a three-pronged evaluation: the athlete's functional strength, movement mechanics competency, and the metabolic demands of their sport. Goals of any program should be based entirely on the needs of the athlete and the requirements of the specific sport [S8, pp.14-15].

For NCAA Division I men's basketball, research shows a guard averages 34.5 minutes of total playing time, participates in ~8 series per half, each ~2.5 minutes long consisting of 19-22 intermittent work bouts. Average bout duration is 8-9 seconds, with ~47% at high intensity. Recovery intervals are typically 25-40 seconds after 11 bouts and 50 seconds-2 minutes after 19 bouts [S8, pp.14-15].

## When to Use
- At the start of an off-season or pre-season training program design
- When selecting conditioning drills and tests for a specific position
- When evaluating whether a training program matches game demands

## Key Principles
1. **Identify the competition model**: level of competition, scheme/style/system of play, time periods, and personnel considerations
2. **Identify intensity and scope**: high-intensity vs. submaximal bouts, duration, goals, and objectives
3. **Videotape specific competitions** or segments that meet the identified criteria
4. **Evaluate exercise-recovery patterns**: subdivide shifts/series into work bouts and recovery intervals with exact timing
5. **Select training and testing drills** according to workload intensity, duration, and position-specific assignments

## Basketball-Specific Metabolic Profile (NCAA D-I Guards)
- Average playing time per game: 34.5 minutes
- ~8 series per half, each ~2.5 minutes long
- 19-22 intermittent work bouts per series
- Average bout duration: 8-9 seconds
- ~47% of bouts at high intensity; remainder submaximal
- Recovery: ~25-40 sec after 11th bout; 50 sec-2 min after 19th bout
- **Training implication**: several series of 22 shuttle runs (baseline to half-court and back), max 9 sec each, 15 sec jog recovery, plus one extended recovery after the 11th and 19th rep [S8, pp.14-15]

## SAQ Testing Checklist

### Functional Strength
- **Basic Strength**: squat 1.5-2x body weight; single-leg squat body weight; lunges (forward, lateral, backward)
- **Reactive Resources**: compare squat jump vs. drop jumps from 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, 56 cm
- **Strength Deficit**: compare absolute involuntary eccentric strength vs. max voluntary concentric strength
- **Speed-Strength**: backward overhead weight throw; 3, 5, or 10 bounds (double or single leg); 1 or 5 long jumps; power clean; power snatch; vertical jump

### Movement Competencies and Techniques
- **Acceleration**: 30-m dash; 30-m dash (flying start); 40-yd dash; 60-m dash
- **Agility**: 18.3-m (20-yd) shuttle; 3-cone drill
- **Deceleration**: 2nd gear = 3-4 step braking; 3rd gear = 5-6 step braking; 4th gear = 7-8 step braking
- **Movement Competency Assessment**: motor skills; movement patterns
- **Running Technique/Errors**: variables (drive, stride, lift); mechanics (posture, arm action, leg action)

### Metabolic Demands
- Core training/testing drills selected according to competitive workload patterns and position/situation-specific assignments [S8, p.16]

## Common Mistakes
1. Selecting conditioning drills without analyzing actual game demands → always model training to competition patterns
2. Using generic endurance tests not matched to sport's energy system → use interval-type drills that mirror game work-to-rest ratios
3. Ignoring position-specific differences → a guard's demands differ significantly from a center's
