# Transition Defense

## Summary
Transition defense occurs after any change of possession — a miss, a steal, a defensive rebound, or a turnover. Herb Brown places transition defense as one of the highest defensive priorities: the first man back must protect the goal, and all teammates must sprint back and get **level with the ball** before attempting any defensive play. Failure in transition leads to easy layups, the most demoralizing scoring plays in basketball. [S1, p.9]

Brown's key prohibition: **never reach or flail at the ball in transition.** This behavior is, in his view, a characteristic of losing basketball — it creates foul trouble, misses the ball, and leaves defenders out of position. [S1, p.9]

## When to Use
- After every missed shot (by either team)
- After every steal, deflection, or intercepted pass
- After every defensive rebound
- After every offensive turnover

## Key Principles
1. **Protect the goal first** — The first defender back must sprint to the paint and protect the basket before all else. [S1, p.9]
2. **Get level with or below the ball** — All remaining defenders sprint back and get level with (parallel to) the ball before engaging any offensive player. Do not stop running. [S1, p.9]
3. **Locate and stop the ball** — Once level with the ball, the nearest defender picks up the ball-handler and applies pressure to stop or redirect the advance. [S1, p.9]
4. **No reaching or flailing** — Reaching and flailing in transition leads to fouls, missed steals, and out-of-position defense. Stay disciplined; get your body between the ball-handler and the basket. [S1, p.9]
5. **May retreat while looking for deflections** — Retreating defenders can actively look for pass deflections while sprinting back, but must not stop running until level with the ball. [S1, p.9]
6. **Quick mental switch** — Transition defense starts the instant a shot goes up; defenders should not admire made or missed shots. [S1, p.26]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Lead the transition defense call; often the first guard back who redirects and slows the ball-handler.
- **SG**: Sprint back immediately; help get level with the ball; pick up the first open offensive player on the wing.
- **SF**: Sprint back; help prevent wide-open wings from receiving outlet passes; rotate to the basket on a numbers disadvantage.
- **PF**: Box out and outlet; sprint back; if already near the paint, protect the goal position.
- **C**: Outlet the rebound to a guard and sprint back; must often be the goal protector on missed shots they don't rebound.

## Variations
### 2-on-1 Transition
Protect the goal with the one defender; do not commit to the ball until the pass is made; force the ball to one side and call for help.

### 3-on-2 Transition
Two defenders stop the ball at the three-point line; one takes the ball, one protects the basket; first help defender rotates to the open player on the pass.

### Retreat While Deflecting
Retreating defenders angle their bodies toward the ball-handler's passing lanes to tip or deflect outlet passes — without stopping their backward sprint. [S1, p.9]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Reaching and flailing** → Get your body back; use positioning, not lunging. [S1, p.9]
2. **Stopping to watch a shot** → Begin transitioning back as the ball leaves the shooter's hands. [S1, p.26]
3. **First man back taking on the ball** → First man back protects the goal — does not engage the ball unless absolutely necessary. [S1, p.9]
4. **Not getting level with the ball** → Defenders ahead of the ball are useless; sprint until level, then engage. [S1, p.9]
