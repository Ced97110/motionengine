# Principles of Offensive Post Play (an iconic post player)

## Summary
an iconic post player's introduction to the post game goes beyond just moves — he establishes the essential mindset and foundational principles that make a post player successful. Post play is physically demanding, requires aggressiveness, toughness, and persistence, and demands a broad repertoire of skills. The chapter establishes that even perimeter players should learn post skills for versatility and defensive insight. [S7, pp.35-36]

## When to Use
- As the philosophical framework for any player being developed in the post
- When teaching post players to establish and maintain position
- As the foundation before teaching individual post moves

## Key Principles

### 1. Use Both Feet
Post players must maintain balance in a physically demanding environment. 
- Stay low, legs bent, feet wider than shoulder-width apart.
- **Receive the ball with both feet on the floor** — allows either foot to be used as the pivot foot (maximum flexibility).
- Having both feet on the floor enables: taking a position, sliding in any direction, shooting power moves from a stable base.
- A power move jumped from one foot is easily disrupted by the defender. [S7, p.36]

### 2. Make Contact With the Defender
Effective physical contact is imperative in establishing low-post position. When the post player reaches the desired spot:
- Use the body (back, hips, forearm) to make and maintain contact with the defender.
- This contact allows the post player to feel where the defender is at all times — essential for choosing the correct move direction. [S7, p.36]

### 3. Develop Basketball IQ
Post play is not just physical — it requires:
- Developing a keen sense for where missed shots will bounce off the rim.
- Anticipating how opponents will attack or defend.
- Maneuvering to the most advantageous court location as a receiver, screener, passer, or defender.

### 4. Continuous Skill Development
TimDuncan's model: even after winning 4 pro titles and 2 MVP awards, he added a new move every summer — both facing the basket and with his back to it. A post player must commit to adding moves and refining skills each year.

### 5. Learn Post Play as a Universal Skill
the iconic post player recommends that every player — including small forwards and guards — learn to play in the post and master a couple of back-to-basket moves:
- Makes players more versatile and valuable offensively.
- Gives insight into post defense — reading when guarded players enter the lane, helping in the lane area when teammates get beat.

### 6. Post Play Mindset
Post play requires: aggressiveness, toughness, persistence. Size is not as critical as attitude. The post player must be mentally ready to fight for position, take contact inside the lane and on screens, and jump 2-3 times to grab a rebound — all while getting banged around by defenders.

### 7. Physical Conditioning is Non-Negotiable
The essential physical tools: quickness, agility, stamina, strong hands, jumping ability, coordination, sturdy upper and lower body. An out-of-shape post player will be exposed early. [S7, pp.35-36]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PF/C**: These principles are the foundation of the post game and must be internalized before any individual moves are taught.
- **SF**: Should learn at least 2-3 post moves from this foundation for versatility.
- **PG/SG**: Even perimeter players benefit from understanding post fundamentals for defensive insight.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Receiving the ball on one foot** → removes pivot foot flexibility; always land with both feet on the floor on post catches
2. **Failing to make contact** → post player cannot read or control the defender without physical contact; must be established before the ball arrives
3. **One-move post player** → the defender adjusts quickly; commit to expanding the post repertoire every off-season
4. **Neglecting conditioning** → physical conditioning is a prerequisite for effective post play, not optional
