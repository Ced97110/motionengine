# Olympic Lift Principles for Basketball

## Summary
Olympic lifts — the power clean, power snatch, and their variations — are foundational total-body exercises for basketball athletes. They develop power, strength, speed, agility, coordination, and flexibility simultaneously, making them uniquely efficient for a sport that demands all these qualities [S11, p.5]. The hang position (bar starting just above knee height) closely simulates the athletic vertical jump stance and is therefore especially applicable to basketball performance.

Basketball requires coordination of lower and upper body movements across all planes (forward, backward, lateral, rotational, vertical), and total-body Olympic-style exercises train the body in this integrated way. A critical additional principle: because basketball movements are inherently unilateral, one-arm and single-leg variations of these exercises are essential complements to bilateral barbell work [S11, p.10].

## When to Use
- **Off-season and preseason**: primary phase for developing Olympic lift technique and building power base
- **In-season**: maintained at reduced volume; hang variations preferred for accessibility
- **All positions**: every basketball player benefits from explosive total-body power regardless of position

## Key Principles
1. **No spotters for Olympic lifts**: Spotting is dangerous for both athlete and spotter; athletes can drop the weight safely if needed [S11, p.6]
2. **Breathing protocol**: Before each rep, take a big breath expanding the abdominal cavity; exhale at the end of the exertion (explosive) phase; do NOT hold breath throughout the set [S11, p.6]
3. **Hang position = athletic position**: Starting the bar above the knees simulates the pre-jump athletic stance — more directly transferable to court movements [S11, p.5]
4. **Three general cues for all total-body exercises**: "Stay focused, stay balanced, and maintain proper posture" [S11, p.6]
5. **Controlled first pull, explosive second pull**: The initial lift off the floor is deliberate and controlled; the second pull (explosive hip/knee/ankle extension) is maximum effort
6. **Unilateral training is essential**: One-arm and single-leg variations must be included to mimic the unilateral nature of basketball [S11, p.10]
7. **Research support**: Olympic lifts improve power, strength, speed, agility, coordination, and flexibility [S11, p.5]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Yanking the bar from the floor** → control the first pull; only the second pull is explosive
2. **Using a spotter for Olympic lifts** → dangerous; athletes must be coached to drop the weight safely
3. **Holding breath throughout the set** → breathe in before each rep, exhale during the explosive phase
4. **Only doing bilateral barbell work** → must include one-arm and single-leg variations to match basketball's unilateral demands
5. **Starting with the snatch before the clean** → teach the clean first (shorter pull distance, more accessible rack position) before adding overhead complexity
