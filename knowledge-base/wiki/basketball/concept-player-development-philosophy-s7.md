# Player Development Philosophy (Kevin Eastman)

## Summary
Kevin Eastman's player development framework from the pro Coaches Playbook establishes that skill improvement is a deliberate, long-term process requiring shared responsibility between coach and player. The coach must bring daily energy and enthusiasm, study both the game and each individual player, and design drills that address team-specific needs. Players must internalize that their game is never finished and commit to consistent daily work. [S7, pp.299-305]

Eastman's "theory of two" encapsulates the timeline of skill development: a coach takes **two minutes** to explain a fundamental, a player takes **two weeks** of daily practice to become comfortable with it, and **two months** before they can use it effectively in a game. Improvement is a process, not an event.

## When to Use
- When designing individual player development programs in-season or off-season
- When players plateau or lack motivation to keep improving
- When building a practice culture that produces game-ready skills
- When evaluating whether your practice structure generates enough meaningful repetitions

## Key Principles
1. **The Theory of Two**: 2 minutes to explain → 2 weeks to become comfortable → 2 months to own it in a game. Communicate this to every player.
2. **Bullet Basketball**: Give 2–3 teaching points per fundamental — no more. Players retain focused cues better than long lists. Example shooting cues: (a) ready on the catch (knees bent, hands up, feet set); (b) perfect feet — same footwork on every shot; (c) perfect follow-through, held until the ball hits the rim.
3. **Reps are everything**: In an average pick-up game to 7 points, each player gets ~3.2 shots per game (12.8/hour). This is insufficient. Practice must dramatically increase repetition volume.
4. **The three-step improvement process**: Proper conditioning → effective practice → improved skills. Conditioning is the foundation. Eliminate the two "workout killers": fatigue and boredom.
5. **Feet first, ball second**: On all offensive moves — post and perimeter — get the footwork advantage before putting the ball on the floor. "It's a physical hand and forearm fight to get open, but it's a foot fight to score."
6. **Play low to high**: Every player should be down in a stance, knees bent and feet ready upon receiving a pass. Move immediately into the shot with no wasted time.
7. **Basketball is a shoulders game**: Cutters' shoulders should be below screeners' shoulders. Post players' shoulders should be lower than the defender's for leverage. Think "shoulders–hips" when driving past a defender.
8. **Find your feet on all shots**: The quality of footwork determines the quality of the shot. Tell shooters: "Point 10 toes at the rim." Feet should be identical on every shot type.
9. **Chart progress**: Players must see measurable improvement (e.g., 12 made shots Monday → 16 Thursday). Written records maintain accountability.
10. **Have a written workout plan**: Work on three areas every day: conditioning, dribbling/ball handling, and shooting.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Develop weak-hand equally — better opponents will force it. Practice playing through contact with workout partners simulating real game defense. Contest all shots — nearly every catch involves a closing defender.
- **PG/SG/SF (perimeter)**: Perfect footwork on every shot, cut, and screen action. Use a pound dribble (hard, quick dribble pounded to the floor) in all dribbling drills.
- **PF/C (bigs)**: Lower shoulders than defender for post leverage. Feet establish position first, then the ball.

## Creating a Buy-In From Players
5 ways to earn player buy-in:
1. Thoroughly study the game — knowledge earns respect
2. Thoroughly study your system — show players the path to improvement
3. Catch them doing something right — belief inspires effort
4. Bring energy and enthusiasm — players like coaches who sweat with them
5. Tell them the truth — verbally, on video, and with statistics; communicate with their best interests in mind [S7, pp.300-301]

## Workout Musts (Required Training Elements)
- Weak-hand development
- Footwork and balance — balanced on every shot and move
- Playing through contact — use workout partners to simulate real game contact
- Contesting shots — practice defending all shots, not just open-look drills

## Keys to a Quality Player-Development Program
- Practice at game speed (or faster — e.g., two-ball drills, toss-back devices)
- Improvement = conditioning → effective practice → improved skills
- Eliminate fatigue and boredom through variety
- Written daily plan with three daily priorities: conditioning, dribbling, shooting
- Chart all progress — players need to see improvement

## 7 Keys to a Quality Shooting Workout
1. Catch-and-shoot shots (spot-ups)
2. Cut-catch-and-shoot shots (shots off the cut)
3. Shots off the dribble
4. Perfect form during all shooting drills
5. Contest all shots — hand in the face every time
6. Game shots, game spots, game speed
7. Chart makes and misses [S7, p.302]

## Common Mistakes
1. Players take too few shots in practice → tape and count; restructure drill time to maximize reps
2. Teaching too many points at once → limit to 2–3 bullet cues per fundamental
3. Skipping weak-hand work → build it into every drill, both directions
4. Practicing uncontested shots only → always add a hand contest
5. No progress tracking → implement shot charts and make-goal records
