# On-Court Facial Injury Management

## Summary
Due to the absence of facial protection in basketball, eye injuries rank among the highest of all sports. Facial injuries — ocular, dental, and maxillofacial — require accurate on-court triage to determine which patients need urgent specialist referral vs. can safely continue play. A properly supplied medical kit with cotton swabs, irrigating fluids, and dental storage solution is essential [S10, pp.159-160].

## When to Use
- Any blow to the head, face, or neck during basketball play
- Visible facial swelling, laceration, or deformity
- Athlete reports visual disturbance, tooth pain, or jaw pain after contact

## Key Principles

### Ocular Injuries
1. Basketball has among the highest eye injury rates of all sports [S10, p.159]
2. Assessment sequence: (a) review symptoms → (b) test visual acuity one eye at a time → (c) assess pupils with penlight → (d) assess extraocular movements
3. Cotton swabs and eye-irrigating fluids must be available to remove foreign bodies
4. **Open globe suspected**: place a shield over the eye (no pressure) → urgent ophthalmology referral

#### Urgent Ophthalmology Referral Triggers (Table 15.3)
| Symptom | Potential Problem |
|---|---|
| Loss of vision | Various |
| Diplopia | Extraocular muscle entrapment (orbital fracture), cranial nerve injury |
| Photophobia | Anterior chamber inflammation (traumatic iritis, microhyphema) |
| Flashes +/− floaters | Vitreous detachment, retinal tear |
| Visual field defect | Retinal detachment |
| Ocular pain with foreign-body sensation | Corneal abrasion |
| Ocular pain with nausea/vomiting | Increased intraocular pressure |

5. Orbital fractures: not all require urgent referral — triage by symptoms (mild pain → manage conservatively; diplopia, reduced vision, loss of extraocular movement → urgent referral) [S10, p.159]

### Dental and Orofacial Injuries
1. **Enamel-only tooth fracture**: return to play generally appropriate; locate all fragments and store moist
2. **Tooth fracture with pulp (nerve) exposed**: remove from competition, refer for treatment — higher infection risk
3. **Tooth subluxation**: return to play at medical discretion; consider mouthguard
4. **Tooth luxation (displacement)**:
   - *Extrusive luxation*: gently push back into socket on sidelines; splinting by dentist required
   - *Lateral/intrusive luxation*: requires local anesthetic or surgical reduction + splinting by specialist
5. **Dental avulsion (complete displacement)**: 
   - Reimplant within 5 minutes ideally
   - Handle by crown only, rinse in cold water/saline/milk
   - Do NOT replant in a child with deciduous teeth or an unconscious individual
   - After replantation: urgent dentist referral [S10, pp.159-160]

### Maxillofacial Fractures
1. **Maxillary fracture**: can drop posteriorly and occlude the airway — reduce anteriorly to open airway if this occurs [S10, p.160]
2. **Mandibular fracture**: suspected fractures require radiographic imaging and removal from competition

## Common Mistakes
1. Allowing return to play after pulp exposure in a tooth fracture → nerve exposure has high infection risk; player must be referred
2. Handling an avulsed tooth by the root → handle crown only, rinse gently, reimplant or store moist
3. Assuming all orbital fractures require urgent referral → triage by symptoms; mild pain only may not require urgent ophthalmology
4. Missing a concussion disguised as a facial injury → facial lacerations to the eye or ear bleeding should increase concussion suspicion [S10, p.158]
5. Not having irrigation fluid or cotton swabs courtside → foreign body removal is a common need in basketball ocular injuries
