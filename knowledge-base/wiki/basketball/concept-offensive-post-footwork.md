# Offensive Post Player Footwork: Getting Open and Scoring Inside

## Summary
Post player footwork is the foundation of interior offense. A post player cannot score until he receives the ball, and he cannot receive the ball until he gets open. Sound footwork governs every phase: getting open, receiving the pass, and executing post moves. As Coach Pete Newell said, "Footwork and balance are necessary every moment of a game." [S6, p.171]

Going inside produces high-percentage shots, free throw opportunities, foul trouble for defenders, and sets up the outside game. Without a viable interior threat, perimeter players become easier to guard. The art of pivoting is the central skill that unlocks all of this. [S6, pp.171-172]

## When to Use
- Any time a post player is establishing position on the low block or high post
- Against any type of post defense (fronting, three-quartering, side denial, physical play)
- When receiving a duck-in pass or flash cut entry
- During ball reversals and perimeter swings that shift the defensive angle

## Key Principles

### Getting Open
1. **Beat your man down the floor** — the simplest way to get good post position is to sprint to the spot just above the low block before the defender arrives. [S6, p.173]
2. **Establish at the first notch above the block** — never start below the block; the notch above gives the best angles for drop steps in either direction. [S6, p.173]
3. **The battle of the feet** — the offensive post player wins by positioning feet and body to create a passing window before the defender can react. "You can't win the war unless you win the battle." [S6, p.173]
4. **Read how you are being defended** — V-cut direction, rip vs. swim, all depend on where and how tightly the defender is playing. [S6, pp.174-177]

### V-Cut to Flash: Reading the Defender
5. **"If they start low, you start lower"** (never below the block) → take defender lower, then V-cut high with a front crossover pivot. [S6, p.176]
6. **"If they start high, you start higher"** → take defender higher, then V-cut low with a front crossover pivot. [S6, p.176]
7. **"If they play you low, take them lower and scrape cut high"** — rip the contact arm across the defender's chest as the crossover is made, giving no pursuit angle. [S6, pp.174-175]
8. **"If they play you high, take them higher and scrape cut low"** — rip the opposite arm past the defender's baseline shoulder as the low cut is made. [S6, p.175]

## Player Responsibilities

### V-Cut Mechanics (Rip Move)
- **Post player facing away from basket** with vision of teammates, defender, and ball location.
- **Fake low, cut high** (when defender plays low):
  1. Plant inside (left) foot in the lane
  2. Step with right foot
  3. Front crossover pivot with left foot across the lane
  4. Rip the left (contact) arm across the defender's chest
  5. Flash to the ball on the opposite side [S6, pp.174-175]
- **Fake high, cut low** (when defender plays high):
  1. First step with top (right) foot, higher
  2. Second step with lower (left) foot
  3. Front crossover step with top (right) foot
  4. Rip right arm past the defender's baseline shoulder
  5. Flash low across lane, post up at notch above block [S6, p.175]

## Variations

### Swim Move (vs. Physical Denial)
When the defender physically jams the flash cut (hugging the post player tightly), there is no space to rip the arm through. The post player instead **swings the contact arm OVER** the defender — a "swim" motion — combined with the same front crossover step footwork. [S6, pp.177-178]

**Teaching phrases:**
- "If they jam you low, take them lower and swim high."
- "If they jam you high, take them higher and swim low."
- "Get into the defender's body before you make your swim move." (Getting close reduces the defender's reaction time.) [S6, pp.177-178]

**Mechanics of Swim Move:**
1. V-cut toward the defender to close distance
2. Make the front crossover step
3. Simultaneously swing the contact arm **over** the defender's shoulder/head rather than through the chest
4. Offensive player ends up on the high side of the defender, closer to the ball than the defender [S6, pp.178-179]

> Note: The swim move is equally effective on the perimeter for wing players facing aggressive denial defense. [S6, p.179]

### Contact and Reverse Pivot Move (vs. Duck-In Denial)
Used when a defender physically jams a duck-in cut by playing on the high side of the post player. [S6, pp.179-180]

**Mechanics:**
1. Step the **contact foot** (baseline/lower foot — right foot) between the two feet of the defender
2. Quickly make a **strong reverse pivot** — swing the left foot toward the baseline while holding the left arm and elbow up as an arm bar
3. End up on the **low (baseline) side** of the defender
4. Seal the defender with the high foot (left) and arm/elbow
5. Sit down on the defender's low leg (right leg in this scenario)
6. Stay at or above the **first notch above the block** for the best drop-step angle in either direction [S6, pp.179-180]

### Spin Screen Move (Two-Player Action)
The most difficult move to defend. Requires a teammate to help. [S6, p.180]

**Setup:** Original ballside post player (05) and a weakside player (02) as the spin screener.
- When the ball is reversed to the top of the key, the weakside player (02) sets an **interior back screen** on the defender of 05
- The screen is called a "spin screen" — essentially a back screen set inside the lane for the post player
- A smaller player as the screener can be advantageous — more difficult for the bigger defender to navigate around
- 05 uses the screen to receive the ball in the lane for maximum scoring opportunities [S6, p.180]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Flashing without a V-cut** → always set up the cut with a misdirection step first; blind flashes are easily denied by good defenders.
2. **Trying to rip when the defender is too tight** → when a defender is hugging, switch to the swim move — there is no space for a rip.
3. **Contact foot placed outside the defender's feet** → the contact foot must go **between** the defender's feet for the reverse pivot seal to work.
4. **Posting below the block** → always establish at the notch above the block — below the block leaves no angle for drop steps.
5. **Not reading defensive positioning first** → the move selected (rip low/high, swim low/high, contact reverse pivot) depends on how the defender is playing; reading first is mandatory.
6. **Not maintaining the arm bar on the seal** → after the reverse pivot, the arm bar and seal must be maintained actively; dropping the arm bar lets the defender recover position.
