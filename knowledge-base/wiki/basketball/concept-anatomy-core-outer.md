# Outer Core

## Summary
The outer core is the muscular layer responsible for producing and resisting spinal movement across flexion, extension, rotation, and lateral flexion. Basketball Anatomy defines it verbatim: "Outer core = movement production — the rectus abdominis, erector spinae (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis), and internal/external obliques produce and resist spinal movement in multiple planes" [S2, p.32].

While the inner core (transversus abdominis, multifidus, pelvic floor, diaphragm — see [[concept-core-anatomy-basketball]]) stabilizes the spine, the outer core moves it and resists unwanted movement under contact. Every contact possession (rebounding, post-seal, boxing out) taxes the outer core against lateral and rotational force. Every explosive action (jump shot on catch, drive start, sprint stride) transmits lower-body force through the outer core to the upper body. A weak outer core leaks power at every action. [S2]

## When to Use
This anatomy matters to coaching decisions involving:
- Rebounding and boxing out — anti-rotation and anti-lateral-flexion resistance under contact
- Post-play sealing — trunk rigidity against post-defender leverage
- Deceleration and jump-stop — eccentric core load to arrest momentum
- Rotation-based actions — dribble drives, pivots, passes
- Landing mechanics — the outer core stabilizes trunk over the lower-body landing platform

## Key Principles
1. **Outer core = movement production** — rectus abdominis, erector spinae, and obliques generate and resist spinal motion in all planes [S2, p.32].
2. **Anterior chain connection** — per `concept-core-anatomy-basketball.md:27`: "rectus abdominis + hip flexors = anterior chain" [S2, p.32]. Outer-core training pairs with hip flexor work. See [[concept-anatomy-hip-flexor-complex]].
3. **Train all planes** — per the same source: "choose exercises from each movement category (flexion, extension, rotation, lateral flexion) for balanced strength and injury prevention" [S2, p.32].
4. **Posterior chain connection** — per `concept-core-anatomy-basketball.md:27`: "erector spinae + hamstrings + glutes = posterior chain" [S2, p.32]. The outer core's back-side half anchors the posterior chain. See [[concept-anatomy-glute-max]].
5. **Anti-rotation is as important as rotation** — resisting unwanted trunk rotation under contact is often the real coaching outcome. Pallof-press-pattern drills train this directly.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG / SG / SF**: rotation-heavy loading from cuts, drives, and pivots; anti-rotation under ball-pressure
- **PF / C**: highest contact load; anti-lateral-flexion and anti-rotation during post seal and rebounding

## Variations
### Rectus Abdominis
Primary flexor of the lumbar spine; anchor of the anterior chain. [S2, p.32]

### Obliques (internal + external)
Drive trunk rotation and resist lateral flexion. Central to change-of-direction and rotational power. [S2, p.32]

### Erector Spinae (superficial — iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis)
Extends and supports the spine; counterbalances rectus-abdominis dominance; essential for upright posture under load. [S2, p.32]

## Common Mistakes
1. **"Mirror muscle" training only** — rectus-only work neglects rotation, lateral flexion, and extension planes.
2. **Momentum-driven rotation** — swinging exercises look explosive but skip the control the game demands. Per `concept-core-anatomy-basketball.md`: "Using momentum or jerky motion in rotation exercises → Control is paramount".
3. **Neglecting anti-extension** — plank variants, Pallof-press, and bird-dog work the anti-movement side of outer-core training.
4. **Isolating outer from inner core** — without inner-core activation ("draw in the abdominals") first, outer-core exercises leak stability. [S2, p.34]
