# Hustle Board

## Summary
The Hustle Board is a supplementary motivational and recognition tool used alongside the [[concept-performance-rating-system]] (PRS). While the PRS captures comprehensive statistical production, the Hustle Board specifically recognizes "extra effort" plays that reflect willingness to sacrifice — taking charges, diving for loose balls, setting effective screens, fronting the post, and going on the floor. Coaches recognize qualifying players with a mention in practice and a symbolic star/emblem placed on the front of their lockers [S13, pp.61-63].

Three variations of the Hustle Board are described: two individual boards tracking specific hustle plays and one combined individual+team board used by a Division I power conference program.

## When to Use
- Throughout the season as a daily or per-game motivational tool
- When the team needs to improve effort-based, non-statistical contributions (screens, charges, deflections)
- When coaches want to celebrate "blue collar" players who don't score much but make winning plays
- As a complement to the PRS — the Hustle Board does NOT replace the PRS

## Key Principles
1. **Any player can qualify** — The categories are effort-based, not talent-based; every player has a fair chance regardless of skill.
2. **Public recognition matters** — The emblem on the locker publicly honors the player's sacrifice and raises the status of blue-collar play.
3. **Coaches and players design the board together** — Categories should reflect what the team most needs to improve.
4. **A basketball-savvy person must keep the stats** — An assistant coach or experienced volunteer should track Hustle Board stats, not a casual observer.
5. **Team Hustle Board depends on collective achievement** — Every player gets an award only when the whole team meets all goals, building collective accountability [S13, pp.62-63].

## The Three Hustle Board Variations

### Hustle Board #1 — Six Objective Individual Categories
| Description | Goals to Qualify |
|---|---|
| Take | 2 offensive charges |
| Retrieve | 2 offensive rebounds |
| Deflect | 2 passes |
| Dig out | 2 steals |
| Block shot | 1 shot |
| Tip out | 1 missed shot or free throw |

All six categories are fully objective and easily verified [S13, p.62].

### Hustle Board #2 — Six Team-Impact Categories
| Description | Goals to Qualify |
|---|---|
| On the floor | 2 charges or dives for loose balls |
| Forced turnover | 2 in which team retrieves ball |
| Screen | 2 that free up a player |
| Front post | 1 denied entry pass |
| Hustle play | 1 sprint back on defense |
| Give foul | 1 on layup to prevent goal |

This board adds screening (often untracked) and post fronting [S13, p.62].

### Hustle Board #3 — Combined Individual + Team Board (Division I Model)
**Individual Hustle Board:**

| Offense | Defense |
|---|---|
| 80% free throws (min. 5 att.) | 5 defensive boards |
| 4 assists | 2 steals |
| 2 offensive rebounds + 1 put-back | 1 charge |
| 50% field goals | Go on floor 1 time |
| No more than 2 turnovers | Block 1 shot |

**Team Hustle Board:**

| Rules | Goals |
|---|---|
| All team categories depend on defensive work | Hold opponent to predetermined FG% |
| Each player gets award when all 5 goals met | Outrebound opponent |
| Subs count same as starters | More steals than opponent |
| Builds team awareness and support | More blocked shots than opponent |
| | More charges than opponent |

The coaching staff sets the opponent-specific defensive FG% target. The team board awards EVERY player when all goals are collectively met, including bench players whose contributions count equally [S13, pp.62-63].

## Coaching Points
- "Performing these actions is difficult, and [players] respect those who make such plays" [S13, p.61]
- Special recognition should always be given publicly — a mention in practice plus the locker emblem
- The Hustle Board is analogous to a football helmet emblem for special accomplishments
- The two-person or team hustle board creates collective accountability that individual recognition alone cannot

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using the Hustle Board instead of the PRS** → The Hustle Board is a supplement only; it does not provide the comprehensive analysis of the full PRS.
2. **Assigning an unqualified person to track stats** → An assistant coach or experienced volunteer must keep these stats; casual observers will miss plays.
3. **Setting goals that are too easy or too hard** → Work with players and opponents to set realistic but challenging thresholds.
