# Defensive Unit Coordination

## Summary
Team defense extends beyond individual defensive techniques — it requires organized coordination in four critical areas: player input, clock management, foul strategy, and end-of-game decisions. Productive results are more likely when coaches and players are on the same page about all four. These are not improvised game-time decisions; they must be taught, practiced, and agreed upon before competition [S13, p.218].

## When to Use
- As an ongoing framework embedded in every practice and game plan
- In all late-game situations (last 2 minutes), time-outs, and foul situations
- When preparing for opponents whose tactical patterns require specific team-level adjustments

## Key Principles

### 1. Player Input
- **The power of player input can be enormous** — systems that encourage player participation in team policies produce greater buy-in and rule adherence.
- Players can provide input: during practice the day before the game, at shoot-arounds, during walk-throughs, after pregame warm-ups, during time-outs (via questions/comments to assistants before the head coach addresses the huddle).
- In pro situations, a player might say "Let me take him this time" or "I've got him" and the changeover works — this is legitimate last-second player input.
- **For last shots to win or tie**: coaches may specifically ask a player which side of the court he wants the ball — this input is "invaluable" [S13, p.218].
- Classic example: UNCC vs San Francisco, 1976 NIT — asking "Who wants this shot?" led to the right player taking the ball and tying the game [S13, p.219].

### 2. Clock Management
- Teams must practice situations where they are 1, 2, and 3 points **ahead or behind**.
- **Trailing team**: Must get an open shot efficiently with minimal clock wasted; must avoid all contact if opponent has fouls to give.
- **Leading team**: Must foul intentionally in the backcourt (if fouls are available) to use clock; must prevent opponent from getting untouched to the three-point line.
- **Classic failure example**: Kentucky vs Indiana (December 10, 2011) — Kentucky had two fouls to give with 5.6 seconds left and a 2-point lead; they failed to foul, and Indiana scored a game-winning three-pointer untouched. The only way to prepare for this is through practice [S13, p.219].

### 3. Foul Strategy
- **Never foul a jump shooter** — risk of a 4- or 6-point play if the shooter misses the free throw and the team captures the rebound to hit another three-pointer.
- Minimize fouls and keep teams **out of the bonus** — all free throws matter, not just end-game ones.
- **Fouling for profit**: Taking away a driving layup and making an opponent hit two 15-foot shots is sometimes the right play.
- **Keep the ball in the hands of the best free-throw shooters** — substitute to get them into the game in late situations.
- **NBA coaches** preferred man-to-man with a 3-point lead and less than 10 seconds — rule of thumb is **not to foul**.
- **College coaches** (with fewer accomplished three-point shooters) were more prone to trap and zone in these situations [S13, p.220].
- **Execution failure example**: Florida held a 3-point lead with 6 seconds left; they applied token pressure on the inbound, allowed the guard to catch in full stride, and gave up an untouched three-pointer. Hard pressure that permits a quick ball handler to get an open look is worse than soft pressure [S13, pp.220-221].

### 4. End-of-Game Decisions — Two Theories
- **Theory 1**: Do not let the opponent's star player beat you; make him give up the ball.
- **Theory 2**: Play your opponent tight and make their star hit the shot.

**NBA Finals Situation 1 (2011 Dallas vs. Miami, Game 2)**:
- Score tied 93-93, 24.5 seconds left; Nowitzki received a pass off a pin-down screen, made a hesitation move past Bosh, and drove for the game-winning layup with no defensive rotation helping.
- Five lessons: (1) Double-teaming was an option. (2) Best double-team moment = on the spin move. (3) Fouling on the spin was available — Dallas was not in the penalty. (4) Everyone should have known Bosh needed help [S13, pp.221].

**NBA Playoff Situation 2 (author as assistant coach)**:
- Down 2 points, 26 seconds left; opponent had ball sideline OOB in frontcourt. Options included: big man on the inbounder, trap the first pass receiver, 1-3-1 trap, or run-and-jump. Instead, played straight man-to-man and let the clock run. Sam Cassell dribbled untouched, shot at the shot clock buzzer, and the ball went out of bounds — lucky escape.
- **Lesson**: Always discuss defensive options with assistants before these situations. Only practice prepares a team [S13, p.222].

## Key Practice Requirements
- Practice specific plays for when 1, 2, and 3 points down with 26, 10, 5, and 3 seconds left.
- Practice specific plays for when 1, 2, and 3 points up — both offense (running out the clock) and defense (executing the foul strategy and containment).
- Coaches and players must be on the same page on ALL four components before the game begins.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not practicing end-game situations** → Players freeze or make wrong decisions without rehearsal.
2. **Token pressure on inbounds with a 3-point lead** → Either apply real pressure (forcing the inbounder to pump-fake, etc.) or be in a clear no-foul-intentional scheme — halfhearted is worst.
3. **Failing to foul when fouls are available** → If the clock favors the trailing team, use available fouls; not doing so is a tactical error.
4. **No discussion of defensive options before the possession** → As the assistant coach example shows, not covering all options can lead to a disastrous default decision.
