# the source coach's Offensive Principles

## Summary
the source coach distilled the Motion Offense's half-court offensive philosophy into five team principles required to make screen-based offense work at the pro level. These principles address not just X-and-O execution but the mindset, fitness, and communication required to make complex screening actions succeed. [S7, pp.67-68]

The team relied on a half-court system rather than elite one-on-one players, making team execution and read-and-react discipline the foundation of their success.

## When to Use
- These are *foundational principles*, applicable to any half-court offensive system
- Especially important when building a team that emphasizes screening, cutting, and ball movement over isolation

## Key Principles

### 1. Read and React (Never Pre-Decide)
- Players must **play against their man** — reacting to the defender actually in front of them.
- The most common error is deciding in advance what cut or shot to take before reading the defense.
- *Coaching cue*: "Play what you see, not what you planned."

### 2. Physical Conditioning
- Basketball is hard work. The player in great shape will prevail, especially when fatigue sets in late in games.
- Every team element — screening timing, cut precision, spacing — degrades when players are tired.

### 3. Team Play
- Make the **extra pass** to a teammate in better position.
- Be willing to **set a pick** to take pressure off a teammate.
- These are the actions that make a team gel — not individual scoring.

### 4. Passing Abilities
Four categories of passing skill:
- **Post entry passing** — how, when, and where to enter the ball to the center.
- **Perimeter passing** — moving the ball quickly and sure-handedly around the arc to make the defense work.
- **Post passing** — the center finding cutters and open teammates from his vantage point; a skilled post passer creates more options than almost any other action.

### 5. Court Balance and Spacing
- **Court balance**: proper distribution of all five players so the offense can dictate action and exploit defensive weaknesses.
- Poor court balance = players clustered, spread too thin, or easy to guard.
- Proper spacing keeps all five defenders occupied and forces rotations on every screen action.

## Teaching Progression for Screens
the source coach's recommended practice progression:
1. **No defenders** — run all the screen options and reads at full speed without resistance.
2. **50% defenders** — add defense but at half intensity, allowing players to feel the reads without pressure.
3. **Game-speed defenders** — full defensive aggressiveness once all reads are mastered.

*"Repeated perfect execution is required for all the moves and options related to screens before a team can fully incorporate them into its offense."* [S7, p.71]

## Common Mistakes
1. Adding defense too early → correct by completing step 1 (no defenders) before adding any coverage.
2. Over-coaching the screen action → let players read and react; the coach should correct reads, not choreograph moves.
3. Neglecting spacing → off-ball players clustering near the screen kill the offense by removing kick-out options.
