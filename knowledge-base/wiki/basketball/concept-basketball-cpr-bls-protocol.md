# CPR and Basic Life Support (BLS) for Basketball — 2015 AHA Guidelines

## Summary
Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) is the foundational life-saving intervention for cardiac arrest on the basketball court. The 2015 American Heart Association (AHA) guidelines organize CPR into Basic Life Support (BLS) and Advanced Cardiac Life Support (ALS). An optimal CPR strategy requires: a pre-established emergency event plan, a dedicated appropriately trained team, an automated external defibrillator (AED) immediately accessible, and early activation of Emergency Medical Services (EMS).

The sequence of CPR has been updated from the traditional ABCD (Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Defibrillation) to **CAB (Compression, Airway, Breathing)**, emphasizing that chest compressions must begin immediately and not be delayed by airway maneuvers. [S10, pp.180-188]

## When to Use
- Athlete is unresponsive
- No breathing or only gasping
- No pulse confirmed within 10 seconds of checking carotid artery
- Witnessed collapse on court

## BLS Algorithm (2015 AHA, Modified)

### Step 1: Scene Safety and Initial Recognition
1. Verify scene safety
2. Check for victim responsiveness (shout, tap shoulders)
3. Shout for nearby help, activate EMS, retrieve AED
4. Simultaneously check for breathing and pulse (≤10 seconds)

### Step 2: Decision Tree
- **Pulse + normal breathing**: Monitor; activate EMS as indicated
- **Pulse + no normal breathing**: Rescue breathing (1 breath every 5–6 seconds = 10–12 breaths/min); check pulse every 2 min; activate EMS after 2 min; if opioid overdose suspected, administer naloxone IM or intranasal
- **No pulse + no normal breathing/only gasping**: Begin CPR immediately; use AED as soon as available

### Step 3: Chest Compressions (C — first priority)
1. Place patient on firm, flat surface
2. Overlap palms, place longitudinally on **lower half of the sternum**
3. Compress **at least 5 cm** (2 inches) at a rate of **100–120 compressions per minute**
4. Do **not compress more than 6 cm** — excessive depth increases thoracic injury risk
5. Allow complete chest recoil after each compression — incomplete recoil increases intrathoracic pressure and reduces coronary perfusion
6. **Minimize interruptions** — shorter interruptions improve physiological outcomes
7. Cycle: **30 compressions to 2 breaths**

### Step 4: Airway (A)
1. Use **head tilt–chin lift** maneuver (if no cervical spine trauma suspected)
2. If cervical spine injury suspected: jaw thrust only
3. Clear oropharynx; insert nasal or oral airway if available and teeth allow

### Step 5: Breathing (B)
1. Mouth-to-mouth ventilation or bag-mask ventilation
2. Tidal volume: **400–600 ml** over 1 second — produce visible chest rise
3. Avoid rapid/forceful breaths → excessive positive pressure reduces preload and cardiac output
4. **Bag-mask ventilation**: requires training; risk of gastric inflation; positive-pressure capable
5. With advanced airway in place: **1 breath every 6 seconds (10 breaths/min)** during continuous compressions
6. After arrival of advanced airway facility: 10 breaths/min to prevent hyperventilation (detrimental to neurological recovery)

### Step 6: AED/Defibrillation (D)
1. As soon as AED arrives, attach immediately — do not delay rhythm analysis
2. Electrode placement: **right of upper sternal border below clavicle** AND **left of nipple at mid-axillary line**
3. Shockable rhythms: Ventricular Fibrillation (VF), Pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia (pVT)
4. Non-shockable rhythms: Asystole, Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA)
5. **Monophasic devices**: initial shock 360 J
6. **Biphasic devices**: initial shock 120–200 J (more effective, less myocardial damage)
7. After shock: **immediately resume CPR for 2 minutes** before rhythm check
8. Each minute of delay in defibrillation is associated with worse outcomes [S10, p.186]

[S10, pp.182-186]

## Key Coaching Points

### For Untrained Lay Rescuers (coaches, staff)
- Ensure scene safety
- Check for response
- Call 911
- Check for no breathing or only gasping
- Follow dispatcher's instructions
- If trained in compression-only CPR: begin chest compressions immediately

### For Healthcare Providers
- Call for nearby help
- Simultaneously assess breathing and pulse
- Activate resuscitation team
- Begin CPR
- Retrieve AED
- Defibrillate if shockable rhythm confirmed
- Provide two-person CPR as help arrives

## Advanced Cardiac Life Support (ALS) Overview

ALS extends BLS with:
1. **Advanced airway management**: endotracheal intubation; goal is to reverse hypoxemia
2. **IV/intraosseous (IO) access**: for medication delivery
3. **Epinephrine**: 1 mg IV every 3–5 minutes for VF/pVT or asystole/PEA
4. **Amiodarone or Lidocaine**: after 2–3 CPR cycles without rhythm restoration
5. **Vasopressin**: alternative vasopressor
6. **Atropine**: 1 mg IV (repeatable) for bradyarrhythmia/asystole
7. **Sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃)**: 1 mEq/kg IV for severe metabolic acidosis
8. **Dopamine**: 2–10 mcg/kg/min for hemodynamic support
9. **Temporary pacing**: for severe bradyarrhythmia or asystole unresponsive to medications
10. **Capnography (end-tidal CO₂)**: guides adequacy of compressions and identifies return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC)

### Reversible Causes to Treat During CPR ("H's and T's")
**H's**: Hypoxia, Hypovolemia, Hypo/Hyperkalemia, Hypothermia, Acidosis (Hydrogen ions)
**T's**: Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade, Toxins (drug overdose/poisoning), Thrombosis (pulmonary embolism, myocardial infarction), Hyperkalemia

[S10, pp.186-188]

## ALS Algorithm Summary (Fig. 17.8)
1. Start CPR + oxygen + attach monitor/defibrillator
2. **If shockable (VF/pVT)**: Shock → CPR 2 min → re-check rhythm → repeat; after 2nd shock cycle add epinephrine every 3–5 min; after 3rd cycle add amiodarone; treat reversible causes
3. **If non-shockable (PEA/Asystole)**: CPR 2 min → IV/IO access → epinephrine every 3–5 min → re-check rhythm; treat reversible causes
4. Continue until ROSC or ALS providers take over

## Survival Documentation
- Record survival to 30 days and to hospital discharge
- Neurological outcome: **Cerebral Performance Category (CPC)** scale 1–5 (1 = good, 5 = dead)
- **Modified Rankin Scale (mRS)**: 0–6 (0 = no symptoms, 6 = dead)
- Favorable outcome = CPC 1 or 2, or mRS 0–3

[S10, p.182]

## Factors Associated With Survival After OHCA
1. Witnessed arrest (bystander or EMS)
2. Bystander CPR initiated immediately
3. Shorter EMS response interval
4. First monitored rhythm is shockable (VF/pVT)
5. Return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC) in the field
6. Public access defibrillation
7. Dispatcher-assisted CPR
8. High quality CPR throughout
9. Post-resuscitation care quality

[S10, p.180]

## Practical Requirements for Basketball Venues
1. AED must be immediately accessible at every practice and game
2. At least one staff member must be trained in BLS/CPR + AED use
3. Written emergency action plan reviewed pre-season
4. EMS contact numbers posted and readily available
5. Pre-competition athlete medical screening including cardiac history and ECG

## Common Mistakes
1. Delaying compressions to find a pulse → start within 10 seconds
2. Compressing the sternum >6 cm → increases thoracic injury risk
3. Allowing incomplete chest recoil → reduces coronary perfusion pressure
4. Over-ventilating with bag-mask → excessive positive pressure reduces cardiac output
5. Long interruptions in compressions for rhythm analysis → minimize all pauses
6. Not resuming CPR immediately after a shock → always resume compressions for 2 min before rechecking rhythm
7. Waiting for AED before starting CPR → CPR first; AED when available
