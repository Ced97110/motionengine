# The Power Pushup Chain (9-Step Progression)

## Summary
The Power Pushup Chain is a systematic 9-step calisthenics progression for developing maximum upper-body explosive pushing power using only bodyweight. It begins with the safest and most accessible variation (incline pop-up) and progresses through increasingly demanding movements that require greater airtime, total-body speed, and coordination. The Master Step — the Superman pushup — requires throwing both hands forward beyond the head while simultaneously lifting both feet off the ground. [S9, pp.82-100]

Each step builds specific physiological qualities: joint conditioning, shoulder speed, chest and triceps power, bicep fast-twitch activation, trunk strength, and total-body explosive coordination.

## When to Use
- During pre-season and in-season upper-body power development
- As a no-equipment alternative to medicine ball and barbell explosive pushing work
- When training environments lack gym access (travel, outdoor training, etc.)
- As a complement to Olympic lifting and plyometric programs
- Progressively: athletes must master each step before advancing

## Key Principles
1. **Each step is a prerequisite for the next** — do not skip steps; mastery at one level provides the speed and power needed for the next
2. **Wider foot stance for stability** — shoulder-width to twice-shoulder-width for most athletes [S9, p.81]
3. **Partial depth (~1/3 ROM)** — optimize for strength-speed, not full stretch [S9, p.81]
4. **No pause at the bottom** — use myotatic rebound; immediately blast back up [S9, p.82]
5. **"Apply rebound" mindset** — think elastic force, not bouncing
6. **Airtime is the performance measure** — greater airtime = greater power output

## The 9 Steps

### Step 1: Incline Pop-Up
- Hands on a chest-height surface, feet wide for balance
- Body inclined toward surface, legs/hips/torso aligned
- Dip arms ~1/3 down, immediately explode off, hands leave surface by several inches
- "Catch" surface elastically and repeat
- **Purpose**: conditions shoulders, elbows, wrists for harder work; accessible entry point
- **Progression**: lower the surface over time (table → box → bottom stair steps)

### Step 2: Kneeling Push-Off
- Kneel with thighs/trunk upright, arms braced out front
- Lean forward until torso falls; catch with palms at shoulder-width
- Dip and explode back to kneeling position; keep thighs/hips/trunk aligned
- **Purpose**: big power jump — reveals whether athlete can convert strength into speed
- **Key insight**: strong athletes often struggle here — they have strength but not the speed to make power
- **Progression**: unilateral version (one arm); foot-pivot version (classic pushup style)

### Step 3: Pop-Up (Flat)
- Full pushup position on floor, hands shoulder-width or wider
- Horizontal alignment of legs, hips, torso
- Dip 1/3 depth, explode to get hands off floor, catch elastically
- **Purpose**: bridge between slow pushups and plyometric variations; builds airtime for harder steps
- **Progression**: maximize airtime/height; hands will barely clear floor early on

### Step 4: Clap Pushup
- Same setup as pop-up
- In mid-air, quickly clap hands together (clap should be audible), return to catch floor
- **Purpose**: the classic upper-body explosive exercise; builds torso power, quick hands, tough shoulders
- **Progression**: add second and third clap (double, triple) before landing — avoid "fluttering" (too many rapid claps without real power); move to body-strike variations next

### Step 5: Chest-Strike Pushup
- Same setup; in mid-air, whip hands upward to slap the chest with palms
- Return hands to catch floor
- **Purpose**: greater hand travel distance than clap = more airtime required = more power; biceps fast-twitch activation to snap hands upward
- **Progression**: touch ribs → obliques → hips (stepping stone to hip-strike)

### Step 6: Hip-Strike Pushup
- Same setup; in mid-air, whip hands back to slap hips
- Return hands to catch floor
- **Purpose**: bridge between front-body clapping and behind-the-back clapping (step 7)
- **Progression**: inch hands farther back over time: hips → thighs → behind-hip kickback → convict clap

### Step 7: Convict Pushup (Behind-the-Back Clap)
- Same setup; in mid-air, whip hands back and clap them behind the back (handcuff position)
- Return hands to catch floor
- **Purpose**: maximum behind-body hand travel; extreme upper-body explosive power
- **Regression**: practice kneeling version first to learn the rear-clap mechanics safely
- **Progression**: front-clap + rear-clap (twice) in same airtime; quintuple clap pushups

### Step 8: Half-Super
- Same setup; in mid-air, thrust both hands directly out in front of body (beyond head)
- Return hands to catch floor
- **Purpose**: upper half of body achieves Superman position; builds extreme shoulder speed
- **Regression**: start with one hand out front at a time on each side
- **Progression**: clap top of head on the return journey before catching the floor

### Step 9: Full Body Pop-Up
- Same setup; simultaneously kick legs out and up behind while hands leave floor
- All four limbs leave the floor at the same time
- "Catch" floor with both hands and feet, apply rebound
- **Purpose**: prerequisite for Superman — teaches the lower-body lift component
- **Regression**: start by popping only 3 limbs, vary which three
- **Progression**: avoid worming (sagging hips); maintain full body alignment throughout

### Master Step: Superman Pushup
- Combines Step 8 (hands thrown out front) + Step 9 (legs lifted behind)
- Both arms extend beyond head AND both feet lift off floor simultaneously
- Entire body achieves the "flying Superman" position in mid-air
- Requires vastly more power/speed than clap pushups — most athletes stall at Step 4

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping steps** → athlete crashes or lacks airtime; build each step to mastery
2. **Worming (sagging hips during pop)** → cheats the trunk; keep hips aligned
3. **"Fluttering" multiple claps** → prioritize powerful single/double claps over trick multi-clapping
4. **Starting convict pushup without kneeling practice** → risk of falling on face; use kneeling version first
5. **Using feet-elevated too early** → intensifier (more bodyweight through arms); only for advanced
