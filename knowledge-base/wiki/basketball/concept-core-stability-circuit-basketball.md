# Core Stability Circuit for Basketball

## Summary
Core development is a non-negotiable component of basketball conditioning. The core — abdominals and obliques — forms "a girdle of muscle that anchors the body through the moves we make in basketball." Rich Dalatri's core stability circuit strengthens the core by constantly changing the base of posture and the position of the limbs, forcing all surrounding muscles to stabilize in many planes. This mimics the multi-directional demands of basketball. [S7, pp.333-334]

The key principle: keep the body as long and straight as possible throughout every exercise. Any sagging of the hips breaks the kinetic chain. Each exercise builds on the previous one by adding a more challenging limb position.

## When to Use
- As part of every strength training session
- Off-season: multiple sets of the full circuit
- In-season: maintenance; abbreviated circuit 2-3x/week
- Can be performed anywhere — no equipment needed for the basic circuit

## The Five Core Exercises

### Core Exercise 1 — Prone Forearm Plank
- Lie facedown, forearms on floor under shoulders, body extended as long as possible.
- Raise up on forearms and toes, body straight.
- Hips at or above body level — "It's better to have hips slightly arched up."
- Hold 3-10 seconds; lower knees to rest. [S7, p.333]

### Core Exercise 2 — Prone Plank with Arm Reach
- Same starting position as Exercise 1.
- Raise one arm **out to the side** and hold 3-5 seconds.
- Extend the same arm **forward** and hold another 3-5 seconds.
- Repeat with the other arm.
- Key: "Keep the body level to the floor, not twisted to the side with the raised arm." [S7, p.334]

### Core Exercise 3 — Prone Plank with Leg Raise
- Same starting position.
- Raise one foot off the floor with the **leg straight**, hold 3-5 seconds.
- Repeat with the other leg.
- Key: "Keep the body level to the floor, not twisted to the side of the raised foot." [S7, p.334]

### Core Exercise 4 — Prone Plank with Contralateral Arm-Leg Raise (Bird Dog Variation)
- Same starting position.
- Raise one arm **forward** and the **opposite foot** off the floor simultaneously.
- Hold 3-5 seconds; repeat on the other side.
- "Try to keep the body as long and straight as possible. Don't allow the hips to drop below the level of the body." [S7, p.334]

### Core Exercise 5 — Side Plank with Hip Abduction
- Lie on one side, legs straight (stacked), upper body on one elbow (placed under the shoulder).
- Raise hips off the floor and hold body in a straight line on elbow and side of foot — hold 3-5 seconds.
- Then raise the **upper leg 6-12 inches** and back down three times.
- Repeat both movements on the other side. [S7, p.334]

## Coaching Points
- "Keep the body aligned as long and straight as possible throughout every exercise."
- If hips sag in the plank, stop and reset — sagging means the core is no longer working; the lower back is compensating.
- The constant change of limb position is what makes this circuit effective — each new position shifts the center of gravity and recruits different stabilizing muscles.
- 3-10 second holds train muscular endurance; progress toward longer holds as the season approaches.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: 3-second holds, 1 set per exercise
2. **Intermediate**: 5-second holds, 2-3 sets per exercise; add the Pilates Abdominal Circuit
3. **Advanced**: 10-second holds; add single-arm or single-leg variations; superset with rotational core exercises (landmine trunk rotation, Pallof press)
