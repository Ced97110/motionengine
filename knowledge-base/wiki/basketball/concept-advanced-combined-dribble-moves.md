# Advanced Combined Dribble Moves

## Summary
After mastering basic one-on-one and individual dribble moves, players can chain two or more moves together in succession. These advanced combined dribble moves are not "circus moves" — they are effective, game-proven tools used by elite pro players (an elite scoring wing, Allen Iverson, Steve Nash, an all-time wing, Dwyane Wade, Tracy McGrady, Mike Bibby, Richard Hamilton) to counter a defender who has read and adjusted to the first move. All combinations are paired with changes of speed and direction, always reading the defender's reaction. [S7, pp.30-32]

## When to Use
- When a single dribble move has been countered by the defender
- When a defender over-commits to one direction after the first move
- In isolation or pick-and-roll situations with enough space for multiple moves
- Late-game shot-creation against focused, locked-in defenders

## Key Principles
1. **Two moves in succession, always based on the defender's reaction** — never combine moves in a pre-planned sequence; the second move is chosen based on how the defender responds to the first.
2. **All combinations are matched with a change of speed and direction** — the pace change is what makes the combinations effective, not just the ball movement.
3. **The goal is always to beat the defender and finish at the basket or with a jump shot** — every combination ends with a drive, layup, or pull-up jumper.
4. **Read the defender's reaction to each individual move** — the sequence is determined play-by-play, not choreographed in advance.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG/SF**: Elite offensive players should develop at least 3-4 combination sequences and be comfortable executing them at game speed in both directions.

## Variations

### Dribble, Step Back, and Jump Shot
The defender is covering aggressively with no driving room. Within shooting range:
- Dribble once with the right hand and step with the left foot to make the defender anticipate a drive.
- When the defender leans forward into the driving path, put full weight on the left foot, push back, and after a small hop land right foot first, then left foot.
- Pull the ball back and shoot a jumper over the defender. Stay low throughout.
- *Variation*: Step back with only the left foot (not the right), using the right foot as the pivot foot, pull the ball back, and shoot. [S7, p.31]

### Dribble, Step Back, Turn, and Jump Shot (an elite scoring wing move)
Same setup as above. After the step back with the left foot forward, use the left foot as the pivot foot, pull the ball back, and make a rear turn to front the basket and shoot a jump shot. [S7, p.31]

### Dribble, Step Back, and Crossover
After the step back, using the right foot as the pivot foot, pull the ball back and perform a crossover dribble — followed by a drive or jump shot. Add a head fake a split second before the crossover to force the defender to straighten their legs. [S7, p.31]

### Dribble, Step Back, and Reverse
After the step back, pull the ball back, pivot on the right foot, front the basket, and execute a reverse dribble — followed by a drive or jump shot. [S7, p.32]

### Dribble, Step Back, Crossover, and Reverse
After the step back, chain a crossover and a reverse in the same action. Always based on the defender's reaction. Finish with a drive or jump shot. [S7, p.32]

### Dribble, Step Back, Hesitation, and Speed Dribble
After the step back, hesitate — slightly raising the head and knees to look like you're slowing down. When the defender stands up or relaxes: execute a hard, quick dribble to beat them. Can continue with a direct drive, crossover, or reverse dribble. [S7, p.32]

### Additional Combinations (Van Gundy List)
- In-and-out dribble → crossover dribble
- Crossover dribble → between-the-legs dribble
- Crossover dribble → reverse dribble
- Stop-and-go dribble → crossover dribble
- Between-the-legs dribble → reverse dribble → crossover dribble
- Between-the-legs dribble → behind-the-back dribble (feet even or one forward)
- Double between-the-legs dribble
- Lateral step and dribble → speed direct drive with the same hand
- Lateral step and dribble → crossover dribble or in-and-out dribble
- Hockey dribble → crossover dribble
- Hockey dribble → lateral step and dribble → speed forward same hand or crossover [S7, p.32]

All of these combinations can end with a drive to the basket or a jump shot.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Pre-planning the combination sequence** → must react to the defender, not run a choreographed series
2. **Rushing the second move** → give the defender time to commit after the first move; the reaction is what creates the advantage
3. **Not maintaining low body position throughout** → standing up during the combination makes the player slow and easy to defend; stay low throughout
4. **Losing pace change** → the combination only works if paired with genuine speed changes; flat-speed combinations are easily defended

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Master each individual dribble move in both directions before combining
2. **Intermediate**: Practice step-back + jump shot and step-back + crossover from both hands against a passive (50% effort) defender
3. **Advanced**: Execute full game-speed combinations against live defenders, reading and reacting throughout
