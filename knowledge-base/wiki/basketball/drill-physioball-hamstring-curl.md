# Physioball Hamstring Curl (ACL Quadriceps Dominance)

## Objective
Strengthen the hamstrings, glutes, and posterior chain to correct quadriceps dominance — a neuromuscular imbalance that prevents force absorption on landing and increases ACL injury risk.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Floor or mat
- Equipment: Physioball (stability ball)

## Execution
1. Lie on your back with both heels resting on top of the Physioball, legs straight.
2. Raise the hips off the floor by contracting the glutes, forming a bridge position (straight line from shoulders to heels).
3. While maintaining the bridge (hips elevated), bend both knees and roll the ball toward the buttocks.
4. Extend the knees back out to the straight position while keeping the hips elevated.
5. Lower the hips to the floor to return to the starting position.
6. Perform the prescribed number of repetitions.

## Coaching Points
- "Don't let the hips drop" — maintaining the bridge throughout the curl is where the core and glute work occurs
- Watch for the hips sagging as the player fatigues; this indicates posterior chain weakness — reduce reps before form breaks down
- Hamstring strains can linger for months if not properly rehabbed; build this strength proactively [S2, p.170]
- Female players especially benefit — many are quadriceps dominant when landing and cutting; hamstring strength protects the knee [S2, p.170]
- Progress to single-leg variation only after bilateral is mastered with good form

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Bridge hold only (no curl) for 3 × 10-second holds, focusing on glute activation
2. **Intermediate**: Full bridge + bilateral curl, 3 × 10 repetitions
3. **Advanced**: Single-leg bridge + single-leg hamstring curl (dramatically increases difficulty and single-leg stability)

## Muscles Involved
- **Primary**: Hamstrings (biceps femoris, semimembranosus, semitendinosus), gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, gluteus minimus
- **Secondary**: Erector spinae (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis), rectus abdominis
