# Sport-Specific SAQ Program Design

## Summary
Designing an effective speed, agility, and quickness (SAQ) training program requires a systematic approach grounded in needs analysis, progressive overload, and movement specificity. The goal is not to randomly select drills but to identify the athlete's limiters, choose drills that address those limiters, and progress from simple to complex over a structured mesocycle. Programs are individualized — even two athletes playing the same position may have very different limiters and needs. [S8, pp.223-226]

The overarching principle is **specificity**: SAQ training must closely mimic the movements athletes perform during competition. Basketball programs, for example, emphasize vertical jumping, lateral mobility, first-step quickness, and acceleration for breakaways. [S8, p.226]

## When to Use
- Pre-season: establish the baseline SAQ mesocycle (Weeks 1–3 skill mastery; Weeks 4–6 combination drills)
- In-season: maintain SAQ work as one component of the full training plan alongside strength, flexibility, and restoration
- Off-season: highest volume and intensity SAQ training with full recovery available
- Immediately following needs analysis to match drills to identified limiters

## Key Principles
1. **Needs analysis first**: Determine each athlete's limiters before programming any drill [S8, p.224]
2. **Specificity**: SAQ drills must mimic competition movements — basketball needs vertical jump, lateral quickness, and acceleration drills [S8, p.226]
3. **Progressive structure**: Weeks 1–3 of each mesocycle are dedicated to mastering individual skills; Weeks 4–6 combine multiple drills to create complex training stimuli [S8, p.224]
4. **Master basics before complexity**: Performing a complex drill sloppily reinforces poor movement patterns. Build the foundation first [S8, p.224]
5. **Defined session structure** (5 components):
   - 3–5 minute session introduction with stated goals
   - 10-minute dynamic warm-up
   - Body of session: 20–25 drill sets at 1:3 or 1:4 work-to-rest ratio
   - 3–4 water breaks of 3 minutes each (especially in heat)
   - Cool-down focusing on static flexibility [S8, pp.224-225]
6. **Work-to-rest ratio**: 1:3 or 1:4 is standard. For conditioning emphasis, match the sport's metabolic work-to-rest ratio [S8, p.226]
7. **More is not always better**: Reduce sets and increase rest if athletes are underconditioned — quality over quantity [S8, p.226]
8. **Warm-up doubles as skill acquisition**: Simple SAQ drills in the warm-up reinforce biomotor skill without consuming training time from the main session [S8, p.225]
9. **Positive reinforcement**: End every session with positive affirmations — focus on successes, communicate improvements constructively [S8, p.226]

## Session Structure Detail

### Dynamic Warm-Up (10–15 minutes, 15–20 yards per drill)
Progress from: simple → complex, slow → fast, low-intensity → high-intensity, lower → higher volume [S8, p.225]

Sample warm-up sequence:
- A-March Walk → B-March Walk → Light Jog → Backpedal → Skipping → Backward Skipping → Straight-Leg Shuffle → Butt-Kickers → Side-Shuffle → Carioca → Four-Point Pop-Up → Single-Leg Balance with Opposite-Leg Reach → Single-Leg Balance with Opposite-Arm Reach → Multiplane Lunges [S8, p.225]

### Body of Session
- Emphasize the athlete's or team's **major weaknesses or limiters**
- Give more sets to challenging drills, fewer to mastered ones
- Provide appropriate rest between sets — development cannot happen in a fatigued state [S8, pp.225-226]
- Each drill: typically 5–10 second maximal effort, 2–4 times over 15–20 yards

### Cool-Down
- Static flexibility exercises focusing on individual ROM deficits
- Performed from a **standing position** to load muscles/joints/connective tissue in a triplane environment consistent with sport demands
- Not a flexibility contest — target specific deficiencies [S8, p.226]

## Mesocycle Structure (6 Weeks)
- **Weeks 1–3**: Master individual drills at progressive intensity (one drill per session twice/week if mastered easily, move to a more complex variation)
- **Weeks 4–6**: Mix and match drills from Weeks 1–3 to create novel complex stimuli and higher transfer to competition [S8, p.224]

## Basketball-Specific 6-Week SAQ Mesocycle
Goals: Improve jumping for rebounding and blocking; improve lateral mobility; improve first-step quickness and acceleration. [S8, p.233]

| Week | Speed | Agility | Quickness |
|------|-------|---------|----------|
| 1 | A-March Walk (#6), A-Form Runs (#7) | 20-Yard Shuttle (#53), Med-Ball Wall Chest Passes (#124) | Repeated Vertical Jumps (#139) |
| 2 | A-Skips for Distance (#7), Partner-Resisted Starts (#35) | Lateral 20-Yard Shuttle (#53), Med-Ball Overhead Throw (#127) | Standing Long Jump (#140) |
| 3 | A-Skips for Height (#7), Bullet Belt (#39) | 30-Yard T-Drill (#54), Med-Ball Wall Scoop Toss (#128) | Power Skips (#142) |
| 4 | Squirm (#55), Hexagon Drill (#104) | Ladder Speed Runs (#10), Repeated Vertical Jumps (#139) | — |
| 5 | X-Pattern Multiskill (#65), Hexagon Drill (#104) | Hop-Scotch to Catch Pass (#150), Vertical Jump to Sprint (#139) | — |
| 6 | Z-Pattern Cuts (#69), Drop and Get Up (#103) | One-Leg Hop to Dribble and Lay-Up (#151), Sprint to Vertical Jump (#139) | Medicine-Ball Lateral Shuffle/Pass (#114) |

Weeks 4–6 blend drills with complex combinations (agility + quickness) for maximum basketball transfer. [S8, p.233]

## Common Mistakes
1. Random drill selection without needs analysis → wasted training time. Always start with limiters.
2. Combining drills before basics are mastered → reinforces poor movement patterns. Progress is essential.
3. Same work-to-rest ratio regardless of conditioning level → overtraining for weak athletes, undertraining for strong ones. Adjust individually.
4. Skipping the warm-up → increased injury risk and lost skill acquisition time.
5. No cool-down → missed flexibility development opportunity and poor recovery.
