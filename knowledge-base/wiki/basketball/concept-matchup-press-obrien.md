# Matchup Full-Court Press (O'Brien)

## Summary
Jim O'Brien's matchup press begins with a zone look on the inbounds and switches immediately to man-to-man once the ball enters the court. Its goal is to force a high tempo, deny all forward passes, pressure the ball handler into an uncontrolled speed dribble, and then trap. O'Brien presents two variants (White and Black) that differ only in whether the inbounder is pressured. The press is predicated on superior conditioning: "Your conditioning must be a weapon in itself." [S7, pp.241-252]

## When to Use
- When your team is out-talented but can out-condition the opponent
- After scoring a basket (White = scored in paint; Black = scored on a perimeter shot or dead-ball situations)
- To disrupt an opponent's tempo and force turnovers
- When the opponent has a weak ball handler who is susceptible to speed-dribble traps
- As a full-game commitment or as a situational change-of-pace

## Key Principles
1. **Conditioning is the weapon** — no press works without the ability to run at full speed for an entire game with knees bent. [S7, p.252]
2. **Start zone, switch to man** — immediately after the inbounds pass, each defender matches up with the nearest offensive player.
3. **Never concede a forward pass** — "Any advance pass must be denied."
4. **Pressure, don't influence the ball** — "We don't want to influence the ball — we want to pressure the ball!" Forcing the dribbler to the sideline reduces the pressure you want to exert. [S7, p.246]
5. **Speed dribble triggers the trap** — only trap when the defender can force an uncontrolled dribble and maintain a close guarding position alongside the ball handler. Never trap a controlled dribbler.
6. **Allow backward passes only** — any pass that takes the offense backward is acceptable; the press remains on. If the second pass reaches the middle of the court behind the front line, the press is off and the defense retreats to half-court.
7. **Middle is the enemy** — as the inbounds pass is in the air, all weak-side defenders sprint to the middle to prevent the lethal middle pass.
8. **Back-tip as a weapon** — when the defender is beaten by the dribble, sprint after the ball handler to tip the ball from behind. Fast dribblers who must slow down to face a defender upcourt are susceptible to back-tips.

## Player Responsibilities

### White Press (Pressures Inbounder)
- **X4 (most mobile big)**: Directly in front of inbounder; feet almost on the line; shadows inbounder along the baseline; deflects the inbounds pass. On a successful inbounds pass to a non-coffin-corner area, X4 drops below the ball line and matches up with the inbounder.
- **X3 (most athletic wing, left side)**: Shoulder-to-shoulder with his player; maintains vision of the inbounder; reads where the offensive player lines up; stays on the side closest to the left sideline.
- **X2 (other wing, right side)**: Generally on his player's left shoulder; closes the gap when the inbounder looks his way.
- **X1 (PG, near half-court)**: Picks up the player closest to half-court on the ball side.
- **X5 (other big, back)**: Picks up the remaining opponent wherever he is.

### Black Press (Does Not Pressure Inbounder)
- X4 is off the ball and **behind the front line** (behind X2 and X3), shading the area to support the front two and make inbounding more difficult.
- If the inbounder runs the baseline, X4 follows to the other side.
- All other assignments identical to the white press.

### Coffin-Corner Rule (White Press Only)
- If the inbounds pass goes to the corner near the baseline ("coffin corner"), X4 immediately traps the ball handler. [S7, p.245]

## Variations

### Using Both Presses
- **White press** after a score inside the paint.
- **Black press** after a perimeter shot or any dead-ball situation.
- Alternating the two confuses opponents, at least initially. [S7, p.244]

### Leveling Instead of Trapping
When a defender is trailing the speed dribbler and cannot trap, the upcourt defender "levels" the ball handler — fakes back and forth to slow him or force a sideways dribble — while the trailing defender sprints to back-tip. [S7, p.248]

### Trap-or-Level Decision
- **Trap** when: the defender can force a speed (uncontrolled) dribble AND maintain a close guarding position alongside the ball handler.
- **Level** when: the defender is trailing and cannot stay on the dribbler's hip.
- Rotations after a trap are mostly instinctive; overburdening players with strict rotation rules hinders quick reaction. [S7, p.247]

### Trap Rotation Options
- X3 and X1 trap the ball.
- X4 has middle responsibility — takes away the pass to the gut.
- Either X2 or X5 can rotate to the open player.
- If X5 rotates up, X2 protects back; if X2 rotates, X5 stays in the basket area.
- All defenders stay below the ball line.
- Only pass undenied: the backward pass. [S7, p.247]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Forcing the dribbler to the sideline** → Correction: pressure the ball without channeling — let the trap be triggered by the speed dribble, not the sideline.
2. **Trapping a controlled dribbler** → Correction: wait for the speed (uncontrolled) dribble to trigger the trap; trapping a controlled ball handler who can change direction easily defeats the press.
3. **Over-specifying rotations** → Correction: teach aggressiveness and let players react; strict rotation rules slow reaction time.
4. **Abandoning the press after 1-2 easy baskets** → Correction: every defense has weaknesses; trust the philosophy and maintain commitment. [S7, p.252]
5. **Insufficient conditioning** → Correction: knees must stay bent for full-game defensive slides and closeout rotations; conditioning must be practiced as hard as the press itself.
