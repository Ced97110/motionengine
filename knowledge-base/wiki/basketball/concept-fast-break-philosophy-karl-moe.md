# Fast-Break Philosophy — Karl & Moe

## Summary
the head coach and a co-author built one of the pro's most up-tempo systems around the belief that players — not coaches — should own the decisions on the court. Their secondary break is not a scripted set; it *is* the passing game itself, flowing naturally out of whatever the defense gives. Practices run 75–90 minutes at maximum intensity, with full-court work dominating once the competitive season begins. [S7, pp.157–161]

## When to Use
- Any time the primary break fails to produce a basket
- When a team wants to punish an ill-formed defense without a scripted set play
- When building a player-empowered, read-and-react offensive culture

## Key Principles
1. **Short, high-intensity practices beat long sessions.** Physical and mental fatigue in lengthy practices is the enemy. 75–90 minutes is the ceiling. [S7, p.158]
2. **Player autonomy.** "The game and the decisions belong to the players." Coaches correct mistakes, but court sense drives the read, not coach signals. [S7, p.158]
3. **The secondary break is the passing game.** If the primary break doesn't score, flow immediately into cuts, backdoor plays, give-and-go, and on/off-ball screens — no formal secondary set needed. [S7, p.158]
4. **Run all drills at top speed.** Sprint court maintenance is a habit built in practice. Game pace during practice raises competition level. [S7, p.160]
5. **Turnover ratio > raw turnover count.** Fast-break teams will have more turnovers. What matters is consistently having *fewer* than the opponent. [S7, p.161]
6. **Conditioning advantage compounds over a season.** Being better trained to sustain the break than opponents is a strategic asset. Shorter pro practices preserve legs late in the season. [S7, p.161]
7. **A capable point guard is non-negotiable.** The PG must advance the ball quickly, read the break, and deliver to moving teammates — or the whole system stalls. [S7, p.160]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Read break opportunities instantly; push ball; make one decisive pass or shoot — do NOT hesitate
- **SG/SF**: Sprint sidelines at full speed; be in position before the PG arrives so passes are delivered in stride
- **PF/C**: Trail the break; post down or reverse the ball on secondary action; be a shooting/driving option, not just a rebounder

## Variations
### Rule-Based Secondary
Deacon Smith's approach: set explicit rules for the passing game if your roster lacks five smart players. Karl/Moe disagree but acknowledge it's a legitimate strategy for less-savvy teams. [S7, p.158]

### Scripted Secondary
Other teams run designed plays once primary fails. Karl/Moe prefer free-flowing reads — but teams with less playmaking talent may benefit from a structured secondary. [S7, p.157]

## Common Mistakes
1. **PG hesitates or wastes dribbles** → Secondary break is dead before it starts. Drill decision speed relentlessly.
2. **Relying on only one player** → A one-man break rarely succeeds at high levels. Everyone must sprint the court. [S7, p.166]
3. **Long practices to compensate for sloppiness** → More time does not fix mental fatigue issues; raise intensity, not duration.
4. **Obsessing over raw turnovers** → Track turnover *ratio* instead. Fast-break teams will lose more possessions per game by nature. [S7, p.161]
