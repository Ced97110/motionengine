# Shooting on the Move

## Summary
Most shots in a real game are taken on the move — off screens, off cuts, off the dribble. Yet most players only practice stationary spot shooting. Shooting on the move requires mastering a specific footwork pattern: **always plant the inside pivot foot first** when catching the ball while moving, then square the body to the basket before rising into the shot. This applies regardless of the angle or direction of movement. [S5, p.1]

The concept also applies to situations where the defender fights through the screen and the player cannot shoot — they must be ready to **immediately dribble with the outside hand** to protect the ball and attack. [S5, p.4]

## When to Use
- Catching off any screen action (down screen, flare screen, back screen, inbounds screen)
- Receiving a pass while cutting (V-cut, curl cut, flare cut)
- Pulling up off the dribble after a ball-screen
- Any perimeter catch where the shooter is moving laterally or toward the ball

## Key Principles
1. **Inside foot is always the pivot foot**: When moving left-to-right, plant the left foot. When moving right-to-left, plant the right foot. [S5, p.1]
2. **Heel-to-toe plant**: The inside foot lands heel first, then toe, generating upward momentum for the jump shot.
3. **Stub the foot when needed**: On some angles (e.g., down screen, left side, right-handed shooter), after planting the inside left foot, bring the right foot **all the way into the shot line** to maintain alignment. A left-handed shooter from the same angle would plant left and **stub** the right foot to maintain alignment. [S5, p.2]
4. **Square up, maintain alignment to the target**: Body and toes must face the basket before releasing the shot.
5. **Start low, stay low**: Maintain an athletic base throughout the cut and plant — do not stand up early.
6. **Shoot and stay**: After releasing a perimeter shot, hold the follow-through and do not chase the ball — this allows long-rebound tracking and defensive positioning. Only follow shots taken inside the paint. [S5, p.4]
7. **Keep eyes on the target**: Even when dribbling after the catch, eyes stay on the rim to think "score" and to see the full floor. [S5, p.4]
8. **Practice at game speed**: "Too many players practice slower than game speed and then have trouble making shots during an actual game." [S5, p.10] First master footwork at half speed, then escalate to full game speed.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Master the inside-foot plant in both directions (left-to-right and right-to-left). Do not neglect one direction in practice.

## Variations
### When the Defender Fights Through the Screen
If the defender recovers and is on the shooter's heels as the ball is received, **do not force the shot**. Plant the inside foot as usual, but immediately dribble with the **outside hand** (right hand when moving left-to-right; left hand when moving right-to-left). Place the inside hand in **armbar position** to protect the ball. Then attack as the aggressor. [S5, p.4]

### Right-to-Left Catch
Plant the right foot (inside foot). If right-handed, bring the left foot into alignment. Keep the same mechanics — only the pivot foot changes. [S5, p.3]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Forgetting the follow-through while focused on footwork** → Remind players: footwork enables the shot, but completing the shooting motion is the goal. "Do not lose sight of the end goal: swishing the shot with proper form." [S5, p.1]
2. **Using the wrong pivot foot** → Drill footwork at half speed first, without shooting, until inside-foot plant is automatic.
3. **Practicing only stationary spot shooting** → "Practice game shots from game spots at game speed." [S5, p.1]
4. **Overstriding on the catch** → Results in loss of balance; insist on a compact, controlled plant.
5. **Following perimeter shots** → Teaches bad court positioning habits; enforce "shoot and stay" on all perimeter attempts.
6. **Dribbling with the inside hand after a screen** → Leaves the ball exposed to the defender; the outside hand protects the dribble.
