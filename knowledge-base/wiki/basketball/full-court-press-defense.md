# Full-Court Press Defense

## Summary
Full-court press defense is a high-pressure defensive system applied from the moment the ball is inbounded, designed to force turnovers, disrupt offensive rhythm, change the tempo of the game, and generate easy offensive baskets. Herb Brown builds his press from individual shell sequences — 1v1, 2v2, 3v3, 4v4 — before putting a full press in front of the entire team. The emphasis is on positioning, peripheral vision, leveling with the ball, and channeling the ball where the defense wants it to go.

The philosophical core: disguise your defense to delay offensive recognition, keep opponents off balance by changing defenses throughout the game, and use an aggressive pressure defense as the engine that triggers your own offense and generates easy baskets. [S1, pp.176-182]

## When to Use
- After made free throws or dead balls (ideal time to set press)
- When you need to change the tempo in a close game or when trailing
- As a surprise element — not necessarily every possession
- Against teams that struggle with ball-handling pressure
- When you want to disrupt the opponent's point guard from initiating their offense early
- In the fourth quarter to force turnovers and speed up the game

## Key Principles
1. **Build from individual shell sequences** — teach 1v1, 2v2, 3v3, and 4v4 full-court before installing the 5v5 press. Each sequence emphasizes positioning to see both ball and opponent.
2. **Off-ball positioning: foot and hand nearest the ball forward** — this improves peripheral vision and enables defenders to see more of the court and passing lanes simultaneously.
3. **Know your help sources** — every player must know exactly where their help comes from and where the defense is channeling the ball.
4. **Take away the wide outlet pass from the middle** — deny the direct advance pass through the center of the court; force everything to the sideline.
5. **Run-and-jump: use when you can get the ball out of the handler's hands** — best applied when the nearest help defender doesn't have far to go; forces hurried, spontaneous decisions from the offense.
6. **Level with the ball, never trail** — a defender whose opponent is trailing the ball should level back to the ball line, not stay with a player behind the play. The leveling defender is positioned perfectly to double-team from behind.
7. **Decision: one-and-done trap or trap every opportunity** — the coach must decide this in advance and teach the philosophy clearly; players must understand which approach is being used on each possession.
8. **After a trap: sprint out and rotate to where help came from** — when the ball is passed out of the trap, the trapping defender does not chase; they rotate to the help position.
9. **Shadow press the point guard** — consider making it difficult for the opposing PG to receive the ball at all, preventing them from establishing offensive rhythm and flow.
10. **Element of surprise is a weapon** — change presses at timeouts, dead balls, end of quarters/halves, and free throws to keep opponents from settling in. [S1, pp.176-178]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (on-ball defender)**: Primary pressure on the ball; execute run-and-jump reads; yell "Ball!" when a pass is made to alert retreating teammates.
- **SG/SF (wing defenders)**: Position with foot/hand nearest the ball forward; deny nearest outlets; be the run-and-jump helper when the dribbler comes close; retreat and look over shoulder to locate ball.
- **PF/C (back defenders)**: Shadow the opposing big men; be the pressure on the inbounds passer (in diamond press); cover the goal in transition; trap the sideline dribbler in diamond alignment.
- **All five**: Fake and feint the trap even when you can't get there — keeps the offense off balance. If a trap is beaten and a pass made, take the free man nearest the direction of the help.

## Variations
### Run-and-Jump (Perimeter)
Nearest defender to the dribbler rotates and jumps the ball handler while the original defender recovers to their opponent. Best used when the helper doesn't have far to go. Forces the dribbler to turn back or make a hurried pass. [S1, p.177]

### Run-and-Trap (Baseline)
Prefer to run-and-jump on the perimeter but trap the baseline — when the dribbler is forced to the corner or baseline, the wing and the inbounds defender double-team and seal. [S1, p.177]

### Hybrid: 3 Man-to-Man + 2 Zoning (Full Court)
Three perimeter defenders play man-to-man full court; two tallest men zone up down the floor; then all five fall back into a 2-3 zone. Sometimes switches to man-to-man once the ball crosses mid-court. [S1, p.178]

### Zone Press → Different Zone (Half Court)
Play 2-2-1 or 1-2-1-1 zone press full court, then drop back into a different zone (1-3-1, 2-3, etc.) to confuse the offense. Change after every timeout regardless of who called it. [S1, p.184]

### Shadow Press
Press the opposing point guard specifically — make it difficult for them to receive the ball at all. Disrupts their ability to initiate the offense and establish early rhythm. [S1, p.177]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Trailing the play after your opponent passes** → Correct: level with the ball; become the double-team threat from behind, not a trailing spectator.
2. **Predictable press alignment** → Correct: change defenses constantly; disguise your press to delay the offense's recognition.
3. **Trapping without a plan for rotation** → Correct: when the ball is passed out of a trap, the trapper sprints to the help position — where the help came from.
4. **Gambling and getting beaten** → Correct: fake the trap if you can't get there; keep your position and live to rotate another day.
5. **Not communicating** → Correct: the ball defender yells "Ball!" on every pass; all defenders call out their match-up changes.
