# Knee Rehabilitation Phases for Basketball Players

## Summary
Rehabilitation of knee injuries in basketball players progresses through five distinct phases, each with a specific focus and objective measurable goals. Progression from one phase to the next must be based on objective criteria established at the outset of rehabilitation — not solely on time since injury or surgery. Rehabilitation must be individualized to the player's position, injury, and goals, and should always include basketball integration (holding a ball, shooting while seated) even in early phases. [S10, pp.714-715]

## When to Use
- Any basketball knee injury (ACL, MCL, meniscus, patellar tendon, PCL, chondral)
- Post-surgical and conservative management pathways
- Applies from initial injury through return to performance and ongoing secondary prevention

## Key Principles
1. Progression is criteria-based, not time-based — objective criteria must be established at the start of rehab
2. Specificity, overload, and progression are the three programming principles governing exercise progression [S10, p.714]
3. A basketball should be integrated from the earliest possible phase (e.g., holding ball during core work, seated shooting)
4. Healing timeframes must be respected: ACL graft healing 9–24 months; bone bruises 12–16 months; MCL grades vary; chondral surgery may require extended non-weight-bearing [S10, p.714]
5. Cardiovascular fitness and upper/general lower extremity strengthening must advance simultaneously with rehabilitation
6. Rehabilitation should be tailored to the athlete's position

## Rehabilitation Phases

### Phase 1: Prehabilitation (Pre-Surgery, Especially ACL)
**Focus:** "Quiet the joint" — reduce inflammation, improve ROM, maximize quadriceps strength to optimize post-surgical outcomes

**Goals:**
- Full active and passive range of motion
- Minimal to no effusion
- 90% quadriceps strength limb symmetry index [S10, p.715]

### Phase 2: Acute (Post-Injury or Post-Surgery)
**Focus:** Reduce inflammation and restore basic function

**Goals:**
- Full active and passive range of motion
- Minimal to no effusion
- No quadriceps lag with straight leg raise
- Symmetrical walking gait pattern [S10, p.715]

### Phase 3: Intermediate
**Focus:** Restore activities of daily living; progress strength and neuromuscular control toward sport demands

**Goals:**
- Pain-free and unrestricted activities of daily living
- ≥80% quadriceps strength limb symmetry
- Control of the knee during dynamic weight-bearing, particularly single-leg tasks
- Able to control knee under load and in closed kinetic chain positions
- Return to running initiated [S10, p.715]

### Phase 4: Late
**Focus:** Complex and dynamic sport-related movements; return to play progressions on- and off-court

**Goals:**
- Identify and expose the athlete to the demands they will face on return to basketball
- Meet or exceed all return to sport criteria set by the medical team
- Able to move on- and off-court with good neuromuscular control
- Able to control knee during high-speed cutting, pivoting, and jumping activities [S10, p.715]

### Phase 5: Return to Performance / Secondary Prevention
**Focus:** Reach or exceed pre-injury performance; prevent subsequent injuries

**Goals:**
- Ongoing performance of a knee injury prevention program
- Meet or exceed pre-injury performance indicators (physical: accelerations/decelerations per game; basketball: points/rebounds per game) [S10, p.715]

## Soreness and Effusion Guidelines
Rehabilitation should be monitored using standardized soreness and effusion protocols [S10, p.714]:

| Soreness Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| No soreness | Advance |
| Sore during warm-up but resolves | Repeat previous day's session |
| Sore during warm-up and continues | Stop; take 2 days off; repeat session from 2 days prior |
| Sore >1 hour after session or next day | Take 1 day off; repeat previous session |

| Effusion Level | Action |
|---|---|
| 1+ or less | Complete planned session and advance |
| 2+ or larger | Hold session, treat effusion; at next session: trace/less = advance; 1+ = maintain; 2+ = continue management |

## Special Considerations by Injury Type
- **MCL:** Monitor shooting technique closely — many players collapse into valgus (hip adduction, hip internal rotation, knee abduction) when shooting; ensure proper squat form even in low-level drills [S10, p.716]
- **Cartilage/Meniscus:** Use accelerometers or IMUs to quantify knee load; adhere strictly to weight-bearing timelines; map a gradual load increase to full basketball demands [S10, p.716]
- **PCL (rare):** Emphasize quadriceps strength; avoid early hamstring activation as hamstrings can pull tibia posterior, stressing healing tissue [S10, p.716]

## Motor Learning Principles for Rehabilitation
Four principles of motor learning guide rehabilitation implementation (Gokeler et al.):
1. External focus of feedback
2. Implicit learning
3. Differential learning
4. Self-controlled learning and contextual interference [S10, p.714]

## Common Mistakes
1. Using time alone as the criterion for return to sport — correction: use objective functional and strength criteria
2. Ignoring basketball specificity in early phases — correction: integrate basketball from day one (holding ball, seated shooting)
3. Progressing before effusion resolves — correction: apply effusion protocol; hold or regress when 2+ effusion present
4. Skipping the prehabilitation phase before surgery — correction: pre-op quadriceps strength predicts 2-year outcomes
