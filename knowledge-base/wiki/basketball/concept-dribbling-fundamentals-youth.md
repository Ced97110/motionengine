# Dribbling Fundamentals (Youth)

## Summary
Before a player can execute any perimeter 1-on-1 move, they must master the physical relationship between hand and ball. The ball must become an extension of the hand — both hands — so that dribbling requires no conscious attention and frees the player's eyes and mind for court reads. This goal is achieved through deliberate, progressive repetition of basic touch drills until the ball obeys fingertip commands automatically.

Three distinct dribbling positions serve different game situations, and youth players must understand and feel the difference between them before adding moves.

## When to Use
- **Speed dribble:** Open court, clearing the backcourt, pushing in transition — when gaining ground is the priority
- **Control dribble:** Half-court, congested areas, against pressure — when ball protection is the priority
- **Change-of-pace dribble:** Against retreating or recovering defenders — when altering rhythm creates the offensive advantage

## Key Principles

### Hand Contact
1. Use only the **first pads (fingertips) of the fingers** — never the palm
2. The palm is a dead zone: never use it to handle the ball in any basketball fundamental
3. Spread the fingers wide, relax them — this forms a perfect cup for ball control
4. A wrist flip should be visible from the back of the wrist as a wrinkle with every dribble touch

### Speed Dribble Position
5. Body **slightly flexed** at the knee and torso — not fully upright
6. Ball bounced **2–3 feet (.6–.9 m) in front of the knee** — carom reaches waist height
7. Push the dribble out in front — away from the foot and knee
8. Eyes straight ahead — never looking at the ball

### Control Dribble Position
9. Body **significantly flexed** at the knee and torso — deep crouch
10. Off-arm **bent 90° at the elbow, parallel to the floor** — shields the ball from the defender
11. Ball **2–3 feet to the side and beside the knee** — body between ball and defender
12. Quickness and protection, not raw speed

### Vision
13. **Never watch the dribble** — eyes straight ahead in all dribbling drills
14. Head-up dribbling is a prerequisite for reading defenses and making plays

### Two-Ball Training
15. Two-ball dribbling (alternating and simultaneous) significantly accelerates ambidextrous development
16. Simultaneous variation: both balls touch hands at the same time and bounce at the same time
17. Alternating variation: one hand holds while the other bounces; alternate on each carom

## Common Mistakes
1. **Palming the ball** → correction: use only the first pads of the fingers; spread hands to form a cup
2. **Watching the dribble** → correction: eyes forward at all times; start drills at slow enough speed that head-up position is sustainable
3. **Upright posture on control dribble** → correction: significant knee/torso flex required; use the 90°-elbow off-arm to shield
4. **Ball too close to the foot/knee on speed dribble** → correction: push it 2–3 feet out in front to avoid turnover
5. **Using only the dominant hand** → correction: every drill is performed with both hands; dribble down with the right, back with the left
