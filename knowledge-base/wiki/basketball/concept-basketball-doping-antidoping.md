# Doping and Anti-Doping in Basketball

## Summary
Doping is fortunately not a major issue in basketball compared to endurance sports, but it is not absent. FIBA has signed the WADA Code (2004) and operates a structured testing program. Between 2010 and 2016, FIBA organized over 500 in-competition tests with ~60 out-of-competition positives per year. The most common positive tests in basketball involve cannabinoids, followed by contaminated nutritional supplements containing prohormones (notably higenamine). Performance-enhancing doping (anabolic steroids, growth hormone, amphetamines) is rare but occurs. [S10, Ch.73]

Prevention through education — especially starting at a young age — is the primary tool. Players bear personal responsibility for any substance they consume, including 'innocent' nutritional supplements.

## When to Use
- Team education sessions on banned substances
- Medical staff advising players on supplement safety
- Understanding testing protocols and player obligations
- Designing compliant recovery and performance nutrition programs

## Key Principles
1. **FIBA operates under WADA Code.** All FIBA national team players are in the registered test pool and must submit whereabouts (6 AM–11 PM, 3-month windows). Three missed tests = positive test result [S10, p.926].
2. **NBA testing is limited.** NBA players complete one test per season during their 1-month training camp after their rookie year; no random off-season testing for veterans. The NBA program historically failed to cover HGH and EPO [S10, p.926].
3. **Most positives are cannabinoids.** As cannabis legalization expands in the USA, positive tests from recreational use are increasing; performance enhancement is not the issue, but it remains prohibited under WADA [S10, p.927].
4. **Contaminated supplements are a major hidden risk.** 21 basketball players worldwide tested positive for higenamine (a prohibited prohormone found in weight loss and sports supplements) — none declared it on the label. This led to suspensions in all cases [S10, p.927].
5. **NSAIDs misuse is an indirect performance issue.** Many players use non-listed painkillers (NSAIDs) to manage chronic overuse injuries. High doses risk gastrointestinal bleeding, renal injury, or renal failure [S10, p.927].
6. **The Athlete Biological Passport tracks biological fingerprints.** ABP detects doping indirectly — not the substance, but its biological effects. FIBA had not yet implemented ABP as of 2019 [S10, pp.926–927].
7. **Players are responsible for everything they ingest.** The prohibited list need not be memorized, but players must inquire about any drug or supplement offered to them.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Verify any supplement with a qualified sports medicine professional before use; use third-party tested products (e.g., NSF Certified for Sport, Informed Sport).
- **Medical/support staff**: Must provide or ensure doping education; consequences of violations can extend to medical personnel careers.

## Variations
### FIBA Test Pools
- **Registered test pool**: National team players — subject to whereabouts requirements and out-of-competition testing
- **National league pool**: Club players — random testing by national anti-doping agencies (e.g., NADA in Germany)

### Athlete Biological Passport Modules
- **Hematological module**: Detects erythropoiesis manipulation (blood transfusions, EPO)
- **Steroidal module**: Detects anabolic hormone abuse — newer development potentially applicable to basketball

## Common Mistakes
1. **Assuming a natural-labeled supplement is WADA-compliant** → Higenamine was found in multiple unlabeled weight-loss products; always check with a sports medicine professional.
2. **Relying on NBA's limited testing protocol as a benchmark** → NBA testing is less rigorous than FIBA international standards; national team players face stricter obligations.
3. **Missing whereabouts submissions** → Three missed tests = positive result regardless of actual substance use.
4. **Using high-dose NSAIDs chronically** → Risk of renal failure; one documented case required kidney transplantation [S10, p.927].
