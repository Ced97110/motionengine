# Meniscal Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Meniscal injuries are common in basketball, occurring through twisting, cutting, or pivoting maneuvers (traumatic) or through arthritic/aging processes (degenerative). In NBA data, 59.7% of isolated meniscal injuries involved the lateral meniscus, 40.3% the medial meniscus. 19.4% of NBA athletes with isolated meniscal injuries failed to return to sport. In the WNBA, 10.5% of athletes had meniscal injuries and meniscal surgery was the second most common surgery in college women basketball players entering the league. Current evidence strongly favors meniscal repair over meniscectomy for long-term outcomes, especially in young players [S10, pp.342-346].

## When to Use
- Evaluating any basketball athlete with joint line pain, clicking, locking, or effusion
- Medical staff making repair vs. meniscectomy surgical decisions
- Coaches understanding why a meniscal injury player may need 4+ months for repair vs. shorter time for meniscectomy

## Key Principles
1. Partial or total meniscus loss creates biomechanical abnormalities — lateral meniscectomy causes proportionally greater contact stress and cartilage damage than medial due to load-transfer role and convex lateral compartment morphology [S10, p.343]
2. Meniscectomy leads to symptomatic osteoarthritis later in life — 132-fold increase in total knee replacement rate compared to age-matched peers [S10, p.343]
3. Meniscus preservation should always be attempted, especially in young basketball players — repair has higher reoperation rate but better long-term outcomes [S10, p.345]
4. Treatment zone determines approach: red-red zone → repair; white-white zone → partial meniscectomy; red-white zone → challenging decision [S10, p.346]
5. ACL reconstruction performed simultaneously enhances meniscal repair healing potential [S10, p.346]
6. RTS after isolated meniscus repair: minimum 4 months vs. shorter for partial meniscectomy [S10, p.346]

## Anatomy
- **Medial meniscus**: covers 50% of medial tibial plateau; longer in AP direction; secondary ACL stabilizer (posterior horn controls anterior tibial translation)
- **Lateral meniscus**: covers 59% of lateral tibial plateau; convex surface; carries most load transfer on lateral compartment; controls internal tibial rotation
- **Three vascular zones**:
  - Red-red (peripheral): vascularized, good healing potential after repair
  - Red-white (middle): intermediate vascularity
  - White-white (central): avascular, repair unlikely to heal [S10, p.343]
- Functions: joint congruency, load transfer, pressure distribution, impact absorption, secondary stabilization, joint nutrition, lubrication [S10, p.343]

## Epidemiology
- NBA: 129 isolated meniscal injuries over 21 seasons; 59.7% lateral, 40.3% medial; higher in players under 30 and BMI >25 kg/m² [S10, p.342]
- WNBA combine (506 players, 2000–2008): 10.5% had meniscal injuries; meniscus surgery = second most common surgery in college women entering WNBA [S10, p.342]
- Meniscal injuries not found to affect career length in WNBA or vary by playing position [S10, p.343]
- ACL injuries associated with increased odds of concomitant lateral meniscus injury in basketball [S10, p.343]

## Diagnosis

### History
- Acute traumatic: sudden joint line pain, effusion, clicking or locking
- Degenerative: chronic recurrent pain with effusion, often no distinct injury event
- Suspect when: joint line pain provoked by hyperflexion, directional change, crossing legs, catching foot on surface [S10, p.343]

### Physical Examination
Assess gait, ROM, laxity, limb alignment, effusion, patellofemoral joint. Specific tests:
- **Joint line tenderness** (palpation): most sensitive and specific test for isolated meniscal pathology [S10, p.344]
- **McMurray test**: supine; knee extended from full flexion while internally then externally rotating tibia; positive = tenderness/crepitation along joint line [S10, p.344]
- **Apley test**: prone, knee at 90°; tibia compressed on distal femur and rotated; positive = pain relieved when maneuver performed without compression [S10, p.344]
- **Thessaly test**: single-leg stance, flat-footed; patient axially rotates knee several times at 5° then 20° flexion; positive = medial/lateral joint line pain or mechanical symptoms [S10, p.344]

### Imaging
- **MRI**: most accurate, noninvasive; high internal signal ending at articular surface = meniscal tear; improved specificity if visible on multiple consecutive images [S10, p.344]
- Tear shapes identified: vertical, horizontal, radial, bucket handle ("double PCL sign" on sagittal view), fish mouth (degenerative) [S10, p.344]
- Posterior root tears challenging to diagnose: posterior medial meniscal root avulsion may show "ghost sign" (absence of posterior horn on MRI) [S10, p.345]

## Treatment Decision-Making

### Zone-Based Treatment
- **Red-red zone** (peripheral): repair — especially when combined with ACL reconstruction
- **White-white zone** (central): partial meniscectomy
- **Red-white zone**: challenging — fibrin clot, rasping, perforations/trephinations to encourage healing; platelet-rich plasma studied but evidence still lacking [S10, p.346]

### Tear-Type Treatment
- **Complete radial tears**: repair mandatory (complete biomechanical dysfunction) even if challenging, especially in young players [S10, p.346]
- **Horizontal cleavage tears**: historically resected; repair now shows good short and long-term results [S10, p.346]
- **Traumatic root tears** (often lateral, combined with ACL): repair in young patients — transosseous tunnels or all-inside technique [S10, p.346]
- **Degenerative tears**: some asymptomatic tears should be left alone [S10, p.343]

### Repair vs. Meniscectomy Outcomes
- Repair: 60% complete healing, 25% partial healing, 15% failure [S10, p.346]
- Partial meniscectomy: good short-term results; deteriorate over time especially when lateral meniscus involved [S10, p.346]
- Repair associated with better long-term outcomes despite higher reoperation rate [S10, p.345]

### Meniscus Replacement
- Allograft transplantation or scaffold for chronic, total/partial meniscus defects
- No evidence that these procedures allow professional basketball players to return to the same level of sport [S10, p.346]

### Professional Athlete Considerations
- Treatment decision may include non-medical factors: time in season, contract status, player goals, proximity to important games
- Risks/benefits of repair vs. meniscectomy and long-term effects must be clearly communicated to the player and coaching staff [S10, p.346]

## Return to Sport Timelines
- Partial meniscectomy: generally shorter RTS
- Isolated meniscus repair: minimum 4 months [S10, p.346]
- Lateral meniscectomy: longer RTS delay than medial meniscectomy due to greater functional significance [S10, p.345]

## Common Mistakes
1. Choosing meniscectomy over repair in a young basketball player for faster RTS → sacrifices long-term knee health [S10, p.345]
2. Treating all degenerative tears — some asymptomatic tears found incidentally on MRI should be left alone [S10, p.343]
3. Overrelying on MRI for decision-making — use clinical exam as the primary guide [S10, p.340]
4. Missing posterior root tears — "ghost sign" on MRI and anteromedial meniscal extrusion are clues [S10, p.345]
5. Performing lateral meniscectomy without recognizing its disproportionate long-term consequences [S10, p.343]
