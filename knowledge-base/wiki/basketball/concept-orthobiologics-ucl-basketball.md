# PRP for UCL (Elbow) Injuries — Application to Basketball

## Summary
Ulnar collateral ligament (UCL) injuries of the elbow primarily affect overhead throwing athletes (baseball pitchers), but are relevant in basketball for players who sustain valgus stress mechanisms. The Tommy John procedure (UCL reconstruction) has historically been the standard of care for UCL deficiency. Orthobiologics — specifically PRP — may play a role in:
1. Mid-season management to postpone surgery
2. Adjunct during surgery to promote healing
3. Sole treatment for mild UCL injuries or patients electing conservative management [S10, p.595]

The evidence base, while limited and primarily from baseball, suggests PRP can be considered for partial UCL tears refractory to initial conservative treatment [S10, p.598].

## When to Use
- Partial UCL tears that have failed 2+ months of conservative treatment
- Athletes who cannot afford time off for surgery mid-season
- Grade 1-2 UCL injuries as a non-operative treatment option
- **NOT recommended** as primary treatment for complete UCL tears requiring surgery

## Key Principles

### Clinical Evidence
1. **Dines et al. (n=44 baseball players, partial UCL tears)**: PRP injection + rehabilitation — 73% good to excellent outcome, 67% of professional players returned to professional play [S10, p.595].
2. **Podesta et al. (n=34 athletes, failed 2 months conservative tx)**: PRP injection under ultrasound — 88% returned to same level of play, average RTP 12 weeks; only 1 patient required surgery [S10, p.595].
3. **McQueen et al. (comparative analysis, professional baseball)**: PRP group had longer time before returning to throwing (64 days vs. 51 days, p<0.001), potentially explained by delay between injury and PRP injection (mean 14.5 days). No significant effect on likelihood of surgical intervention [S10, p.595].
4. **Kato et al. (n=30 baseball players, LP-PRP + ultrasound-guided trephination)**: 26/30 returned to sport at pre-injury level in avg 12.4 weeks; 4 required surgery (3 grade 2, 1 grade 3; mostly distal tears) [S10, pp.595-596].

### Take-Home Message from Source
5. **Sufficient evidence to consider PRP for UCL injuries refractory to first-line conservative treatment** — however, conflicting results exist and athletes with significant RTP timeline restrictions must weigh the decision carefully [S10, p.598].
6. Limited evidence on cell-based therapies for UCL injuries [S10, p.596].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Injecting immediately after injury without conservative trial** → PRP should be reserved for cases that have failed 2+ months of conservative treatment [S10, p.595].
2. **Delaying injection too long** → McQueen et al. found a mean 14.5-day delay from injury to injection may account for suboptimal outcomes [S10, p.595].
3. **Using PRP for complete UCL tears** → surgical management (Tommy John) remains the standard of care for complete UCL deficiency [S10, p.595].
