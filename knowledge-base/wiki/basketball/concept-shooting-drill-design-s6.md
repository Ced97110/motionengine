# Shooting Drill Design Principles (S6)

## Summary
Effective shooting drills must be game-realistic AND time-efficient. They must incorporate competitive pressure, pre-set standards for attempts and makes, clock-based urgency, and the same passes, cuts, and locations that players will experience in games. Simply shooting from a spot without these elements does not prepare players for game performance. [S6, pp.16-18]

The design framework applies to every shooting drill — individual, small group, or full team — and creates a practice environment where shooters, passers, and rebounders are all working at maximum intensity simultaneously.

## When to Use
- When designing any new shooting drill for practice
- When evaluating whether existing shooting drills are producing game-speed improvement
- When building an accountability and competitive culture within shooting practice

## Key Principles
1. **Stress both accuracy AND quantity.** Quantity standards force game speed; accuracy standards ensure technique is maintained. Both must be pre-set. [S6, p.17]
2. **Every shooting drill has a pre-set attempt standard AND a make standard.** The specific number of shots that must be taken, and the number that must be made, are set before the drill begins. [S6, p.17]
3. **Compete against the clock.** Using the scoreboard clock gives all players a common, visible, constant opponent. It prevents shooters from taking too much time. Coaches can use John Wooden's cue: "Be quick, but don't be in a hurry." [S6, p.17]
4. **Use game-realistic locations.** Study the offense to identify where shots will be created. Place passers where they will pass in game situations. Require shooters to cut and break to spots they will actually use in games. [S6, p.18]
5. **Use game-realistic pass types.** Passers must use the same types of passes they will use in games — at game speed. [S6, p.18]
6. **Incorporate rewards and light penalties.** Winners get water breaks or recognition; losers do sprints, push-ups, or sit-ups. Light penalties should be true penalties but never harsh. [S6, p.17]
7. **Shoot free throws after strenuous drills.** Players must learn to shoot when fatigued. Always shoot two FTs at a time (game-accurate). [S6, p.5]
8. **Accelerate rebounders, passers, AND shooters.** All three roles must go at game speed — not just the shooter. The rebounder's hustle to outlet and the passer's quickness to deliver both determine the shooter's rhythm. [S6, p.17]
9. **Conditioning is a by-product.** When all players meet quantity and quality standards consistently, conditioning improves automatically within shooting drills. [S6, p.18]
10. **Incorporate multiple offensive techniques in each drill.** Design drills to simultaneously practice passing, rebounding, cutting, coming off screens, catching, pivoting, and shooting — not just the shot itself. [S6, p.17]
11. **Sometimes add dummy defenders.** Have managers hold hands up in front of the shooter to simulate contest pressure and make the drill more realistic. [S6, p.18]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Setting only attempt standards, not make standards** → Without a make requirement, players unconsciously rush shots with no accuracy accountability. Set both. [S6, p.17]
2. **Starting shooters at the shooting spot** → In games, shooters cut to spots. Begin shooters at their initial offensive location and require a realistic cut. [S6, p.18]
3. **Using convenient passes instead of game passes** → Require passers to deliver the same pass type (bounce, skip, overhead) they would use in that game situation. [S6, p.18]
4. **Only accelerating the shooter** → Rebounders and passers who are slow undermine the shooter's rhythm and reduce total attempts. All three must be pushed. [S6, p.17]
5. **Ending on a missed shot** → Always require a final make to conclude a shooting drill. [S6, p.6]
