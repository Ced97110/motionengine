# 1-on-1 Reads and Attacks

## Summary
Every 1-on-1 offensive situation presents a defender in a specific stance with a specific coverage. The skilled offensive player reads these precisely and responds with the correct counter move — not the move they prefer, but the move the defense gives them. This read-and-react system is organized into three phases: before the dribble (live ball), during the dribble, and after the dribble (dead ball).

The core principle is that offensive players must create **separation** to earn a quality shot — one that is on balance, within the shooter's range, and taken without a hand in the face. Defense wins when it denies separation. Offense wins when it exploits whatever the defense gives.

## When to Use
- Any time a player receives the ball in the half court with a live dribble (before-dribble reads)
- Any time a player is attacking off the dribble against a closing or overplaying defender (during-dribble reads)
- Any time a player has used their dribble and must create a shot or pass from a dead ball (after-dribble reads)
- When scouting specific defenders who have predictable coverage tendencies

## Key Principles

### Before the Dribble
1. **Loose coverage → shoot.** If the defender gives space, take the open shot immediately — never dribble into pressure you don't need
2. **Tight coverage, no overplay → drive.** Use a direct drive step (front-foot-to-free-foot) or crossover step (front-foot-to-pivot-foot)
3. **Overplay → attack toward the overplay.** If the defender overplays the left side ("overplay left"), they want the attacker to go right — attack left instead with a crossover step
4. **Defender stationary and tight → get them moving.** Use a jab step or jab-step crossover to force a defensive reaction before committing to a drive or shot
5. **Jab-step direct drive and jab-step crossover are counter moves** to each other — one goes right, the other left; the attacker chooses based on defender's reaction

### Reacting to Defensive Footwork (Before the Dribble)
- **Defender advance step** → treat as tight coverage; attack with direct drive or crossover
- **Defender retreat step** → pull back and shoot, or start dribbling to initiate crossover or spin dribble-drive
- **Defender swing step** → pull back and shoot, or use crossover/spin dribble-drive — swing step creates significant separation

### During the Dribble
6. **Rule reversal:** Before the dribble, attack toward the overplay. During the dribble, attack **away from** the overplay
7. Continuing to dribble into an overplay typically results in a charging foul
8. **Defender slides even with dribble** → step back for a jump shot (creates separation)
9. **Defender hustles into overplay during dribble** → crossover dribble-drive or spin dribble-drive to the open side
10. Designate a "moment of decision" point (e.g., free-throw circle): read the defender and commit to either step-back or drive at that moment

### After the Dribble (Dead Ball)
11. Execute a **jump stop** to preserve choice of pivot feet
12. **Step-back jump shot** against overplay: step back with outside foot → bring inside foot to heel-toe position → square shoulders → jump
13. **Spin move** against overplay: plant inside foot toes → swing outside foot 180° → spin to face basket → heel-toe position → jump
14. **Pump fake** option: land on both feet, pump-fake, read defender's reaction, execute appropriate move
15. **Rule:** No shot after the dribble unless separation has been created; if no separation, pass

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG/SF (perimeter):** Master all before-dribble reads from triple-threat position; develop both crossover and direct drive as equal weapons; be comfortable with jab-step series and rocker step
- **PF/C (post):** Still need before-dribble reads when receiving at the elbow or short corner; the after-dribble step-back and spin are primary post-up weapons

## Variations
### Paye Drill Implementation
The complete 3-part Paye Drill teaches all three phases progressively with a coach/defender providing authentic coverage and reacting to every offensive move. See [[drill-paye-drill]].

### Two-Player Self-Coaching
In a 6-basket gym, pairs work simultaneously; players explain their reads and reactions to each other rather than receiving coach instruction — builds basketball IQ and vocabulary.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Attacking away from the overplay before the dribble** → correction: overplay means the defender gave the attacker a side — take it
2. **Continuing to dribble into an overplay** → correction: once defender overplays during the dribble, go away from the overplay or risk a charge
3. **Dribbling against loose coverage** → correction: loose coverage is an invitation to shoot — take it immediately
4. **Not creating separation before shooting after the dribble** → correction: if no separation exists, pass the ball; contested off-balance shots are low-percentage
5. **Failing to get the defender moving** → correction: against a stationary tight defender, use a jab step or jab-step crossover to force a reaction before committing
