# Lateral Ankle Sprain Rehabilitation in Basketball

## Summary
Lateral ankle sprains are the most common lower extremity injury in basketball players, affecting 40–70% of athletes and comprising 14% of NBA/WNBA injuries [S10, p.741]. While typically minor, 20% of players who sustain a lateral ankle sprain go on to develop **chronic ankle instability**. Basketball has particularly high reinjury rates because the predominant mechanism — landing on another player's foot — is unpredictable and non-modifiable. Bracing and proprioception training are the two evidence-based preventive strategies.

## When to Use
- Any basketball player presenting with lateral ankle pain, swelling, or instability following landing or cutting
- Players with history of prior ankle sprain — highest risk group for recurrence
- Preventive programming for the entire roster given the high base rate

## Key Principles

### Epidemiology
1. Ankle sprains occur more frequently during **game play than training**
2. More common in **offensive than defensive positions**
3. More common in **women than men** [S10, p.741]
4. Most common mechanism: landing on another player's foot → inversion mechanism

### Prevention
1. **Bracing** decreases ground reaction force during cutting maneuvers more than taping
2. **Taping** may be superior for jumping and sprinting performance (less impairment)
3. Both bracing and taping are recommended prophylactically for players with prior sprain history; bracing is more economical
4. **Proprioception training programs** significantly reduce inversion sprain incidence

### Acute Management (RICE + Bracing)
1. Rest, ice, compression, elevation (general inflammatory management)
2. Bracing that **allows movement** while protecting the healing ligament — not rigid immobilization
3. Screen for associated injuries: Lisfranc injury (plantar midfoot bruising), foot fracture, cuboid syndrome, fibular dysfunction, peroneal subluxation/tendinopathy

### Critical Rehabilitative Targets
1. **Strength** — peroneals (eversion), foot intrinsics, lower leg musculature
2. **Proprioception** — weight-bearing balance and neuromuscular control
3. **Dorsiflexion ROM** — limited dorsiflexion is a key reinjury risk factor; use joint mobilization as needed
4. **Cardiovascular endurance** — maintain via low-load modalities (stationary bike)
5. **Balance and neuromotor control** — closed-chain exercises, unstable surfaces

### Strengthening Progression
1. **Isometric** strengthening in neutral position (safe for healing ligament in early phase)
2. **Isotonic resistive exercise** — progress as severity allows
3. **Inversion range** — added as symptoms resolve in middle phase
4. **Closed-chain proprioception** — bilateral → unilateral stances

### Return to Play Progression
1. Straight-plane activities at **slower speeds**
2. → Cutting and pivoting at **faster speeds**
3. → Full game-speed basketball-specific activity
4. Criterion: 85–90% limb symmetry in strength and jumping; pain/swelling controlled

### Peroneal Strengthening Progression (Fig. 58.3 reference)
1. Non-weight-bearing ankle eversion with elastic band resistance
2. Foot eversion against band resistance during side-stepping
3. Foot eversion against band resistance during side-stepping **with a ball toss task** (dual-task progression)

### Foot Intrinsic Strengthening Progression (Fig. 58.2 reference)
1. "Short foot" / doming exercise — bilateral standing
2. Doming — unilateral standing with internal perturbation (opposite foot on ball)
3. Doming — unilateral standing with large trunk perturbation (touching ball to ground and returning)

## Diagnosis
- **Anterior drawer test** — assesses ATFL integrity
- **Talar tilt in 20° plantar flexion** — assesses ATFL and CFL

## Return to Play Considerations
- No well-developed standardized RTP criteria exist specifically for lateral ankle sprain [S10, p.742]
- Combine time-based AND function/performance-based criteria
- Basketball's contact mechanism of injury is unpredictable → proprioception training + bracing to reduce reinjury risk even after RTP
- Key factors: limb symmetry (strength/jumping), pain/swelling response to activity, self-reported function (Foot and Ankle Outcome Score, FAAM, Patient Specific Functional Scale)

## Common Mistakes
1. **Rigid immobilization** → prevents functional healing of proprioceptive system; use bracing that allows movement
2. **Ignoring dorsiflexion ROM deficits** → one of the strongest biomechanical risk factors for reinjury
3. **Treating as a minor injury without full rehab** → 20% develop chronic instability
4. **No proprioception training after RTP** → highest modifiable factor for reinjury prevention

## Variations
### Chronic Ankle Instability
When a player develops recurrent sprains and instability despite conservative treatment, anatomical reconstruction (modified Broström repair) may be considered. Rehabilitation post-reconstruction follows the same progressive loading framework but is more protracted.
