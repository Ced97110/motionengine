# Basic Rules of Defense

## Summary
Herb Brown's basic rules of defense are the non-negotiable principles established in every practice and team meeting. They represent the minimum defensive standard — the rules that must be honored on every single possession to give the team a chance to win. The ultimate goal underlying all 11 rules is: **limit opponents to one bad shot per possession.**

These rules form the core of Brown's man-to-man defensive system and serve as the foundation for the more detailed 44-point daily checklist. Every breakdown in a game can typically be traced to a violation of one or more of these rules.

## When to Use
- Posted in the practice facility and reviewed before every practice
- Used as the diagnostic framework when watching defensive film
- Taught to new players as the first defensive lesson before any scheme work
- Referenced by assistant coaches when correcting players mid-drill

## Key Principles
1. **Stop dribble penetration.** Control your man by keeping him in front. Keep pressure on the ball. Guard him one yard to either side — never let him split you.
2. **Rebound defensively.** Limit and eliminate offensive rebounds and second-shot put-backs. Never permit layups or easy baskets.
3. **Deny easy transition baskets.** Maintain proper defensive floor balance. Quickly changing from offense to defense stops good transition teams. The first defender back must get into the paint to protect the goal and then build out when help arrives.
4. **Always get level with the ball.** This is especially critical when your man trails the action. Get back in numbers and stop the offense from outnumbering you in transition.
5. **Deny penetrating entry passes.** Overplay opponents to force them to catch farther out on the perimeter.
6. **Pressure all passers**, especially when the dribble is dead. **Stop ball reversal.** Do not let the opposition easily change sides or reverse the basketball.
7. **Bump or chuck all cutters.** Body up to them and ride them up and over screens. Do not give shooters or cutters an option — force them to go only one way.
8. **Slip screens.** Don't allow yourself to be picked. Try to slip the pick, go inside the screen, or step in and take an offensive charge.
9. **Front or three-quarter the post on the ball side.** Force your man off the block. How you defend him is dictated by whether the ball is above or below the free-throw line. Be ready to double the baseline drive whether fronting or not. **Always stress fronting the low post.**
10. **Deny the high-low action** on post feeds. Pressure both the ball and the passer.
11. **Double the baseline drive.** Easier when fronting the low post, but can also be accomplished by stepping out on the baseline side of the post man when playing behind him.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Primary ball pressure; force the ball handler to change direction repeatedly; deny penetrating entry passes to the wings.
- **SG/SF**: Deny wing entry passes; body up to cutters and ride them over screens; rotate back first in transition.
- **PF/C**: Front the low post; double the baseline drive; eliminate all offensive rebounds and second shots; get level with the ball in transition.

## Variations
### Weak-Side Help on Post Fronting
When X1 fronts the post, the weak-side big must pre-rotate to cover the lob over the front. All other elbow and box positions must be covered by the remaining three defenders. Communication before the ball is fed to the post is mandatory.

### Ball Pressure After the Dribble Dies
Once the dribbler picks up the ball, pressure intensifies significantly. Smother the ball with active hands to prevent clean passes, force the passer to hold the ball longer, and turn the possession into a shot-clock violation.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Allowing the offense to easily reverse the ball** → Stop ball reversal aggressively; side-to-side movement of the ball resets and stretches the defense.
2. **Hiding behind the post player** → The post defender must be visible, active, and ready to double — don't disappear behind the offensive player.
3. **Not doubling the baseline drive** → The baseline drive is one of the highest-percentage offensive plays; the nearest defender must rotate and double immediately.
4. **Running past the shooter on a closeout** → Close out under control; an out-of-control closeout leaves the defender vulnerable to a ball fake and drive.
