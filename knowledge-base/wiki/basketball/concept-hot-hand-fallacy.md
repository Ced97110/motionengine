# The Hot Hand Fallacy

## Summary
The belief in the "hot hand" — that a player who has made several shots in a row is more likely to make the next one — is one of basketball's most widely held convictions. Amos Tversky (with Thomas Gilovich and Robert Vallone) tested this belief against actual NBA data in their 1985 paper "The Hot Hand in Basketball: On the Misperception of Random Sequences" (*Cognitive Psychology*, 1985).

Their conclusion: people's *perceptions* of streaks are stronger than any statistical evidence for them. Examining the 1981 Philadelphia 76ers' field goal attempt records, none of the players showed a consistently higher shooting percentage after making one or more shots in a row. The data suggests players' shooting sequences are largely random — not dependent on previous makes or misses [S16, pp.101-102].

## When to Use
- Deciding whether to feed a "hot" player at the cost of better offensive design
- Evaluating streaky shooter reputation vs. underlying data
- Player/coach education on cognitive biases in basketball decision-making
- Roster evaluation and shot selection philosophy

## Key Principles
1. **The hot hand is a cognitive bias** — people perceive patterns in random sequences more readily than they actually exist ("misperception of random sequences") [S16, p.102]
2. **Streak perceptions can be driven by framing effects** — Tversky's general/route experiments show that identical situations framed differently lead to different choices; basketball observers are similarly susceptible [S16, pp.101-102]
3. **1981 76ers data**: none of the Philadelphia players showed a statistically meaningful higher FG% after makes vs. misses [S16, p.102]
4. **Andrew Toney** — reputed to be a streaky shooter — actually showed a slight tendency to *shoot better after a miss* than after a make; the opposite of the hot hand [S16, p.102]
5. **Dr. J** — the one player with even weak hot-hand indicators, improving FG% from 52% to 53% after a make [S16, p.102]
6. **Significance testing applies here too** — any player-level hot streak claim needs to clear the statistical significance bar before being actionable [S16, pp.101-102]

## The Tversky Framing Experiments (Context)
Tversky demonstrated human irrationality with the following paired scenarios:
- **Scenario A**: Route saves 200 of 600 soldiers (certain)
- **Scenario B**: 1/3 chance all 600 saved, 2/3 chance none saved (gamble)

Most people chose Route A. When reframed as "Route A loses 400 soldiers" vs. the same Route B, most people switched to Route B — despite the scenarios being mathematically identical. This reveals that how information is framed (gains vs. losses) systematically distorts decision-making [S16, pp.101-102].

The same cognitive machinery drives hot-hand belief: we see a streak and pattern-match it to "this player is hot" rather than evaluating the actual probability.

## Coaching Implications
- **Don't disrupt good offensive structure** to feed a "hot" player — the opponent also adjusts their defense
- **Streaks are real experiences** but may not reflect predictive shooting ability
- **Shot selection** should be based on positional advantages, defensive coverage, and play design — not perceived heat
- A player who "feels hot" may be more confident, but the defense also responds — the net effect may be neutral

## Common Mistakes
1. **Disrupting offensive flow for hot players** → The defense tightens on a perceived hot player; there may be no net advantage
2. **Interpreting recent makes as evidence of sustained ability** → Random variation produces apparent streaks in short samples
3. **Treating "feel" as statistical evidence** → Players and coaches genuinely feel streaks; this doesn't make them predictive

## Caveats
- More recent research (Miller & Sanjurjo, 2018) suggests a methodological bias in the original Tversky analysis may have obscured a real but small hot-hand effect — the debate continues
- The hot hand may exist in some forms (free throws, practice shooting) even if not in game situations
- Oliver notes this chapter introduces the concept with full treatment to follow [S16, p.102]
