# ACL and Shoulder Injury Prevention

## Summary
Basketball has the highest rate of ACL tears of any sport [S2, p.163]. The two most important joints for basketball performance are the knee and the shoulder — knees are involved in virtually every movement (jumping, running, cutting), while shoulders are engaged in all shooting, blocking, and rebounding motions. Smart conditioning and prehabilitation training reduces the incidence of both ACL tears and overuse shoulder injuries.

ACL injuries are predominantly noncontact injuries — caused by how an athlete moves rather than by direct trauma. The majority occur due to a combination of three simultaneous motions: increased tibial external rotation, increased hip internal rotation and adduction, and **dynamic knee valgus** (knock-knee collapse). Prevention is best thought of as *injury reduction*: no program can guarantee zero tears, but proper training can significantly reduce the likelihood [S2, p.164].

## When to Use
- Year-round as part of team warm-up or strength training program
- Especially critical for female athletes (4–6× higher ACL injury risk than males) [S2, p.163]
- High school athletes are more susceptible than college/professional players
- Any player with a history of knee instability, stiff-knee landings, or shoulder overuse pain

## Key Principles
1. **Dynamic valgus is the primary ACL danger position** — the knee collapsing inward during landing, cutting, or pivoting is the most common mechanism of noncontact ACL injury [S2, p.164]
2. **Four neuromuscular imbalance patterns** predispose athletes to ACL injury: ligament dominance, quadriceps dominance, leg dominance, and trunk dominance [S2, p.165]
3. **Ligament dominance (dynamic valgus)** — correct by strengthening lateral hip muscles (gluteus medius, gluteus minimus)
4. **Quadriceps dominance** — correct by strengthening the entire posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, low back) to attenuate landing forces
5. **Leg dominance** — correct with single-leg strength exercises to balance left-right asymmetry
6. **Trunk dominance** — correct with core stability exercises to control trunk position during dynamic movements
7. **Exercises progress from stationary strength → dynamic power** — never begin plyometric ACL training without a strength foundation [S2, p.165]
8. **Shoulder prehabilitation** focuses on rotator cuff integrity, scapular stability, and flexibility — overuse injuries are common due to repetitive overhead shooting and defending motions [S2, p.163]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions:** Participate in prehab exercises and warm-up protocols; report knee or shoulder pain early
- **PF, C:** Pay extra attention to landing mechanics — larger, heavier players absorb greater forces on the knee
- **PG, SG, SF:** Greater cutting and direction-change demands increase ACL exposure; hip strength training is critical

## ACL Imbalance Patterns and Exercises

### Ligament Dominance (Dynamic Valgus)
- Knock-knee position during landing or cutting
- Corrective exercises: [[drill-band-squat]], [[drill-band-defensive-slide]], [[drill-side-lying-clam]]

### Quadriceps Dominance
- Stiff-knee landings; hamstrings unable to attenuate force
- Identifiable by coach observation: player does not achieve deep knee flexion on landing
- Corrective exercises: [[drill-physioball-hamstring-curl]], Russian hamstring curl

### Leg Dominance
- One leg significantly stronger than the other; imbalances stress the weaker side
- Corrective exercises: single-leg squat off box, Bulgarian split squat

### Trunk Dominance
- Poor trunk control during dynamic movements shifts stress to the knee
- Corrective exercises: BOSU round double crunch, Physioball lateral crunch

## Shoulder Prehabilitation
Shoulder stretches address the posterior capsule, latissimus dorsi, and pectoralis to restore flexibility. Strengthening exercises target scapular stabilizers and the rotator cuff:
- Stretching: [[drill-posterior-capsule-stretch]], [[drill-sleeper-stretch]], [[drill-latissimus-dorsi-stretch]], [[drill-pectoralis-stretch]], [[drill-thoracic-spine-mobility]]
- Isotonic strengthening: [[drill-ytw-shoulder-strengthening]], [[drill-side-lying-shoulder-external-rotation]], [[drill-cheerleader-shoulder-strengthening]], [[drill-overhead-press-shoulder]]
- Plyometric strengthening: [[drill-overhead-medicine-ball-toss]]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping the strength phase before plyometrics** → Always build a static strength base before adding dynamic or plyometric ACL drills
2. **Ignoring female athletes' higher risk** → Implement ACL prevention programs proactively, especially for female teams [S2, p.163]
3. **Landing with stiff knees** → Coach deep knee flexion on all landing patterns; stiff knees indicate quadriceps dominance
4. **Knee caving inward on cuts and landings** → Cue: "push knees out over toes" during all landing and cutting movements
5. **Neglecting shoulder flexibility** → Tight posterior capsule and latissimus muscles restrict shooting mechanics and increase overuse injury risk
