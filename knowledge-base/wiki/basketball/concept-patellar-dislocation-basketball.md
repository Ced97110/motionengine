# Patellar Dislocation in Basketball

## Summary
Patellar dislocation is the most common patellar injury requiring surgical intervention in basketball. It is part of the patellar instability spectrum and involves abnormal lateral movement of the patella in the patellofemoral groove. Basketball has the highest sport-specific rate of patellar dislocation (18.2%) among all athletic activities, and patellofemoral injury accounts for 10.1% of all NBA player injuries [S10, pp.334-335].

Patella dislocation typically occurs through an indirect mechanism combining rotation, quadriceps contraction, and valgus stress — the patella often reduces spontaneously with knee extension. Peak incidence is between 15-19 years of age, with females at higher overall risk. Recurrence rates are 15-60%, making accurate risk factor identification critical to management planning [S10, p.335-336].

## When to Use (Clinical Recognition)
- Acute knee swelling and pain following a rotation/cutting movement on the court
- Palpable MPFL tenderness along the medial patella border
- Apprehension when the patella is pushed laterally
- History of prior dislocation or subluxation episodes
- Adolescent player (15-19 years) with knee giving way

## Key Principles

### Mechanism and Pathoanatomy
1. **Indirect mechanism**: rotation + quadriceps contraction + valgus → lateral patella displacement; patella often self-reduces with extension [S10, p.336]
2. **MPFL**: medial patellofemoral ligament is the primary restraint to lateral patellar displacement in the first degrees of flexion — almost always disrupted during first lateral dislocation [S10, p.336]
3. **Trochlear groove function**: lateral trochlea ridge (larger, more proximal, more anterior) prevents lateral patellar excursion; dysplasia of this ridge allows unchecked lateral displacement [S10, p.336]

### Risk Factors
4. **Bony/morphologic risk factors**: trochlear dysplasia (shallow/flat/convex trochlea), patella alta (late trochlear engagement during flexion), lateral tibial tuberosity (TT-TG >20 mm), valgus knee alignment, increased femoral anteversion + external tibial torsion [S10, p.336]
5. **Soft tissue risk factors**: MPFL laxity/disruption, VMO (vastus medialis oblique) weakness — VMO is the primary dynamic medial patella stabilizer [S10, p.336]

### Clinical Examination
6. **Acute presentation**: ROM limited by pain and effusion; aspiration of hemarthrosis may be required; medial patella and MPFL areas painful on palpation [S10, p.336]
7. **Chronic instability assessment**: standing position for genu valgum, Q angle, hindfoot valgus, foot pronation, squinting patella; gait and hip rotation [S10, p.336]
8. **Apprehension test**: examiner pushes patella laterally — feeling of apprehension = positive [S10, p.336]
9. **J-sign**: sudden lateral patellar shift over the proximal lateral trochlea ridge as patient actively extends from 90° [S10, p.336]
10. **Patella glide test**: mediolateral patellar translation in full extension with relaxed quadriceps — quantified in quadrants, compared bilaterally [S10, p.336]

### Imaging
11. **X-rays**: AP (standing), lateral at 30° flexion, sunrise view — assess subluxation, osteochondral fractures, dysplasia, patellar height (patella alta), trochlear "crossing sign" (present in 96% with true dislocation history) [S10, pp.336-337]
12. **Dejour trochlear dysplasia classification**: Type A (shallow, >145°), Type B (supratrochlear spur, flat/convex), Type C (double contour), Type D (crossing sign + spur + double contour + cliff sign) [S10, p.337]
13. **MRI**: MPFL integrity, cartilage surfaces — bone marrow edema on lateral femoral condyle and medial patella border is classic post-acute dislocation finding [S10, p.337]
14. **CT**: bony morphology (trochlear dysplasia, patellar shape), TT-TG distance quantification — TT-TG >20 mm = frequent patellar instability association → consider tibial tuberosity osteotomy [S10, p.337]

### Management
15. **First-time dislocation**: majority managed conservatively EXCEPT when osteochondral fracture or major chondral injury requires acute fixation, or when dislocation is persistent [S10, p.337]
16. **Operative indications**: recurrent dislocations + failure of conservative management + predisposing anatomic factors [S10, p.337]
17. **MPFL reconstruction**: primary surgical option for recurrent lateral patellar dislocation in the absence of significant anatomic morphologic abnormalities [S10, p.337]
18. **VMO strengthening**: a primary rehabilitation target for physiotherapy in lateral patellar instability [S10, p.336]
19. **Management algorithm** must account for: specific risk factors present, dislocation history, and level of play [S10, p.337]
20. **RTS timelines**: 20% of high school athletes require >3 weeks; 37% of dislocation patients and 11% of subluxation patients missed the rest of their season [S10, p.336]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: report any "giving way" of the knee, especially with rotation or cutting — especially adolescents
- **Female players**: higher risk overall; VMO strengthening programs should be standard
- **Medical staff**: aspiration of hemarthrosis may be necessary for adequate exam; always obtain imaging to rule out osteochondral fracture before conservative management

## Common Mistakes
1. **Missing osteochondral fracture** → Always image after first-time dislocation; osteochondral fragments require acute surgical fixation
2. **Neglecting VMO rehabilitation** → VMO is the primary dynamic stabilizer; all conservative programs must include aggressive VMO strengthening
3. **Early return to play** → Patellar dislocation and subluxation are among the injuries most likely to require surgical repair if RTP is premature; 37% miss the rest of the season even with non-surgical management
4. **Ignoring TT-TG distance** → A TT-TG >20 mm alters the surgical decision tree toward tibial tuberosity osteotomy
