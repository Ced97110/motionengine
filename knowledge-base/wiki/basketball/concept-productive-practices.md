# Productive Practices — the head coach

## Summary
the head coach (pro team head coach) presents a comprehensive practice philosophy built on the principle that preparation is more than just showing up — it encompasses film sessions, individual skill development, strength and conditioning, staff meetings, and networking. His system is built around a master calendar, minute-by-minute practice plans, part-whole teaching, and a team culture of unity and accountability. The framework applies from youth basketball through the pro. [S7, pp.273-276]

## When to Use
- Season-long practice planning starting at training camp
- Daily practice design throughout the season
- Any time a coach wants to transition from reactive to proactive planning

## Key Principles
1. **Philosophy before plan**: "A team needs to have both a philosophy and a plan, and all members of the team need to buy into both." Philosophy is the basis; the plan implements it. [S7, p.273]
2. **Never practice just to practice**: Every session must have a goal — early-season goal is getting players clear on basketball philosophy. [S7, p.273]
3. **Practice what you want in games**: "If your practices are detailed and precise, your players will probably carry that into games as well... You can't practice one way and expect to play another." [S7, p.274]
4. **Part-whole teaching**: Break down and isolate specific skills; then build the whole back up. From individual breakdown drills to 5v5, every step must ladder into game execution.
5. **Minute-by-minute planning**: Every practice has a detailed plan created in a pre-practice staff meeting. The plan keeps practices organized and moving at an efficient, intense pace.
6. **Pace and recovery**: "Sometimes a crisp 90-minute practice is more effective than a grueling three-hour session." Read the team; allow recovery as necessary. [S7, p.274]

## Master Calendar
- Created at the start of the season; labels every practice day, game day, and off day
- Lists skills to work on and objectives for each day, week, and month
- Objectives cover: individual player development, team offense and defense, special situations, message of the day
- Once constructed, individual practices are scripted within it

## Practice Format

### Pre-Practice (30 min before official start)
- Individual or small-group skill development
- Daily skill work is essential

### Opening Teaching Segment (before stretch)
- Walk-through or talk-through of any skill, concept, or play set to introduce, review, or improve
- Teaching *before* physical activity so players absorb concepts while fresh
- "If we walk through and teach things slowly at the beginning, practice flows smoothly once we get the players moving." [S7, p.276]
- Example: If side PnR defense was poor the night before → walk through adjustments at the start → no stopping for review during the PnR defensive drill segment

### Warm-Up Routine (~20 min)
- Strength and conditioning staff leads: movement, stretching, Pilates, yoga, stationary bikes, kinetic stretching
- Final 5 minutes: closeout or stance drill to transition into basketball-specific movement

### Practice Body
- Organized according to the day's predetermined plan
- Flexibility built in for unexpected needs or player adjustments
- Flow: individual skill work → team concept installation → competitive team drills → situational work → free throws → review

### Team Huddle (opening ritual)
- Players sprint to midcourt and start clapping — no hiding behind teammates
- Circle formation; brief personal and team updates (birthdays, new fathers, etc.) to build camaraderie
- "With all the practices, meetings, trips, and games, we end up spending more time with each other during the season than we do with our own families." [S7, p.276]
- Review previous day's practice or previous night's game: what was done well, what needs improvement — kept brief

## Staff Meeting Before Practice
- Head coach arrives with a clear agenda but solicits staff input
- "We all value each other's opinion and, even if we differ in the meeting room, we're always united on the court." [S7, p.274]
- Adjustments made based on player condition, last game/practice performance, upcoming opponent

## Common Mistakes
1. **Practicing without a goal** → every segment must have a stated purpose that transfers to game execution
2. **Teaching on the fly during drills** → walk through new concepts *before* the contact portion of practice begins
3. **Overtraining** → "There's a fine line between long, demanding practices and overtraining." Read the team; fresh players play better. [S7, p.274]
4. **Skipping pre-practice skill work** → individual development before team practice is where individual skill gaps are closed
5. **Uniform standards** → requiring everyone (coaches included) to tuck in shirts and follow practice rituals establishes non-negotiable standards
