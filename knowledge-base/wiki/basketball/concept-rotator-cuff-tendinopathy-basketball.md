# Rotator Cuff Pathology in Basketball: Orthobiologic Management

## Summary
Rotator cuff tendinopathy and tears affect basketball players through repeated overhead actions (shooting, rebounding, shot-blocking, passing). PRP has been extensively studied for both rotator cuff tendinopathy (injection) and rotator cuff repair (surgical augmentation). Current evidence is conflicting and heterogenous: PRP injection shows mixed results for tendinopathy, and meta-analyses of PRP augmentation at rotator cuff repair show no improvement in clinical outcomes or retear rates. BMAC augmentation shows more promising early results [S10, Ch.45].

## When to Use
- Rotator cuff tendinopathy refractory to physical therapy, NSAIDs, and corticosteroid injections
- As a biological augmentation adjunct during surgical rotator cuff repair (within clinical trial or institutional protocol)
- BMAC may be considered for larger tears requiring stronger biological stimulus

## Key Principles

### Rotator Cuff Tendinopathy — PRP Injection
1. Evidence is conflicting across level-I RCTs
2. Rha et al. (2013): LR-PRP superior to dry needling — better SPADI scores and reduced pain at 6 weeks to 6 months (17.7 vs. 29.5, p<0.05) [S10, p.576]
3. Kesikburun et al. (2013): LR-PRP vs. saline — no difference in WORC, SPADI, or VAS at 1-year follow-up [S10, p.576]
4. Ilhanli et al. (2015): PRP better DASH scores; physical therapy group had better ROM and VAS [S10, p.576]
5. Nejati et al. (2017): Physical therapy *superior* to PRP for functionality, ROM, and pain reduction at 1 and 3 months [S10, p.576]
6. Systematic review: PRP may NOT be beneficial short-term for rotator cuff disease; confounded by lack of PRP cytology standardization [S10, p.572]

### Rotator Cuff Repair — PRP Augmentation
1. Multiple meta-analyses (Saltzman et al., Filardo et al.) demonstrate NO improvement in clinical outcomes or retear rates with PRP augmentation of rotator cuff repair [S10, p.572]
2. Individual studies show variable short-term pain benefits but inconsistent structural outcomes
3. Platelet-rich fibrin matrix (PRFM) studies (Gumina, Weber, Zumstein) similarly show no functional benefit [S10, pp.577-578]

### BMAC Augmentation
1. Hernigou et al. (case-controlled, n=45+45): BMAC augmentation of rotator cuff repair
   - 100% BMAC group showed healed rotator cuff on MRI at 6 months vs. 67% control
   - Fewer additional ruptures in BMAC group at 10-year follow-up [S10, p.572]
2. Kim et al. (n=24): BMAC-PRP injection vs. rotator cuff exercise
   - BMAC-PRP group: lower VAS at 3 months (p=0.039)
   - BMAC-PRP group: significantly greater ASES scores at 3 months (p=0.011) [S10, p.572]

## Clinical Recommendation
> Current literature is conflicting and heterogenous regarding the use of PRP for rotator cuff pathology. PRP augmentation of rotator cuff repair has not demonstrated consistent improvement in clinical outcomes or retear rates in meta-analyses. BMAC shows the most promising early results for rotator cuff repair augmentation but requires further investigation. [S10, p.579]

## Common Mistakes
1. Choosing PRP over physical therapy for rotator cuff tendinopathy without trialing PT first — multiple studies show PT is at least equally effective
2. Expecting PRP repair augmentation to reduce retear rates based on individual studies — meta-analyses do not support this
3. Ignoring leukocyte composition — it may be critical for the rotator cuff specifically
4. Not standardizing PRP preparation — variation in cytology makes results non-generalizable
