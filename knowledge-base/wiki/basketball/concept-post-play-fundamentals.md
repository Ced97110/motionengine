# Post Play Fundamentals — an iconic post player

## Summary
an iconic post player's foundational principles for playing the post position cover everything from how to sense the defender through contact, to calling for the ball, to deciding within three counts whether to attack or pass. The overarching theme is that elite post play is about capitalizing on defensive mistakes rather than relying on size and strength alone. A post player's will and heart matter more than stature or bulk. [S7, pp.37-50]

## When to Use
- Any time a post player establishes position on the block or high post
- Against all types of defensive coverage (fronting, three-quarter, behind)
- Before initiating any post move or shot

## Key Principles
1. **See through touch**: Know where the defender is by maintaining physical contact within the rules — using buttocks, legs, and arms. "The post must see his defender through touch."
2. **Give a definite target**: Call for the ball by yelling "Ball!" and extend the open hand away from the defender, arm extended or bent at 45 degrees. Do NOT extend both arms straight — it invites steals from behind.
3. **Stay open 2-3 seconds**: Constantly adjust positioning and force the defender to maintain lane position by repeatedly trying to back into the three-second area.
4. **Secure the catch**: As soon as the ball arrives, the other hand immediately slaps it. Grip with fingers, one hand below and one above. Forearms at chest level, elbows forming a T with the body.
5. **Look over the high shoulder**: Immediately after catching, look inside the lane over the top shoulder to locate the defender and spot any cutting teammates.
6. **Attack the direction of the pass**: Go to the rim on the side from which you received the pass — the defender normally cannot cover that direction. Only use a countermove if that direction is blocked.
7. **Check for the double team**: Before making any offensive move, sense whether a double team is arriving. If yes, wait a count for it to develop, then pass to the open teammate created by the double.
8. **Read the defense**: React to what the defender does — force a mistake (jumping a fake, leaning, sliding). Outstanding post players capitalize on defensive errors.
9. **Make a move within three counts**: The post must decide within three counts to play one-on-one or pass. Holding the ball longer allows the defense to rotate and trap.
10. **Sell a good fake**: Actually start the movement to make a fake believable. Ball slightly over the forehead, held firmly with two hands, looking toward the rim, legs bent and ready to explode.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PF/C**: Execute all principles above; be aggressive about making contact; do not overfake (arms too high, legs extended) or underfake (ball barely moves).

## Variations
### Overfaking vs. Underfaking
A post is **overfaking** when arms extend too much and legs straighten — losing power and balance. A post is **underfaking** when the ball barely moves — the defender has no reaction to beat. The correct fake brings the ball slightly over the forehead while keeping legs bent.

### Shooting With Defender Contact
When a defender is on the side of an offensive player going up for a shot: put the inside leg between the defender's legs, lean so the shoulder makes contact with the defender's chest, then shoot with the opposite hand. This freezes the defender and reduces the chance of a blocked shot. Do NOT push the defender away with the shoulder or nonshooting arm. [S7, p.38]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Both arms extended for the catch** → Keep the target hand open and away from the defender; extend only one arm to prevent steals from behind.
2. **Holding the ball more than three counts** → Gives defense time to rotate and trap; commit to a move or pass within three counts.
3. **Going opposite the direction of the pass** → The default first move should always attack the direction of the pass unless blocked.
4. **Fake without bending legs** → Standing straight on a fake removes the explosiveness needed to actually shoot or drive.
