# Bank Shot

## Summary
The bank shot is a shot that uses the backboard as an intermediary surface — the ball is aimed at the backboard square and redirected into the basket. Despite being a high-percentage shot due to its forgiving target and trajectory, it has fallen out of fashion in modern basketball in favor of aesthetically appealing direct shots. Coaches like John Wooden made the bank shot a daily practice staple, and players who master it gain a virtually unguardable weapon in the mid-post and midrange areas.

Notable practitioners include Tim Duncan, a legendary scorer, a legendary point-forward, Julius Erving, George Gervin, and the classic High-Post teams of the 1960s–70s. The bank shot is especially effective against taller defenders because its higher arc makes it harder to block. [S5, pp.82-84]

## When to Use
- Whenever the offensive player is at approximately a **45-degree angle** to the basket and within **15 feet (4.5 m)**
- When attacking a taller or longer defender who is in good shot-blocking position
- On pull-up jump shots after beating a defender off the dribble
- In low-post and mid-post turnaround scenarios
- Avoid: when the angle flattens out below the low-post block toward the baseline; avoid beyond 15 feet unless the player has exceptional touch [S5, p.84]

## Key Principles
1. **Target the backboard square** — aim for the top corner of the small painted square closest to you, not the middle of the rim as on a standard jump shot [S5, p.84]
2. **45-degree angle rule** — the optimal angle is just above the low-post block, extended out to 15 feet; the low-post block is a reliable on-court landmark for this angle [S5, p.84]
3. **Eyes on the target** — never watch the ball in flight; keep eyes locked on the corner of the backboard square throughout the shot [S5, p.84]
4. **Same mechanics as the jump shot** — stance, arm position, ball receipt, release, and follow-through are identical; only the target and angle differ [S5, p.83]
5. **Don't shoot higher or harder** — a common misconception; touch and arc develop through practice, not by changing force [S5, p.84]
6. **Commit and shoot** — hesitation causes short shots; visualize the ball kissing off the square and dropping through, then execute without second-guessing [S5, p.84]
7. **Higher trajectory is an advantage** — banking the ball requires a higher arc, which makes it harder for defenders to block [S5, p.90]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: The bank shot is position-agnostic at the 45-degree midrange zone. Post players (PF, C) use it heavily on turnaround bank shots. Guards (PG, SG) use it on pull-up situations after driving off the dribble.

## Variations
### Pull-Up Bank Shot
Shooting the bank shot after a dribble drive. Attack from the three-point line along the wing, speed-dribble toward the lane, pull up at 12–15 feet (3.6–4.5 m), and bank. Use the dribble as a "pass to yourself" — keep the ball out in front, not to the side, so your one-two step is clean. Practice from both sidelines with both hands. [S5, pp.88-90]

### Turnaround Bank Shot
An especially potent post move. Begin on the low block, spinout toward the wing at a 45-degree angle, take short choppy steps then a longer stride, and use either an inside or front pivot to square to the basket and elevate into the bank shot. All four pivot combinations should be mastered: outside right, outside left, inside right, inside left. The turnaround bank shot is described as "virtually unguardable" when executed off either pivot foot from both sides. [S5, pp.88-89]

### Stop-and-Pop Bank Shot
A variation popularized by John Wooden's High-Post teams: the ball handler attacks the floor wide, angles in at 45 degrees, and stops into a jump shot off the glass. A natural bridge between the moving layup and the full pull-up bank shot. [S5, p.82]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Watching the ball in flight** → Keep eyes locked on the backboard square corner from pre-shot through follow-through [S5, p.84]
2. **Shooting higher and harder** → Maintain identical mechanics to the jump shot; trust the geometry of the backboard square [S5, p.84]
3. **Hesitating when in the right position** → Hesitation causes short shots and missed opportunities; commit to the shot immediately [S5, p.84]
4. **Using the bank shot from below the low-post block angle** → Flattening out toward the baseline makes the geometry too severe; switch to a direct shot [S5, p.84]
5. **Attempting bank shots beyond 15 feet without the requisite touch** → Build skill from inside out; only extend distance after mastering the core 45°/15ft zone [S5, pp.84-85]
