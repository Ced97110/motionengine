# The Three Explosive Qualities: Power, Functional Speed, and Agility

## Summary
True athletic explosiveness requires three distinct but interconnected qualities: **Power**, **Functional Speed**, and **Agility**. Paul Wade defines these precisely to expose a critical gap in most modern training programs — that box jumps, cone drills, and Olympic lifting each address only one or two of these qualities, never all three simultaneously. Only traditional calisthenics explosives (kip-ups, flips, muscle-ups) train all three together [S9, pp.5-6].

For basketball players, understanding this framework matters enormously. A player who only trains plyometrics (box jumps) develops power but lacks true agility. A player who only does cone drills develops agility and speed but not power. Complete basketball athleticism — the ability to explode off the floor, change direction at full speed, and land safely — requires training all three qualities.

## When to Use
- As a framework for evaluating any athletic training method
- When designing off-season conditioning programs for basketball players
- When explaining to players why different training modalities are included
- When assessing which explosive quality a player is lacking

## Key Principles
1. **Power = Strength × Speed**: The athlete must move a load (including their own bodyweight) with both strength and speed. A slow strong person is not powerful [S9, p.5].
2. **Functional Speed = Entire body moves rapidly**: Speed of an isolated body part (fast hands, fast feet) is a misnomer. The whole body must move quickly — legs, waist, torso, shoulders, arms all simultaneously [S9, pp.8-9].
3. **Agility = Direction change at high velocity**: The body must radically alter its path while moving fast. Going straight up and down (box jumps) or straight along a bar (Olympic lifts) does not constitute agility [S9, p.5].
4. **Reflexes are the fourth quality**: Reflexes — the nervous system's automatic movements in response to stimuli — are the sum of all three. They bypass the conscious mind and are trained automatically through ballistic practice [S9, pp.1-2].
5. **All three must be trained together**: Any method that checks only two of the three boxes produces an incomplete explosive athlete [S9, p.6].

## Comparison of Training Methods

| Method | Power | Functional Speed | Agility |
|---|---|---|---|
| Box Work (Plyometrics) | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ |
| Cone Training | ✗ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Olympic Lifting | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Explosive Calisthenics | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |

[S9, p.5]

**Why box work fails agility**: Techniques are variations of going up and down — no change of body angle [S9, p.5].

**Why cone drills fail power**: Load demands are barely above running — no explosive force production [S9, p.5].

**Why Olympic lifting fails both speed and agility**: The feet barely leave the floor; the body doesn't move far or change direction [S9, pp.5-6].

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Develop all three qualities — power for vertical jump and contact, functional speed for fast breaks and closeouts, agility for change of direction on defense and drives.
- **PG/SG**: Agility is the highest priority for on-ball defense and creating off the dribble.
- **SF/PF**: Power is equally critical for rebounding and posting up.
- **C**: Functional speed often the most underdeveloped quality — train whole-body movement, not just isolated leg drills.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Only doing box jumps** → Add rotational and direction-change movements to build true agility alongside power.
2. **Only doing cone drills** → Add loaded explosive work to develop the power component.
3. **Mistaking isolated speed for functional speed** → Train movements that use the entire kinetic chain, not just fast hands or quick feet.
4. **Ignoring reflexes** → Reflexes improve automatically through ballistic practice; you cannot isolate them. Train the explosive movements and reflexes follow [S9, p.2].

## Variations
### Conditioned vs. Unconditioned Reflexes
Some reflexes are innate (unconditioned), but reflexes can also be conditioned through deliberate practice of explosive movements. Every time an athlete performs a ballistic calisthenics exercise, the nervous system is trained to react faster and more efficiently in that pattern [S9, p.2].
