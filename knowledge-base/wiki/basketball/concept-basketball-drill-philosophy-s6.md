# Basketball Drill Philosophy (S6)

## Summary
Successful basketball coaches must be great teachers and motivators, but they also need tools and instruments that allow their players to learn and improve skills. The quality of a drill depends entirely on the quality of its teaching. A drill is not a good drill unless the coach teaches it in a great manner. Every drill must be as game-realistic as possible, must demand full concentration, and must incorporate competition and accountability.

This philosophy was developed over many years of coaching at the high school and junior college levels and refined through observation of top coaches at every level — practices, clinics, books, and film. [S6, pp.1-6]

## When to Use
- As the foundational framework for designing any practice session
- When evaluating whether existing drills are achieving their intended purpose
- When building or rebuilding a program's practice culture

## Key Principles
1. **Make practice game-realistic** — use game-speed passes, game locations for shooters, game-type cuts. Do not allow artificial comfort. [S6, p.3]
2. **Teach at practice** — the practice facility is the coach's classroom. Assume players know nothing and start with basics. [S6, p.3]
3. **Pay attention to all details** — be a stickler for the smallest details in a positive manner. [S6, p.3]
4. **Be enthusiastic** — players must see the coach love to teach. If the coach loves practice, players will love to come. [S6, p.3]
5. **Have a detailed practice plan** — "Plan your work, and work your plan." The practice plan is the coach's lesson plan. [S6, p.5]
6. **Incorporate the whole-part-whole method** — show the full skill, break it into parts, return to the whole. [S6, p.3]
7. **Demand attention** — players must give coaches their eyes and ears at all times. [S6, p.3]
8. **Make practice tougher than games** — physically and mentally more demanding. [S6, p.3]
9. **Drills must require concentration** — players who concentrate in practice focus better in games. [S6, pp.3-4]
10. **No standing around** — keep all players and coaches active during all drills. [S6, p.4]
11. **Stress teamwork and communication** — on both offense and defense. [S6, p.4]
12. **Reward correct performance enthusiastically** — more reward for good performance than punishment for errors. [S6, p.4]
13. **Use free throws as built-in accountability** — after physical work, shoot 1-and-1: miss front end = full sprint, make front/miss second = half sprint, make both = water. [S6, p.4]
14. **Set pre-set quantity AND quality standards** — a specific number of attempts AND a specific number of makes for every shooting drill. [S6, p.4-5]
15. **Use the clock as an opponent** — scoreboard clock makes everyone compete against a common, visible adversary. "Be quick, but don't be in a hurry." [S6, p.17]
16. **Shoot free throws after strenuous activity** — players must learn to shoot when winded and fatigued. [S6, p.5]
17. **Include competition in every drill** — individual, small group, and team competition; losers run sprints, do push-ups, or sit-ups. [S6, p.5]
18. **Critique positively** — before criticizing, ask: "What did you do correctly?" then "What did you do incorrectly?" [S6, p.5]
19. **Conditioning is embedded in drills** — do not omit physical conditioning; build it into drill design. [S6, p.5]
20. **Never end on a negative note** — never finish a shooting drill on a miss; never conclude a scrimmage on a turnover. [S6, p.6]
21. **Do not end practice with conditioning** — the last activity should be positive and motivating, making players eager for the next practice. [S6, p.6]
22. **Occasionally cancel practice** — mid-season mental/physical breaks restore intensity; alternative activities (volleyball, movie, pizza) can re-energize the team. [S6, p.6]
23. **Post manager-recorded statistics** — players respond when their numbers are visible to the team. [S6, p.5]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Execute every drill at game speed. Know the drill's purpose. Rotate assignments quickly (≤5 seconds). Compete to win every rep.

## Common Mistakes
1. Players standing around during drills → assign all players a role (shooter, passer, rebounder) at all times.
2. Shooting drills at practice pace, not game pace → use the scoreboard clock as a visible opponent.
3. Ending practice with conditioning → end with a positive, rewarding activity to build next-day motivation.
4. Only punishing errors → consistently reward correct performance first.
5. Running drills without pre-set standards → set specific attempt and make targets before every drill begins.
