# Agility and Balance Training

## Summary
Agility encompasses two interrelated motor functions: (1) the ability to explosively start, decelerate, change direction, and re-accelerate while minimizing loss of speed and maintaining body control, and (2) the ability to coordinate multiple sport-specific tasks simultaneously (e.g., dribbling while scanning the court). Studies show that agility is the **primary determining factor for predicting success in sport** [S8, p.72].

Contrary to common belief, agility is not primarily genetic and can be significantly improved through systematic training. Neural adaptations from agility training develop over weeks and months of repetition, meaning it must be an **integral component of the annual training plan** — not just a preseason add-on [S8, p.72].

## When to Use
- Year-round: agility must be trained throughout the off-season, preseason, and in-season
- Emphasized particularly in preseason blocks and early in-season sessions
- Essential for guards (primary ball handlers) and forwards who must defend multiple positions
- All positions benefit from direction-change mechanics and coordination training

## Key Principles
1. **Train agility at sport-specific speeds** — motor adaptations only occur at the intensities at which they're trained.
2. **Minimize loss of speed during direction changes** — the key metric of agility is how little speed is lost when the center of gravity shifts.
3. **Neuromuscular adaptation takes time** — plan agility training in multi-week blocks; do not expect improvements from a single preseason week.
4. **Strength and agility are correlated** — strong correlation between lower-body strength and agility; strength training is a prerequisite for effective agility development [S8, p.73].
5. **Power drives agility** — power (force × velocity) is "the single most important aspect of training" — increasing speed of movement increases power output [S8, p.73].
6. **Deceleration is a high-injury-risk skill** — eccentric muscle loading during deceleration places the greatest stress on joints and is the primary source of athletic injury; must be specifically trained.
7. **Coordination involves multiple joints** — nearly all athletic movement requires smooth interplay of multiple muscle groups; agility training develops this coordination.
8. **Dynamic balance is foundational** — the ability to maintain body control while in motion (using sight, kinesthesia, and nervous system feedback) underpins all agility movement.

## The Six Components of Agility Programs
A complete agility program should incorporate: [S8, p.73]
- **Strength** — maximal force at specified velocity; lower-body strength is directly correlated to agility
- **Power** — rate of work (force × velocity); increasing speed increases power
- **Acceleration** — change in velocity per unit time; critical for going from rest to top speed and re-accelerating after direction changes
- **Deceleration** — decreasing speed to allow direction change; involves eccentric muscle actions; main source of injury
- **Coordination** — controlling multiple muscle movements simultaneously for athletic skills
- **Dynamic balance** — maintaining control while in motion; uses sight, kinesthetic awareness, and perturbation feedback

## Common Mistakes
1. Training agility only in the preseason → correction: embed agility training year-round
2. Emphasizing physical attributes (size, strength) while neglecting agility work → correction: agility training complements strength; natural athleticism alone is insufficient
3. Training at low speeds and expecting game-speed agility transfer → correction: agility must be practiced at competition speeds
4. Ignoring deceleration training → correction: program specific deceleration drills, as eccentric loading is both high-injury-risk and performance-critical
