# On-Court Extremity Injury Evaluation

## Summary
Basketball's physical demands — cutting, jumping, direction changes, contact — produce a spectrum of extremity injuries requiring on-court evaluation. While ankle sprains are the most common injury, knee injuries cause the most time lost. Some injuries (knee dislocation, Jones fracture, Achilles rupture) carry catastrophic consequences if missed or mismanaged on court. Early, correct evaluation improves outcomes [S10, pp.161-163].

## When to Use
- Any extremity injury during basketball play requiring initial field assessment
- Determining whether a player can safely return to play vs. requires removal
- Initial management decisions before radiology is available

## Key Principles

### Upper Extremity

#### Shoulder Dislocations
1. Represent up to 1.4% of basketball injuries [S10, p.161]
2. Players with prior dislocations may be reduced on-site; first-time dislocations may require sedation or local anesthetic
3. Imaging (radiographs) is necessary for first-time dislocations to rule out associated fracture
4. Thorough neurovascular assessment required pre- AND post-reduction
5. **Luxatio Erecta (inferior dislocation)**: requires arm extension at elbow with overhead longitudinal traction + assistant applying cephalad pressure over humeral head. Associated with higher rates of brachial plexus and neurovascular injury [S10, p.161]

#### Hand and Finger Injuries
1. **Mallet fingers**: splint in extension immediately to reduce long-term complications
2. **MCP, PIP, DIP dislocations**: reduce before swelling increases, if tolerated; all require follow-up imaging
3. **Persistent instability or crepitus** after reduction: postpone return to play until full evaluation complete
4. **Scaphoid fractures**: heightened suspicion for snuffbox tenderness, volar scaphoid tubercle tenderness, scaphotrapezial joint tenderness — immobilize until imaging obtained [S10, p.161]

### Lower Extremity

#### Knee Dislocation (Most Urgent)
1. Defined as disruption of the tibiofemoral articulation, typically involving ≥2 ligaments
2. ~50% of knee dislocations reduce spontaneously before formal evaluation [S10, p.162]
3. **On-court assessment**: evaluate deformity, sensory deficits, vascular deficits
4. **Reduce on court** if deformity is present; conscious sedation may be needed after removal from court
5. **Post-reduction**: examine lower leg again; splint between full extension and 20° of flexion
6. **Ankle Brachial Index (ABI)** required even with palpable symmetrical pulses
   - ABI <0.9 OR diminished/absent pulses → arteriogram
   - Absent pulses or limb ischemia → emergent surgical exploration with on-table arteriogram
   - Normal pulses + ABI >0.9 → still requires minimum 24 hours observation [S10, p.162]

> ⚠️ Knee dislocations can cause limb-threatening vascular injury that may be missed with palpable pulses alone.

#### ACL, Meniscus, Patellar Tendon
1. All warrant removal from play — returning with these injuries risks further damage
2. Mechanism + brief physical exam is usually sufficient to recognize and determine return safety
3. **Patellar tendon rupture signs**: defect in tendon, patella alta, inability/difficulty with straight leg raise
4. ACL and meniscus tears: often present with knee swelling; large meniscal tears may cause loss of range of motion [S10, p.162]

#### High-Risk Foot and Ankle Injuries
1. **Talus fractures**: associated with poor healing regardless of etiology; early recognition and immobilization paramount; surgery often indicated
2. **Navicular fractures**: same poor healing profile
3. **Jones fractures (proximal 5th metatarsal)**: poor healing, high non-union rate; surgical intervention improves return to play
4. **Sesamoid stress injuries/fractures**: higher rate in basketball; significant missed-diagnosis risk
5. **Achilles tears**:
   - Complete tear → remove from participation immediately
   - Partial tear → early recognition reduces risk of complete rupture and significantly reduces time lost [S10, p.163]

## Common Mistakes
1. Accepting palpable pulses as sufficient after a knee dislocation → ABI is mandatory regardless of pulse quality; vascular injury can be occult
2. Allowing return to play after suspected ACL, meniscal, or patellar tendon injury → further participation exposes the athlete to catastrophic additional damage
3. Missing a Jones fracture as a simple ankle sprain → proximal 5th metatarsal tenderness in a basketball player requires X-ray
4. Not imaging after a first-time shoulder dislocation → fracture may be present and affect management
5. Delaying Achilles partial tear treatment → progression to complete rupture dramatically increases recovery time
