# Team Height and NBA Performance

## Summary
Taller teams tend to perform better on both offense and defense, but the effect is smaller than most coaches intuitively expect. The top 25 NBA offenses averaged roughly 0.3–1.2 inches taller than the league average in their season, while the worst offensive teams were often slightly shorter than average. The correlation exists but is **weaker than intuition suggests** at the NBA level [S16, pp.43, 55].

Importantly, average team heights (weighted by minutes played) rarely deviate more than **about half an inch** from the league average in the NBA. Oliver notes this surprised him — he expected greater deviation. The implication is that in the NBA, **talent and skill differences within similar height ranges matter more than raw height differences** [S16, p.59].

Height matters more at high school and college levels because there are more inches separating teams — the same marginal inch is no more valuable, but the spread is wider [S16, p.59].

## When to Use
- Evaluating roster construction decisions (is going small a significant disadvantage?)
- Understanding limitations of height-based analysis in the NBA
- High school/college coaches: height differences are more predictive at lower levels
- Scouting: identifying matchups where height differential is unusual enough to be exploitable

## Key Data Points
From Tables 3.6, 3.13, 3.15, 3.17 [S16]:

**Top 25 Offenses (Table 3.6)**:
- Most teams are within ±1.4 inches of league average height
- Dallas Mavericks 2002 (#1 raw): 79.1 in. vs. 79.0 league avg (+0.1)
- Denver Nuggets 1982 (#1 adjusted): 77.4 in. vs. 78.7 league avg (-1.2) — proves height not determinative
- Average increment: approximately +0.3 to +0.5 inches above league average

**Top 25 Defenses (Table 3.13)**:
- Best defensive teams average slightly *shorter* than best offensive teams
- Range: -0.9 to +0.8 inches above league average
- Average increment: approximately +0.2 to +0.3 inches above league average

**Worst Offenses (Table 3.15)**:
- Most teams 0.3–1.2 inches *below* league average height
- The pattern is consistent but the margins are small

**Worst Defenses (Table 3.17)**:
- Worst defensive teams tend to be shorter than average
- Pattern mirrors worst offenses

## Key Principles
1. **Height helps on both ends** — taller teams generally perform better offensively and defensively [S16, p.43, p.54]
2. **The effect is small in the NBA** — average heights deviate from league average by roughly half an inch; outliers are rare [S16, p.59]
3. **Best defensive teams are slightly shorter than best offensive teams** — possibly because rim protection and shot-blocking require height but perimeter defense requires quickness over height [S16, p.54]
4. **The correlation is stronger at lower levels** — high school and college have wider height spreads between teams [S16, p.59]
5. **Height is not determinative** — the 1982 Denver Nuggets were the #1 offense by standard deviations while being 1.2 inches *below* league average height [S16, p.43]

## Practical Coaching Implications
- **Do not sacrifice skill for height** — a shorter but highly skilled player is often preferable to a taller but limited player
- **In high school/college, height advantages are more exploitable** — target mismatches more aggressively because height differences between teams are larger
- **Measure by minutes-weighted height, not roster height** — a 7-footer who plays 5 minutes contributes less to "team height" than a 6'8" player who plays 35 minutes [S16, Table 3.6 note]
- **Identify true positional mismatches** — team average height differences of 1–2 inches at a specific position are much more significant than 0.5-inch team-wide differences

## Common Mistakes
1. **Overvaluing tall players who cannot execute** → height without skill/athleticism does not translate to higher offensive or defensive ratings
2. **Using roster height instead of minutes-weighted height** → inactive or low-minute tall players inflate perceived team height without contributing to actual performance
3. **Applying NBA height-performance correlations to youth basketball** → correlations are stronger at lower levels where height spreads between teams are larger
