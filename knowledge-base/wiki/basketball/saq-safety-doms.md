# SAQ Safety and DOMS Management

## Summary
High-intensity speed, agility, and quickness training places significant eccentric stress on muscles and connective tissue. Proper safety protocols — including warm-up, progressive loading, and recovery management — are essential to prevent injury and manage delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS). Because SAQ training universally involves the stretch-shortening cycle (eccentric loading), DOMS is a predictable outcome for new exercises and must be planned around rather than ignored. [S8, pp. 5–6]

## When to Use
- Before every SAQ training session (warm-up protocol).
- When introducing a new drill or higher-intensity phase to an athlete.
- When a player complains of persistent soreness (distinguish DOMS from injury).

## Key Principles
1. **Always warm up first**: Begin every session with a low-intensity whole-body activity (e.g., jogging) to increase heart rate, blood flow, and tissue temperature. Follow with a specific warm-up that includes lower-intensity versions of the session's exercises. [S8, p. 5]
2. **Simple → complex**: Progress drills from simple movements to complex, multi-directional patterns before adding speed or resistance. [S8, p. 5]
3. **Low → high intensity**: Never introduce maximum-effort SAQ drills without a prior foundation of moderate-intensity work at the same movement pattern. [S8, p. 5]
4. **General → sport-specific**: Begin with general athletic movements; add sport-specific motor patterns as the athlete improves. [S8, p. 5]
5. **Strengthen before SAQ**: Prioritize knee, hip, back, and ankle strength through resistance training before introducing SAQ drills. This reduces injury risk significantly. [S8, p. 5]
6. **Understand DOMS**: Delayed onset muscle soreness typically peaks 24–72 hours post-exercise. It is caused by micromuscle tears from eccentric loading (not lactic acid). The only adaptation is progressive exposure — repeatedly performing the same exercises with adequate rest between sessions. [S8, pp. 5–6]
7. **DOMS recovery schedule for novices**: Maximum 2 SAQ sessions per week, separated by 2–3 days, until the athlete adapts to the exercise stress. [S8, p. 6]
8. **Equipment and environment**: Verify all equipment is in working order. Clear the training area of hazardous objects (rocks, uneven surfaces). Wear proper footwear. [S8, p. 5]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping the warm-up** → Cold muscles and tendons are injury-prone during SSC-intensive SAQ exercises. Always jog first, then specific warm-up.
2. **Confusing DOMS with injury** → DOMS is symmetrical, dull/achy, and resolves in 48–72 hrs. Sharp, localized, or unilateral pain = stop and evaluate for injury.
3. **Returning too quickly after DOMS** → Respect the 2–3 day recovery window for novices; rushing back increases injury risk and inhibits supercompensation.
4. **Jumping to complex drills** → Athletes who can't yet perform a correct single-leg squat or lunge should not attempt depth jumps or agility ladder drills at speed.
