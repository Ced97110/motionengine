# Basketball on Paper — Summary

## Overview
*Basketball on Paper* by Dean Oliver (Potomac Books, 2004) is the seminal text in basketball analytics. Oliver, a former player, assistant coach, and collegiate scout who provided statistical consulting to the Seattle Supersonics and WNBA, synthesizes ideas from baseball sabermetrics, sports economics, and statistics to build a rigorous quantitative framework for evaluating basketball players and teams.

The book's central thesis is that basketball can be understood through possessions — the period between when one team gains the ball and when the other does — rather than raw counting statistics. Every major concept in the book flows from this foundation: offensive and defensive ratings (points per 100 possessions), floor percentage (fraction of possessions resulting in a score), and the Four Factors framework that explains team offensive and defensive efficiency.

Oliver applies these tools to evaluate individual players (including WNBA analysis), historical greats (Chamberlain vs. Russell), team strategies (pace, consistency, zone defense), and coaching decisions. The final chapters develop individual win-loss records, net points frameworks, and methods for comparing players across eras using multivariate regression to estimate missing historical statistics.

## Key Themes
1. **Possession-based analysis** — All meaningful basketball statistics should be expressed per 100 possessions to control for pace differences between teams and eras.
2. **The Four Factors** — Shooting efficiency (floor/field percentage), offensive rebounding, turnovers, and free throw rate are the four independent drivers of team scoring and defense.
3. **Floor Percentage & Scoring Possessions** — The fraction of possessions on which a team or individual produces at least one point is the master offensive efficiency metric.
4. **Offensive and Defensive Ratings** — Points scored (or produced) per 100 possessions vs. points allowed per 100 possessions; the gap between them predicts wins.
5. **Individual Player Evaluation** — Points produced (including assists, field goals, free throws, and offensive rebounds) converted into per-possession ratings allow fair cross-player and cross-era comparison.
6. **Difficulty Theory** — The value of a statistical contribution depends on how difficult it is to produce, accounting for opponent quality and context.
7. **Coaching and Team Context** — Defensive ability is strongly a function of the defensive system; individual defensive evaluations cannot be cleanly transferred across teams.
8. **Bell Curve and Pythagorean Methods** — Winning percentage can be estimated from average points scored/allowed and their variability, with the Bell Curve method adding the variance component over the simpler Pythagorean formula.
9. **Historical Regression** — Missing pre-1974 statistics (turnovers, offensive rebounds) can be estimated via multivariate linear regression on known box-score variables, explaining ~80% of variance.

## Chapter Breakdown
- **Ch. 1–2: Score Sheets and Team Efficiency** — Reading box scores through possessions; the Utah Jazz 1997-98 case study; best/worst offensive and defensive teams by rating.
- **Ch. 3: Individual Offensive Ratings** — Floor percentage, points produced, and percentage of team possessions — the foundational individual offensive evaluation framework.
- **Ch. 4: The Four Factors** — The four predictors of offensive/defensive efficiency: effective shooting %, offensive rebound %, turnover %, free throw rate.
- **Ch. 5: Pace** — Why teams play at different speeds and how to control for pace in all evaluations.
- **Ch. 6–7: Hot Hand & Win Streaks** — Statistical analysis showing streaks are largely random; competitive balance across leagues.
- **Ch. 8: Teamwork** — How individual contributions interact and the difficulty of isolating individual defensive value.
- **Ch. 9–10: Bell Curve Method** — Estimating winning percentage from points-scored distribution mean and standard deviation.
- **Ch. 11–12: Coaching Effects** — How coaches drive team offensive and defensive ratings; coaching efficiency analysis.
- **Ch. 13: Floor Percentage Deep Dive** — The complete floor percentage database for players, with historical leaders.
- **Ch. 14: Difficulty Theory** — Adjusting individual ratings for the difficulty of contributions based on position, teammates, and opponents.
- **Ch. 15: Three-Point Specialists** — Evaluating specialists whose floor percentages understate their value due to shot difficulty.
- **Ch. 16–17: Optimal Player Use** — How to maximize team offensive ratings through lineup construction and role clarity.
- **Ch. 18: Kobe Bryant & Shaquille O'Neal** — Case study in optimizing a two-star roster.
- **Ch. 19: Individual Defensive Ratings** — Stops, defensive possessions, and the multi-variable formula for individual defense; limitations acknowledged.
- **Ch. 20: Player Rating Methods** — Comparative review of existing rating systems (IBM, Tendex, Hoopstat, SAPD, Sagarin/Winston) and Oliver's own approach.
- **Ch. 21: Individual Win-Loss Records** — Allocating team wins and losses to individual players based on offensive and defensive production.
- **Ch. 22: Historical Greats** — Applying the framework to Chamberlain, Russell, Jordan, Magic, Bird, and others across eras.
- **Ch. 23: WNBA Analysis** — Applying all methods to women's basketball; Lisa Leslie and Cynthia Cooper as case studies.
- **Ch. 24: Toronto Raptors / Vince Carter** — A full-team analysis case study.
- **Appendix 4: Historical Regression** — Multivariate linear regression formulas for estimating TOV and OREB from pre-1974 NBA team statistics (R² ≈ 0.80–0.81).
- **Glossary** — Dean Oliver's formal definitions for possession, play, scoring possession, floor percentage, offensive/defensive rating, and related terms.

## Unique Contributions
- **First systematic possession-based framework** for basketball, predating widespread NBA adoption of similar metrics.
- **Individual Defensive Rating formula** — the first published attempt to isolate player defensive value from box-score data.
- **Floor Percentage** as a master efficiency metric, later renamed "True Shooting %" and "eFG%" in modified forms by other analysts.
- **The Four Factors** — still the dominant framework for explaining team efficiency differences 20+ years later.
- **Individual Win-Loss records** — a player-accountability framework analogous to pitcher wins in baseball.
- **Historical regression** to estimate missing pre-1974 statistics, enabling cross-era comparisons of Chamberlain and Russell.
- **Subjectivity acknowledgment** — Oliver explicitly notes that even rigorous statistical methods embed assumptions; how you frame the question shapes the answer [S16, p.366].
