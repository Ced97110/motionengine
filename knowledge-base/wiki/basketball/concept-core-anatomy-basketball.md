# Core Anatomy for Basketball

## Summary
The core is the part of the body that connects the force generated by the lower body and transfers it to the upper body. Without a strong core, athletic performance on the basketball court will suffer significantly. The core is divided into two functional categories: the **inner core** (stability) and the **outer core** (movement production). Understanding which muscles belong to each category allows coaches and players to train the core systematically and prevent injury. [S2, pp.31-32]

Every basketball movement — running, jumping, landing, pivoting, rebounding, and defending — relies on a functional core. A weak core breaks the kinetic chain and reduces force production while increasing injury risk to the lower back. [S2, pp.31-32]

## When to Use
- Year-round strength and conditioning programs
- Pre-season foundation building
- Injury prevention and rehabilitation planning
- Explaining the "why" behind core training to players

## Key Principles
1. **The core is a force connector** — it links lower-body force production to upper-body movement; without it, power leaks occur at every action ("your core is the bridge between your legs and your hands")
2. **Inner core = stability** — the transversus abdominis, transversospinalis group (multifidus and rotators), pelvic floor, and diaphragm stabilize the spine and provide a stable platform for all movement [S2, p.31]
3. **Outer core = movement production** — the rectus abdominis, erector spinae (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis), and internal/external obliques produce and resist spinal movement in multiple planes [S2, p.32]
4. **Kinetic chain thinking** — the rectus abdominis + hip flexors = anterior chain; erector spinae + hamstrings + glutes = posterior chain; obliques drive trunk rotation and lateral flexion [S2, p.32]
5. **Train all planes** — choose exercises from each movement category (flexion, extension, rotation, lateral flexion) for balanced strength and injury prevention [S2, p.32]
6. **Drawing in the abdominals** activates the transversus abdominis as a corset around the abdominal cavity — cue: "pull your navel toward your spine" [S2, p.34]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Develop both inner and outer core strength; every player must be able to produce and resist force in all planes during game actions
- **PF / C**: Posterior chain and anti-rotation strength are especially critical for contact under the boards, boxing out, and maintaining low-post position
- **PG / SG / SF**: Rotation and lateral flexion strength support quick direction changes, dribble drives, and pivoting moves

## Variations
### Inner Core Focus
Prioritize transversus abdominis activation and spinal stabilization exercises (plank variations, bird dog) for players recovering from lower back issues or needing postural correction.

### Outer Core Power Development
For advanced players, add resistance and explosiveness to extension (weighted back extension) and rotation (landmine, cable press) exercises to build sport-specific power.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Only training the "mirror muscles" (rectus abdominis)** → Train all planes — flexion, extension, rotation, and lateral flexion are equally important
2. **Forgetting to draw in the abdominals** during exercises → Cue "navel to spine" before every set to activate the inner core
3. **Using momentum or jerky motion** in rotation exercises → Control is paramount; the core must produce and resist force, not rely on swinging
4. **Neglecting the posterior chain** → Erector spinae, glutes, and hamstrings are core muscles that drive jumping and defensive posture
