# Basketball Facial Injuries

## Summary
Facial injuries are the third most common injury area among US high school athletes, with declining prevalence at collegiate and professional levels. Although basketball is technically a non-contact sport, head-to-head impacts, elbow strikes, falls, and ball impacts result in facial injuries. Between 3% and 29% of all facial injuries are sports-related. Common mechanisms are head-to-head contact and elbow strike to the face. Courtside medical personnel must be able to manage common injuries and recognize which require emergency referral [S10, pp.215-217].

## When to Use (Relevance)
- Immediate sideline evaluation following head or face collision
- Training room management of nosebleeds, lacerations, and tooth injuries
- Return-to-play decision-making after facial trauma

## Key Injury Types and Management

### 1. Nasal Injuries
**Types**: Nasal bone fracture, septal hematoma, nasal laceration, epistaxis

**Initial assessment**: Rule out airway compromise, concussion, eye injury, CSF leak [S10, p.215]

**Nasal Bone Fracture**:
- Most common type of facial fracture
- Examination: observe from multiple angles, palpate for crepitus/step-off, check for septal deviation, mucosal laceration, epistaxis, clear-fluid leak
- Courtside: high-resolution ultrasonography superior to plain radiographs
- Immediate reduction by experienced courtside physician is an option for isolated, minimally displaced fractures (medially directed broad thumb pressure)
- Surgeon evaluation: 3–5 days post-injury (swelling reduction)
- Return to sport: 6–8 weeks; facial protective device may allow earlier return [S10, pp.215-216]

**Classification of Nasal Fractures** (Marston et al.):
- Laterality: unilateral or bilateral
- Fracture type: open/closed, displaced/nondisplaced, simple/comminuted
- Position: deviated, medial/lateral, impacted, open book
- Septal: cartilaginous or bony
- Adjacent bones: orbital rim, nasoorbitoethmoidal, midface (zygoma/maxilla), LeFort-type, mandible [S10, p.216]

### 2. Septal Hematoma
- Collection of blood between septal cartilage and perichondrium
- **Critical**: separates cartilage from nutrient source → avascular necrosis if uncorrected within 3 days
- Complications if missed: septal abscess, septal perforation, cosmetic deformity (saddle nose)
- Appearance: large blue- or purple-colored fluctuant areas on septum
- **Drainage required within 2 days** by otorhinolaryngologist or skilled physician [S10, p.216]

### 3. Epistaxis (Nosebleed)
- Most commonly from Kiesselbach's plexus in the anteroinferior nasal septum
- Management: direct pressure distal to nasal bones × 15 min; ice packs; anterior packing with oxymetazoline/phenylephrine
- Return to play: once bleeding controlled
- Uncontrolled bleeding → nasal balloon catheter, posterior packing, or ED transfer [S10, p.216]

### 4. Facial Fractures
- Midface, mandibular, and orbital fractures are uncommon in basketball
- Mandibular fracture: tenderness, malocclusion, swelling, trismus, intraoral bleeding → refer to oral/maxillofacial surgery
- Healing time: 8–12 weeks before return to sport; custom mouthguard may allow earlier return on the lower end [S10, p.217]

### 5. Dental Injuries
- Basketball has a 5× higher dental trauma risk than football (no mandatory mouthguards)
- Treatment goals: retain tooth in arch, maintain dental pulp viability, prevent root resorption, restore function
- Superficial (enamel chip): mouthguard + routine dentist referral
- Deeper injuries (dentin/pulp): pain; risk to tooth viability; attempt to align before referral
- **Avulsed tooth protocol**: handle by crown only, wash with sterile saline, reimplant within 15–20 min, splint with available materials; if cannot reimplant → store in Hank's balanced salt solution (Save-A-Tooth), cool milk, saliva, or normal saline [S10, p.217]
- Lip lacerations: irrigate, repair; lacerations crossing vermillion border → align border first; sutures removed after 5 days [S10, p.217]

## Key Principles
1. Always rule out concurrent airway compromise, concussion, or eye injury after nasal trauma
2. **Never miss a septal hematoma** — drain within 2 days to prevent cartilage necrosis
3. Epistaxis: direct pressure first; escalate only if uncontrolled
4. Avulsed teeth require immediate action — minutes matter for tooth survivability
5. Mandibular and maxillofacial fractures always require specialty referral

## Common Mistakes
1. **Missing septal hematoma** → avascular necrosis and permanent cosmetic deformity
2. **Attempting nasal fracture reduction without experience** → inadequate reduction; patient discomfort; refer if unsatisfactory
3. **Discarding an avulsed tooth** → immediate reimplantation or proper storage preserves viability
4. **Returning player with uncontrolled epistaxis** → risk of posterior bleed requiring ED management
