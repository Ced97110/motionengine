# Consolidation Training

## Summary
Consolidation training (CT) is Paul Wade's frequency-based method for breaking through skill plateaus in *Explosive Calisthenics*. Instead of a structured session of sets × reps every few days, the athlete practices the target movement **multiple times throughout the day**, performing a handful of fresh, high-quality reps each time — spread over days or even weeks — without ever pushing to fatigue [S9, pp.15-17].

"It is, in fact, the most natural way for humans to train. It is the default, intuitive method — but only if we haven't been brainwashed by years of sets and reps." [S9, p.16]

For basketball coaches, CT has direct application for any complex motor skill a player is struggling to master — a new footwork pattern, a tricky finishing move, or any coordination-heavy action where the player can barely execute it once in practice. The principle is simple: more total contacts with the skill, each one fresh and high-quality, beats cramming hard sessions.

## When to Use
- When the athlete is **transitioning to a harder step** they are struggling with
- When the athlete **can barely perform the technique once**
- When standard weekly training frequency is producing slow or no progress
- When traditional sets × reps format creates fatigue that prevents quality execution [S9, p.16]

**Do not use CT** for all movement chains simultaneously — focus on one or two movements at a time. Adding too many dilutes adaptation and creates "mental logjam" in the nervous system [S9, p.17].

## Key Principles
1. **Practice daily (or multiple times daily)** — "Instead of working the new exercise once or twice a week and struggling to improve your reps every time, try working the new exercise every day" [S9, p.15]
2. **Never strain** — "Use good form, but don't strain. The name of the game is to spread your effort by performing lots of reps over a period of several days, not to push your muscles hard in any single attempt" [S9, p.16]
3. **Back off if sore** — "If you get excessively sore, back off for a couple of days" [S9, p.16]
4. **Singles or very low reps per contact** — Each visit to the movement should be brief: a rep or two, rest, another rep or two, done for now
5. **Train fresh every time** — The whole point of CT is that each rep is performed when the athlete is neurologically fresh. This is how skills are actually acquired
6. **Focus on one skill at a time** — CT works best when concentrated on one, maybe two movements. More than that and adaptation gets diluted [S9, p.17]

## The Natural Learning Model
Wade's example: a kid trying to master a breakdancing trick won't do it in sets of 10 then come back three days later. They'll try it a few times before school, a few times at recess, a few times at night. Eventually — with the occasional day off — they get the hang of it. "That's the only way us humans learn anything." [S9, p.16]

This maps directly to basketball skill acquisition: a player learning a new post footwork pattern benefits far more from brief, frequent reps throughout the week than from 3 sets of 10 done once in practice.

## Execution
1. Identify one or two movements where progress has stalled or that are barely achievable
2. Throughout each day, return to the movement 2-5+ times
3. Each visit: perform 1-3 reps with perfect form, rest naturally, maybe 1-2 more reps
4. Never push to muscular fatigue during any visit
5. Continue for 1-2 weeks
6. "The once nearly-impossible technique will gradually seem easier. When you go back to your regular training, you will find that multiple reps are much, much more attainable" [S9, p.16]

## Coaching Points
- CT cannot be shoe-horned into a fixed workout template — it requires "flexibility, intuition, discretion" [S9, p.20]
- Think of each CT visit as a "touch" on the skill, not a training session
- "The more you add in [to CT], the more any adaptation gets diluted" — be selective about which skill to consolidate [S9, p.17]
- Take an occasional full rest day to let the mind and body recover from residual fatigue [S9, p.13]

## Limitations
- Requires good body wisdom to avoid under- or over-doing it — it's unstructured
- Difficult to maintain for athletes with busy schedules (work, school, family)
- Works best when focused on only 1-2 movements at a time
- Does not fit neatly into a conventional workout program [S9, p.16-17]

## Recommended Blend
Most athletes should **combine** time surfing and consolidation training:
- Use **time surfing** to fit the bulk of skill practice into a clean session structure
- Use **CT** as an augment when hitting a difficult movement or wanting faster progress [S9, p.17]
