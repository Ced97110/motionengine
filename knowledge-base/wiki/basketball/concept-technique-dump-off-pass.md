# Dump-off Pass

## Summary
A dump-off pass is the short interior delivery from a ball-handler who has committed to a drive but runs into help defense, feeding a sealing post or a filling cutter in the restricted area for an uncontested finish. It is one of the three primary reads off a paint attack (shoot, kick, dump). The pass is delivered one-handed with a flip or wrap, timed to the receiver's seal and the helper's rotation. Clean dump-offs require the ball-handler's vision to be up on the drive, not on the rim.

## When to Use
- On a drive that collapses two defenders — one on the handler, one rotating from the weak-side post
- When the help defender steps up and loses body on the sealing post player
- As a counter to a defense that traps drives above the free-throw line

## Key Principles
1. Eyes up on the second dribble. The helper's first movement determines the dump-off window.
2. Pass one-handed, off the near hand to the near-side receiver — bounce or air depending on the helper's arm position.
3. Keep the body between the defender and the ball on the delivery. The pass is late if the defender sees the ball cross.
4. Receiver must seal early and extend hands low-to-high. A late seal leaves the pass unreceived.
5. Finish assumption: contact is coming. The dump-off receiver should catch-and-finish in one motion, not gather.

## Player Responsibilities
- **Ball-handler (1, 2, or 3)**: live-dribble read, one-handed delivery, pick-up-and-finish if no rotation
- **Receiver (4 or 5)**: seal the helper, hands low-ready, catch-and-finish
- **Weak-side spacer**: stay at the arc; defensive rotation to help-the-helper is the backside three

## Common Mistakes
- Ball-handler picks up the dribble before reading the helper — defender recovers, no delivery window
- Dump-off thrown hard at the receiver's chest — fumbled in traffic
- Receiver reaches for the ball instead of sealing — defender steals or fouls
