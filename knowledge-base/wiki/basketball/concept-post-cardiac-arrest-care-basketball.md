# Post-Cardiac Arrest Stabilization Care in Basketball

## Summary
Once return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC) is achieved after resuscitation, basketball players (and all athletes) who experience cardiac arrest require admission to an intensive care unit for at least 2 days of post-cardiac arrest stabilization. The goals are improving cardiopulmonary function, maintaining adequate organ perfusion, and supporting neurological recovery. Coaches, athletic trainers, and team physicians need a working knowledge of what happens after a courtside resuscitation so they can coordinate care and communicate with families and medical teams.

This phase of care is managed by ICU specialists, but the team medical staff play a critical role in facilitating rapid transfer to an appropriately equipped center and ensuring seamless communication.

## When to Use
- Any athlete who has experienced sudden cardiac arrest (SCA) and achieved ROSC — either on-court or in-hospital
- Athletes with complicated acute myocardial infarction complicated by ventricular fibrillation/ventricular tachycardia (VF/VT)
- Applies at all levels: youth, high school, college, and professional

## Key Principles
1. **ICU monitoring for ≥2 days** — continuously monitor cardiopulmonary status after ROSC [S10, p.189]
2. **Oxygenation target** — maintain arterial O₂ saturation >94%; avoid hyperoxia and hyperventilation [S10, p.189]
3. **Ventilation target** — PaCO₂ of 35–40 mmHg; advanced airway placement without hyperventilation [S10, p.189]
4. **Blood pressure maintenance** — keep BP at or above 90/50 mmHg, especially with coronary or cerebral ischemia; use IV fluids and vasoactive drugs as needed [S10, p.189]
5. **12-lead ECG immediately and daily** — if acute ST-segment elevation noted, emergent angiography for primary percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) regardless of neurological status [S10, p.189]
6. **Avoid hyperthermia** — hyperthermia worsens ischemic brain injury; recommended therapeutic hypothermia of 32–34°C [S10, p.189]
7. **Control metabolic parameters** — blood glucose, anemia, electrolyte imbalances, and metabolic abnormalities are associated with poor neurological outcome [S10, p.189]
8. **Neurological monitoring** — cerebral MRI and electroencephalogram (EEG) to evaluate recovery, cognitive function, and epileptic status [S10, p.189]
9. **Diagnostic workup** — chest radiograph, 12-lead ECG, echocardiogram, serum lactate, and serial cardiac enzymes [S10, p.189]
10. **IV amiodarone** — for VF/VT complicated by acute MI, IV amiodarone (300 mg bolus, repeat at 150 mg) should be initiated as soon as possible and not deferred [S10, p.189]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Players should wear medical alert identification if they have known cardiac conditions. Players should not self-discharge from medical care after a cardiac event.

## Variations
### Complicated Acute Myocardial Infarction
When SCA is caused by acute MI complicated by VF/VT, immediate mortality ranges from 59–89% depending on hemodynamic abnormalities. Anti-ischemic therapy including invasive procedures should not be deferred. [S10, p.189]

### Transfer to Regional Center
If local hospital lacks capability, patient should be transferred to a larger regional center equipped to manage post-cardiac arrest care. [S10, p.189]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Hyperventilation** → Keep PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg; hyperventilation worsens outcomes
2. **Allowing hyperthermia** → Actively maintain therapeutic hypothermia 32–34°C
3. **Deferring PCI for neurological status** → Emergent angiography should proceed regardless of neurological status when ST elevation is present
4. **Inadequate BP management** → Keep BP ≥90/50 mmHg to protect coronary and cerebral perfusion
