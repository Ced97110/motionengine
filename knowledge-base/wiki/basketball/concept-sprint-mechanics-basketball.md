# Sprint Mechanics for Basketball Athletes

## Summary
Properly trained sprint mechanics allow athletes to maximize the force their muscles generate, increase neuromuscular efficiency, and produce faster running speeds. While true maximum speed is seldom achieved in most basketball settings, the factors that improve track sprinters' times apply equally to court athletes chasing loose balls, executing fast breaks, and closing out on shooters. Speed can be improved through training — it is not purely genetic [S8, pp.17-22].

Maximum running speed is reached within 4 to 5 seconds for most athletes. Close to 75% of maximum velocity is established within the first 10 yards (9 meters) — making acceleration, not top-end speed, the most basketball-relevant component [S8, p.18].

## When to Use
- When coaching sprint technique in pre-season or off-season speed workouts
- When correcting running mechanics observed during practice or games
- As the framework for selecting speed drills (A-skips, B-skips, arm swings, etc.)

## Key Principles

### The Three Pillars of Sprint Mechanics
1. **Posture** — alignment of the body relative to each phase of sprinting
2. **Arm Action** — range of motion and velocity of the arms; counteracts rotational leg forces
3. **Leg Action** — relationship of hips and legs to torso and ground

### Posture
- During acceleration: pronounced forward lean (~45° from horizontal) to overcome inertia
- At maximum speed: more erect posture (~80°)
- At full leg extension, draw a straight line from ankle through knee, hip, torso, and head
- "Running can be seen as a controlled fall" [S8, p.20]

### Arm Action
- Each arm moves as one piece; elbow bent at ~90°
- Hands relaxed and come up to about shoulder level in front, pass the gluteus in back
- Arm swing is directly forward and backward — **never side to side**
- Originates from the shoulder; avoid excessive flexion/extension at the elbows
- Thumb side of hand pointed forward and up at all times; do not allow wrist to move
- Aggressive arm action is a must — especially in the acceleration phase [S8, p.20]

### Leg Action
- Foot remains dorsiflexed (toes up) throughout running cycle except at ground strike
- Ground contact: ball of the foot (never the heel), directly under the athlete
- Foot leaves ground → travels straight up toward buttocks
- Knee rises up; thigh nearly parallel to ground
- Foot drops below the knee; knee is at ~90°
- Leg straightens down and under the body to ground contact point
- Greater running speed = higher heel kick up; failure to achieve high heel kick reduces stride frequency [S8, p.20]
- "Practice running as lightly and quietly as possible with correct foot-to-ground contact" [S8, p.20]

### At Top Speed
1. Head held high
2. Torso becomes more upright
3. Shoulders and head are relaxed
4. Driving leg fully extended to the ground
5. Heel of recovery foot comes close to the gluteus [S8, p.21]

## Stride Frequency and Stride Length
- Both are the main factors in running speed
- **Stride frequency**: number of strides per unit of time; increased only when feet contact the ground
- **Stride length**: distance covered from center of mass in one stride; optimal = 2.3-2.5x the athlete's leg length at max speed
- Increasing one often reduces the other — must balance both
- Common mistake: overstriding (reaching too far forward with lower leg) decreases stride frequency and efficiency [S8, pp.18-19]
- Sprint-assisted training (downhill, towing) can help athletes develop the feel of faster stride frequency

## Developing Speed: 8 Guidelines
1. Perform speed workouts when fully recovered — speed training at the **beginning** of a session
2. Master technique through many perfect repetitions over months
3. Allow adequate rest between sets (heart rate/respiration nearly normal); sprints lasting 6-8 seconds → use 1:4 work-to-rest ratio
4. Vary intensity: light, medium, and heavy days — avoid back-to-back hard days
5. Track total distance run each session
6. Learn to run **relaxed** while producing maximum effort — overexertion creates extraneous movement
7. Speed endurance: run longer intervals (165-440 yards) or decrease rest between short intervals (20-65 yards)
8. Precede every speed workout with a dynamic warm-up and flexibility routine [S8, pp.21-22]

## Warm-Up Protocol for Speed Training
- General warm-up: 5-10 min of jogging (forward/backward), lunge walking, calisthenics, skipping — goal is to break a sweat
- Dynamic flexibility (10-15 min): arm circles, trunk twists, stepping knee hugs, high kicks, lunging walks with rotations, walking on tiptoes/heels, ankle rotations, leg swings
- **Avoid static stretching before speed work** — recent research suggests it impairs maximal force production [S8, p.22]
- Static stretches belong at the cool-down after all speed/power movements are complete

## Common Mistakes
1. Allowing the head to sway or jerk → maintain neutral relaxed head position
2. Arms crossing the midline of the body → arm swing must be directly forward and backward
3. Landing on the heel → contact should be on the ball of the foot, directly under the athlete
4. Overstriding to try to increase stride length → develops naturally as technique and strength improve
5. Tensing up to go faster → overexertion reduces power available for speed
6. Static stretching before sprinting → save for cool-down
