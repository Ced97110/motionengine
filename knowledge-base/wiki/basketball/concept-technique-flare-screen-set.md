# Flare Screen (Setter Side)

## Summary
The flare screen is an off-ball screen set on the cutter's defender, angled so the cutter breaks AWAY from the ball (rather than toward it) to receive a skip pass on the wing or corner. The setter's job is to arrive early, establish a legal stance on the defender's lead shoulder, and hold long enough that the cutter clears into a catch-and-shoot window. Execution depends on screen angle, contact timing, and an immediate flare or slip read once the cutter leaves.

## When to Use
- When the cutter is a shooter and the defense overplays ball-side pressure
- Off ball-reversal actions where the skip pass opens a backside three
- As a change-up off standard down-screen looks that condition defenders to expect curl

## Key Principles
1. Arrive with screen angle open to the passing lane — the setter's back faces the receiver's eventual catch window.
2. Plant wide base, feet shoulder-width, head up. No moving-screen risk once positioned.
3. Time contact to the cutter's last step — premature contact telegraphs the flare direction.
4. After contact, read the defender: if the defender fights over → hold and watch for shooter catch; if defender switches → slip to the opposite direction and look for the short roll.
5. Shoulders square on release. Rotate hips toward the ball so the slip-option catch is one-step away.

## Player Responsibilities
- **Setter (4 or 5)**: screen angle, stance, hold, slip read
- **Cutter (2 or 3)**: late set-up step, clean flare to the three-point arc, shot-ready on catch
- **Passer (1)**: hold passing lane, cross-court delivery on the high line, two-hand target

## Common Mistakes
- Setter arrives square-to-the-cutter's-direction — defender slides under and meets the flare early
- Setter forgets the slip option on a switch — breakdown becomes a dead possession
- Cutter sets the screen up too early — defender anticipates and denies the flare
