# Dribble Moves (Van Gundy)

## Summary
Once a player has initiated the dribble, they need a complete arsenal of moves to beat the defender in the open court or in the half-court. Stan Van Gundy organizes these moves into two categories: (1) change-of-pace moves with the same dribbling hand, and (2) change-of-hand-and-direction moves. Every dribble move must be paired with an explosive change of pace. The goal is always to go straight to the basket — curved lines give the defense more time to recover. [S7, pp.27-32]

## When to Use
- After initiating the dribble off a triple-threat or jab-step action
- When approaching a defender while already in dribble
- To create space or beat a defender in the open court

## Key Principles
1. **Dribble straight, not laterally** — curved dribbles give the defense time to recover; always drive toward the basket in a straight line.
2. **Pump the ball hard and low** — especially on the driving dribble: fingers point toward the floor at the end; the ball returns quickly to the hand. A speed dribble is always lower than a stationary dribble.
3. **Read the defender's reaction throughout** — the choice of dribble move must be based on what the defender does, not pre-decided.
4. **Match every move with a change of pace** — decelerate to lure the defender, then explode.
5. **Fake with the whole body, not just the ball** — head, shoulder, and body weight shifts sell the fake.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG/SF**: Must have a full arsenal of all dribble moves, both hands.

## Variations

### Change-of-Pace Moves (Same Hand)

#### Hockey Dribble
Freeze the defender with short, choppy steps back and forth with feet nearly parallel (like skating slides on ice). Combine with head and shoulder fakes. On the drive:
- Dribbling hand over the ball, pushing very hard to the floor.
- On the last dribble, bring hand behind the ball to push it hard and low over the defender.
- Body weight even on the balls of the feet during hockey steps, then transfer to the foot on the same side as the dribbling hand for power on the drive. [S7, p.27]

#### Change-of-Pace Dribble
Approach the defender at a moderate, controlled speed to get them to relax. When their knees straighten or they relax, quickly drop your knees for a sudden sprint with a long step and a strong dribble forward.
- Avoid short, nervous steps when approaching — they signal intent.
- A head-and-shoulder fake in the opposite direction can help.
- On the drive: bring the dribbling hand behind the ball to push it hard and low past the defender. [S7, pp.27-28]

#### Speed Dribble: Stop and Go
Dribble quickly toward the defender, then stop suddenly while keeping the dribble alive. Raise head and legs slightly to look like you're decelerating. When the defender stands up or relaxes, explode back into a speed dribble. Sequence: speed dribble → controlled dribble → pause → speed dribble. Never fully stop the dribble — just decelerate. [S7, p.28]

#### Step-Back Dribble
When the defender is covering aggressively with no driving room:

*Footwork:* Near the defender while dribbling with the right hand, take a strong step with the left foot toward the defender (full body weight on ball of forward foot), simultaneously lifting the right foot. Then step back by pushing off the left foot and lifting it to create space.

*Dribble:* As the left foot steps forward on the last dribble, shift the right-hand fingers from the back to the front of the ball, pulling it back. When the defender tries to close, explode with a power dribble with the same hand.

Stay low throughout. Fake with the whole body. If in range and balanced, take the jump shot after the step-back. [S7, p.28]

### Change-of-Hand and Direction Moves

#### In-'n-Out (Crossover Fake)
While dribbling with the right hand, take a step outside with the left foot and shift the right hand slightly on top-and-lateral of the ball, bringing it momentarily to the middle of the body — making the defender think crossover is coming. At the last second, as the defender begins to lean, bring the ball back to the right side and dribble hard forward.

- Body weight must be on the ball of the left foot; the left foot pushes the body to the right — in a straight line, not laterally.
- Keep dribble low for the entire movement.
- Add a head and shoulder fake leaning the opposite direction. [S7, p.29]

#### Crossover Dribble
Simplest change of hand but most dangerous — made in front of the defender.

*Footwork:* Bring body weight to the inside of the right foot and push in the new direction before taking a long but balanced step with the left foot. Left foot toes point toward the basket (not laterally) — critical to prevent moving away from the rim.

*Dribble:* On the last lateral dribble, when the ball bounces back to the right hand, pump the side of the ball very hard below knee level and as low to the floor as possible. Simultaneously step forward with the left foot. The crossover bounce should land on the side, over the toes of the left forward foot. Left hand positioned laterally to stop the ball for a split second, then immediately bends backward and behind the ball for a speed dribble.

Turn shoulders in the new direction to help. Stay low; change pace. Finish with a jump shot, layup, or pass. [S7, p.29]

#### Behind the Back
Safer than the crossover because the ball is not in front of the defender.

*One foot forward:* While stepping forward with the left foot on the last right-hand dribble, shift the right hand in front of the ball and bring it behind the back; control ball until it nears the opposite hip, then push hard to the floor. Ball bounces on the left side of the body, over the toes of the left foot. Control with the left hand and speed dribble.

*Two feet even:* On the last right-hand dribble, bend knees to get lower with back straight and feet parallel. Right hand on the side of the ball, forcefully push the ball behind the back and below the buttocks from one side to the other. Ball bounces over the left hip. Left hand (fingers toward floor) stops ball briefly, then palm turns backward for speed dribble. [S7, pp.29-30]

#### Between the Legs
While stepping forward with the left foot on the last lateral right-hand dribble, the right hand shifts laterally on the ball and pushes it between the legs to the other side. Ball bounces laterally and over the toes of the left foot. Left hand (fingers toward floor) stops ball briefly, then shifts behind ball for speed dribble. [S7, p.30]

#### Reverse (Spin) Dribble
Difficult but highly effective. While dribbling right hand:
- Step forward with the left foot.
- Right hand shifts to the side of the ball, forearm bent ~45 degrees.
- Rotate body in the new direction, pivoting on the ball of the left foot.
- Bounce ball with a hard, quick finger movement (or slap) to the floor; ball returns quickly.
- Protect ball between body and defender.
- **Head must anticipate the rotation** — look over the right shoulder in advance to read the defender's reaction and avoid a charging call.
- After the bounce-back, continue dribbling to the basket.

Key details:
- Stay low throughout and after.
- Legs spread more than shoulder-width, knees bent for a quicker/more explosive move — don't reverse with legs together.
- Do not overextend the arm; keep arm near body, slightly above waist level, ~45 degrees bent. Wider angle sends ball away from body.
- Dribbling hand leaves the ball only after the reverse is nearly completed — any earlier exposes ball to the defender.
- Start new-hand dribble only AFTER the reverse is completed.
- Toes of the forward foot after the reverse point straight forward — not laterally. [S7, p.30]

#### Half-Reverse (Half-Spin)
Same mechanics as the reverse, but when the defender has recovered and blocked the new direction, dribble back to the original position and attack from where the reverse started. Dribble with the same hand throughout. [S7, p.30]

## Shooting in Traffic
When a player beats the first defender but faces a second:
- **If the first defender has inside foot up**: use a reverse dribble around them, approach the second defender, lean inside shoulder and hip into their chest, and shoot a layup.
- **If the first defender has feet parallel**: use a hesitation dribble with head-and-shoulder fake, then explode to the other side, driving straight with the left hand; lean inside shoulder and hip into the second defender to freeze them, and shoot a layup. [S7, p.32]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Short, nervous steps when approaching a defender** → signals intent; use long, calm approach steps
2. **Curved drives** → give defense more time; always drive in a straight line
3. **Exposing ball in the crossover too high** → easy steal; keep crossover bounce below knee level
4. **Starting the new-hand dribble before the reverse is complete** → ball exposed to the defender who can slap it from behind
5. **Feet too close together on the reverse** → too slow and unexplosive; spread legs wider than shoulder-width
6. **Left foot toes pointing laterally on crossover** → body goes sideways; toes must point toward the basket
