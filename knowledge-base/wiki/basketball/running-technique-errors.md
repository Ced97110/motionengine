# Running Technique Errors

## Summary
Proper sprinting mechanics are essential for basketball players who need to accelerate, decelerate, and change direction at high speed. While sprinting is a natural activity, athletes frequently develop faulty movement habits that limit speed and increase injury risk. This checklist of common errors, causes, and corrections can be used by coaches to evaluate and correct running technique during athlete assessment or practice [S8, p.13].

Three aspects of running mechanics are addressed in every assessment: **body position**, **arm action**, and **leg action** [S8, p.12].

## When to Use
- During the [[athlete-assessment-saq]] needs analysis phase
- When observing players in transition or full-court drills
- After returning from lower-extremity injury
- When a player's first-step quickness or court speed seems disproportionate to their fitness level

## Key Principles
1. **Eyes lead the body** — athlete should look in the direction of intended movement; head and neck alignment affects total body position [S8, p.12]
2. **Aggressive arm action** — facilitates leg action through aggressive hand and knee hammering/punching motions, especially during acceleration [S8, p.12]
3. **Explosive leg action** — move legs explosively and minimize ground support time [S8, p.12]
4. **Initiate direction changes with the head** — turn head first to new target, then use footwork (open or crossover step); turning shoulders/hips first causes rounding off the turn [S8, p.12]

## Running Technique Error Checklist
*(Adapted from Schmolinsky, 1993)* [S8, p.13]

### 1. Insufficient Leg Extension at Push-Off (Athlete "Sits")
- **Cause**: Inadequate power transmission; push-off not powerful enough and too hasty
- **Correction**: Ankle joint work in forward movement; running and hopping; running and jumping; bouncing; special strengthening exercises

### 2. Feet Turned Excessively Outward
- **Cause**: Faulty running form
- **Correction**: Running in a lane; walking, jogging, and slow running with feet turned slightly inward

### 3. "Bouncing" With Marked Vertical Swaying
- **Cause**: Push-off force directed too vertically (wasted energy going up instead of forward)
- **Correction**: Longer push-off; hitting chalk marks at regular intervals; starting exercises; increased stride rate

### 4. Forward Swing of Lead Leg Too Wide; Flat Foot Plant
- **Cause**: Trunk/thigh weakness; fatigue
- **Correction**: Snatching thigh in diagonal support (with/without added load); high-knee lift drills in difficult conditions (deep snow/sand, uphill, weighted footwear); strengthening exercises

### 5. Ineffective Arm Movements (Transverse Movement, Excessive Backward Swing, Hunched Shoulders)
- **Cause**: Excessive shoulder movement; insufficient shoulder-joint flexibility
- **Correction**: Practice proper arm movements during easy stride, side-straddle position, or jogging

### 6. Head and Neck Hyperextended or Hyperflexed
- **Cause**: Fatigue; misunderstanding of movement
- **Correction**: Normal erect head carriage; eyes focused ahead

## Common Coaching Cues
- "Drive your knee and elbow together" (arm-leg synchronization)
- "Push the ground away" (vs. pulling toward you)
- "Run tall" (neutral spine, no forward lean at trunk)
- "Eyes on target" (head leads direction changes)
- "Quick feet, quiet feet" (minimize ground contact time)
