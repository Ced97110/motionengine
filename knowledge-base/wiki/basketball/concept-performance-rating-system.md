# Performance Rating System (PRS)

## Summary
The Performance Rating System (PRS) is a comprehensive, data-driven player evaluation tool that assigns weighted numerical values to every basketball statistic — shooting, rebounding, ballhandling, defense, and violations — and converts them into a single numeric score (the Performance Rating, or PR) for each player after every practice and game. The coach and players collaborate to set the weighted values, ensuring buy-in and perceived fairness. The player with the highest PR at each position earns playing time.

Created by coach Lee Rose, the PRS is designed to eliminate favoritism, expose one-dimensional players, build team chemistry, and give all athletes a transparent, objective path to the starting lineup. The system proved transformative at the University of South Florida (from 6-21 to 18-11 and NIT qualification in a single season) and in international competition at the 1984 World University Games [S13, pp.47-50].

## When to Use
- From the first day of practice through the final game of the season
- During all 5-on-5 scrimmages, controlled drills, practice games, and intrasquad games
- Applied to regular-season and postseason games (statistics taken from video for maximum accuracy)
- Any time playing-time decisions must be made objectively and transparently
- When introducing a new team to fair competition standards (e.g., select/all-star teams with high-ego players)

## Key Principles
1. **Objectivity over opinion** — The PR number removes subjective bias from playing-time decisions. "Five people who watch the same game will likely give five different responses" about any player's performance [S13, p.48].
2. **Players help set the values** — Coaches walk players through each statistic and let them vote/discuss the weighted values. This buy-in is critical to acceptance.
3. **Total game is rewarded** — The system is explicitly designed so that a pure scorer who ignores defense, rebounding, and ball security cannot lead the PR rankings.
4. **Daily posting** — The PR is tabulated and posted daily in a designated location so players can track their standing at each position.
5. **No practice = no PR = no playing time** — Players who miss practice have no PR number. This single rule eliminates most attendance problems.
6. **Violations are severely penalized** — Mental mistakes (3-second violations −5, technical fouls −5, over-and-back −3) reflect the coach's philosophy of eliminating the errors that cost games.
7. **Offensive and defensive sheets are separate** — Table 3.1 covers offensive production; Table 3.2 covers defensive production [S13, pp.53-54].

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Compete every possession in practice so that statistics are trackable. Review the posted PR after each session and identify negative categories to improve.
- **PG**: Especially impacted by assist (+3) vs. turnover (−2) differential; passing quality matters as much as scoring.
- **C/PF**: Especially impacted by rebounding values — offensive rebound (+2) vs. defensive rebound (+1) vs. failing to box out (−2) vs. permitting offensive put-back (−3).

### 3. Depth Chart
After each practice or game, players are ranked 1-through-N at each position (PG, SG, SF, PF, C) by their PR score. The depth chart is posted publicly [S13, p.55].

### 4. Shooting Threshold Logic
To have a **positive** PR from shooting alone:
- Must shoot **above 50%** on 2-pt field goals (made FG +2, missed −2)
- Must shoot **above 70%** on free throws (made +1, missed −2)
This means scorers who take too many low-percentage shots hurt their own PR [S13, p.61].

## Implementation
1. **Recruit 12+ volunteer stat-keepers** from student body/interns/student managers.
2. **Orientation meeting**: assign each volunteer one specific stat category (e.g., offensive rebounds only, assists only).
3. **PRS Director**: a staff member who oversees volunteers, collects sheets, tabulates, and posts daily.
4. **Statistics are kept on all 5-on-5 activity**: half-court work, controlled full-court work, scrimmages, practice games, regular-season games.
5. Volunteers sign their worksheets and hand them to the PRS Director immediately after each session.
6. For road games, video analysis is used to record the statistics [S13, pp.51-52].

## Scrimmage Evaluation Protocol
A 30-minute scrimmage slot is built into every practice. With 15 players, rotate three teams (A, B, C) in 10-minute blocks:
- 10 min: A vs. B
- 10 min: A vs. C
- 10 min: B vs. C

Each player receives 20 minutes of tracked scrimmage per day. Five requirements ensure fairness:
1. The evaluation offense is fair and gives all players equal scoring/rebounding chances.
2. Playing time is equal for everyone.
3. Teams rotate daily so stacking is impossible.
4. Outside officials referee for impartiality.
5. The same PRS is applied to everyone [S13, p.59].

## Benefits of the PRS
- Analytical tool for identifying individual weaknesses and strengths
- Operates on objective data, eliminating favoritism
- Indicates daily progress toward team and individual goals
- Motivates players to develop a complete, well-rounded game
- Provides data for coach–player discussions (eliminates emotional arguments)
- Exposes one-dimensional players
- Great system for selecting team captains
- Builds team harmony and trust in coaching decisions [S13, pp.50-51]

## Case Study: 2010 NCAA Championship (Duke vs. Butler)
Applying the PRS to the 2010 championship game, Duke's overall PR was +27 vs. Butler's −12. Key differentiators:
- Duke had 7 blocked shots; Butler had 0
- Duke's top 3 producers (Singler +21, Zoubek +19, Scheyer +16) combined for +56 vs. Butler's top 3 at +6
- Duke shot fewer missed shots (lower negative from shooting) and committed fewer personal fouls (14 vs. 18) [S13, pp.55-58]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Setting values without player input** → Players must vote/discuss values for true buy-in; otherwise they reject the system as just another coach's tool.
2. **Keeping stats only in games** → The system must run in practices for it to drive daily effort and playing-time decisions.
3. **Using only one stat keeper for all categories** → Assign one dedicated volunteer per category to ensure accuracy.
4. **Ignoring violations** → The violation penalty values are among the system's most powerful motivators; do not skip them.
