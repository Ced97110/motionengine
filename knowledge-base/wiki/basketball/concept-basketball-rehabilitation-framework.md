# Basketball Rehabilitation Framework: Local-Regional-Global

## Summary
Basketball rehabilitation and return-to-play should be approached systematically, progressing through three levels of intervention: **local** (joint-specific impairments), **regional** (surrounding joints and functional limitations), and **global** (whole-body load demands and sport-specific movements). This framework recognizes that basketball athletes have unique biomechanical demands — long lever arms from tall stature, rapid growth-spurt histories, high jump volumes, and multi-directional movement — that must be systematically re-introduced as the athlete progresses through rehabilitation.

The hip complex is especially critical in basketball rehabilitation because lower hip strength is directly associated with greater lower extremity valgus during single-leg jump landings. Strengthening the gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, and hamstrings can reduce dynamic valgus and balance sagittal plane tibial translation forces.

## When to Use
- After any lower extremity injury requiring significant rehabilitation (ACL, ankle sprain, patellar tendinopathy, stress fractures)
- As the structural framework for designing return-to-play progressions
- When differentiating which exercises to prioritize at each stage of recovery

## Key Principles
1. **Local interventions first**: Address joint-specific impairments (e.g., regaining quadriceps function after ACL reconstruction; multi-directional ankle stability after sprain) — may include therapeutic exercise AND manual therapy [S10, p.45]
2. **Regional interventions second**: Hip complex is the most critical regional focus — target gluteus maximus (hip extensor + external rotator), gluteus medius (hip abductor), and hamstrings (hip extensor + knee flexor) to reduce dynamic valgus [S10, p.45]
3. **Global interventions last**: Match external load demands of the sport — use game-average values for running distance, jump count, and change-of-direction frequency as return-to-play targets [S10, p.46]
4. **Frontal plane emphasis**: Basketball-specific rehabilitation must include frontal plane exercises and both double-leg and single-leg jumping/landing activities [S10, p.46]
5. **Lateral biased drills for late-stage rehab**: Lateral bounds, lateral tuck jumps, and resisted lateral jumps are ideal late-stage basketball rehabilitation tools [S10, p.46-47]
6. **Medial/lateral triple hop**: Combines medial/lateral movement and single-leg hopping — an ideal late-stage rehabilitation assessment and training tool for basketball [S10, p.46]

## Hip-Focused Exercise Progressions
### Lunge Progressions (Fig. 4.3 reference)
- **Traditional lunge** (sagittal plane): Targets hamstrings
- **Rotational lunge** (transverse plane added): Elicits greater gluteus maximus and hip rotator recruitment to combat abnormal movement patterns
- **Contralateral weighted lunge** (frontal plane added): Weight in opposite upper extremity forces gluteus medius of forward limb to create extra torque

### Sidestepping Progression
- **Resisted side-step**: Elicits gluteus medius torque in basketball-specific defensive position — progression from bodyweight to band resistance

### Posterior Chain / Hamstring
- **Eccentric hamstring exercise** (Russian hamstring curl): Recruits posterior hip and thigh musculature
- **Unilateral straight-knee bridge**: High hamstring muscle activity; excellent for post-operative protocols
- **Single-leg deadlift**: High hamstring activity; builds unilateral hip stability
- **Kettlebell swings + Romanian deadlifts**: Target medial hamstrings (semitendinosus)
- **Supine leg curls + hyperextensions**: Target lateral hamstrings (biceps femoris) [S10, p.45]

### Lateral Basketball-Specific Drills (Late Stage)
- **Lateral bound**: Single-leg lateral explosive hop — mimics defensive slides and cutting mechanics
- **Lateral tuck jump**: Bilateral lateral jump with tuck — builds reactive power in the frontal plane
- **Resisted lateral jump**: Band or partner-resisted lateral jump — most sport-specific late-stage drill [S10, pp.46-47]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping regional (hip) work** → Isolated local work on the injury site without addressing hip strength allows dynamic valgus to persist and drive re-injury
2. **Neglecting frontal plane loading** → Most rehabilitation is sagittal plane dominant; basketball demands require frontal plane loading progression
3. **Progressing by time alone** → Game-average load values (distance, jumps, direction changes) should be the targets, not arbitrary time windows
4. **Returning before achieving single-leg control** → All basketball athletes must demonstrate adequate single-leg jump landing control before returning to full competition
