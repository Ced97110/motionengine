# Continuity Reverse Dribble Options

## Summary
No offense can operate without contingency plans for when every pass entry is denied. The Continuity offense uses the reverse dribble as its primary dribble-entry system. When the ball-handler cannot pass to any teammate, they dribble toward a receiver, forcing that receiver's defender to overcommit, then reverse dribble away to trigger backdoor cuts, screens, and pick-and-roll actions. The reverse dribble flows seamlessly back into the standard Continuity passing game without resetting. [S7, pp.125-127]

## When to Use
- When the ball-handler cannot make an entry pass to the wing, high post, or any perimeter player
- When all receivers are aggressively overplayed and no easy pass is available
- When the primary entry is being scouted and denied by the defense
- As a change-of-pace rhythm disruptor within a possession that has already started with passing

## Key Principles
1. **Dribble toward the denied receiver** — the dribble-toward action forces the receiver's defender to react; the receiver then cuts backdoor or the ball-handler reverses
2. **Reverse dribble creates new angles** — the direction change opens up passing lanes that didn't exist from the original position
3. **Everyone else moves simultaneously** — while the ball-handler reverse dribbles, all other players relocate: backdoor cuts, screen setups, and elbow flashes happen simultaneously
4. **Post (5) flashes to the weak-side elbow** — the standard response to a reverse dribble; 5 positions at the elbow to receive the pass and continue the offense
5. **Flow back to the passing game** — after a reverse dribble, the same sets (Pass and Screen Away, etc.) apply; no new call needed

## The Three Reverse Dribble Options

### Option 1 — Basic Reverse Dribble Into Back Pick
1 can't pass to any teammate → 1 dribbles toward 2 (overplayed); 2 cuts away to opposite side
1 reverse dribbles toward 4; 4 goes backdoor (or screens for 2) and posts low
5 flashes to the weak-side elbow
After the reverse dribble, 1 passes to 2 → 2 passes to 1 (off 5's side screen) for a jump shot or drive; 2 can also pass to 5 rolling after the screen
Alternative: 2 screens down for 4 after passing to 1; 4 receives from 1 [S7, p.125]

### Option 2 — Double Reverse: Guard Reverse Into Post Entry
2 dribbles toward 1 (overplayed), then reverse dribbles and passes to 3
5 has flashed outside the lane; receives from 3
2 and 1 both cut back to their starting side and receive down screens from 3 and 4 respectively
5 distributes: to 2 or 3 off screens, or to 4 or 5 rolling to basket [S7, p.125]
Secondary: 5 passes to 1; 5 posts low; 4 goes outside arc; 1 passes to 2 → 2 dribbles toward 1 (reverse dribble) → passes to 1 off 3's screen → 1 passes to 3 → 5 side-screens for 2 then pops; 3 distributes to 1, 2, 5, or 4 [S7, p.126]

### Option 3 — Overplayed Guard Backdoor + Strong-Side Options
2 is overplayed → 1 dribbles toward 2; 2 cuts backdoor to opposite side
1 reverse dribbles and reads three strong-side options:
- **4 screens for 2 and rolls to basket** [Fig 7.43]
- **4 fakes the screen and cuts to basket directly**
- **4 posts up down low** [Fig 7.44]

Alternative: 1 passes to 2, receives 5's side screen, gets the ball back from 2, and plays **pick-and-roll with 5** [Fig 7.45]

### Reverse Dribble With Double Screen High
The most complex reverse dribble variant:
1 dribbles toward overplayed 2; 2 and 3 exchange spots; 1 reverse dribbles and passes to 5 (flashed to elbow)
3 and 1 set a double screen on the opposite side of the court
5 distributes with four options:
- Pass to 2 rubbing off the double screen high or low
- After dribbles, pass to 4 on a backdoor cut if overplayed [Fig 7.47]
- Pass to 2 who fakes to rub off the screen then cuts backdoor
- Pass to 1 who pops out of the double screen [Fig 7.48]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (1)**: primary reverse dribbler; reads which option the defense gives; delivers to post at elbow or attacks the screen action
- **SG (2)**: reads the reverse dribble as a signal — if overplayed, go backdoor; if not, rub off the screen that sets up
- **SF (3)**: sets screens or exchanges as signaled by the ball-handler's direction; rolls to basket after screening
- **PF (4)**: backdoor cutter, screener, or post depending on which option is called by the ball-handler's read
- **C (5)**: always flashes to the weak-side elbow on the reverse dribble signal; receives the pass and becomes the distribution hub

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stopping the reverse dribble and holding the ball** → correction: keep moving; the reverse dribble is a transitional action, not a reset
2. **Post (5) not recognizing the signal** → correction: anytime the ball-handler reverse dribbles, 5 immediately flashes to the elbow
3. **Other players standing still during the reverse dribble** → correction: the reverse dribble triggers simultaneous movement by everyone — cuts and screens must start at the same moment
4. **Ball-handler not reading the strong side** → correction: after the reverse, look at the strong side for the backdoor, screen-and-roll, or post-up option before forcing a weak-side pass
