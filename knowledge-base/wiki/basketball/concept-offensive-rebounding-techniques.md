# Offensive Rebounding Techniques

## Summary
Offensive rebounding is as important as defensive rebounding to a successful basketball team. A team that crashes the offensive glass relieves pressure on its shooters (missed shots don't necessarily mean lost possessions), creates the most high-percentage shots available (stickbacks close to the basket), and attacks a defense at its most disorganized moment — immediately after a shot. Three pivot-based techniques exist to defeat a defensive box-out and secure an offensive rebound. All three require pivots. [S6, pp.215-217]

Coaching philosophy: *"Look at every missed shot by yourself or a teammate as a pass to you."* Against zone and trapping defenses, offensive rebounders have an especially large advantage because box-out assignments are nonexistent in those schemes. [S6, p.216]

## When to Use
- On every missed shot — all offensive players should pursue the offensive rebound unless assigned to protect against the break
- Especially valuable against zone defenses where no formal box-out assignment exists
- Stickbacks are among the highest-percentage shots available — taken close to the basket against a completely disorganized defense
- Offensive rebounding relieves pressure on shooters: they are more willing to shoot knowing a miss is not necessarily a turnover

## Key Principles
1. **Every missed shot is a pass** — offensive players must maintain an aggressive mentality toward the offensive glass [S6, p.216]
2. **All three offensive rebounding methods require pivots** — footwork is as critical offensively as defensively [S6, p.215]
3. **Sell the team on the value before teaching the technique** — players won't crash hard unless they believe offensive rebounds matter [S6, p.216]
4. **Stickbacks are the most ideal shots an offensive team can have** — closer to the basket, against a disorganized defense, with no set defensive scheme protecting the rim [S6, p.202]
5. **Zone/trapping defenses = greater offensive rebounding advantage** — no formal box-out assignments makes offensive glass very accessible [S6, p.216]
6. **Even a second missed stickback allows no time for the defense to re-organize** for another box-out [S6, p.202]

## Three Methods of Offensive Rebounding

### Method 1: Butt-to-Butt Reverse Spin
- When the shot goes up, the offensive rebounder makes a quick **reverse pivot** and makes contact with the defender — literally spinning off of him
- Either foot can serve as the pivot foot for the reverse pivot
- Cue: *"Go butt-to-butt and spin off"*
- Best used when the offensive player is ahead of the defender and can initiate contact first [S6, p.215]

### Method 2: Front Crossover Pivot + Swim Move
- The offensive rebounder uses a **front crossover pivot** and simultaneously performs the **swim move** with the arm closest to the defender
- To attack the defender's right side: left foot is the pivot foot, right foot makes the front crossover step
- To attack the defender's left side: right foot is the pivot foot, left foot makes the front crossover step
- The swim move drives the arm over the defender's arm, creating space to get around the box-out [S6, pp.215-216]

### Method 3: Step-Back and Read
- The offensive player steps backward, **breaking physical contact** with the defensive box-out player
- When the defensive player looks over his shoulder to locate the offensive player, he reveals which shoulder he turned to
- The offensive player immediately makes a **front crossover pivot** and attacks the **opposite shoulder** — the side the defender did NOT look to, which is now unprotected [S6, p.216]
- Best used against disciplined defenders who maintain strong box-out contact

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Know and practice all three offensive rebounding methods; pursue every missed shot with the mentality that it's a pass to you
- **Post players (PF, C)**: Primary offensive rebounders — crashing is expected; use all three methods; second-effort stickbacks are high-percentage scoring plays
- **Wings (SG, SF)**: Must also crash — shooters who follow their shot contribute to offensive rebounding and take psychological pressure off their shooting
- **PG**: Usually balances crashing with protecting against the defensive fast break; situational offensive rebounding

## Common Mistakes
1. **Passive mentality about offensive rebounding** → Coaches must sell the team on the value before teaching technique; players who don't believe in crashing won't execute [S6, p.216]
2. **Not using a pivot to counter the box-out** → Standing still against a box-out never works; all three methods require an active pivot-based response [S6, p.215]
3. **Swim move without a pivot** → The swim without a crossover pivot step puts the offensive player in a bad angle to get around the defender [S6, p.216]
4. **Step-back without reading the defender's turn** → The step-back only creates an opportunity when the defender looks over one shoulder; the offensive player must read which shoulder was turned and go the opposite way [S6, p.216]
5. **Not crashing against zone defenses** → Zone defenses have no box-out assignments; offensive players who don't crash are leaving free rebounds [S6, p.216]
