# Jump Shot Mechanics

## Summary
The jump shot is built on a precise sequence: calling for the ball, catching into the shot pocket, using a one-two step to generate power and momentum, elevating, releasing just before the top of the jump, and freezing a disciplined follow-through. Every element of this sequence is interdependent — a flaw in one phase cascades into missed shots. The one-two step (non-shooting foot first, then shooting foot) is the engine of the shot, transferring leg drive into the ball and keeping the shooter as the aggressor rather than allowing the defender to close out comfortably [S5, p.61].

The same core mechanics apply regardless of shot distance — midrange or three-point. The major variable that changes with distance is the power required, which is addressed by adjusting the length and aggressiveness of the one-two step and the depth of the pre-reception position [S5, pp.62-63].

## When to Use
- Catch-and-shoot opportunities off pass from any direction
- Pull-up jump shots off the live dribble
- Midrange shots (12–18 ft) as the primary scoring weapon
- Three-point shots after mastering the midrange

## Key Principles
1. **One-two step, not jump stop.** Receive the ball with a one-two step (non-shooting foot first, then shooting foot). A jump stop kills momentum, making the defender the aggressor and forcing the shooter to reload. The one-two step keeps momentum flowing into the shot [S5, p.61].
2. **Do not catch and pause.** As the two of the one-two step lands, elevate immediately. Pausing resets your momentum and slows the shot [S5, p.61].
3. **Release before the top of the jump.** Release the ball just before reaching peak height. Waiting until the top causes momentum to swing downward, producing short shots [S5, p.62].
4. **Elbow above the eyebrow on follow-through.** The shooting elbow must finish fully extended, above the eyebrow and in front of the face — not behind the ear (too much arc) and not below the eyebrow (flat shot, rim-clangers, no swishes) [S5, p.63].
5. **Balance hand stays still.** The balance hand finishes on its own side of the body with fingertips pointing toward the ceiling at ~45 degrees. Dropping the balance hand causes short misses and disrupts shot line [S5, p.63].
6. **Freeze the follow-through.** Hold the follow-through until the ball passes through the net. Dropping early signals lack of focus on swishing and often accompanies "dancing feet" (imbalance) [S5, pp.63, 73].
7. **Shoot and stay on longer shots.** On shots from 15 ft and beyond, stay in position rather than following the shot. Longer shots produce longer rebounds. Staying puts the shooter in position to track long misses or transition back [S5, p.63].
8. **Catch in the shot pocket.** Hand positioning and grip should be set at the catch — catching in the shot pocket eliminates the need to adjust before shooting [S5, p.62].

## Calling for the Ball
Calling for the ball is an active, three-part process that prepares the shooter to receive in rhythm [S5, p.61]:
- **Verbal demand**: Loud and insistent — "Ball, ball, ball, ball!" A quiet request in a gymnasium is useless.
- **Eye contact**: Make eye contact with the passer before the catch so both are on the same page.
- **Hand target**: Show a palm-facing-passer hand target (like a catcher's mitt). This pre-loads the shooting arm into the L-shaped position, keeping the shooter on shot line from the moment of the catch.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Master the one-two step catch-and-shoot before developing the pull-up or three-point shot.

## Variations
### Pull-Up Jump Shot
Applies one-two step mechanics to a live dribble. The dribble acts as a "pass to yourself" — push the ball out in front so it leads into the shot. Long stride with the live (non-pivot) foot gets the shooter low and past the defender. When going to the weak hand, "load the gun" — let the ball come up into the weak hand, then bring it across to the shooting hand — to maintain shot line alignment [S5, pp.63-64].

### Straight-On Three-Point Shot
Same mechanics as midrange, but the one-two step must be slightly more pronounced to generate power for the added distance. Start 3–5 ft behind the arc to allow room for the step. Right-handed players step left foot first, then right foot [S5, pp.72-73].

### On-the-Move Three-Point Shot
Moving from left to right (right-handed): step aggressively with the left (inside) foot in a heel-to-toe open step toward the target, then bring the right (shooting) foot around. Moving from right to left (right-handed): step with the right foot, open toward the target, then "stub" the left foot to create a proper shooting base. Overstriding leads to misalignment and missed left/right [S5, pp.75-77].

### Shuffle-Step Three
Used when hugging the three-point line without forward momentum (like a defensive slide). The most difficult three-point technique — the shooter lacks forward momentum into the shot. Practice shot fakes and jab steps before the shuffle-step three to simulate game pressure [S5, p.78].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Jump stop instead of one-two step** → Kills momentum, allows defender to close out; use one-two step to stay the aggressor [S5, p.61].
2. **Catching and pausing** → Breaks momentum chain; the two of the step must already be in motion as the ball arrives [S5, p.61].
3. **Releasing at the top of the jump** → Shot falls short; release just before the peak [S5, p.62].
4. **Elbow below the eyebrow at follow-through** → Flat, streaky shooting; shots clang front/back of rim; no swishes [S5, p.63].
5. **Elbow finishing behind the ear** → Too much arc, inconsistent depth [S5, p.63].
6. **Dropping the balance hand** → Shot line pulled off, short misses; balance hand must stay still with fingertips to sky [S5, p.63].
7. **Dropping follow-through early** → "Dancing feet," signals imbalance and lack of focus on the target [S5, p.63].
8. **High-low-high motion (dipping the ball)** → Creates negative motion, slows shot release, allows defender to contest; catch low and stay low into the shot [S5, pp.65-66].
9. **Dribbling ball straight down on pull-up** → Ball bounces off foot; push the ball out in front [S5, p.64].
10. **Overstriding on the on-the-move three** → Shot foundation lost, alignment breaks down, misses left or right [S5, p.76].
