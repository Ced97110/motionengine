# Late-Game Defensive Adjustments (Del Harris)

## Summary
Del Harris provides a systematic late-game defensive framework developed with the pro team. The core insight is that late-game strategy must be planned in advance — "from the security of your office, not the heat of the moment." Harris identifies the three plays most likely to beat a lead-protecting defense and provides specific time-and-score thresholds for every fouling decision. [S7, pp.266-269]

## When to Use
- Final 3 minutes of any competitive game
- Any situation where the lead could be erased by a single possession or two
- Whenever the opponent needs a three-pointer to tie

## Three Plays That Beat a Lead-Protecting Defense
1. **The three-pointer** — fastest route for the trailing team
2. **The quick two-point score with minimal clock consumption**
3. **The offensive rebound put-back** — decides most 1- or 2-point games

## Key Principles
1. Plan every late-game scenario ahead of time — never improvise under pressure
2. Have players practice all late-game situations regularly (Jim Calhoun drills 9 per practice)
3. Switching is critical when protecting a lead — use a lineup that can switch readily
4. **Do NOT drop to help on penetration** when protecting against a three-pointer — the natural help drop opens rhythm catch-and-shoot threes
5. Block out aggressively — the put-back is the most common game-decider
6. Always try to steal the inbounds pass before fouling

## Fouling Rules (When Trailing)

### pro-Level Guidelines
- **Deficit of 10+ points**: Begin fouling at **3 minutes** remaining
- **Deficit of 7-9 points**: Begin fouling at the **90-second mark**
- **Deficit of 6-1 points (1-2 possession game)**: Foul with **30 seconds or less**
- **All situations**: Try to steal the inbounds pass before committing a foul

### Lower Level Adjustments
- The lower the level, the SOONER to start fouling — teams are less likely to make free throws under pressure
- Always know the opponent's best and worst free-throw shooters from scouting
- "Hack-a-a dominant center" approach: intentionally foul the worst free-throw shooter to prevent high-percentage scoring (dunk → FT)

## Fouling With a Three-Point Lead

### Data-Based Decision
- pro team statistical analysis: odds favor fouling with **6 or fewer seconds remaining**, even if the opponent shoots 90% from the FT line
- Harris's prior rule of thumb was 5 seconds or less, which proved effective in practice
- **Critical constraint**: The foul must come in a situation where the player cannot go into a quick shooting motion (which would award 3 free throws) or — worst case — a four-point play opportunity

## Defending the Three-Point Line (Late Game)

### When Opponent Needs a Three to Tie
1. All 5 defenders must play on the **top side** of their assignments (Figure 16.15)
2. Prevent passes to the strong-side corner (most dangerous three-point location)
3. **Do NOT drop back** to help on penetration — this creates rhythm catch-and-shoot opportunities
4. Switch out on ALL picks — put in a lineup that can switch readily; remove big/slow or poor defenders
5. Remain in **containment mode** while switching — defend ball against quick score AND block out for the rebound
6. Be ready for the trailing team's pattern: get quick 2 first, then go for the three

### Five-Man Arc Strategy
- Most extreme option: all 5 defenders line up in an arc ABOVE the three-point line
- Requires practicing this in advance
- Must still communicate on all switches within the arc

## Late-Game Drills

### OOB Games
- Series of 3 games to 3 baskets from each OOB position: side out, end out, full court, 3/4 court, baseline corners
- Vary the game clock: 20, 10, 5, and 1 second remaining
- Every change of possession starts an OOB play

### Two-Minute Game
- Defense is always 2 points behind regardless of actual score
- Each possession change: ball team protects a 2-point lead, defense tries to close
- Only the clock changes — the margin stays constant
- Teaches both protecting a 2-point lead AND closing a 2-point deficit in a single drill
- Vary the margin: tied, 1 pt, 3 pts, 5 pts up/behind

### Seven- or Nine-Possession Game
- Each team keeps the ball for 7 or 9 possessions, cycling through all late-game scenarios:
  1. Full-court inbounds vs. man-to-man press
  2. Full-court inbounds vs. zone press
  3. Three-quarter court inbounds vs. man or zone
  4. Front-court SLOB vs. man-to-man
  5. End BLOB vs. man-to-man
  6. Set play vs. man-to-man (need to score)
  7. Set play vs. zone (need to score)
  - Optional additions: SLOB vs. zone, half-court inbound needing three, 5 seconds left

### Score-and-Time Series
- 1 pt behind, opponent shooting 2 FTs
- 1 pt behind, opponent's SLOB
- 1 pt ahead, opponent's BLOB
- 1 pt ahead, opponent's full-court inbound
- Repeat for 2-, 3-, 4-, 5-point deficits and leads

### O-D-O (Three-Possession Game)
- Coach sets score/time situation
- Team A runs 3 consecutive possessions (full court, side OOB, or end OOB)
- Offense scores = 1 point; defense gets stop = 1 point
- After 3 possessions: either 2-1 or 3-0 winner
- Winner keeps the ball; coach picks a new scenario

### 100-Situation Cards
- 100 game situation cards printed in advance
- Players draw a card and "coach" their team through the situation
- Both teams play out 2 possessions; discuss what went right and wrong

## Common Mistakes
1. **No predefined fouling formula** → Not having a plan leads to indecision; settle the thresholds before the season
2. **Dropping to help on penetration late** → The natural instinct to drop is exactly what creates open catch-and-shoot threes; fight this with drilling
3. **Not practicing late-game scenarios** → Players freeze in situations they haven't rehearsed; drill at least 9 scenarios per practice (Calhoun method)
4. **Forgetting the rebound** → Even good late-game defense loses if the offensive rebound put-back is surrendered; never forget to block out
