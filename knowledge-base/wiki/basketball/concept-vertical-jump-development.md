# Vertical Jump Development for Basketball

## Summary
Vertical jump ability is one of the most important physical attributes in basketball, directly affecting rebounding, shot contesting, layup finishing, and transition plays. Jump height is not merely a product of leg strength — it is primarily driven by **reactive power**: the ability to rapidly transition from an eccentric (landing/loading) phase to a concentric (takeoff) phase through the stretch-shortening cycle of the musculotendinous system.

Effective vertical jump training for basketball must be **game-specific**: training bilateral jumps off two feet (rebounding scenarios), unilateral jumps off one foot (layups, finish moves), and reactive jumps with minimal ground contact (second-effort rebounds, putbacks, and consecutive play situations). [S2, pp.111-125]

## When to Use
- **Pre-season**: Foundation phase of explosive training before skill integration
- **Individual player development**: Any player whose rebounding, shot-contest, or layup-finishing is limited by jump height
- **Post-injury return**: Systematic re-introduction of jump training after ankle, knee, or lower-body injury

## Key Principles
1. **Arm drive is a multiplier**: Throwing both arms upward simultaneously on takeoff significantly increases jump height — "throw the arms, the legs follow."
2. **The stretch-shortening cycle is the engine**: The faster the transition from landing to takeoff, the more elastic energy is stored and released. Ground contact time should be minimized.
3. **Single-leg and double-leg jump patterns are different skills**: Train both. Double-leg jumps for rebounding; single-leg jumps for layup and driving finish scenarios.
4. **Landing mechanics enable jumping**: Poor landing mechanics (stiff legs, knee cave, forward trunk) limit the load the stretch-shortening cycle can handle. Fix landings first.
5. **Deceleration training is part of vertical jump training**: The ability to decelerate rapidly on landing is what protects the joints and enables immediate reactive takeoff.
6. **Game-specific simulation accelerates transfer**: Simulating a jump shot at the peak of a vertical jump (as in the depth jump into jump shot) improves the ability to shoot over defenders.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Neglecting arm drive** → Players who jump with arms at their sides lose significant height — coach arm throw on every jump
2. **Long ground contact time** → Players who settle into a deep squat before jumping rather than reacting off the floor lose the stretch-shortening advantage
3. **Only training double-leg jumps** → Basketball requires explosive single-leg takeoffs constantly; unilateral training is essential
4. **Skipping the depth jump** → Depth jumps (stepping off a box) are among the most effective methods to develop reactive power but are often overlooked in favor of simple box jumps
5. **Incorrect box height selection** → Too high too soon damages joints and teaches poor mechanics; progress conservatively
