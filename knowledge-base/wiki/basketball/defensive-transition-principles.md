# Defensive Transition Principles

## Summary
Transition defense is the first and most urgent defensive responsibility in Herb Brown's system. Every missed shot, turnover, or made basket creates the opportunity for an opponent's fast break — and the first five seconds of a defensive transition determine whether the defense will be organized or scrambling. Brown's system prioritizes: (1) get back before the offense gets ahead of you, (2) protect the paint first, then build out, and (3) communicate assignments immediately to avoid defensive confusion.

The critical rule: **the fifth player in a fast-break opportunity must stay back.** Running all five players in transition leaves the defense exposed to a long pass and easy layup — catastrophic floor imbalance. [S1, p.28]

## When to Use
- After every missed or made shot — defensive transition begins the moment possession changes
- After every turnover by the defense
- As a daily drill emphasis in every practice
- When scouting shows the opponent prefers to push in transition

## Key Principles
1. **Get back first.** The defensive team's first obligation after a shot or turnover is to get back to the paint before the opponent gets ahead of them.
2. **Protect the paint, then build out.** The first defender back secures the paint — not the perimeter. Help arrives and builds the defense out from the goal.
3. **Stay level with the ball.** Never let an offensive player get behind you in transition. Getting "behind the play" means allowing an unchecked cutter toward the basket.
4. **The fifth player in a fast break MUST stay back.** When four players are pushing transition offense, the fifth player cannot join — he must trail the play and maintain defensive floor balance. An opponent steal of a long pass leads to a fast-break layup at the other end. [S1, p.28]
5. **Communicate assignments immediately.** When picking up a free man (a teammate's opponent), call it out: "I've got him!" This prevents two defenders from covering the same player and leaving another opponent open.
6. **Stop ball reversal in transition.** Do not let the offense easily change sides in early offense — this stretches the defense before it can organize.
7. **All five players make the transition from offense to defense together.** No exceptions — not in practice, not in a game.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: First back in most defensive transitions; calls out assignments; directs teammates to cover free men.
- **SG/SF**: Sprint back to at least free-throw-line level before the ball crosses half court; prevent any opponent from getting behind them.
- **PF**: Sprint back to the paint if not rebounding; do not float in the backcourt.
- **C**: If not rebounding, sprint back immediately; protect the paint; do not let the opponent's big receive the ball in the paint in transition.

## Variations
### If the Point Guard Penetrates
Wings must cover back defensively. Do not let the wings chase their own offensive assignment — transition coverage is the immediate priority. [S1, p.27]

### If a Wing Penetrates
The point guard and the opposite wing cover back defensively. Three players are distributed to prevent any unchecked cutters to the basket. [S1, p.27]

### Against a Good Transition Team
Extend defensive pressure up the court to intercept the offense before they build momentum. The first defender back pins the paint; subsequent defenders build a wall from the paint outward. Never let the opponent outnumber your team in the front court.

## Common Mistakes
1. **All five players running transition offense** → The fifth player must stay back; fast breaks given up off offensive leak-outs are among the most preventable easy baskets in basketball.
2. **Defender trailing the play** → Always get level with the ball; trailing means the offense has already gotten ahead of you.
3. **Two defenders picking up the same transition opponent** → Communicate immediately; one open player in transition leads directly to an open layup.
4. **First defender stopping at the three-point line** → The first man back must get to the paint, not the perimeter; protect the rim first, then recover outward.
