# Snatch Pull From Hang Position

## Objective
Develop explosive lower-body power and triple extension using a wide barbell grip, serving as the prerequisite exercise before learning the full power snatch.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Barbell with appropriate weight (start light, prioritize technique)
- Open floor or weightroom
- Should be performed after mastering the kettlebell swing

## Execution
1. Grip the barbell with hands **significantly wider than shoulder width** (snatch grip). Place the barbell against the midthighs, just above the knees.
2. Shoulders should be slightly over (in front of) the barbell. Knees slightly bent, back flat, head facing straight ahead. This is the hang starting position.
3. Initiate by **extending the legs** while simultaneously **thrusting the hips forward** and bending the elbows as the barbell rises toward the shoulders. Keep the bar close to the body — do not let it swing away.
4. The bar should rise to approximately **chest level** as the ankles, knees, and hips reach full extension at the top.
5. Once maximum bar height is achieved, slowly decelerate and lower the bar to starting position by flexing the knees and sitting back with the hips.
6. Perform the prescribed number of repetitions. [S2, pp.94–95]

## Coaching Points
- Bar path is vertical — "pull the bar straight up the body"
- Triple extension happens simultaneously: ankle plantar-flexion, knee extension, hip extension
- Elbows lead the bar upward; arms do not initiate the movement
- Do NOT allow the bar to drift forward — it should stay within 2–3 inches of the torso throughout
- Decelerate slowly on the descent — control is part of the exercise

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Kettlebell swing to groove the hip thrust pattern before adding the barbell
2. **Intermediate**: Snatch pull from hang with moderate load, focusing on bar speed and triple extension
3. **Advanced**: Power snatch from hang position (adding the overhead catch)

## Muscles Involved
**Primary**: Gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, semitendinosus, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis, vastus intermedius, trapezius, lateral deltoid, anterior deltoid

**Secondary**: Gastrocnemius, biceps femoris, semimembranosus, rectus femoris [S2, p.95]

## Basketball Focus
The snatch pull improves jumping ability through rapid force production. Because the bar must be decelerated at the top and returned under control, it also develops the eccentric strength needed for safe landing — important for reducing knee injury risk when coming down from rebounds or jump shots. [S2, p.95]
