# Youth Basketball Dribbling Fundamentals

## Summary
Before players can execute any dribble move, they must master the five universal dribbling principles that apply across all situations — from a simple speed dribble to a complex crossover. These principles form the foundation of every ballhandling drill in youth basketball and are repeated consistently across all drill teaching points in this source.

The four **basic perimeter dribbling moves** are: (1) in-and-out, (2) crossover, (3) spin/reverse, and (4) half-spin. Each is a change-of-direction tool with specific mechanics and an appropriate game situation.

## When to Use
- Every dribbling situation in practice and games
- Particularly critical when under defensive pressure or approaching a trap
- When initiating a drive, attacking a closeout, or escaping a double team

## Key Principles
1. **Ball position**: Keep the ball 2–3 feet (.6–.9 m) away from and beside the knee — body between the ball and the defender at all times. "Ball to the side, away from the foot and knee to avoid turnovers."
2. **Eyes up**: Eyes must be straight ahead — **never** looking at the basketball. Court vision depends on training this habit from day one.
3. **Change of pace**: Players must train their feet to move quickly, stop on a dime, then explode — varying pace unpredictably is the foundation of getting open off the dribble.
4. **Fingerpads**: Players **always** dribble with the first pads of the fingers — never the palm. This gives control, touch, and the ability to execute any move.
5. **Control vs. speed stance**: Choose one — use control stance when under pressure or executing a move; shift to speed stance when clear of defenders. Do not mix them arbitrarily.

## The Four Basic Perimeter Dribble Moves
1. **In-and-out**: One-handed fake crossover — ball comes in front of the body then is pulled back to the same side. Attacks the gap between the defender's feet.
2. **Crossover**: Ball transferred low and quick from one hand to the other. Ball must be no higher than mid-lower leg during the transfer — its least protected moment.
3. **Spin (reverse)**: 180° reverse spin protecting the ball through the turn. Ball stays in the original hand until the spin is **complete** — switching too early exposes the ball.
4. **Half-spin**: Partial reverse spin that sends the ball back in the **original direction**. Counters defenders who read and react to a full spin.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Looking at the ball** → Constant enforcement: "Eyes up, always up." Cone drills and tag games naturally cure this.
2. **Ball too close to the foot/knee** → Ball gets kicked; give it the 2–3 ft buffer to the side.
3. **High crossover** → Crossover above mid-lower leg height is easily stolen; keep it low and quick.
4. **Switching hands too early on the spin** → Leaves the ball vulnerable; wait for the full 180° turn.
5. **One-speed dribbling** → Defenders can time a predictable pace; change of pace is the primary deceptive weapon.
6. **Palm dribbling** → Loss of touch and control; fingerpads provide the tactile feedback needed for deceptive moves.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG**: Master all four moves and both hands — primary ballhandlers must be unpredictable in all directions.
- **SF/PF/C**: Must master at least the crossover and spin for high-post and elbow situations; retreat dribble is critical for avoiding backcourt traps.
