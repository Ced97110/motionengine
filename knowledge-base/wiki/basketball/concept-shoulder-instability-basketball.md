# Shoulder Instability in Basketball — Management Framework

## Summary
Basketball athletes are at elevated risk for shoulder instability due to frequent low-velocity contact with the arm in an overhead position. The natural history of shoulder instability is a high propensity for recurrent dislocation — especially in young athletes — making surgical management the preferred treatment in most competitive basketball players. Choosing the correct procedure depends on patient age, number of previous dislocations, degree of glenoid bone loss, and presence of engaging Hill-Sachs lesions. Return to play is typically achievable by 6 months postoperatively with high success rates. [S10, pp.274-276]

## When to Use This Framework
- Initial shoulder dislocation (traumatic, anterior) in a basketball player
- Recurrent shoulder instability
- Pre-surgical decision making: arthroscopic vs. open Bankart vs. Latarjet vs. free bone block
- Post-surgical rehabilitation planning
- In-season vs. off-season management decisions

## Key Principles

### Risk Stratification — Instability Severity Index Score (ISIS)
The ISIS is a 10-point preoperative scoring tool to select between arthroscopic and open procedures [S10, p.274]:

| Prognostic Factor | Points |
|---|---|
| Age ≤ 20 years | 2 |
| Age > 20 years | 0 |
| Competitive sport participation | 2 |
| Recreational/no sport | 0 |
| Contact or forced overhead sport (basketball) | 1 |
| Other sport type | 0 |
| Shoulder hyperlaxity (anterior or inferior) | 1 |
| Normal laxity | 0 |
| Hill-Sachs visible on AP X-ray in external rotation | 2 |
| Hill-Sachs not visible | 0 |
| Glenoid loss of contour on AP X-ray | 2 |
| No lesion | 0 |
| **Total** | **10** |

**ISIS score > 6 = 70% recurrence risk after arthroscopic Bankart repair.** Open procedure indicated. [S10, p.274]

### Surgical Procedure Selection

**Arthroscopic Bankart Repair** — Indicated for:
- First-time dislocation (preferred)
- Recurrent dislocation with ALL of: no previous surgery, <15% glenoid bone loss, non-engaging Hill-Sachs, no/minimal hyperlaxity [S10, p.275]

**Open Bankart Repair** — Indicated for:
- Failed arthroscopic surgery
- First-time or recurrent dislocation with same criteria as arthroscopic (surgeon preference)
- Note: recurrent dislocations may overstretch the capsule making open repair preferable [S10, p.275]

**Latarjet Procedure** — Indicated for:
- Failed arthroscopic or open surgery
- Glenoid bone loss >15%
- Engaging Hill-Sachs lesion
- Moderate to significant hyperlaxity [S10, p.275]

**Free Bone Block (iliac crest autograft or distal tibial allograft)** — Indicated for:
- Alternative or salvage when Latarjet fails
- Maintains normal coracoid anatomy
- Similar clinical results to Latarjet
- Drawbacks: iliac crest harvest morbidity, loss of conjoint tendon sling effect, bone resorption risk [S10, p.274]

## Postoperative Rehabilitation Phases

### Phase 1: Immobilization (Weeks 0–4)
- Period of immobilization
- Pendulum exercises
- Isometric muscle contractions [S10, pp.274-275]

### Phase 2: Passive/Active-Assisted ROM (Weeks 4–8)
- Passive range of motion
- Progress to active-assisted ROM without resistance
- Duration: approximately 4 weeks [S10, p.275]

### Phase 3: Full ROM + Strength Reacquisition (Weeks 8–12)
- Regain full active range of motion
- Progressive strengthening exercises [S10, p.275]

### Phase 4: Return-to-Sport Preparation (Month 3+)
- Plyometric exercises
- Advanced upper extremity strengthening
- Sport-specific exercises [S10, p.275]

### Return-to-Play Clearance
- Typical timeline: **4–6 months**
- For basketball players (overhead + contact): **minimum 6 months** recommended [S10, p.275]
- NBA study (Minhas et al.): 46 NBA players undergoing surgical stabilization — **93.5% return-to-play rate**, increased games played, minimal change in player efficiency rating [S10, p.275]
- General surgical return-to-play rates: 63–93% depending on athlete type

## Common Mistakes
1. **Conservative management in young competitive players** → Conservative treatment is "usually doomed to failure" in basketball due to high overhead contact demands; early surgical intervention is key [S10, p.276]
2. **Arthroscopic repair with >15% glenoid bone loss** → Unacceptably high failure rates; bone augmentation procedure required [S10, pp.274-276]
3. **Returning too soon** → Athletes performing overhead movements with unplanned collisions must wait minimum 6 months postoperatively [S10, p.275]
4. **Missing engaging Hill-Sachs** → Requires Latarjet or bone augmentation, not simple Bankart repair [S10, p.275]

## Risk Factors for Recurrent Instability
- Age < 20 years
- Competitive basketball participation (contact + overhead)
- Glenoid bone loss
- History of previous shoulder instability
- Collision athletes are at highest risk for surgical failure [S10, pp.275-276]
