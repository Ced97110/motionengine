# Orthobiologics for Tendon and Fascia Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Orthobiologics — corticosteroids, platelet-rich plasma (PRP), bone marrow aspirate concentrate (BMAC), and other cell-based therapies — are increasingly used to manage the tendon and fascia injuries common in basketball players. These injuries include patellar tendinopathy ("jumper's knee"), Achilles tendinopathy, Achilles tendon rupture, plantar fasciitis, and rotator cuff pathology. While conservative management (rest, ice, physical therapy, analgesics) remains the primary treatment, orthobiologics offer the potential to promote healing, reduce pain, and expedite return to play. Evidence quality and effect sizes vary substantially across injury types and biologic modalities. [S10, pp.561-567]

## When to Use
- Persistent patellar or Achilles tendinopathy that fails conservative management (>6 months)
- High-level athletes requiring expedited recovery with minimized re-injury risk
- As adjunct to standard rehabilitation, not replacement
- Post-surgical augmentation of tendon healing (e.g., Achilles repair, rotator cuff)

## Key Principles

### 1. Treatment Goal Varies by Injury Type
- **Acute tears**: Goal = increase cellular proliferation and promote/enhance healing
- **Chronic tendinopathy/tendinosis**: Goal = inhibit matrix-degrading proteases, modulate inflammation, and "jump-start" a healing response
- Other considerations: Specific tendon, injury location (myotendinous junction, intra-tendon, avulsion), timing, and dosing [S10, p.565]

### 2. Corticosteroids — "Use With Caution"
Corticosteroids decrease leukotrienes, prostaglandins, thromboxane A2, and prostacyclin; stabilize lysosomal membranes; decrease vascular permeability; alter neutrophil chemotaxis; and influence RNA transcription. They are powerful anti-inflammatory agents but carry significant risks:

**Benefits**:
- Improved return to play in hamstring injuries (NFL data — Levine et al., 58 players)
- Short-term symptom relief (6 weeks) for medial epicondylitis

**Risks (Nichols meta-analysis)**:
- Plantar fascia rupture: 53.7%
- Quadriceps/patellar tendon rupture: 9.5%
- Achilles tendon rupture: 8.4%
- Biceps tendon rupture: 8.4%
- Also: post-injection pain flares, subcutaneous fat atrophy, skin hypopigmentation
- Systemic: diabetes, weight gain, hypertension, psychosis
- No benefit over NSAIDs for Achilles tendinopathy [S10, pp.561-562]

**Coaching implication**: Corticosteroid injections into load-bearing tendons (patellar, Achilles) in basketball players carry substantial rupture risk and must be weighed carefully against return-to-play pressure.

### 3. Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP)
PRP is created by centrifugation of the patient's own blood to concentrate platelets above 1 million/mL. Platelets release growth factors (TGF-β1, PDGF, bFGF, VEGF, EGF, IGF-1) that modify inflammation and promote cell differentiation/proliferation.

**PRP Classification**:
- **LR-PRP** (Leukocyte-Rich): May have pro-inflammatory effects via IL-1β, TNF-α, and metalloproteinases — potentially adverse to tendon healing
- **LP-PRP** (Leukocyte-Poor): Fewer inflammatory mediators
- No significant difference between LR-PRP and LP-PRP demonstrated for Achilles tendinopathy (Hanisch et al.) [S10, p.563]

**WADA Note**: PRP itself is NOT prohibited; however, use of independent growth factors (PDGF, VEGF, IGF-1, FGF) IS prohibited. [S10, p.563]

**Evidence Summary by Injury**:

#### Patellar Tendinopathy
- **Dragoo et al. (RCT, n=23)**: LR-PRP superior to dry needling at 12 weeks (VISA-P +25.4 vs +5.2); no difference at 26 weeks
- **Vetrano et al. (RCT, n=46)**: Two PRP injections vs. three ESWT sessions — both improved; PRP showed significantly greater improvement at 6 and 12 months (P<0.05)
- **Scott et al. (Level I RCT)**: LR-PRP vs. LP-PRP vs. saline — NO significant differences at any time point. Saline itself may have some therapeutic benefit
- Multiple lower-evidence studies support PRP for patellar tendinopathy [S10, pp.565-566]

#### Achilles Tendinopathy
- **De Vos et al. (RCT, n=54)**: LR-PRP + eccentric exercise vs. saline + eccentric exercise — NO greater improvement in pain or activity
- **DeJonge et al.** (1-year follow-up): Same results; no difference in tendon appearance on ultrasound
- **Krogh et al. (RCT)**: No improvement in Achilles VISA after PRP vs. saline; PRP group showed significant increase in tendon thickness (potentially adverse for elite athletes)
- **Boesen et al. (RCT, n=60)**: PRP (4 injections, 14 days apart) vs. high-volume injection (HVI) vs. placebo — BOTH PRP and HVI effective at reducing pain, improving activity, reducing tendon thickness/vascularity [S10, p.567]

### 4. Cell-Based Therapies
**Mesenchymal Stem Cells (MSC)**: Potential to improve symptoms and augment healing of tissues with poor intrinsic healing (cartilage, tendon, ligament). Limited clinical evidence.

#### Bone Marrow Aspirate Concentrate (BMAC)
- FDA-approved stem cell delivery method
- Contains progenitor cells, growth factors, cytokines
- Aspirated from iliac crest (percutaneous, low donor site morbidity)
- MSC concentration after centrifugation: ~0.001–0.01% — very low
- Multiple aspiration sites increase progenitor concentration
- Strong evidence for cartilage/meniscal pathology; limited evidence for soft tissue injury

**Patellar tendinopathy — Pascual-Garrido et al.** (n=8, 5-year follow-up): Significantly higher Tegner, IKDC, and KOOS scores after BMAC injection [S10, p.566-567]

#### Other Cell Sources
- **Amniotic tissue MSC**: Higher MSC concentration (0.9–1.5%) than BMAC; high plasticity, low immunogenicity, no donor site morbidity; allogenic
- **Adipose-derived MSC**: Abundant, easily harvested; autologous; less ethical concern
- **Tendon-derived stem cells (TDSC)**: Preclinical rat model showed improved tensile properties in patellar tendon defects

#### Adipose SVF for Achilles Tendinopathy
- **Usuelli et al. (RCT)**: SVF vs. PRP — SVF superior at 15 and 30 days for pain/function; no difference at later follow-ups [S10, p.567]

**Key Limitation**: No laboratory cell culture/expansion is permitted in the USA (FDA prohibition). True delivered viable MSC counts are unknown. Lack of standardization exposes athletes to infection risk.

## Jumper's Knee Classification (Patellar/Quadriceps Tendinopathy)
Prevalence: Up to 30% of basketball players (Lian et al.: 31.9%; Zwerver et al.: 11.8% non-elite).

Location: Patellar tendon proximal insertion 65–70%; quadriceps tendon at superior patellar pole 20–25%; patellar tendon tibial tuberosity 5–10%.

| Grade | Blazina | Ferretti | Roels |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | — | No pain | — |
| I | Post-activity pain, no impairment | Post-intense activity pain, no restriction | Infrapatellar/suprapatellar pain after training |
| II | During and after activity, satisfactory performance | Moderate pain, no restriction | Pain at activity start, disappears with warm-up |
| III | Prolonged pain, difficulty performing | Slight performance restriction | Pain during and after, unable to participate |
| IV | — | Severe performance restriction | Complete tendon rupture |
| V | — | Daily activity pain, unable to participate | — |

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using corticosteroids in load-bearing tendons** → high rupture risk (patellar, Achilles, plantar fascia) — reserve for low-risk scenarios with patient fully informed
2. **Expecting all PRP to be equivalent** → LR vs LP-PRP, platelet concentration, preparation method, and activation status all vary; standardization is lacking
3. **Over-promising cell-based therapies** → no high-quality clinical evidence for MSC in soft tissue injuries; unsubstantiated claims abound in direct-to-athlete marketing
4. **Neglecting rehabilitation alongside biologics** → PRP/BMAC are adjuncts; eccentric loading programs remain essential for tendinopathy management
5. **Ignoring PRP for Achilles tendinopathy based on single studies** → Boesen et al. shows benefit when combined with eccentric exercise; earlier RCTs (De Vos) used LR-PRP which may be inappropriate for this tissue
