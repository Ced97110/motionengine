# Elbow Anatomy and Biomechanics for Basketball

## Summary
The elbow is a modified hinged joint providing hinged motion at the ulnohumeral and radiocapitellar joints, and rotational motion at the radiocapitellar and proximal radioulnar joints. Stability is provided by osseous, capsuloligamentous, and muscular structures that work in concert. Understanding this anatomy is essential for properly diagnosing and treating the common elbow injuries seen in basketball players. [S10, pp.281-283]

## Osseous Anatomy

### Proximal Articular Surface (Distal Humerus)
- **Trochlea** — pulley-shaped structure on distal humerus; medial ridge extends more distally than lateral ridge creating 6–8° of valgus tilt; posterior tilt prevents posterior translation [S10, p.281]
- **Capitellum** — hemispheric structure lateral to the trochlea [S10, p.281]

### Distal Articular Surface (Proximal Ulna and Radius)
- **Coronoid** — prevents posterior displacement; 50% of coronoid needed for stability in extension [S10, p.281]
- **Olecranon** — triceps insertion; tendon size correlates with olecranon size [S10, p.282]
- **Greater sigmoid notch** — highly congruent with trochlea; forms primary constraint [S10, p.281]
- **Radial head** — concave surface articulating with capitellum; covered by 280° of articular cartilage; significant role in valgus stability [S10, p.281]

### Normal ROM
- Flexion: 145°
- Extension: 0°
- Pronation: 75°
- Supination: 85° [S10, p.283]

## Ligamentous Anatomy

### Medial Collateral Ligament (MCL)
- Three components: Anterior Oblique Ligament (AOL — strongest), Posterior Oblique Ligament (POL), Transverse Ligament
- Provides restraint to valgus and internal rotatory loads [S10, p.281]

### Lateral Collateral Ligament (LCL)
- Components: Lateral Ulnar Collateral Ligament (LUCL), Radial Collateral Ligament (RCL), Annular Ligament (AL), Accessory Lateral Collateral Ligament (ALCL)
- Resists varus and external rotation stresses with capsule and bony architecture
- Complete LCL transection → posterolateral rotatory and varus instability [S10, p.281]

## Muscular Anatomy (Dynamic Stabilizers)
- **Medial flexors** — resist valgus force
- **Lateral extensors** — resist varus force
- **Distal biceps tendon** — crosses anterior elbow, inserts at posterior/ulnar aspect of radial tuberosity [S10, p.282]
- **Brachialis** — two heads (superficial and deep) both inserting on the ulnar tuberosity [S10, p.282]
- **Triceps** — crosses elbow posteriorly, inserts on olecranon [S10, p.282]

## Neural Anatomy
- **Median nerve** — crosses anteriorly medial to brachial artery; covered by bicipital aponeurosis; passes between two heads of pronator teres [S10, p.282]
- **Radial nerve** — emerges between brachialis and brachioradialis; divides at the radiohumeral joint [S10, p.282]
- **Ulnar nerve** — crosses elbow in groove posterior to medial epicondyle; enters forearm between two heads of FCU [S10, p.282]
- **Brachial artery** — runs lateral to median nerve in cubital fossa; divides into radial and ulnar arteries [S10, p.282]

## Olecranon Bursa
- One of 150 bursae in the body
- Situated between the olecranon and skin
- Formed in response to pressure; acts as lubricating structure [S10, p.282]

## Clinical Evaluation Framework

### Physical Examination Sequence
1. **Inspection** — bilateral elbow alignment (normal carrying angle = 11–16° valgus), musculature atrophy/hypertrophy, skin changes, effusion in the soft spot (triangle of olecranon, radial head, lateral epicondyle) [S10, pp.282-283]
2. **Palpation** — epicondyles, olecranon, radial head, distal biceps tendon contour [S10, p.283]
3. **ROM assessment** — note crepitus, pain, mechanical symptoms; soft vs. firm endpoint [S10, p.283]
4. **Strength testing** — compare bilateral [S10, p.283]
5. **Special tests** — injury-specific (described under each pathology)

### Imaging
- **Radiographs** — AP and lateral; oblique views supplemental; used for osseous structure and joint alignment [S10, p.283]
- **MRI** — modality of choice for soft tissue evaluation; limited in acute injury but definitive for ligaments, tendons [S10, p.283]
- **Ultrasound** — tendon injuries, effusions, intra-articular loose bodies [S10, p.283]
- **CT** — defines osseous anatomy following radiographs [S10, p.283]
