# Shooting Off a Screen

## Summary
Shooting off a screen is one of the most difficult skills to develop in basketball because it requires communication between teammates, elusiveness by the cutter, and precise tempo to get open and receive the catch. When executed correctly, the screen-to-shot action is a powerful offensive weapon — it forces the defender to fight through a series of stationary players and either concede a catch-and-shoot opportunity or give up a catch with a defensive closeout at a poor angle.

For a screen to be effective, the cutter must read the defender and choose the correct cut (curl, fill, or fade), while the screener must communicate, establish a wide base, and react to what the cutter calls. [S3, pp.133-134]

## When to Use
- When the offense has a perimeter shooter who needs to get off a defender.
- Against man-to-man defense where the screener's matchup can be exploited.
- In half-court sets when the ball-handler needs to create a catch opportunity for a teammate.
- When the defender is playing tight — the curl is available; when the defender goes under — the fade/fill is available.

## Key Principles

### For the Cutter:
1. **Set the defender up** — make the defender think you are going one way before cutting another. "Set the defender up and make him think you are going where you are not."
2. **Watch the defender, not the ball.** The ball will find you when you get open.
3. **Take your time** — wait for the screen to be set before making your cut.
4. **Come off the screen shoulder-to-shoulder** with the screener — not wide or early.
5. **Read and call your cut** — verbalize "curl," "fill," or "fade" so the screener, passer, and teammates know your decision.
6. **Base your cut on how the defense played the screen:**
   - **Curl:** Defender tries to fight through the screen → curl tight to the basket.
   - **Fill (pop):** Defender goes under the screen → fill to the open spot for a shot.
   - **Fade:** Defender trails and forces you baseline → fade away from the screen for a shot.
7. **Always catch ready to shoot.** If there is no shot opportunity, establish a dominant pivot foot immediately and be ready to play. [S3, p.134]

### For the Screener:
1. **Verbalize the screen** — call out "Use me" to alert the cutter that a screen is coming.
2. **Get a wide, balanced base** and set a stationary, legal screen — don't lean or move into the defender.
3. **React to what the cutter calls** — if the cutter curls, the screener may roll to the rim; if the cutter fades, the screener may pop. Be ready to play after the screen is used. [S3, p.134]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/Ball-handler:** Reads the cut call from the screened player and delivers the pass in rhythm to the correct spot (curl = bounce pass low, fade = skip pass).
- **SG/SF (cutter):** Sets up the defender, times the cut, reads the defense, calls the cut, and catches ready to shoot.
- **PF/C (screener):** Communicates screen, establishes legal stationary position, holds, reacts to cutter's call.

## Variations
### Curl Cut
The cutter reads the defender going over the top of the screen and wraps tightly around the screener toward the basket for a layup or close-range shot.

### Fill (Pop)
The defender goes under the screen. The cutter pops or fills out to the open spot (wing or three-point line) for a catch-and-shoot.

### Fade
The defender trails and beats the cutter over the top. The cutter fades away from the screen (toward the baseline or corner) for a catch-and-shoot from distance, typically 15–20 feet.

### Dribble Fade
The cutter receives on the wing but the defender closes out at a high angle, leaving the baseline open. The cutter establishes a dominant pivot foot and attacks the baseline with a dribble fade — any scoring opportunity (pull-up, floater, finish) is available once the baseline is attacked. [S3, p.138]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Cutter reads the ball, not the defender** → reminder: "Watch the defender, not the ball — the ball will find you."
2. **Coming off the screen too early** → defender hasn't engaged the screen yet; wait for full contact.
3. **Not verbalizing the cut** → creates confusion for the screener and passer; always say "curl," "fill," or "fade."
4. **Screener moves before the cutter** → illegal screen; hold the position until the cutter has passed.
5. **Catching flat-footed** → always catch ready to shoot with the inside pivot foot planted; do not fumble into a shooting stance.
6. **No dominant pivot foot on the catch** → establishes a poor triple-threat position; always land with a defined pivot foot.
