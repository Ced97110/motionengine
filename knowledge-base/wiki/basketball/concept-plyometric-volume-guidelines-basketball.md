# Plyometric Volume Guidelines for Basketball

## Summary
Plyometric training volume for basketball must be calibrated by the athlete's level, training experience, and the specific performance goal. Basketball athletes respond to plyometric training better than athletes in other sports [S11, p.301]. However, more volume is not always better — a low-volume program (480 contacts) can be equally effective as a high-volume program (1,920 contacts) for improving reactive strength within a six-week block [S11, p.301]. The optimal approach combines multiple jump types (countermovement, drop, broad), appropriate rest periods, and level-specific volume targets.

## When to Use
- Off-season and preseason training blocks
- After establishing a minimum strength base (particularly for youth and untrained athletes)
- Combined with (or in addition to) basketball practice for youth athletes
- As a PAP (Post-Activation Potentiation) tool within strength sessions (e.g., drop jumps after heavy squats)

## Key Principles
1. **Basketball athletes are plyometric-responsive** — they adapt to plyometric training better than athletes in most other sports; prioritize it [S11, p.301]
2. **Mix jump types for COD gains** — combinations of countermovement jump (CMJ), drop jump, and broad jump have greater influence on change-of-direction ability than a single jump type [S11, p.301]
3. **Optimal volume for COD: 800-1,200 total jumps** — distributed over 2-3 sessions per week for 6-8 weeks, with mixed moderate-to-high intensities and 60-120 seconds rest between sets [S11, p.301]
4. **Low volume works for reactive strength** — 40 jumps performed twice per week for 6 weeks (480 total) can match a 4× higher volume program for improving reactive strength [S11, p.301]
5. **PAP drop jump protocol** — 1 set of 3 bounce drop jumps with 15 seconds rest after each jump improved 5-, 10-, and 20-meter sprint performance at various post-protocol windows (15 seconds to 12 minutes) [S11, p.301]
6. **Youth/untrained volume cap** — no more than 50-60 bodyweight jumps per session; these athletes will likely not take part in resisted plyometrics (AEL) [S11, p.301]
7. **AEL athletes need less volume** — for trained athletes using accentuated eccentric loading, reduce total volume compared to standard plyometric prescriptions [S11, p.301]
8. **Youth plyometrics improve multiple qualities** — 117-183 jumps performed alongside basketball practice improved agility, speed, lower-body strength, and power vs. practice-only controls [S11, p.301]

## Volume Guidelines by Goal

| Goal | Volume | Frequency | Duration | Rest |
|------|--------|-----------|----------|------|
| Change-of-direction | 800-1,200 jumps total | 2-3×/week | 6-8 weeks | 60-120 s/set |
| Reactive strength | 480 total (low vol) | 2×/week | 6 weeks | Adequate |
| Sprint potentiation (PAP) | 3 drop jumps × 1 set | Before sprints | Per session | 15 s between jumps |
| Youth/untrained | 50-60 BW jumps | Per session | Progressive | Adequate |
| Combined with practice (youth) | 117-183 jumps | Per session | 6-8 weeks | Adequate |

## Jump Type Selection
- **Countermovement Jump (CMJ)**: double-leg reactive jump; builds vertical power
- **Drop Jump**: depth jump with minimal ground contact; builds reactive/elastic strength
- **Broad Jump**: horizontal power; improves acceleration and COD
- **Combination**: mixing all three produces greater COD improvement than any single type

## Progressions
1. **Beginner (youth/untrained)**: 40-60 BW jumps/session; squat jumps, CMJ, standing broad jumps; no external load
2. **Intermediate**: 400-800 total jumps over 6-week block; add drop jumps at low box height (6-12 in); 2 sessions/week
3. **Advanced (trained, off-season)**: 800-1,200 total jumps; mixed intensities (CMJ + drop jump + broad jump); 2-3 sessions/week; AEL variations (barbell jump squat with 20-40% load)

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using only one jump type** → combine CMJ, drop jump, and broad jump for maximum COD adaptation [S11, p.301]
2. **Excessive volume for youth** → capped at 50-60 BW jumps/session; too much volume increases injury risk without additional benefit [S11, p.301]
3. **Ignoring rest between sets** → 60-120 seconds rest between sets is required to maintain quality and avoid fatigue accumulation that defeats the purpose of plyometric training [S11, p.301]
4. **Performing plyometrics without a strength base** → for high school and untrained athletes, establish basic movement competency before adding drop jumps or AEL [S11, pp.301-302]
