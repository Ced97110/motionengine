# Acute Spinal Trauma Management in Basketball

## Summary
Although basketball is relatively low-risk for spinal injuries compared to collision sports, a study of NBA players showed that lumbar spine injuries constitute 10.2% and cervical spine injuries 1.6% — a combined 11.8% of all injuries [S10, p.196]. Despite their lower frequency, spinal injuries can produce devastating consequences including paralysis. Optimal outcomes require pregame preparation, trained personnel, proper equipment, and a defined protocol.

The key principle: **when spinal injury is suspected, establish neutral cervical stabilization immediately and do not apply traction.** Transfer to a trauma board using the lift-and-slide technique (preferred) or prone log roll technique.

## When to Use
- Any mechanism suggesting possible cervical or lumbar spine injury: falls, collisions, heading contact, player landing awkwardly
- Player unable to move limbs, reports numbness/pain/weakness in extremities, or has significant neck/back pain after contact
- Player with altered consciousness after any head/neck trauma (assume cervical spine involvement)

## Key Principles

### Pregame Preparation (5 Essential Steps)
1. **Proper education** — all team healthcare providers trained in recognition and management of spinal trauma [S10, p.196]
2. **Create a spinal trauma protocol** — written, practiced protocol for the specific venue [S10, p.196]
3. **Communicate with emergency transfer officials** before the game [S10, p.196]
4. **Check presence of appropriate equipment** — cervical collar, trauma board — before every game [S10, p.196]
5. **Locate possible transfer centers** before the game [S10, p.196]

### On-Court Management Steps
1. **Primary survey** — standard ABCD trauma protocol [S10, p.196]
2. **Neutral cervical stabilization immediately** — cup the occiput, grasp mastoid process; maintain throughout [S10, p.196]
3. **No traction** — under no circumstance apply traction to the player's head [S10, p.196]
4. **Move to neutral position** — unless movement causes increased pain or loss of function [S10, p.196]
5. **Prone players**: use prone log roll technique to return to supine position [S10, p.197]
6. **Apply cervical collar carefully** [S10, p.197]
7. **Neurological screening** — conscious players: ask about extremity numbness, painful dysesthesias, weakness, neck and back pain [S10, p.197]
8. **Limited exam** — check ability to move all limbs, gross weakness, numbness, significant palpation pain [S10, p.197]
9. **Transfer to trauma board** — use lift-and-slide technique (preferred) or prone log roll; airway must be easily accessible [S10, p.197]
10. **Immediate transfer** to nearest medical facility [S10, p.197]

## Prone Log Roll Technique
- **Requires at least 4 trained people** [S10, p.197]
- Team leader maintains neutral spine using cross-arms technique while cupping head
- Rescuers synchronously pull player toward themselves
- Fifth rescuer slides the back board under player during midpoint of the roll [S10, p.197]

## Lift and Slide Technique (Preferred — Less Cervical Motion)
- **Requires at least 5 trained people** [S10, p.197]
- Team leader maintains neutral spine position
- Three rescuers lift the player synchronously, lifting 15 cm off ground
- Fifth rescuer slides the board under the player [S10, p.197]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Applying traction to head** → Never apply traction; maintain neutral position only
2. **Insufficient personnel for log roll** → Minimum 4 people required; attempt with fewer risks rotation of injured spine
3. **Using log roll when lift-and-slide is possible** → Lift-and-slide produces less cervical motion
4. **Skipping neurological screening** → Always assess extremity movement, sensation, and pain before transfer
5. **Missing cervical injury in unconscious player** → Head trauma and cervical spine trauma are frequently associated; always assume both
