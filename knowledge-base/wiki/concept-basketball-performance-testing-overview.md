# Basketball Performance Testing Overview

## Summary
A comprehensive basketball performance testing battery covers five physical quality categories: muscular strength (bench press), reactive strength (drop jump, repeated jump), power (vertical jump, running vertical jump, rotational medicine ball throw), and speed/agility (reactive lane shuttle, lane agility test, three-quarter court sprint). Together these tests allow strength and conditioning professionals to profile athletes, track development, compare athletes to normative standards, and aid in talent identification.

Normative data exist at three levels: high school (NBCCA norms), NCAA Division I/II/III (published research), and the NBA Combine (2000–2020 seasons). Where normative data are unavailable, pre-/post-testing comparisons remain valid tools for measuring individual improvement [S11].

## When to Use
- Pre-season baseline profiling of all players
- Post-season or off-season reassessment to measure training gains
- Talent identification and draft evaluation (NBA Combine context)
- Injury return-to-play clearance (comparing to individual baseline)
- In-season spot-checks when fatigue monitoring is needed

## Key Principles
1. **Calibrate all instruments before every testing session** — timing mats, photocell mats, and force plates must be zeroed and synchronized
2. **Allow adequate rest between trials** — minimum 2 minutes between attempts on all jump and sprint tests to ensure maximum-effort results
3. **Standardize warm-up and testing order** — test order should move from less to more fatiguing (strength → power → speed/agility)
4. **Record the best trial, not the average** — most basketball performance tests (sprint, jump, agility) report the single best score
5. **Use both preplanned and reactive agility tests** — the lane agility test and reactive lane shuttle test capture different qualities and both are needed
6. **Test bilaterally where applicable** — rotational throws and reactive agility tests should be performed in both directions to identify asymmetries

## Test Categories

### Muscular Strength
- **Bench Press Test** — maximum repetitions at 185 lb (men) or bodyweight (women); normative data from NBCCA and NBA Combine

### Reactive Strength
- **Drop Jump Test** — reactive strength index (RSI = airtime ÷ floor contact time); 30 cm box baseline height; no normative data available
- **Repeated Jump Test** — 10 consecutive CMJs; average RSI from 5 lowest-contact-time jumps; identifies ankle-dominant reactive ability; no normative data available

### Power
- **Vertical Jump Test** — standing CMJ; Vertec or contact mat; normative data for high school, NCAA D1/D2, and NBA
- **Running Vertical Jump Test** — running approach within 15-ft arc; maximum vertical leap; normative data for high school, NCAA D1, and NBA
- **Rotational Medicine Ball Throw** — 3–4 kg medicine ball; bilateral rotational distance; no normative data available

### Speed and Agility
- **Reactive Lane Shuttle (RLS)** — NBA combine test; reactive COD from external stimulus; NBA normative data available
- **Lane Agility Test** — preplanned multidirectional COD; sprint + shuffle + backpedal around key; normative data for high school, NCAA, and NBA
- **Three-Quarter Court Sprint** — 75-foot linear sprint; acceleration and speed; normative data for high school, NCAA, and NBA

## Common Mistakes
1. Testing fatigued athletes → always test in a fresh state, early in the session
2. Using contact times above 0.25 seconds on drop jump → discard those trials; reactive strength is not being expressed
3. Allowing steps before the CMJ in the vertical jump test → enforce the no-step rule; use countermovement only
4. Not standardizing the running vertical jump approach → mark a 15-ft arc boundary to ensure reproducibility
5. Single-direction testing on rotational and reactive tests → always collect both left-to-right and right-to-left trials
