# Basketball Shoulder Assessment

## Summary
Shoulder injuries in professional basketball are not the most common injuries, but shoulder function is essential for shooting and dribbling, making any shoulder injury potentially performance-limiting. Because medical staff see shoulder injuries infrequently, misdiagnosis or underestimation is a real risk. A systematic clinical assessment approach — including detailed history, kinetic chain examination, scapular evaluation, and targeted special tests — is required to accurately diagnose and manage shoulder pathology in basketball athletes [S10, pp.630-644].

No single clinical test can reliably diagnose a shoulder injury in isolation. Clusters of tests, combined with imaging (MRI, musculoskeletal ultrasound), provide the best diagnostic accuracy. Key to assessment is understanding that alterations in the kinetic chain — poor posture, scapular dyskinesis — frequently contribute to shoulder problems and must be included in every clinical examination [S10, p.643].

## When to Use
- Athlete presents with shoulder pain, weakness, or loss of motion
- Pre-season physical examination screening
- Return-to-play clearance after shoulder injury
- Monitoring athletes with history of shoulder instability or overuse
- Any time shooting or dribbling performance is compromised by shoulder symptoms

## Key Principles
1. **Take a detailed history first**: site of pain, onset nature, mechanism, shoulder position at time of injury (if acute), aggravating movements/positions (if chronic), severity, and impact on performance [S10, p.643]
2. **No single test is pathognomonic**: use clusters of tests; supplement with MRI and/or musculoskeletal ultrasound [S10, p.632]
3. **Assess the kinetic chain**: poor posture or scapular dyskinesis often contribute to shoulder pathology [S10, p.643]
4. **Start with observation**: posture, scapular position, and movement quality before provoking tests
5. **Use symptom alteration tests** to link observable scapular dyskinesis to the shoulder complaint before attributing causation [S10, p.630]

## Assessment Framework

### Step 1: History
- Precise site of pain
- Nature of onset (acute vs. gradual/overuse)
- Mechanism and shoulder position at injury (if acute)
- Aggravating movements/positions (if chronic)
- Pain severity and impact on performance

### Step 2: Posture and Kinetic Chain
- Assess cervical spine (forward head position), thoracic spine (kyphosis), lumbar spine (lordosis)
- Pectoralis minor length: distance from coracoid process to sternocostal junction of 4th rib (caliper/tape); or supine posterolateral scapula-to-table distance >2.54 cm suggests tightness [S10, p.630]

### Step 3: Scapular Assessment

#### Scapular Dyskinesis Observation
- Player performs 3–5 arm elevations in scapular and sagittal planes
- Record "yes" for any abnormal inferior angle, medial border, or superior margin prominences
- Multi-plane asymmetries most visible with forward flexion motions
- **Key limitation**: dyskinesis found in both symptomatic and asymptomatic individuals — use in conjunction with symptom alteration tests [S10, p.630]

#### Scapular Symptom Alteration Tests
**Scapular Assistance Test (SAT)**: Clinician manually assists upward rotation and posterior tilting of scapula during shoulder elevation. Significant pain decrease = dyskinesis is a contributing factor.

**Scapular Reposition Test (SRT)** (Tate et al.): Clinician corrects scapula posterior tilt and external rotation using fingers on AC joint + thenar eminence on spine of scapula + forearm toward inferior angle. Repeat positive impingement tests (Neer, Hawkins, Jobe). Pain decrease = scapular malposition contributing. Identifies players who would benefit from taping, bracing, or strengthening interventions [S10, p.630].

#### Scapular Muscle Strength (Hand-Held Dynamometer — HHD)
HHD is valid, portable, and cost-effective vs. isokinetic dynamometry gold standard [S10, pp.631-632]:

**Serratus Anterior (Protraction)**:
- Player seated, back/head against wall, feet flat, knees 90°
- Shoulder flexed 90°, elbow neutral and fully extended
- Clinician places HHD between trunk and player's hand, resists protraction with trunk
- Player finds midpoint between retraction/protraction (maximizes isometric force)
- "Make" test for peak force
- *Note: poor inter-rater reliability* [S10, p.631]

**Lower Trapezius**:
- Player prone, arm in 140° elevation
- Clinician applies superior/lateral force parallel to long axis of humerus, HHD midway between root of spine of scapula and acromion
- Player attempts to depress and adduct scapula
- Test position: mid-position of scapula depression/elevation [S10, p.631]

**Middle Trapezius**:
- HHD testing with high inter-rater reliability [S10, p.631]

## Common Basketball Shoulder Conditions

### Shoulder Instability
- One of the most common **severe** shoulder complaints in basketball
- Can be anterior, posterior, inferior, or multidirectional
- NBA data (1999–2018): 50 players with instability events; 14 subluxations, 36 dislocations; 25 treated operatively (88% were dislocations); 16% recurrence [S10, p.632]
- NBA orthopedic procedures 1984–2012: shoulder stabilization = 13.2% of all procedures [S10, p.632]
- WNBA 2000–2008: instability incidence 1.25 per season [S10, p.632]
- **Tests**: Apprehension, Relocation (Surprise), Load-and-Shift (anterior/posterior), Gagey (Hyperabduction) — see [[concept-basketball-shoulder-instability-tests]]

### Rotator Cuff Tears (RCT)
- Multifactorial etiology: microtrauma, altered mechanics, fatigue
- Symptoms: sudden pain onset over shoulder radiating to deltoid, pain with overhead activities and at rest, ROM and strength deficits
- No single clinical test reliably separates partial from full-thickness tears [S10, p.634]
- **MSK Ultrasound**: useful for full-thickness RCT; less reliable for partial tears → use MRI if conservative management fails
- **Supraspinatus**: Jobe's (empty can) test, Drop Arm test
- **Subscapularis**: Lift-Off (Gerber's), Internal Rotation Lag Sign, Belly-Press, Bear-Hug tests
- **Infraspinatus**: Drop Sign + Resisted External Rotation Test (RERT)
- **Teres Minor**: External Rotation Lag Sign (lag >10° = infraspinatus weakness; lag >40° = infraspinatus AND teres minor), Drop Sign, Patte Maneuver — *imaging unreliable for teres minor; clinical exam is best* [S10, p.636]

### Acromioclavicular (AC) Joint Sprain
- Acute: direct fall onto shoulder; pain localized over AC joint; step deformity in Type I+
- Chronic: multiple minor injuries or post Type II/III; associated with "impingement"
- **Tests**: Cross-Arm Adduction (Scarf), Active Compression (O'Brien), Resisted Extension [S10, p.636]
- Scapular dyskinesis associated with AC sprains — include SAT/SRT in evaluation

### Subacromial Pain Syndrome (SPS)
- Preferred term over "Subacromial Impingement Syndrome" (Cools & Michener)
- Umbrella term covering: bursitis, RCT, laxity/instability, labral tears, scapular dyskinesis, biceps pathology, muscle imbalances, GIRD, overuse
- **Etiology**: gradual onset related to overuse, technique changes, or postural changes from distant kinetic chain injuries
- **Symptoms**: lifting provokes shoulder/upper arm ache or sharp pain; painful arc; stiffness/weakness with arm use; difficulty lying on affected side [S10, p.639]
- **Diagnostic cluster** (95% likelihood for SPS): Hawkins-Kennedy + Painful Arc Sign + Infraspinatus Muscle Test [S10, p.641]
- **CKCUES Test**: reliable functional performance test for SPS [S10, p.641]

## Clinical Test Reference Tables

### Shoulder Instability Tests (Table 49.1)
| Test | Key Procedure | Positive Finding |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| Apprehension | Supine, 90° abd, 90° elbow flex; move to maximal ER | Subjective/objective apprehension and/or pain |
| Surprise | After apprehension test position, relocate then suddenly remove hand | Apprehension and/or pain returns |
| Load-and-Shift (Anterior) | Arm in 90° abd scapular plane; shift humerus anteriorly | 4-level laxity scale (0–3) |
| Load-and-Shift (Posterior) | Arm in 20° abd scapular plane; shift humerus posteriorly | >1 cm movement |
| Gagey (Hyperabduction) | Seated, 90° abd; stabilize acromion, passively abduct further | Abduction >105° |

### Supraspinatus Tests (Table 49.2)
| Test | Key Procedure | Positive Finding |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| Jobe's (empty can) | Arm in scapular plane, thumb down; examiner pushes arm down | Pain and weakness vs. opposite shoulder |
| Drop Arm | Passively abduct to 90°; patient slowly lowers arm | Unable to lower arm in controlled manner |

### Subscapularis Tests (Table 49.3)
| Test | Key Procedure | Positive Finding |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| Lift-Off (Gerber's) | Back of hand on mid-lumbar; lift hand off back | Unable to lift hand off back |
| Internal Rotation Lag Sign | Same position; examiner lifts hand off lumbar; patient holds | Unable to maintain position off lumbar spine |
| Belly-Press | Palm on abdomen, elbow anterior to mid-axillary line; press stomach | Unable to maintain elbow position |
| Bear-Hug | Palm on opposite shoulder, elbow parallel to floor; resist ER | Weakness/pain vs. opposite shoulder |

### Infraspinatus Tests (Table 49.4)
| Test | Key Procedure | Positive Finding |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| Drop Sign | Scapular plane, 90° elevation, 90° elbow; release wrist | Arm "drops/lags" — measure degrees |
| RERT | Arm at side, elbow 90°; medial force on distal forearm | Weakness vs. asymptomatic shoulder |

### Teres Minor Tests (Table 49.5)
| Test | Key Procedure | Positive Finding |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| External Rotation Lag Sign | Elbow 90°, shoulder 20° elevation; passively ER to 5° short of max | Lag >10° = infraspinatus; lag >40° = infraspinatus + teres minor |
| Drop Sign | 90° abduction, 90° ER; release wrist | Unable to resist gravity (IR occurs) |
| Patte Maneuver | 90° abduction, neutral rotation; resist ER | ER <grade 4 strength |

### AC Joint Tests (Table 49.6)
| Test | Key Procedure | Positive Finding |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| Cross-Arm Adduction (Scarf) | Horizontal adduction; examiner pushes further | Localized pain on top of shoulder |
| Active Compression (O'Brien) | 90° flex, 15° horiz. add, full IR, pronate; push up vs. neutral | Pain in IR position absent in neutral = AC; pain deep = labral |
| Resisted Extension | 90° flex, IR; resist horizontal abduction | Localized pain on top of shoulder |

### SPS Tests (Table 49.7)
| Test | Key Procedure | Positive Finding |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| Hawkins-Kennedy | Elbow 90°, shoulder 90° flex; gentle IR | Pain at end-range IR |
| Painful Arc | Lift arm in scapular plane to full elevation then lower | Pain between 60–120° |
| CKCUES Test | Push-up position, 36" apart; alternate touches to opposite hand for 15 s × 3 | Normalized by height; meaningful difference = 3+ touches |

## Common Mistakes
1. **Relying on a single test** → always use clusters of tests; confirm with imaging when uncertain [S10, p.632]
2. **Ignoring the kinetic chain** → always assess posture and scapular mechanics, not just the shoulder joint itself [S10, p.643]
3. **Over-relying on MRI for teres minor and infraspinatus** → clinical exam is more sensitive for these muscles than MRI [S10, p.634]
4. **Skipping the history** → mechanism and onset type guide the entire test selection process
5. **Using dyskinesis finding in isolation** → scapular dyskinesis appears in asymptomatic athletes; only meaningful if symptom alteration tests are positive [S10, p.630]
