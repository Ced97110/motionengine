# Ankle Sprain Rehabilitation for Basketball

## Summary
Ankle sprains are the most common basketball-related injury to the foot and ankle, occurring in the general public at a rate of 30,000 per day. The most frequently injured ligaments are the anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL), calcaneofibular ligament (CFL), and posterior talofibular ligament (PTFL). The typical mechanism is a combined motion of **ankle plantar flexion and inversion** — most often occurring when a player lands on an opponent's foot during a jump, cut, or rebound. [S2, pp.127-130]

**All injuries vary in severity and may involve associated anatomical injury or preexisting pathology. A qualified medical practitioner should be consulted before initiating a rehabilitation program.**

## Ankle Sprain Grades

### Grade 1 — Mild
- **Pathology**: Mild stretch to ligamentous structures; no functional ankle instability; single-ligament involvement
- **Physical Exam**: No or minimal swelling or ecchymosis; tenderness around the ligament; negative clinical special tests
- **Functional Limitations**: No or minimal limitations; mild gait deviations
- **Rehabilitation**: Aggressive — approximately **1 week**

### Grade 2 — Moderate
- **Pathology**: Partial tearing of ligaments; mild to moderate ankle instability; single or multiple ligaments involved
- **Physical Exam**: Ankle swelling and ecchymosis; tenderness around the ligament; positive clinical special tests for ligamentous laxity
- **Functional Limitations**: Occasional ROM deficits; strength deficits; inability to raise heel unilaterally; gait deviations; inability to jump or run
- **Rehabilitation**: Conservative — approximately **3–4 weeks**

### Grade 3 — Severe
- **Pathology**: Complete tear of ligament; gross ankle instability; single or multiple ligaments involved
- **Physical Exam**: Moderate swelling and ecchymosis; tenderness around ligament and malleoli; positive clinical special tests; guarding and pain
- **Functional Limitations**: ROM and ankle strength deficits; gait deviations; inability to bear weight; requires assistive device
- **Rehabilitation**: Very conservative — approximately **5–12 weeks**

## Acute Treatment: PRICE Protocol
The **PRICE** acronym governs acute management of ankle sprains immediately following injury: [S2, pp.128-129]

- **P — Protect**: Use ankle bracing or assistive devices to alleviate stress and pain
- **R — Rest**: Prevent undue tissue damage
- **I — Ice**: Decrease pain and blood flow, reducing bruising
- **C — Compression**: Increase external pressure to decrease swelling
- **E — Elevation**: Decrease swelling and assist venous and lymphatic return

## Rehabilitation Progression

### Phase 1: Acute Management
- Reduce the inflammatory process, manage pain, promote repair, and remodel collagen fibers
- Controlled stresses via exercise promote healing; **excessive loading prolongs inflammation**
- As pain and swelling subside, progress to more aggressive activities

### Phase 2: ROM Restoration
- Primary focus: restore **ankle range of motion**
- Dorsiflexion (DF) and plantar flexion (PF) can be treated aggressively
- Inversion (IV) and eversion (EV) should be treated **as tolerated**
- Methods: stretching the gastrocnemius/soleus complex; soft tissue massage to gastrocnemius, soleus, peroneals, tibialis posterior, flexor digitorum longus, flexor hallucis longus

### Phase 3: Strengthening
- After ROM is normalized, progress ankle strengthening
- Begin with **open-chain exercises** → advance to **closed-chain exercises** as weight-bearing is tolerated
- Key exercises: Resistive ankle motion, Standing heel raise, Band lateral walk

### Phase 4: Balance and Proprioception
- **Critical for preventing future ankle sprains** and the foundation for advanced performance
- Key exercises: Single-leg stance, Rocker board, Seated physioball perturbation, Hopping, Contralateral kick
- Progress from stable surface → unstable surface (foam pad, Dynadisc, BOSU) → eyes closed

### Phase 5: Return to Play
- After balance and proprioception are mastered, advanced rehabilitation and conditioning are integrated
- Sequence: Dynamic warm-up → agility → jogging → sagittal-plane running → jumping → cutting and change of direction → deceleration training

## Recurrent Sprains
Recurrent ankle sprains are usually caused by insufficient balance/proprioception, strength deficits, or poor movement quality. Players with chronic ankle dysfunction may require:
- Long-term use of external supports (athletic taping or bracing)
- Progressive balance/proprioception and strength training ongoing [S2, p.129]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Returning to play too early (Grade 2/3)** → Insufficient healing time leads to re-injury and chronic instability
2. **Skipping proprioception training** → Balance and proprioception deficits are the primary cause of recurrent sprains
3. **Aggressive inversion/eversion work too soon** → These planes of motion are vulnerable in early healing; dorsiflexion and plantar flexion should be prioritized first
4. **Excessive loading during inflammation** → Interferes with healing and prolongs recovery timeline
