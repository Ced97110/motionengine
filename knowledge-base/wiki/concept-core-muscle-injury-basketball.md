# Sports Hernia / Athletic Pubalgia / Core Muscle Injury in Basketball

## Summary
Core Muscle Injury (CMI) — also known as sports hernia, athletic pubalgia, or Gilmore's groin — involves dysfunction of the lower abdominal musculature and inguinal region. The pathology is thought to involve injury to the abdominal wall at the fascial attachments of the rectus abdominis and adductors onto the pubic symphysis, or an actual tear in the transversalis fascia forming the posterior inguinal wall. While less common than adductor and hip flexor injuries in basketball, CMI can cause significant disability and prolonged absence from play. The constant abrupt direction changes in basketball — requiring abdominal hyperextension and hip abduction — make players susceptible to this condition [S10, pp.317-318].

CMI is notorious for being difficult to resolve conservatively. In elite football, nearly 50% of players with inguinal-related groin injury miss more than 4 weeks, with injury time almost double that of adductor injuries. Its true prevalence in professional basketball remains incompletely described [S10, p.317].

## When to Use (Clinical Recognition)
- Basketball player presenting with proximal adductor pain AND inguinal canal pain near the rectus abdominis insertion on the pubis
- Symptoms worsen with activity and resolve with rest
- Pain with coughing or radiation into the groin or testicular region (nerve entrapment)
- History of abrupt direction changes, cutting, or pivoting overload
- Symptoms commonly unilateral; bilateral in up to 43% of athletes [S10, p.317]

## Key Principles
1. **Mechanism**: abdominal hyperextension + hip abduction during rapid direction changes — common in basketball pivoting and cutting actions [S10, p.317]
2. **Pathology spectrum**: ranges from fascial attachment injury (rectus/adductors at pubic symphysis) to a transversalis fascia tear with incipient posterior hernia [S10, p.317]
3. **Physical examination**: tenderness at pubic tubercle and symphysis; resisted hip adduction and resisted sit-up with palpation at the inferolateral distal rectus abdominis recreate symptoms [S10, p.317]
4. **First-line imaging**: AP pelvis and lateral femur X-rays (may show osteitis pubis); MRI best for detecting rectus abdominis and adductor aponeurosis pathology; dynamic ultrasound can detect abdominal wall weakness during Valsalva [S10, p.317]
5. **Conservative management first**: physical therapy focusing on core stabilization, postural retraining, and hip/pelvis muscle balance re-stabilization; gradual return-to-sport (RTS) after rest and activity modification [S10, p.317]
6. **Surgical management**: indicated when conservative treatment fails; options include open or laparoscopic mesh reinforcement plus excision/ablation of surrounding nerves; laparoscopic approach allows bilateral treatment through same incisions [S10, pp.317-318]
7. **RTS outcomes**: 86% of professional hockey players returned to professional play after external oblique aponeurosis repair; basketball-specific surgical outcomes not yet fully described [S10, p.318]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: recognize that groin/inguinal pain worsening with cutting and direction changes may not be a simple adductor strain — early evaluation is key
- **Coaches and medical staff**: do not rush RTS; CMI takes a long time to recover and may not resolve with conservative treatment alone — surgical consultation should be sought proactively if conservative care fails after adequate trial

## Variations
### Osteitis Pubis
Inflammation and sclerosis of the pubic symphysis often co-presenting with CMI. Identifiable on X-ray and MRI (pubic bone marrow edema). May require targeted injections to the symphysis [S10, p.325].

### Bilateral CMI
Up to 43% of athletes present with bilateral symptoms. Laparoscopic technique allows simultaneous prophylactic treatment of the asymptomatic side [S10, p.317].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating as adductor strain only** → Palpate the inguinal canal and rectus insertion; perform resisted sit-up test to differentiate CMI from pure adductor pathology
2. **Premature return to play** → CMI is deceptive — symptoms resolve with rest but recur with sport-specific loads; ensure full core stability before clearance
3. **Skipping advanced imaging** → MRI is essential for detecting fascial tears and ruling out concomitant intra-articular hip pathology that can mimic or coexist with CMI
4. **Ignoring the contralateral side** → Nearly half of cases are bilateral; assess and treat both sides
