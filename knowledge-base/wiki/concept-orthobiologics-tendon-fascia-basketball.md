# Orthobiologics for Basketball Tendon and Fascia Injuries

## Summary
Orthobiologics — platelet-rich plasma (PRP), bone marrow aspirate concentrate (BMAC), and amniotic-derived products — are increasingly used in sports medicine to enhance healing of soft tissue injuries common in basketball players. These agents leverage growth factors and cellular components to modulate the biological healing environment. The evidence base varies considerably across injury types, ranging from sufficient evidence to recommend PRP for patellar tendinopathy and plantar fasciitis, to conflicting or insufficient evidence for Achilles tendinopathy and rotator cuff repair augmentation [S10, Ch.45].

Basketball's ballistic demands (jumping, cutting, sprinting, rapid deceleration) place repetitive high loads on the patellar tendon, Achilles tendon, plantar fascia, and rotator cuff, making these structures among the most injury-prone in the sport. When conservative management fails, orthobiologic injections offer an intermediate treatment option before surgery [S10, Ch.45].

## When to Use
- After failure of first-line conservative treatment (rest, physical therapy, NSAIDs, ESWT) for patellar tendinopathy, plantar fasciitis, Achilles tendinopathy, or rotator cuff tendinopathy
- As biological augmentation during surgical repair of Achilles tendon or rotator cuff (evidence mixed)
- When early return to play is a priority and conservative care alone is insufficient
- PRP is NOT recommended as a first-line treatment for any of these conditions

## Key Principles

### Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) — General
1. PRP is a blood-derived product with supraphysiological concentrations of platelets and growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β, VEGF, IGF-1) that modulate tissue healing [S10, Ch.45]
2. Preparation methods vary widely — volume, centrifugation speed, leukocyte content (LR-PRP vs LP-PRP) all affect composition and likely outcomes [S10, Ch.45]
3. WADA does not prohibit PRP use in most contexts [S10, Ch.45]
4. Standardization of preparation protocols is a critical unmet need in the field [S10, Ch.45]

### Bone Marrow Aspirate Concentrate (BMAC)
1. BMAC contains mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs), growth factors, and anti-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1Ra)
2. MSCs have multilineage differentiation potential and paracrine signaling effects
3. Iliac crest, tibia, and calcaneus are harvest sites with varying MSC yield [S10, Ch.45]
4. Smaller syringe and lower volume aspiration improve MSC concentration [S10, Ch.45]

### Amniotic-Derived Products
1. mDHACM (micronized dehydrated human amniotic/chorionic membrane) contains growth factors, cytokines, and extracellular matrix components
2. Administered via injection; promising early results for plantar fasciitis
3. Not yet sufficient evidence for formal clinical recommendations [S10, Ch.45]

## Injury-Specific Evidence Summary

### Patellar Tendinopathy (Jumper's Knee)
- Prevalence among elite basketball players is high given the repeated jumping demands
- **PRP**: Sufficient evidence to recommend for cases refractory to conservative care; considered a safe option. Two injections of LR-PRP showed significantly better VISA-P, VAS, and modified Blazina scores vs. ESWT at 12 months (Vetrano et al., 2013) [S10, p.568]
- LR-PRP superior to dry needling control in one RCT (Dragoo et al., 2014) at 12 weeks; no difference at 26 weeks [S10, p.568]
- A 2019 level-I RCT (Scott et al.) showed no significant difference between LR-PRP, LP-PRP, and saline — raises doubt about PRP benefit [S10, p.568]
- **Skin-derived tenocyte-like cells**: One RCT showed faster improvement in VISA-P; one late patellar tendon rupture reported (Clarke et al., 2011) [S10, p.568]
- **BMAC**: Small case series (Pascual-Garrido) showed positive 5-year results
- **Recommendation**: PRP is the most evidence-supported option for refractory patellar tendinopathy, but recent RCT data introduce uncertainty [S10, Ch.45]

### Achilles Tendinopathy
- **PRP**: Current best evidence does NOT support PRP for chronic Achilles tendinopathy [S10, p.570]
- Multiple level-I RCTs (DeJonge 2011, Kearney 2013, Krogh 2016) showed no significant difference in VISA-A vs. saline or eccentric exercise
- Boensen et al. (2017): High-volume injection (HVI) and PRP both more effective than placebo for pain and VISA-A; HVI may be better than PRP short-term [S10, p.569]
- Usuelli et al. (2017): Adipose-derived SVF showed better early improvement (15-30 days) vs. LR-PRP; no difference at later follow-up [S10, p.569]
- **Recommendation**: Evidence does not support routine PRP use for chronic Achilles tendinopathy [S10, p.572]

### Achilles Tendon Rupture
- Only 61.1–68% of NBA players return to professional play; most suffer performance decline [S10, p.570]
- **PRP augmentation of repair**: Evidence is conflicting
  - Schepull et al.: PRP had a *detrimental* effect (lower Achilles Tendon Total Rupture Score, p=0.014) [S10, p.570]
  - De Carli et al.: No difference in structural/functional outcomes with PRP [S10, p.570]
  - Zou et al.: LR-PRP augmentation improved ankle ROM, Leppilahti score, SF-36 at 2-year follow-up (n=36) [S10, p.570]
- **BMAC augmentation**: Stein et al. retrospective series — 92% return to sport at 5.9 months, mean Achilles Tendon Rupture Score = 91 [S10, p.570]
- **Recommendation**: Current best evidence does NOT support PRP or cell-based therapies for augmentation of Achilles tendon repair [S10, p.570]

### Plantar Fasciitis
- Plantar fascia is highly vulnerable in basketball due to ballistic demands; increased plantar peak pressure during 3-point shots and layups [S10, p.570]
- First-line: rest, plantar fascia-specific stretching, NSAIDs, foot orthosis, shock wave therapy
- **Corticosteroids**: Short-term pain relief up to 3 months; ultrasound guidance improves outcomes and reduces recurrence; HIGH rate of plantar fascia rupture (53.7%) and fat pad atrophy — significant concern [S10, p.571]
- **BTX-A**: More rapid and sustained functional improvement vs. dexamethasone injection in one RCT (Elizondo-Rodriguez et al.) [S10, p.571]
- **PRP**: Most level-I/II studies report favorable outcomes of PRP over corticosteroids
  - Meta-analysis (Singh et al., 10 studies): PRP improved pain and function at 3 months (p<0.05); no difference at 1, 6, 12 months [S10, p.571]
  - Meta-analysis (Yang et al., 9 RCTs): Greater long-term (24-week) pain relief with PRP (p=0.03); no short/intermediate-term differences [S10, p.571]
  - PRP vs. PPP: No significant difference (Malahias et al.) [S10, p.571]
- **mDHACM (amniotic products)**:
  - Cazzell et al. (2018): 76% VAS decrease in mDHACM vs. 45% in saline at 3 months (p<0.0001) [S10, p.571]
  - Zelen et al. (2013): Significantly greater AOFAS scores at 8 weeks for both mDHACM doses vs. saline [S10, p.572]
  - Hanselman et al. (2014): Two injections of c-hAM superior to one injection at 18 weeks [S10, p.572]
- **Recommendation**: High-quality evidence supports PRP for plantar fasciitis — slightly greater long-term efficacy vs. corticosteroids with fewer complications [S10, p.579]

### Rotator Cuff Tendinopathy
- **PRP injection**: Evidence is conflicting
  - Rha et al. (2013): LR-PRP superior to dry needling for SPADI and pain at 6 weeks to 6 months [S10, p.576]
  - Kesikburun et al. (2013): No difference vs. saline at 1-year follow-up [S10, p.576]
  - Systematic review: PRP may not be beneficial short-term for rotator cuff disease [S10, p.572]
- **PRP augmentation of rotator cuff repair**: Multiple meta-analyses (Saltzman et al., Filardo et al.) showed NO improvement in clinical outcomes or retear rates with PRP augmentation [S10, p.572]
- **BMAC augmentation**: Hernigou et al. — 100% BMAC group healed at 6 months vs. 67% control on MRI; fewer re-ruptures at 10 years [S10, p.572]
- Kim et al.: BMAC-PRP injection superior to exercise alone at 3 months for VAS and ASES scores [S10, p.572]
- **Recommendation**: Current literature is conflicting; PRP not routinely recommended for rotator cuff pathology [S10, p.579]

## Common Mistakes
1. Using PRP without standardized preparation → variable growth factor composition makes outcomes unpredictable; standardization is critical [S10, Ch.45]
2. Applying Achilles tendinopathy PRP evidence to Achilles rupture repair (or vice versa) → these are distinct pathologies with different evidence bases
3. Assuming PRP is uniformly beneficial — evidence is injury-specific and must be interpreted independently for each condition
4. Using corticosteroids as a primary treatment for plantar fasciitis without counseling patients on the 53.7% rupture risk with repeated injections [S10, p.571]
5. Ignoring PRP leukocyte composition — LR-PRP vs. LP-PRP may produce different outcomes for different tissues [S10, Ch.45]

## Take-Home Messages
- **Patellar tendinopathy**: Sufficient evidence for PRP (but recent doubt from 2019 RCT) [S10, p.572]
- **Achilles tendinopathy**: Evidence does NOT support PRP [S10, p.572]
- **Achilles tendon rupture augmentation**: Evidence does NOT support PRP or cell-based therapies [S10, p.572]
- **Plantar fasciitis**: PRP recommended — better long-term efficacy than corticosteroids, fewer risks [S10, p.579]
- **Rotator cuff**: Conflicting evidence; not yet routinely recommended [S10, p.579]
- **Cell-based / amniotic products**: Early promising results, insufficient for formal recommendations [S10, p.579]
- Orthobiologics in basketball players specifically remain understudied — most evidence comes from non-athlete populations [S10, p.579]
