# Front Flip Progressive Chain (10 Steps)

## Objective
Develop whole-body explosive power, rotational tolerance, landing mechanics, and kinesthetic coordination through a ten-step progressive calisthenics chain that culminates in the standing front flip.

## Setup
- **Players**: 1 per session (individual skill development)
- **Space**: Open floor — gymnasium, outdoor hardcourt, grass, or soft surface
- **Equipment**: Thick padding/mattress recommended for Steps 5–8 (back-drop drills); soft surface (grass, carpet) helpful for early rolls
- **Safety**: Never skip steps. Always use maximum padding when attempting back-drop or flip stages.

## Execution

### Step 1: Shoulder Roll
1. Squat toward the ground with strongest foot slightly forward.
2. Place the edge of the palm heel on the ground (strongest side). Free hand optional on floor for balance.
3. Lower slowly forward until you overbalance. Brace the arm to absorb most of the weight.
4. Let the braced arm/shoulder guide the roll — head tucked, protected, no weight on head.
5. Roll over the back without veering sideways.
6. Finish in a seated position. (Progression: roll all the way to standing.)
- **X-Ray**: Easiest roll on joints — upper-back absorbs force, wrists/elbows/shoulders unloaded. Equivalent to martial arts *ukemi* (breakfall). [S9, p.6]

### Step 2: Press Roll
1. Feet symmetrical, squat toward the ground.
2. Place both palms on the floor as you dip down.
3. Lower slowly forward, squatting and bringing torso down. Straighten legs until you overbalance.
4. Braced arms guide the roll — head tucked, no weight on head.
5. Roll over the back without veering.
6. Finish in a seated position.
- **X-Ray**: Hands begin functioning as levers to control body; progressively loads upper limbs as a precursor to handspring arm strength. [S9, p.8]

### Step 3: Jump Roll
1. Feet symmetrical, bend forward aiming palms toward the floor.
2. Jump up as you descend, letting the torso fall as hands drop to the floor.
3. Land on palms (arms fairly straight) just as feet leave the floor — "catching yourself with palms."
4. Braced arms bend to guide the roll — head tucked, no weight on head.
5. Roll over the back without veering.
6. Finish by rolling all the way to standing position.
- **X-Ray**: Introduces upward power (jump up more than forward — this is different from the gymnast's dive roll which goes forward). Trains brain to tolerate the turning motion. [S9, p.10]

### Step 4: Handstand Roll
1. Split stance, strongest foot forward. Place palms on floor and raise the back leg behind you.
2. Push up through strongest foot, swinging other foot back and up. Torso becomes more vertical.
3. Let leg momentum carry legs above the torso; straighten until the body aligns in a handstand.
4. Do NOT hold the handstand — let arms bend and trunk curl forward immediately as you begin to fall.
5. Braced arms guide the roll — head tucked, no weight on head.
6. Roll all the way to standing.
- **X-Ray**: Final true roll. Requires explosive leg power to kick up AND begins developing solid arm strength for front handsprings. [S9, p.12]

### Step 5: Back-Drop Handspring
1. Take one step to build momentum. Hands up overhead. Kick off with strongest leg.
2. Let the torso fall as hands drop to the floor. As hands approach the floor, jump hard, swinging highest leg.
3. Arms fairly straight as feet leave the floor — palms catch the body.
4. Momentum carries legs above the torso.
5. Allow forward momentum to turn you over. Tuck head, curl trunk, whip hands up off the floor.
6. Feet hit ground first (shock absorbers), then glutes/arms/back contact.
- **X-Ray**: Very close to the front handspring. Teaches first portion of the spin out of the handstand. Requires minimal upper-body explosiveness — even weak athletes can practice this. Use padding. [S9, p.14]

### Step 6: Front Handspring
1. Take a run-up to build momentum.
2. Block (punch down with strongest foot), simultaneously swinging rear leg up.
3. Kick up hard through strongest leg; other foot swings up behind, assisting rotation. Hands drop to floor.
4. Land on palms (arms slightly bent) as feet leave the floor.
5. Momentum carries legs above torso into a bent-leg handstand.
6. Don't hold the handstand — forward momentum turns you over.
7. **Extend through arms and hands to gain extra height.**
8. Continue pulling torso forward; land on both feet. Step forward to dissipate momentum if needed.
- **X-Ray**: A pivotal step and a phenomenal explosive drill in its own right. Demonstrates mastery of: running, blocking, jumping, explosive upper-body pressing, rotational tolerance, and landing. [S9, p.16]

### Step 7: Flyspring (Bounder)
1. Take a run-up.
2. Perform a **block jump with BOTH feet** (feet together), throwing hands down.
3. Kick up hard through both legs simultaneously; hands catch the floor as feet leave.
4. Momentum carries legs above torso.
5. Extend through arms and hands for extra height.
6. Land on both feet symmetrically.
- **X-Ray**: Symmetrical version of the front handspring (both feet block equally). Requires significantly more total-body power. This is the bridge between the front handspring and the true front flip. [S9, p.18]

### Step 8: Back-Drop Flip
1. Take several steps run-up.
2. Block jump with both feet, throwing hands DOWN (not to the floor — the hands don't touch in a flip).
3. Jump hard while simultaneously pulling curled upper body down at the hips.
4. Pull knees to chest immediately — this is the **tuck**.
5. As you rotate over, begin extending the legs. (Feet should contact the floor before the back.)
6. Feet hit ground first; they act as shock absorbers before glutes and back contact.
- **X-Ray**: The "missing link." Athletes must embrace landing on their back/glutes — this is the process of building rotation power. Use maximum padding (a thick mattress) on every attempt. [S9, p.20]

### Step 9: Running Front Flip
Full front flip with a short run-up. The five-phase framework applies: run-up → take-off (dive up and forward) → tuck (knees to chest) → unfurl (when tuck is deepest) → land (feet first). See [[concept-front-flip-mechanics]] for full detail.

### Step 10: Standing Front Flip
Gradually remove run-up steps from Step 9 until the flip is performed from a standing position. Requires maximum lower-body power and body leanness.

## Coaching Points
- **Progress sequentially** — Each step builds specific muscular and neurological prerequisites for the next; skipping steps invites injury
- **"Head always tucked"** — In every rolling/flipping variation, the head stays in and out of the way; the back and arms absorb force, not the neck
- **Jumping up vs. forward** — Steps 3–10 all require jumping UP more than forward; emphasize vertical power at the block
- **Padding is mandatory for Steps 5–8** — Grass, gymnastics mat, or thick mattress; especially for Step 8 (back-drop flip) where spine impact is possible
- **Rolling nausea is normal** — Rotating motion can make non-tumblers nauseous; lower the head close to the floor first; progress slowly
- **Flyspring = both feet** — Common error in Step 7 is favoring one foot. The flyspring specifically requires a symmetrical two-foot block
- **Arms create height in handspring/flyspring** — "Extend through arms and hands" at push-off is what gets height and rotation; arms are not passive

## Progressions
1. **Beginner (Steps 1–2)**: Shoulder roll and press roll — controlled, slow, on grass or soft surface; finish seated
2. **Intermediate (Steps 3–5)**: Jump roll, handstand roll, back-drop handspring — introduce upward jumping power and arm weight-bearing
3. **Advanced (Steps 6–8)**: Front handspring, flyspring, back-drop flip — full explosive pressing, blocking, rotational tolerance; use padding
4. **Elite (Steps 9–10)**: Running and standing front flip — maximum whole-body power and kinesthetic mastery
