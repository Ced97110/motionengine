# Pool Drills

## Objective
Use water resistance and buoyancy for active recovery (clearing lactate and hydrogen ions via hydrostatic pressure), injury rehabilitation, and low-impact resisted conditioning.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Swimming pool or rehabilitation pool
- **Chest-high water** (primary variation) or **1–2 feet (31–61 cm) deep water** (shallow-water variation)

## Execution
### Chest-High Water Drills
1. Perform virtually any speed drill (high knees, bounding, sprinting, skipping) in chest-deep water.
2. Water provides resistance in all directions — movements are slowed but musculature is fully loaded.
3. Buoyancy reduces impact forces on the joints significantly — ideal for injured athletes.
4. Use for 15–30 minute active recovery sessions after intense practice or competition.

### Shallow-Water Running (Complex Variation)
1. Run in water that is **1 to 2 feet deep** (31–61 cm).
2. The water's resistance forces the runner to drive the knees higher than normal — automatic high-knee drill.
3. Provides resistance without full buoyancy, so impact is reduced but body weight is still present.

## Coaching Points
- Hydrostatic pressure helps the body flush lactate and hydrogen ions — a primary reason pool work accelerates recovery.
- Athletes recovering from ankle, knee, or hip injuries can maintain conditioning through pool work without impact stress.
- Movements in water are slower — use this time to focus on perfect mechanics rather than speed.
- High-knee running is automatically reinforced in shallow water — a useful drill for athletes who struggle with knee drive.
- Keep pool sessions sport-specific: mimic basketball movements (slides, pivots, high knees, bounding) rather than swimming.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner/Injured**: Chest-high water walking and easy high knees for 15 minutes
2. **Intermediate/Recovery**: Chest-high sprint mechanics drills, 20–25 min
3. **Advanced**: Shallow-water running intervals — 30-second sprint bouts, 30-second walk recovery × 10
