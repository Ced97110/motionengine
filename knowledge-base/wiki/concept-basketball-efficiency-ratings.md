# Basketball Efficiency Ratings

## Summary
Offensive and defensive efficiency ratings — points scored (or allowed) per 100 possessions — are the fundamental unit of basketball team analysis in the *Basketball on Paper* framework. Raw point totals and win-loss records obscure true team quality because they conflate pace (number of possessions) with efficiency (quality of each possession). By normalizing to possessions, efficiency ratings reveal how well teams actually convert opportunities into points and prevent opponents from doing the same. [S16]

The efficiency rating is the aggregate outcome that the [[concept-four-factors-basketball]] drive: a team's offensive efficiency is entirely determined by their shooting percentage, offensive rebounding, turnover rate, and free throw rate.

## Key Definitions
- **Offensive Efficiency (ORtg)**: Points scored per 100 possessions
- **Defensive Efficiency (DRtg)**: Points allowed per 100 possessions
- **Net Efficiency (NetRtg)**: ORtg − DRtg; the single best predictor of wins and losses
- **Pace**: Number of possessions per 48 minutes (or per game); controls for tempo differences between teams and eras

## Historical Benchmarks [S16, pp.66-67]
- 1979 (pre-three-pointer): League average **105.0**
- 1980 (year 1 of three-pointer): **106.4**
- 1984: **107.5**
- 1988 (three-pointer fully adopted): **108.5** (league peak in this era)
- Post-1988: Declining trend through physical play era of the 1990s
- 2002 (zone + defensive three-seconds rules): Rose again by 1-2%

## Why Possessions, Not Points
Two teams can both score 100 points per game but have very different efficiencies:
- A team playing at 90 possessions/game scores at **111 per 100 possessions**
- A team playing at 110 possessions/game scores at only **91 per 100 possessions**
Raw points would make them appear equal; efficiency reveals the first team is far better offensively.

## Relationship to the Four Factors
The Four Factors are the *causes*; efficiency rating is the *effect*. The formula for floor percentage (equivalent to offensive efficiency) mathematically contains all four factors:
- **Shooting %** → higher FGM/FGA → higher efficiency
- **Offensive rebounds** → second possessions → more scoring opportunities per possession
- **Turnovers** → wasted possessions → lower efficiency
- **Free throw rate × FT%** → points without using a field goal attempt → higher efficiency

## When to Use
- **Evaluating teams**: Net efficiency predicts playoff success better than win% alone
- **Halftime/in-game adjustments**: Which factor is causing the efficiency gap — shooting, boards, turnovers, or FTs?
- **Cross-era comparisons**: Pace-adjusted efficiency allows comparing the 1972 Lakers to the 1996 Bulls meaningfully
- **Scouting**: Identify opponent's offensive/defensive efficiency gaps to target in game planning

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using raw points instead of per-possession rates** → Misleading in fast-paced vs. slow-paced matchups
2. **Ignoring defensive efficiency** → Many teams are praised for offensive output while having terrible defensive ratings that doom them
3. **Not adjusting for opponent quality** → Raw efficiency against a strong schedule is more impressive than the same number against weak opponents
