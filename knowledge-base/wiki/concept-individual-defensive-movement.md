# Individual Defensive Movement

## Summary
Before a defender can be part of a team defense, they must master individual on-ball defensive movement. This framework begins with the starting stance and progresses through four foundational steps — advance, retreat, swing, and slide — that allow a defender to dictate to the ball handler where they are allowed to go. Once the dribble begins, overplay technique takes over. Help-side responsibilities (ball-you-man, flat triangle, help-and-recover, rotation) address teammates' assignments.

All coaches agree on the same four steps; mastery of these is required before any team defensive concept can function. Team man-to-man defense is only as strong as the weakest individual defender [S15, p.283].

## When to Use
- Every possession of man-to-man defense
- On-ball matchups at every position
- Help-side positioning whenever a teammate's assignment has the ball

## Key Principles

### Starting Stance Options
1. **Front-foot-to-pivot-foot** (most common): dictates where the offense can go
2. **Parallel stance**: plays even with the ball handler
3. **Front-foot-to-free-foot overplay**: puts a foot on the offensive player's free foot to force one direction

### The Four Steps (Before the Dribble)
1. **Advance Step**: Attacker's jab step/free foot returns to parallel → defender steps front foot forward to attacker's pivot foot, widens base. Arm on same side raises high to discourage jump shot. Defender does not leave floor until shooter does.
2. **Retreat Step**: Attacker jab-steps farther than normal → trail foot drops half a step, front foot follows. Arm extends straight in direction of the fake. Stay low.
3. **Swing Step**: Attacker reverse-pivots (spins) → defender's front foot steps wide just as attacker is halfway through the spin, slightly backward to create new front-foot-to-pivot-foot stance. Trail foot pulls to attacker's pivot foot side. Attacker cannot shoot while spinning.
4. **Slide Step**: Once dribble begins → lateral shuffle, never crossing feet. On slide-step drills: tap palms on floor each step; always face court center.

### Overplay Technique (During the Dribble)
- Defender's nose is level with the basketball — overplay of approximately one half a body
- Head below the dribbler's armpits; no bouncing up and down ("bucket of water")
- Eyes on the belt buckle — moves least during fakes
- Front foot slightly behind parallel to trail foot
- Arms out to sides, constantly striking in toward the dribble ("snake strike"): quick in and back out
- Trail hand low to deflect crossover dribble
- Draw the charge if attacker dribbles into the overplayed position

### Help-Side Framework
- **Ball-you-man triangle**: draw a line from the ball to your assignment; defender stands one step off that line, two-thirds the distance toward the ball
- **Flat triangle**: all off-ball defenders position in a flat triangle relative to ball and assignment
- **Interception stance**: arm and foot toward the passing lane
- **Hedging**: fake toward ball, back off — buy time for on-ball defender to recover
- **Help and recover**: hedge → return to assignment once dribbler is controlled
- **Rotation**: fully commit to stopping breakaway dribbler; on-ball defender covers rotating defender's assignment

## Player Responsibilities
- **On-ball defender**: Use the four steps to control the attacker; overplay the dribble; draw the charge
- **One-pass-away defenders**: Deny stance (fence slide) — prevent direct pass reception
- **Help-side defenders**: Ball-you-man, flat triangle, interception stance — hedge/recover or rotate on drives; draw the charge if necessary

## Variations
### Jump Switch on Ball Screens
X1 aligns at least half a body above and ahead of the dribbler to force them away from the basket or back toward the screener. X2 switches to cover the roll. X3 covers both assignments until the switch is complete.

### Hedge and Recover on Ball Screens
X1 hedges a fraction of a second (looks like jump switch) then returns to assignment. X2 goes through or over the screen and returns to assignment. No switch.

### Trap on Ball Screens
X1 and X2 trap the ball handler; X3 covers both remaining offensive players using interception positioning.

## Common Mistakes
1. Advance-stepping before the jab foot returns → defender is off-balance and can be beaten
2. Retreating on a straight jab step → gives the attacker a straight drive lane
3. Failing to widen the base on the advance step → defender is too narrow and off-balance
4. Sliding with feet crossing → can't change direction; turns into a footrace
5. Head bouncing up and down → gets faked out by shoulder fakes
6. Eyes on ball instead of belt buckle → loses the drive to either side
7. Jump switching too early → creates a gap for the ball handler to split the two defenders
8. Help-side defender moving to ball position too early → leaves assignment for easy pass and score
