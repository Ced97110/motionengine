# Back Squat (Basketball Strength Exercise)

## Objective
Develop bilateral lower-extremity strength in the quadriceps, glutes, hamstrings, and core to improve force production for jumping, acceleration, deceleration, and injury prevention.

## Setup
- 1 player (individual strength exercise)
- Weight room / squat rack with barbell
- Equipment: barbell, weight plates, squat rack or power cage
- Start with an unloaded barbell to master technique before adding weight

## Execution
1. **Setup**: Stand erect and place the barbell behind your neck on the upper trapezius. Distribute weight evenly across both shoulders. Grip the bar with both hands for control.
2. **Foot position**: Place feet slightly wider than shoulder-width apart, toes outwardly rotated approximately 15 degrees.
3. **Descent**: Keeping the back as straight as possible, slowly descend by bending at the hips and knees simultaneously. The trunk will hinge at the pelvis — this is normal and necessary to keep the barbell over the base of support. The spine must maintain a neutral posture throughout; do not round the lower back.
4. **Depth**: Lower until the thighs are slightly below parallel to the floor. During descent, keep elbows down and in line with the trunk.
5. **Ascent**: Without bouncing at the bottom position, immediately change direction and extend the hips and knees to return to the starting position. Drive through the full foot.
6. **Breathing**: Inhale on descent, exhale on ascent.

## Coaching Points
- **"Back straight, hinge at the hips"** — The trunk does lean forward slightly to keep the bar balanced, but this is hip hinge, not spinal flexion. The spine stays neutral.
- **"Knees track over toes"** — Prevent knee valgus (knees caving inward), which stresses the ACL and medial knee structures.
- **"No bounce at the bottom"** — Bouncing out of the hole bypasses the eccentric-to-concentric transition that develops the most strength and uses ligamentous structures dangerously.
- **Watch for heel rise** — If heels come off the floor, ankle mobility is limited; use a heel elevation plate or address ankle flexibility.
- **Start light, earn the weight** — Master technique with the empty barbell before adding plates.

## Primary Muscles Worked
- **Primary**: Quadriceps (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis), gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, biceps femoris
- **Secondary**: Gastrocnemius, adductor longus, rectus abdominis, internal oblique, external oblique [S2, p.10]

## Basketball-Specific Connection
- The triple extension pattern (ankle, knee, hip) of the ascent phase directly mimics the biomechanics of jumping
- Deep descent develops the eccentric strength needed for safe landing from high vertical jumps
- Bilateral loading builds the base strength from which all unilateral basketball movements (cuts, drives, landings) derive

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Bodyweight squat — master the movement pattern, foot position, and neutral spine before adding external load. 3×10-15 reps.
2. **Intermediate**: Barbell back squat with moderate load (50-70% of 1RM). 3-4×6-10 reps. Focus on depth and technique.
3. **Advanced**: Progressive loading to near-maximal effort (80-90% 1RM). 4-5×3-5 reps. Include tempo variations (slow eccentric 3-4 seconds, explosive concentric).
