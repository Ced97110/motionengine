# Defensive Strategies (Del Harris)

## Summary
Del Harris organizes defensive strategy into three phases: pregame plan, in-game adjustments, and late-game adjustments. The pregame plan includes selecting a primary defense (man-to-man vs. zone), choosing matchups strategically, deciding on press deployment, and establishing a pick-and-roll coverage system. Harris argues that a team needs a primary defense and a secondary backup — not multiple equal defenses — because "you can't do a great job at any one thing in basketball if you try to do too many things." [S7, pp.253-256]

## When to Use
- As a coaching framework for scouting and game preparation
- When evaluating personnel mismatches and adjusting assignments
- When a primary defense is failing and a tempo change is needed
- When building a season-long defensive identity

## Key Principles

### Pregame Planning
1. **Choose one primary defense** (man-to-man ~90% of the time at the highest levels) and teach a secondary defense as a backup. One cannot be equally successful with several defenses simultaneously.
2. **Cross-matching is often superior to positional matching** — a versatile perimeter defensive stopper can be matched on the opponent's best player regardless of position; timing the switch (immediately, after the player gets hot, or in the last quarter to avoid foul trouble) is a strategic decision.
3. **Small-on-big can work briefly** — a smaller, quicker player can front a big man, deny entry, and outrun him to his spots, especially when it throws off momentum for a critical stretch.
4. **Press deployment is tactical** — a full-court press can speed up tempo, wear down an opponent, disrupt timing, or paradoxically slow the game down depending on how aggressively you trap.

### Matchup Strategy
- Put small defenders on quick guards if you have the athletes; if the opponent is quicker still, try a taller man with enough space to contain while still challenging the shot with length.
- Powerful bigs can sometimes be disrupted by a smaller, quicker defender who fronts, denies, and outworks them — use the "ants under the knees" principle.
- Cross-matching can throw off game momentum just long enough to swing the result. [S7, pp.253-254]

### Pressure Defense Timing
- Full-court pressure disrupts timing, wears opponents down, gears up your own aggressiveness, or speeds the game up.
- A 2-2-1 zone press run at three-quarter court can *slow* a game — be selective about trapping; fall back into half-court man or zone after one trap.
- To increase tempo: trap initially and continue trapping as the ball advances; relentless pressure prevents the opponent from ever settling.
- "Both teams used a lot of pressing, but the styles and results were entirely opposite." [S7, p.255]

## Pick-and-Roll Coverage Options

### Five PnR Attack Locations
- Top of the key
- Wing
- High wing (between top of the key and free-throw line extended)
- Elbows of the free-throw lane
- Sideline angles (may be covered identically)

Top and elbow angles generally require different techniques than wing/high-wing angles. [S7, p.255]

### Coverage Types

**Help-and-Recover (Hedge/Show)**
The defender on the screener helps at a prescribed angle to stop the ball handler, then each defender recovers to his original man.

- *Hard help*: Helper jumps quickly at 90° (facing the sideline) in the path of the ball handler the instant the pick is set. Prevents "turning the corner" and forces a charge or angle change. Ball handler's defender uses the time and space to go under the helper but over the screener to reengage. Helper stays with the ball handler for zero to three steps depending on the situation. If over-extended, helper rotates to the paint to find an open player.
- *Soft help*: Helper helps at 45° or less, "covering the turn" by dropping roughly in line with the strong-side foul lane box — zone-like coverage on both the ball handler and the foul lane. Ball handler's defender must pursue hard over the pick. Less aggressive; prevents splits; tends to give up the jump shot over penetration or the roll.

**Over or Under the Pick**
- **Under** the pick when: the pick is set far from the basket, or the ball handler is a poor shooter.
- **Over** the pick when: closer setups, or the ball handler is a strong shooter.

The decision is based on (1) distance of the pick from the basket and (2) the ball handler's shooting ability relative to his penetration threat. [S7, p.256]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Trying to master multiple defenses equally** → Correction: establish one primary, one secondary; master the primary before relying on the secondary.
2. **Always matching by position** → Correction: cross-match strategically to neutralize the opponent's best offensive player.
3. **Abandoning the press after easy baskets** → Correction: every defense concedes some — maintain commitment and trust the philosophy.
4. **Over-prescribing PnR rotations** → Correction: set the principles (over/under, hard/soft help angle), let players react within the framework.

## Variations

### Zone Adjustments
Zones tend to slow opponents more effectively than man-to-man; use half-court zone as the retreat after a trap to change tempo. [S7, p.255]
