# Knee Cartilage Injuries in Basketball (Focal Chondral Defects)

## Summary
Focal chondral defects (FCDs) are among the most common orthopedic challenges for basketball athletes. Damaged articular cartilage has limited self-healing capacity and can progress to osteoarthritis if left untreated. The prevalence of FCDs in NBA players has been reported at 41–50%, with 70–77% of defects located at the patellofemoral joint — directly relevant to basketball's jumping demands. Early diagnosis and individualized treatment are essential to maximize return-to-sport outcomes [S10, pp.379–380].

## When to Use
- Any basketball player presenting with persistent knee pain, swelling, effusion, crepitus, catching, or locking
- Athletes with a history of ACL injury, patellar dislocation, or hemarthrosis (high suspicion for concurrent FCD)
- Mid-season symptomatic management vs. off-season definitive surgical treatment decisions
- Return-to-sport planning after cartilage surgery

## Key Principles
1. **Early diagnosis is critical** — delay from diagnosis to intervention increases risk of worsening cartilage damage and progression to osteoarthritis [S10, p.380]
2. **Symptoms are often non-specific** — pain is the most common complaint; effusion, crepitus, catching, and locking may also be present [S10, p.380]
3. **MRI is the most sensitive imaging modality** — conventional T1/T2-weighted and fat-suppressed sequences; diagnostic accuracy varies by defect severity (Outerbridge grade 3–4) [S10, p.381]
4. **Diagnostic arthroscopy is the gold standard** — allows visualization, sizing, grading (ICRS 0–4 or Outerbridge scale), and location determination [S10, p.381]
5. **Conservative treatment is first-line** — analgesics, chondroprotective agents (glucosamine/chondroitin), steroid injections, physical therapy, and bracing, especially mid-season [S10, p.381]
6. **Surgical treatment is size- and location-driven** — lesions <2 cm → debridement ± microfracture ± OAT; lesions >2.5 cm → OCA or MACI [S10, p.382]
7. **Season timing matters** — a less invasive procedure (e.g., chondroplasty) mid-season with a more definitive procedure off-season is a valid strategy [S10, p.382]
8. **Return-to-sport rates vary by procedure** — OAT has the highest RTS rate (89–94%), followed by OCA (80–84%), ACI (73–84%), and microfracture (67–83%) [S10, pp.386–387]

## Diagnosis Framework

### Patient History
- Typical presentation: persistent knee pain and swelling, often insidious without a clear inciting event
- Acute pain can occur; ~50% of patellofemoral FCDs occur with traumatic injury
- Red flags: acute patellar dislocation, ligament injury, hemarthrosis
- Past history: prior ACL injury is associated with chondral injury [S10, p.380]

### Physical Examination
- Look for effusion, deformity, patellar maltracking, malalignment
- In patellofemoral FCD (most common in basketball): in-toeing, valgus alignment, or hip abductor weakness often observed
- Tenderness over femoral condyle or tibial plateau depending on lesion location
- Patients usually retain full range of motion
- Full knee exam to rule out meniscal tears, ligamentous injury, extensor mechanism pathology [S10, p.380]

### Radiographic Imaging
- Four X-ray views recommended: bilateral standing AP, 45° flexion weight-bearing PA (Rosenburg), non-weight-bearing 30° lateral, patella sunrise (Merchant)
- Mechanical axis X-ray (long-leg alignment views) to assess for malalignment
- Evaluate for radiolucencies, subchondral cysts, sclerosis, fragmentation, loose bodies, joint space narrowing [S10, p.381]

### MRI
- Most sensitive modality; fat-suppressed T2-weighted FSE and T1-weighted spoiled gradient-echo preferred
- Novel: dGEMRIC (glycosaminoglycan distribution), T2 relaxation mapping, T1rho mapping
- Note: 47.5% of basketball players in one study had asymptomatic cartilage lesions on MRI [S10, p.381]

### Diagnostic Arthroscopy
- Gold standard for grading and sizing
- **ICRS grading**: 0 (normal) to 4 (penetration beyond subchondral bone)
- **Outerbridge grading**: based on appearance — swelling, fragmentation, erosion [S10, p.381]

## Surgical Treatment Options

### Palliative
- **Abrasion Chondroplasty (Chondroplasty)**: smooths fragmented/damaged cartilage with a curved shaver; removes unstable and calcified cartilage while preserving healthy cartilage and subchondral bone; first-line for lesions <2 cm with flaps/loose tissue [S10, pp.382–383]

### Reparative (Marrow Stimulation)
- **Microfracture**: multiple small holes 2–4 mm apart drilled into subchondral bone to stimulate pluripotent progenitor cells, cytokines, and growth factors; most studied in basketball but lower RTS rates and performance decline documented [S10, p.383]
  - NBA outcomes: 67–83% RTS; performance declines (points, minutes, steals) commonly documented; player efficiency rating decreased by ~2.7 on average [S10, pp.386]
  - Players 8.15× less likely to remain in the NBA after microfracture in one study [S10, p.386]
- Augmentation with BMAC or PRP still under investigation [S10, p.383]

### Restorative
- **Osteochondral Autograft Transplantation (OAT)**: healthy cartilage harvested from non-load-bearing area (lateral trochlea/femoral condyle) and transplanted to defect; graft sizes 6/8/10 mm; indicated for smaller, higher-grade lesions in younger active athletes; RTS 89–94%; mean time to return 5.6 months [S10, pp.383, 387]
- **Osteochondral Allograft Transplantation (OCA)**: used for larger defects (>2 cm); cylindrical press-fit plugs or free-shell grafts; donor tissue size-matched to patient; press-fit technique eliminates need for additional fixation; basketball-specific study found 80% RTS at average 14 months, no significant performance decline [S10, pp.384, 387]
- **Autologous Chondrocyte Implantation (ACI/MACI)**: two-stage procedure; biopsy and 3–6 week culture, then reimplantation; periosteal or collagen membrane flap; MACI uses a biodegradable porcine type I/III matrix with fibrin glue; particularly suited to patellofemoral lesions; RTS 73–84%; 5-year survival 78.2%, 10-year 50.7% [S10, pp.384–385, 387]

### Novel Techniques
- Minced cartilage (De Novo NT, Biocartilage, Cartiform): one-stage procedure; limited comparative data [S10, pp.385]
- Cryopreserved osteochondral allografts (Cartiform, Chondrofix, Prochondrix): alternative to ACI; implanted with fibrin glue [S10, p.385]

## Rehabilitation Guidelines

### Patellofemoral Procedures
- Immediate: cryotherapy, elevation, bracing
- Days 1–3: progressive passive motion and weight-bearing as tolerated
- Goal: 90° of flexion within 2–3 weeks [S10, p.385]

### Tibiofemoral Procedures
- Phase 1 (until 1 week post-op): weight-bearing <20% body weight; ROM 0–30°; protective knee brace at all times
- Full passive motion by 1 week
- Full active ROM by 3 months
- Full weight-bearing at 3 weeks; brace continued until ~3 months [S10, p.386]

## Return-to-Sport Outcomes Summary
| Procedure | RTS Rate | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Microfracture | 67–83% | Performance declines documented; lowest long-term outcomes in basketball |
| OAT | 89–94% | Highest RTS rate; mean 5.6 months |
| OCA | 80–84% | Average 14 months in basketball; no performance decline found |
| ACI/MACI | 73–84% | No basketball-specific studies; frequency/duration of exercise may decrease |

Factors reducing RTS: age >30 years, BMI >27 kg/m² [S10, p.386]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Delaying diagnosis** → increased risk of cartilage damage progression and osteoarthritis; early intervention is key
2. **Using microfracture as the default** → OAT has higher RTS rates and lower failure rates (11% vs. 32%); consider OAT for smaller high-grade lesions in active young players [S10, p.387]
3. **Ignoring concurrent pathology** → ligament insufficiency, malalignment, or meniscal deficiency must be addressed simultaneously or cartilage repair will fail [S10, p.382]
4. **Not accounting for season timing** → aggressive surgery mid-season may not be appropriate; chondroplasty mid-season with definitive repair in off-season is a valid strategy
5. **Applying adult protocols without modification** → basketball players are unique as jumping athletes; outcomes from non-jumping sports may not apply [S10, p.386]
