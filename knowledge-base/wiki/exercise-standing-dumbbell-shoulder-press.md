# Standing Dumbbell Shoulder Press (Basketball Strength Training)

## Objective
Build deltoid and triceps strength through a bilateral vertical pressing pattern — directly applicable to rebounding over defenders, shooting under contact, and overhead passing.

## Setup
- **Equipment**: Two dumbbells
- **Players**: 1 athlete (spotter optional, seated variation)
- **Location**: Weight room

## Execution
1. **Beginning Position**: Stand in an athletic stance. Hold a dumbbell in each hand with palms facing each other. Position hands approximately shoulder-width apart, directly over the elbows. Eyes fixed straight ahead.
2. **Push the dumbbells upward** until the elbows are **fully extended**.
3. Keep a firm grip throughout — **do not allow wrists to flex**.
4. Lower the dumbbells back to the beginning position under control, ensuring wrists and dumbbells remain directly over the elbows throughout.

## Coaching Points
- Do **not allow excessive back arching** or the torso to fall backward
- **Allow the dumbbells to move from wide to narrow naturally** through the concentric phase — do not force a rigid path
- Do **not lock out the knees** if performing while standing
- Keep the core braced to prevent lumbar extension

## Spotting Guideline
For spotting, athlete should **sit on a bench** so the spotter can stand behind in athletic stance with hands ready to support the **wrists** (not the elbows). The spotter follows the path of the wrists throughout the lift.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Seated dumbbell shoulder press — removes lower-body stability demand
2. **Intermediate**: Standing dumbbell press as described
3. **Advanced**: Single-arm variation; alternating arms; kneeling (one or two knees) to increase core demand; kettlebell "bottom-up" grip for grip strength; split stance for anti-rotation challenge

## Variations
- **Barbell shoulder press**: Heavier loading option
- **Split stance**: Staggered foot position adds anti-rotation stability demand
- **Kneeling (one or two knees)**: Eliminates lower-body base, maximizes core demand
- **Pronated grip**: Rotate hands to palms-forward — changes deltoid emphasis
- **Kettlebell bottom-up**: Inverted kettlebell grip to develop grip strength
- **Alternating arm**: Press one dumbbell at a time while the other stays in start position
- **Single-arm only**: Maximum core anti-rotation challenge
