# Principles of Offensive Post Play

## Summary
an iconic post player's post play philosophy begins with the right mindset: post play is physically demanding, requiring aggressiveness, toughness, and persistence — and size is not as critical as attitude. Every player — including small forwards and guards — should learn to play in the post and master at least a couple of back-to-basket moves. This makes them more versatile offensively and gives them defensive insight into post situations [S7, pp.35-36].

The modern post player must also be a threat from 15–20 feet, fill the lane in transition, and apply defensive pressure farther from the basket. The era of the one-move post player is over — Tim Duncan's example of mastering a new move every summer is the standard to emulate.

## When to Use
These principles apply whenever a player operates in the post — receiving the ball, fighting for position, executing moves, or making decisions as a post scorer and passer.

## Key Principles

### 1. Use Both Feet
- Maintain balance by staying low, legs bent, feet **wider than shoulder-width apart**
- Receive the ball with **both feet on the floor** to preserve either left or right foot as the pivot foot
- Both feet should be on the floor as much as possible when taking position, sliding to a new spot, and shooting
- Single-foot landings on power shots allow defenders to knock the shooter off-balance

### 2. Make Contact With the Defender
When the low-post player reaches their spot on the floor:
- Establish contact with the back/side on the defender
- Active physical contact pins the defender, preventing them from taking away the catch
- This is legal basketball — use forearm, hip, and back without pushing

### 3. Maintain Strong Position
- "Battle of the feet" mindset: fight for position before the ball arrives, not after
- Stay low — a low center of gravity is harder to move
- Continually read and adjust to the defender's repositioning

### 4. Approach With the Right Mindset
- Post play demands mental readiness to fight for position, absorb contact, collide on screens, and pursue multiple offensive rebounds on a single possession
- "It's not a role for the faint of heart" [S7, p.35]

### 5. Develop Basketball Intelligence
- Develop a keen sense of where missed shots will bound off the rim (shot angle, shooter's location)
- Anticipate how an opposing player will attack or defend
- Maneuver to the most advantageous court location as receiver, screener, passer, or defender

### 6. Commit to Annual Skill Expansion
- Add new moves and refine existing ones every year
- Do not rely on one signature move — every post player can be taken out of their comfort zone
- Tim Duncan's model: one new facing-basket or back-to-basket move per summer [S7, p.36]

### 7. Essential Physical Attributes
The post game requires: quickness, agility, stamina, strong hands, jumping ability, coordination, and a sturdy upper and lower body. Physical conditioning is non-negotiable — an out-of-shape post player "is a lame duck on the basketball court" [S7, p.36].

## Modern Post Player Requirements
- Scoring threat from **15–20 feet** (midrange face-up game)
- Filling the lane on the **primary break** (outlet pass and sprint)
- Applying **defensive pressure** further from the opponent's basket than ever before
- All four traditional big responsibilities: **setting screens, rebounding, blocking shots, throwing outlet passes**

## Recommendation for All Players
> "I recommend that every player — even small forwards and guards — learn how to play in the post and to master a couple of moves with their back to the basket." — an iconic post player [S7, p.35]

Benefits for non-traditional post players:
- **Offensive versatility** — forces mismatches and creates 1-on-1 opportunities inside
- **Defensive insight** — understanding the post from the offensive side helps when defending the paint

## Common Mistakes
1. **Catching on one foot** → defender can easily knock shooter off-balance on power moves; always catch on both feet
2. **Standing upright in the post** → low center of gravity is essential; always stay bent and wide
3. **Relying on one move** → a defender who knows one move can take it away; build a repertoire
4. **Passive post positioning** → don't wait for the ball to fight for position; establish contact before the pass
5. **Poor conditioning** → post play is the most physically demanding position; aerobic and strength base must be solid
