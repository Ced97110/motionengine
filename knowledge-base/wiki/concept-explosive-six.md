# The Explosive Six Movement Framework

## Summary
The Explosive Six are the six movement families that form the core of Paul Wade's explosive calisthenics system. Just as his *Convict Conditioning* "Big Six" are the finest movements for strength and muscle, the Explosive Six are the best possible movements for explosive power and bodyweight speed. Each movement family is organized into ten progressive steps — from beginner to advanced — with Step 10 being the **Master Step** [S9, pp.13-19].

Hard work on even a single member of the Explosive Six will add speed, agility, and explosive power to the entire body. Training all six develops the complete explosive athlete.

## The Six Movement Families

| # | Movement Family | Purpose | Master Step |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Jumps** | Posture, explosive leaping, tucking, landing | Suicide Jump |
| 2 | **Power Pushups** | Upper-body velocity, shoulder conditioning, upper-limb reflexes | Superman Push-Up |
| 3 | **Kip-Ups** | Waist speed/power, basic agility | No-Hands Kip-Up |
| 4 | **Forward Rotations** | Forward revolution at optimal speed | Front Flip |
| 5 | **Backward Rotations** | Backward revolution at optimal speed | Back Flip |
| 6 | **Explosive (Up-and-Over) Bar Work** | Pulling upward with maximum power and skill | Muscle-Up |

*Source: [S9, pp.15, 19]*

## Training Order
The Explosive Six must be approached in a specific order:
1. **Begin with Jumps and Power Pushups** — these are basic power/speed exercises that condition joints and build movement proficiency.
2. **Reach at least Step 5 of both** before beginning the skill-based movements.
3. **Then add Kip-Ups, Front Rotations, Back Rotations, and Bar Work** — these are skill exercises requiring the joint conditioning built by steps 1 and 2 [S9, p.15].

Attempting kip-ups, flips, or muscle-ups before building foundational jump/pushup strength risks joint injury.

## Why This Framework Works for Basketball
Each movement family directly develops qualities that transfer to basketball:
- **Jumps** → vertical leap, rebounding, shot-blocking, explosive first step
- **Power Pushups** → upper-body explosiveness for passing, shooting through contact, boxing out
- **Kip-Ups** → floor-recovery speed, core explosiveness for driving and contact
- **Forward/Backward Rotations** → total-body agility, kinesthetic awareness during contact and landing
- **Bar Work (Muscle-Ups)** → upper-body pulling power for rebounding, hanging strength, contact under the basket

## Key Principles
1. **Each family develops all three explosive qualities** — Unlike box jumps (no agility) or cone drills (no power), each Explosive Six movement requires power, functional speed, AND agility simultaneously [S9, pp.5-6].
2. **Six is the optimal number** — Fewer than six misses key speed-power skills; more than six creates overkill and loss of focus [S9, p.10].
3. **Jumps and pushups first** — They are conditioning movements; the other four are skill movements requiring a foundation.
4. **No end point** — The Master Steps are superior goals, not finish lines. Beyond each Master Step lie super-advanced variations [S9, p.20].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Jumping to skill movements (flips, kip-ups) without foundational power** → Risk of joint injury; must build to Step 5 of jumps and pushups first.
2. **Ignoring the skill-nature of movements 3-6** → Flips and kip-ups must be trained like skills (progressive, patient mastery) not like strength exercises (add load, grind reps).
3. **Treating the Master Steps as the finish line** → When athletes reach a Master Step, they should aspire to super-advanced variations.
