# Foot and Ankle Injuries in Basketball — Epidemiology and General Rehabilitation

## Summary
The foot and ankle is the **most commonly injured body region** in high school and collegiate basketball players, comprising 20–40% of all reported injuries. It is also the most commonly injured region during game play in both the NBA and WNBA [S10, p.737]. Lateral ankle sprains account for 14% of all NBA/WNBA injuries and are the dominant foot/ankle pathology. Less common but more challenging injuries include syndesmotic sprains, foot fractures (especially Jones fractures), and Achilles tendon ruptures.

Despite the high frequency of foot/ankle injury, surgery is more frequent in other body regions, making rehabilitation professionals a critical part of the health-care team for these injuries.

## When to Use
- Any basketball player presenting with foot or ankle pain, swelling, or limited weight-bearing
- Preventive programming for players with prior ankle sprain history
- Return-to-play decision-making after lower extremity injury

## Key Principles

### General Rehabilitation Framework (Three Phases)
1. **Early phase** — protect the tissue, manage inflammation, maintain ROM, normalize gait with assistive devices (boots, bracing, crutches), alter weight-bearing as needed
2. **Middle phase** — progressive loading, restoration of muscle control, optimization of movement patterns and limb symmetry; bilateral → unilateral stances; stable → unstable surfaces; uniplanar → multiplanar agilities; low → high velocity
3. **Late phase** — gradual return to sport with basketball-specific drills; straight-plane activities at slow speeds → cutting/pivoting at game speed

### Key Rehabilitation Principles
1. Even during immobilization, strengthen unaffected structures (hip, knee, trunk) using weight machines to prevent deconditioning
2. Maintain cardiovascular fitness throughout — stationary biking places low loads on foot/ankle; body-weight supported or pool-based jogging are options
3. Strengthen **foot intrinsics** (short foot/doming exercise) AND **lower leg musculature** (peroneals) throughout all phases
4. Include closed-chain balance and proprioception activities to integrate neuromotor control for return to sport
5. Address biomechanical factors (especially limited dorsiflexion ROM) that may predispose players to reinjury
6. **Reinjury rates are high** in basketball — carefully address movement pattern deficits before clearance

### Return to Play Benchmarks
- **85–90% limb symmetry** during strength and jumping tasks is a common threshold
- Pain and swelling fluctuations provide important guidance for progression
- Consider player self-reported function (validated tools below) and readiness to return
- No single standardized RTP criterion exists — use a combination of functional, imaging, and subjective indicators

## Validated Outcome Measures for Foot and Ankle

| Measure | Type | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Player Efficiency Rating (PER) | Performance | Per-minute pro statistics (FT, 3PT, assists, etc.) |
| Wearable Technology | Performance | Total running distance, running intensity |
| Foot and Ankle Outcome Score | Self-report | Includes sports function subscale |
| Foot and Ankle Ability Measure (FAAM) | Self-report | Includes sports function subscale |
| Patient Specific Functional Scale | Self-report | Player lists and rates their own sport-specific activities |

## Common Injury Types in Basketball

### Lateral Ankle Sprain
- Most common; 14% of NBA/WNBA injuries
- Diagnosis: anterior drawer test + talar tilt in 20° plantar flexion
- Most common mechanism: landing on another player's foot → inversion sprain

### Syndesmotic "High" Ankle Sprain
- Less common but longer recovery
- Diagnosis: external rotation stress test + talar tilt in 0° plantar flexion
- X-ray: diastasis ≥6 mm between tibia and fibula 1 cm proximal to plafond

### Jones Fracture (5th Metatarsal)
- Diagnosis: pain to palpation at 5th metatarsal base + difficulty walking
- 61% of pro league (NBA/NFL/MLB/NHL) foot fractures occur in the NBA

### Achilles Tendon Rupture
- Diagnosis: Matles sign + palpable gap in tendon
- 39% of NBA players unable to return to play after rupture

[S10, pp.737–744]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Comply with progressive loading protocols, maintain conditioning of unaffected limbs, use prescribed bracing/taping, communicate pain levels honestly to medical staff
- **Coaches**: Modify practice demands (reduce minutes, substitute form shooting for jump shooting) during rehabilitation phases

## Common Mistakes
1. **Complete rest without any conditioning** → leads to deconditioning; instead, maintain cardiovascular fitness and strengthen unaffected structures
2. **Time-based only RTP criteria** → should be supplemented with function/performance-based criteria
3. **Ignoring biomechanical risk factors** (limited dorsiflexion, varus foot type) → increases reinjury risk
4. **Stopping rehabilitation when pain resolves** → impairments often persist after symptoms disappear
