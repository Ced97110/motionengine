# Overhead Cable Core Press — Core Lateral Flexion

## Objective
Develop oblique strength and shoulder stability through an overhead cable press against lateral resistance — building the core control needed when reaching for rebounds, shooting over defenders, and landing from awkward positions.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Cable column machine (set to middle/navel height)
- Both hands grip the handle

## Execution
1. Stand perpendicular to and directly in front of the cable column machine.
2. Assume an athletic stance: feet shoulder-width apart, knees slightly flexed, torso upright.
3. Grasp the handle with both hands at navel height, close to the abdomen. Draw in the abdominals and flex the glutes.
4. While maintaining a straight torso, extend (press) the arms **directly overhead**.
5. Return to the starting position and repeat for the prescribed number of repetitions. [S2, pp.48-49]

## Coaching Points
- The press overhead creates a lateral pull (the cable is to the side) that challenges the obliques to resist — "it's a lateral flexion challenge disguised as a press" [S2, p.49]
- Maintain a straight, upright torso throughout — do not lean away from the cable
- This exercise also trains shoulder stability in the overhead position, which is directly relevant to shooting and rebounding [S2, p.49]
- "When you land awkwardly from a rebound, these muscles prevent you from falling and injuring your back" — injury prevention framing helps player buy-in
- Muscles involved match the Pallof press but the overhead direction shifts demand to lateral flexion

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Light resistance; focus on straight torso and overhead reach
2. **Intermediate**: Moderate resistance; controlled press and return; 2-3 sets × 10-12 reps each side
3. **Advanced**: Increase resistance; perform in single-leg stance to add balance and anti-lateral-flexion demand

### Muscles Involved
- **Primary**: Multifidus, rotators, transversus abdominis, internal oblique, external oblique
- **Secondary**: Rectus abdominis, erector spinae (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis), tensor fasciae latae, adductor magnus, gluteus maximus [S2, p.49]
