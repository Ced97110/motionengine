# Player Promise vs. Performance: Analytics Case Studies

## Summary
Some of the most analytically revealing player files in *Basketball on Paper* (S16) are the "promise" players — those who showed great early potential but failed to deliver. Through career statistics tables (Off. Rtg., Def. Rtg., Floor%, Stop%, W-L per 82 games), the book demonstrates that talent alone is insufficient: injuries, attitude, weight management, drug problems, or simply being placed in the wrong role can derail even physically gifted players. The numbers often show decline well before the eye test does, and in some cases (Washburn), the numbers never showed any promise at all.

## Case Studies

### Chris Washburn — No Statistical Promise
- Number-2 pick in 1986 draft; left NC State after one troubled season
- Career Off. Rtg.: **84**, Def. Rtg.: **110**, W-L: **0.3-2.4 (0.099)** in 72 games [S16, p.297]
- "His performance on the court was not very good, not even for a rookie" — teammates sensed distraction [S16, p.297]
- Drug problem ended his career; no statistical sign of his supposed talent [S16, p.297]
- Peak season: Off. Rtg. 100 with Atlanta (29 games) — the only season above replacement level

### Cedric Ceballos — Survived the Promises Group
- Part of 1993 Phoenix Suns' promising young trio (with Dumas and O. Miller)
- Career Off. Rtg.: **110**, Def. Rtg.: **107**, W-L: **38-29 (0.567)** [S16, p.298]
- "At least an average NBA starter" — had incidents but proved to be a legitimate contributor [S16, p.298]
- Best years: 1993-96 (Off. Rtg. 116-120 range with PHO/LAL)

### Richard Dumas — Drug Problem Derailed Brilliance
- Dominated Long Beach summer league; valuable off-bench scorer in 1993 for Suns
- 1993 season: Off. Rtg. **111**, Floor% 0.55, W-L **4.0-2.0** — efficient and productive [S16, p.298]
- Forced to sit out 1994 in rehab; returned with diminished role in 1995-96
- Career ended with: Off. Rtg. 107, W-L 4.6-5.1 (0.478) [S16, p.298]
- Key lesson: promising rookie numbers can be trusted if they show efficiency, not just volume

### Oliver Miller — Weight Limited Defensive Talent
- 6'9", 290 lbs center with great hands, shooting touch, and passing from Arkansas
- Stop% was over 60% his first THREE seasons (1993: 0.60; 1994: 0.63; 1995: 0.61) — elite defensive potential [S16, p.299]
- 1994 peak: Off. Rtg. **108**, Def. Rtg. **101**, W-L **5.8-2.3** [S16, p.299]
- Weight problems caused stop% to fluctuate at lower levels; minutes were limited
- Career: Off. Rtg. 102, Def. Rtg. 104, W-L 20-26 (0.435) [S16, p.299]
- Key lesson: early stop% is an excellent predictor of defensive potential — if the body holds up

### Walter Berry — Selfishness Killed a Good Player
- Wooden Award winner at St. John's; reported best individual skills in draft class
- 1988 (best season): Off. Rtg. **109**, Floor% 0.55, W-L **3.1-5.5** [S16, p.299]
- Problem: didn't like to practice, reportedly told Larry Brown he "wasn't a fundamental player" [S16, p.299]
- Brown traded him; Berry played very well overseas for years — "which seems to have been where the NBA wanted him" [S16, p.299]
- Career: Off. Rtg. 107, W-L 8-13 (0.363) [S16, p.299]
- Key lesson: numbers can validate talent but can't compensate for coachability issues

### Ralph Sampson — Injury Ended Enormous Promise
- #1 pick 1983; 3-time First Team All-American; won Rookie of the Year
- Rookie 1984: Off. Rtg. **104**, W-L **7.0-6.9** — solid start [S16, p.300]
- Move to PF with Olajuwon hurt his defense (guarding quicker players); Off. Rtg. slowly declined: 102 → 101 → 99
- 1987: knee problems began; drug scandal surrounded team
- Career numbers declined more dramatically than anyone else examined [S16, p.300]
- Final seasons: Off. Rtg. 72-78 — catastrophic decline
- Career: Off. Rtg. **99**, Def. Rtg. **104**, W-L **24-41 (0.374)** [S16, p.300]
- Key lesson: fragile physical structure (too thin and fragile for NBA physicality) can doom even elite talent

### Derrick Coleman — Context-Dependent Liability
- #1 pick; through most of career, teams played better defense **without** him than with him [S16, p.300]
- Three exceptions (1992, 1996, 1997) were his three seasons with the **worst** team defenses — suggesting he was only valuable when the defense around him was already terrible [S16, p.301]
- Career: Off. Rtg. **105**, Def. Rtg. **104**, W-L **61-53 (0.535)** — slightly above .500 [S16, p.301]
- "Not all you want out of a number-one pick" [S16, p.301]
- Key lesson: a player who improves a bad defense but hurts a good one has limited value to winning teams

## Key Principles
1. **Early stop% predicts defensive ceiling**: Oliver Miller's 60%+ stop% in his first three seasons was the clearest early indicator of what he could become [S16, p.299]
2. **Efficient rookie seasons are real signals**: Dumas' efficient 1993 numbers (Off. Rtg. 111, Floor% 0.55) were genuine; his career failure was life circumstances, not lack of talent [S16, p.298]
3. **Injuries show up in the numbers before the eye test**: Sampson's gradual decline in Off. Rtg. (104→102→101→99) mirrored his physical deterioration before it was obvious to casual observers [S16, p.300]
4. **Context-dependent value is limited value**: A player who helps only terrible teams has minimal value because good teams have no use for him [S16, p.301]
5. **No statistical signal can compensate for off-court problems**: Washburn showed no promise even statistically; Berry showed statistical talent but not the character to use it in the NBA [S16, pp.297-299]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Trusting scoring average as a proxy for potential** → Washburn had no statistical backing for his supposed talent at any point in his career
2. **Assuming early promise will be realized** → Miller showed elite early defensive metrics, but weight management (an external factor) derailed the development
3. **Valuing a player equally on all teams** → Coleman helped bad defenses but hurt average-to-good defenses; his value was highly team-dependent [S16, p.301]
