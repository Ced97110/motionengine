# Band Backward Walk

## Objective
Develop lower-extremity and hip strength endurance in a defensive-stance-like position, reinforcing the quad-dominant force application pattern for acceleration, jumping, and deceleration.

## Setup
- **Players:** 2 (athlete + partner)
- **Location:** gym floor, court, or open space with at least 50 yards of room
- **Equipment:** long heavy-resistance rubber band
- Partner holds the opposite end of the band and walks forward, applying continuous resistance [S2, p.24]

## Execution
1. Facing a partner, stand and assume a **quarter-squat position** with feet shoulder-width apart. Secure a long, heavy-resistance rubber band around the waist. The partner holds the opposite end.
2. Maintaining proper posture, walk backward against the manual resistance of the band for a prescribed distance. The partner walks forward simultaneously, maintaining band tension.
3. Begin with **25-yard backward walks**; progress to **50-yard backward walks** for up to five sets. [S2, pp.24-25]

## Coaching Points
- **"Stay in the quarter squat"** — players tend to stand upright when fatigued; maintaining the athletic position is the point of the exercise.
- Posture must remain erect — no forward lean.
- The partner should apply consistent band tension — not jerky pulls.
- Start with lighter band resistance and increase to heavier resistance as lower-body endurance improves.
- Can be progressed to longer distances before adding band resistance levels.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner:** 25-yard backward walks × 3 sets, light band.
2. **Intermediate:** 50-yard backward walks × 5 sets, medium band.
3. **Advanced:** 50-yard backward walks × 5 sets, heavy band; reduce rest time between sets.

## Basketball Application
Develops strength and endurance of the lower extremities and hips for optimal force output performed repetitively over time. Strength in the quadriceps and hips is important for applying force into the floor, resulting in acceleration and jumping. These muscle groups also play an active role in deceleration — important for changing direction, beating an opponent, and landing from a jump. Strength endurance supports repeated performance throughout practice and games. [S2, pp.24-25]

**Primary muscles:** quadriceps (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis, vastus intermedius), gluteus maximus

**Secondary muscles:** hamstrings (semitendinosus, semimembranosus, biceps femoris), gluteus medius
