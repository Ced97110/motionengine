# Ankle Sprain Rehabilitation

## Summary
Ankle sprains are the most common basketball-related injury, occurring at 30,000 cases per day in the general public. The majority involve the anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL), calcaneofibular ligament (CFL), and posterior talofibular ligament (PTFL). The typical mechanism is combined ankle plantar flexion and inversion — landing on an opponent's foot during jumping and rebounding. [S2, pp.127-128]

Rehabilitation follows a progressive three-phase model: acute management → ROM and strength restoration → balance/proprioception and return-to-play conditioning. Severity determines the timeline.

> **Important:** Always seek qualified medical evaluation before initiating or continuing a rehabilitation program. [S2, p.127]

## When to Use
- Any time a player sustains a ligamentous ankle sprain during practice or competition
- When returning a player from ankle injury to full basketball activity
- For players with chronic ankle instability (recurrent sprains)

## Key Principles

### Grade Classification
| Grade | Pathology | Functional Limitations | Rehab Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 (Mild)** | Mild ligament stretch, no instability, single ligament | Minimal — mild gait deviation | ~1 week (aggressive) |
| **2 (Moderate)** | Partial ligament tear, mild–moderate instability | ROM deficits, strength deficits, cannot jump/run | ~3–4 weeks (conservative) |
| **3 (Severe)** | Complete ligament tear, gross instability | Cannot bear weight, requires assistive device | ~5–12 weeks (very conservative) |

[S2, pp.127-128]

### PRICE Protocol (Acute Phase)
- **P**rotect — use ankle bracing or assistive devices to alleviate stress and pain
- **R**est — prevent undue tissue damage
- **I**ce — decrease pain and blood flow, reducing bruising
- **C**ompression — increase external pressure to decrease swelling
- **E**levation — decrease swelling and assist venous and lymphatic return [S2, p.129]

### Rehabilitation Progression
1. **Acute management:** PRICE; protect from excessive load; control inflammation
2. **ROM restoration:** Prioritize dorsiflexion (DF) and plantar flexion (PF) aggressively; treat inversion (IV) and eversion (EV) as tolerated. Stretch gastrocnemius and soleus complex; soft tissue massage to gastrocnemius, soleus, peroneals, tibialis posterior, flexor digitorum longus, and flexor hallucis longus
3. **Strengthening:** Progress from open-chain to closed-chain exercises as weight-bearing is tolerated
4. **Balance and proprioception:** Critical for preventing recurrence; foundational before return to sport
5. **Return-to-play conditioning:** Dynamic warm-up → agility → jogging → sagittal-plane running → jumping → cutting/change of direction → deceleration training [S2, p.129]

### Rehabilitation Exercises (Chapter 8 sequence)
**Ankle Balance:** Single-leg stance, Rocker board
**Ankle Proprioception:** Seated Physioball perturbation, Hopping, Contralateral kick
**Ankle Strengthening:** Resistive ankle motion, Standing heel raise, Band lateral walk
[S2, p.129]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Returning too soon after a Grade 2/3 sprain** → re-injury and chronic instability; correction: complete all phases before full clearance
2. **Skipping proprioception work** → recurrent sprains; correction: balance and proprioception drills are non-negotiable before return to play
3. **Using excessive loading during acute phase** → prolongs inflammation; correction: controlled, pain-free loading only
4. **Neglecting gastrocnemius/soleus flexibility** → restricted dorsiflexion increases re-injury risk; correction: aggressive calf stretching throughout rehab

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions:** Ankle stability affects every aspect of basketball movement — cutting, landing, planting, and jumping all require a healthy ankle
