# Trapping Footwork and Pivots

## Summary
When a double-team trap is broken by an escape pass, the trappers must release and rotate instantly. The correct technique is a **reverse pivot off the outside foot**, swinging the inside foot away from the passer toward the landing location of the escape pass. Critically, trappers should **not** watch the flight of the ball — instead, they anticipate where the pass will land and try to beat it there. Proper footwork is what makes this defensive goal achievable. [S6, pp.280-282]

This pivoting principle applies whether trapping in the full-court press phase or the half-court trap defensive phase.

## When to Use
- Any time a double-team trap is beaten by an escape pass
- Full-court press trap situations (sideline traps, mid-court traps)
- Half-court trap situations (corner traps, side-ball traps, low-post traps)
- When the trap requires one trapper to re-trap at the new ball location and the other trapper to shift to an off-ball position

## Key Principles
1. **Reverse pivot off the outside foot**: Each trapper pivots off the foot that is farther from the passer (the "outside" foot), swinging the inside foot away and toward the new ball location.
2. **Do not watch the ball flight**: Anticipate the destination of the escape pass rather than tracking it visually. Pivot immediately and pursue the landing spot.
3. **Try to beat the pass to its location**: The goal is to arrive at the landing spot at the same time as or before the ball — this is only achievable with immediate, anticipatory footwork.
4. **Role differentiation**: Not all trappers pursue the ball. Some release to re-trap; others shift to off-ball positions to become interceptors or cover specific zones.
5. **Two-trapper mechanics (example)**:
   - X1 (left trapper): Reverse pivots off the right (outside) foot, swings the left foot toward the new location, rotates to an off-ball position.
   - X2 (right trapper): Reverse pivots off the left (outside) foot, swings the right foot toward the pass location, joins X4 to re-trap at the new ball location.
6. **No wasted motion**: Every step after the escape pass must be purposeful — toward the new ball or toward a help position. Standing and watching the ball costs possessions.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Watching the ball flight instead of reacting to the destination** → Costs 1–2 steps of pursuit time; the offensive player catches the ball with space to operate.
2. **Pivoting off the inside (wrong) foot** → The pivot direction is toward the passer rather than away — the trapper turns the wrong way and faces backward.
3. **Both trappers chasing the ball** → Leaves lanes open for a second escape pass or a skip pass. Role differentiation (re-trapper vs. rotator) must be predetermined.
4. **Slow release from the trap** → Hesitating to see if the escape pass is made before moving. The release must begin as the ball is released by the passer.
5. **Soft pivot without power** → The reverse pivot must be explosive to generate pursuit speed toward the new ball location.
