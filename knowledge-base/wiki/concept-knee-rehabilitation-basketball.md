# Knee Injury Rehabilitation for Basketball Players

## Summary
Knee injuries are among the most common and most severe injuries in basketball at every level. In the NBA, knee injuries account for **19.1% of all injuries** and result in more missed games than ankle and lumbar spine injuries combined. In the WNBA, knee injuries account for **22.5% of all injuries**, with a rate of 4.4/1000 athletic exposures — almost twice the NBA rate of 2.5/1000. [S10, p.711]

Although ACL injuries receive the most rehabilitation attention, common basketball knee injuries also include meniscal, chondral, patellofemoral/anterior knee pain, patellar tendinopathy, MCL, LCL, and PCL injuries. Rehabilitation must account for basketball's unique demands: repeated accelerations/decelerations, multidirectional cutting, vertical jumping, and physical contact — with strategies tailored to the athlete's position. [S10, pp.711-713]

## When to Use
- Any basketball player with a knee injury (ACL, meniscus, chondral, patellofemoral, patellar tendon, MCL/LCL/PCL)
- Pre-operative (prehab) or post-operative rehabilitation
- Return-to-play following knee surgery or significant time-loss knee injury
- In-season knee pain management with progressive load management

## Key Principles

### Prehabilitation (Pre-surgical)
1. Pre-operative rehabilitation (prehab) allows the athlete to regain ROM, decrease effusion, and build strength before surgery [S10, p.711-712]
2. Building quadriceps strength pre-operatively is linked to **greater strength post-operatively** [S10, p.712]
3. Athletes who complete both pre- and post-operative rehabilitation have better patient-reported outcomes 2 years after ACL reconstruction [S10, p.712]
4. Prehab does not replace high-quality postoperative rehabilitation but is an important long-term outcome contributor

### Outcome Measures
1. **IKDC (International Knee Documentation Committee 2000 Subjective Knee Form)** — self-reported knee function [S10, p.712]
2. **KOOS (Knee Injury Osteoarthritis Outcome Score)** — 5 subscales: symptoms, pain, ADL function, sport/recreation function, QOL — allows targeted assessment of specific deficits [S10, p.712]
3. **ACL-RSI (ACL Return to Sport after Injury Scale)** — psychological readiness via emotions, confidence, and risk appraisal questions — critical for longer ACL rehabilitations [S10, p.712]
4. **Baseline preseason measurements** — strength data serves as individual reference for rehabilitation progress and RTP readiness [S10, p.712]

### Rehabilitation Content Principles
1. **Milestone-based progression** — guided by objective markers; early milestones include ROM, strength, and normalizing gait (deficits in extension ROM, quadriceps strength, and aberrant biomechanics are linked to osteoarthritis development) [S10, p.712]
2. **Athlete engagement** — involving the athlete in goal-setting, planning, and assessment improves motivation and sets realistic expectations — especially important for longer ACL rehabilitations [S10, p.712]
3. **RICE/POLICE for acute management** — reduce inflammation to minimize quadriceps inhibition; neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES) can facilitate quadriceps activation [S10, p.712]
4. **Full lower extremity strengthening** — hamstrings, quadriceps, glutes, gastrocnemius, and soleus all play critical roles in running, jumping, and cutting — do not focus on quadriceps alone [S10, p.712]
5. **Neuromuscular control** — knee valgus collapse (hip adduction + internal rotation + knee abduction during jumping/cutting) is linked to knee injury risk; build single-leg balance, stability, and control to prevent high-risk movement patterns [S10, pp.712-713]
6. **Progressive neuromuscular training sequence**: stationary single-leg balance with a basketball → slow controlled footwork patterns → higher speeds and more demanding moving tasks [S10, p.712]
7. **Progressive load management** — basketball's horizontal and vertical demands (jumping, rebounding, shooting, cutting) place high forces on the knee; joint must be progressively loaded to handle impact [S10, p.713]
8. **Gradual contact exposure** — particularly at higher levels, physical contact is constant; rehabilitation must gradually introduce and prepare athletes for unexpected perturbations [S10, p.713]

## Player Responsibilities (Position-Specific Rehabilitation Focus)
- **PG**: Dribbling exercises, endurance in squat position, cutting/pivoting at high speeds — more ball-handling emphasis [S10, p.713]
- **SG/SF (Forwards/Wings)**: Jumping, rebounding, and shooting — driving to the basket (can involve contact); agility through tight spaces (with/without contact) [S10, p.713]
- **C (Center)**: Rebounding and scoring from under the basket; setting screens (significant contact); must be stable in squat/screen position with unexpected perturbations; role may vary team-to-team toward a wing role [S10, p.713]

## Rehabilitation Progression Guidelines
Progression is guided by:
- Athlete's subjective reports
- Objective clinical measures: effusion, joint soreness, pain
- Soreness and effusion guidelines for adjusting exercise progression [S10, p.713]
- Principles: **specificity, overload, progression** [S10, p.713]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Focusing only on quadriceps** → Full lower extremity strengthening (hamstrings, glutes, calf) is equally important for basketball-specific movement demands [S10, p.712]
2. **Skipping prehab** → Pre-operative rehabilitation significantly improves post-operative outcomes — don't move directly to surgery without at least attempting prehab [S10, p.712]
3. **Ignoring psychological readiness** → ACL-RSI assessment is essential; fear of reinjury and lack of confidence are independent barriers to safe return to sport [S10, p.712]
4. **Not tailoring to position** → Guards need cutting/pivoting readiness; forwards need jumping/rebounding exposure; centers need screen-contact stability — generic knee rehab misses sport-specific demands [S10, p.713]
5. **Insufficient contact preparation** → Clearing an athlete for unrestricted play without graded physical contact exposure increases reinjury risk during live play [S10, p.713]
