# Post Receiving Footwork and Catch Fundamentals

## Summary
Every offensive post move begins with the same universal catch routine: a small two-foot simultaneous hop, chinning the ball with elbows out, and a quick look over the high shoulder to read the defender and identify any collapsing double-down defenders. This routine is non-negotiable regardless of how the post player got open — duck-in, flash, spin screen cut, or stationary post-up [S6, pp.186-187].

The two-foot simultaneous landing gives the post player the freedom to choose either foot as the pivot foot, maximizing offensive optionality. The chin-and-look sequence serves both ball protection and defensive reading purposes.

## When to Use
- On every post entry pass, whether the player is stationary or has flashed
- When the post player is in any position with back toward the basket
- Applicable to all players who post up, not just traditional centers/power forwards

## Key Principles
1. **Two-foot simultaneous hop on the catch** — The hop must happen at the exact instant the ball is caught to avoid a travel call. Landing on both feet simultaneously gives the post player a free choice of pivot foot [S6, p.187].
2. **Chin the ball** — Hold the ball with both hands tight to the chest/chin with elbows sticking out. This protects the ball from reaching defenders and creates space [S6, p.187].
3. **Look over the HIGH shoulder** — Snap the head and look over the shoulder that is away from the basket (the "top" shoulder). This sight line reveals high-side defenders AND any perimeter players attempting to double-down from above [S6, p.187].
4. **Stagger feet when stationary** — If catching from a stationary position rather than a flash cut, stagger the feet so the contact foot (closest to the defender) is farther forward to pre-seal the defender [S6, p.186].
5. **Reach with both hands outstretched** — When flashing to the ball or catching without being completely fronted, extend both hands to create the largest possible target and secure the pass [S6, p.186].
6. **Elbows sticking out** — Keep elbows extended at all times while holding the ball to keep defenders at maximum distance and protect the catch [S6, p.186].
7. **Dribble only to advance** — Do not use the dribble unless actively advancing the ball to the basket after reading the defense. The dribble is a scoring tool, not a comfort habit [S6, p.186].

## Reading the Defender After the Catch
After looking over the high shoulder, the post player faces three scenarios:

### Scenario A — Double-down Defender Visible
- Kick the ball back out to the open perimeter teammate for a probable open shot
- If perimeter players score on these kicks, the frequency of double-downs will decrease over time, giving the post player more 1-on-1 opportunities [S6, p.187]

### Scenario B — Defender on High Side
- Defender visible over the high shoulder means they are shading the top (middle) side
- Use the **show-and-go-opposite drop-step move** toward the baseline [S6, p.187]

### Scenario C — Defender NOT Visible (Low/Baseline Side)
- Not seeing the defender over the high shoulder means the defender is on the low (baseline) side
- Show the ball low and **drop-step toward the middle** with the higher foot as the free foot [S6, p.187]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PF/C**: Execute the two-foot hop simultaneously with the catch; chin ball immediately; snap head to look over top shoulder; identify defender position; choose the appropriate post move.
- **Perimeter passers (PG/SG/SF)**: After making the post entry pass, immediately space away from the ball or cut off the post player — do not crowd the post entry area; be ready to catch a kick-out pass if the post player reads a double-down.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Hop happens AFTER the catch** → Traveling violation; correction: the hop must coincide exactly with the catch.
2. **Looking over the LOW shoulder** → The post player sees only the baseline area, misses the high-side defender read and double-down threats; correction: always look over the TOP (high) shoulder first.
3. **Catching with one hand** → Ball is vulnerable to deflection; correction: both hands outstretched with two-hand catch.
4. **Dribbling immediately without reading** → Gives up pivot foot options prematurely; correction: chin the ball and complete the read sequence before deciding to dribble.
5. **Elbows tucked in** → Defenders can reach in more easily; correction: elbows out, chinning position maintained.
