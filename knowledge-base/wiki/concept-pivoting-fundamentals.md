# Pivoting: The Universal Basketball Fundamental

## Summary
Pivoting is the most undertaught fundamental in basketball — a point Coach Bob Knight has emphasized repeatedly and that John Kimble makes the foundation of his entire coaching guide. While players and coaches often pay lip service to pivoting as a fundamental, its practical importance is frequently underemphasized in daily practice. Yet pivoting is not a single isolated skill; it is an integral component of every phase of the game. [S6, pp.9-10]

The pivot foot is the anchor of all offensive ball security, all transition from catching to attacking, all post move initiation, and all defensive footwork. Mastery of when to use a forward pivot vs. a reverse pivot, how to establish the correct pivot foot, and how to maintain balance on the pivot foot are prerequisites for advanced skill in every area of the game.

## When to Use
Pivoting is relevant in virtually every on-court situation:
- **Ball handling**: Protecting the ball after catching a pass; initiating a dribble or drive
- **Shooting**: Getting into triple-threat and executing a shot off a pass or off the dribble
- **Post play**: Executing drop steps, up-and-unders, and all post offensive moves
- **Rebounding**: Boxing out (offensive and defensive), gaining position after securing the ball
- **Screening**: Setting the legal screen angle; sealing after the screen is set
- **Defending**: Stopping a dribbler from attacking the front foot (requires reverse pivot); on-ball defensive footwork

## Key Principles
1. **Pivot foot is established on the catch** — The moment a player receives a pass with both feet on the ground (jump stop) or with a two-step landing, the pivot foot is determined by the rules of basketball. [S6, p.9]
2. **Forward pivot vs. reverse pivot** — A forward pivot rotates the body over the pivot foot toward the basket; a reverse (drop) pivot swings the free foot backward. Choice depends on defensive position and game situation.
3. **Reverse pivot protects the front foot** — To prevent a defender from attacking the front foot and creating a charge situation, the ball handler must reverse pivot away from the pressure. [S6, p.10]
4. **Balance is maintained through the pivot** — All pivoting must be executed from an athletic base (knees bent, weight centered) to maintain balance and be ready for the next action.
5. **Pivoting initiates triple threat** — After catching a pass, the pivot is the first action that gets a perimeter player into a triple-threat position (shooter, passer, or dribbler). [S6, p.9]
6. **Post pivoting enables all post moves** — Aggressive post play requires pivoting as the initiating move for every offensive action at the low block or high post. [S6, p.9]
7. **Screening requires legal pivot angles** — Setting a screen legally (stationary, correct angle) requires footwork and pivot awareness. [S6, p.9]
8. **Rebounding pivots secure the ball** — After securing a rebound, pivoting is required to chin the ball, protect it from defenders, and initiate the outlet pass. [S6, p.9]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Uses pivoting constantly — receiving passes in traffic, protecting the ball, initiating offense, pulling back from pressure
- **SG**: Pivots to get into triple threat after catch; pivot footwork in shooting off screens
- **SF**: Pivots for triple threat, face-up post moves, screening, and rebounding positioning
- **PF**: Heavy pivot use in post moves, rebounding, and setting legal screens
- **C**: Most pivot-intensive position — all low-post moves (drop step, up-and-under, jump hook) require mastery of the pivot foot

## Variations
### Forward (Front) Pivot
Rotates the body forward (toward the basket) over the pivot foot. Used to face up, square to the basket, or swing to an open passing lane.

### Reverse (Drop) Pivot
Swings the free foot backward around the pivot foot. Used by post players to execute a drop step, by perimeter players to protect the front foot from a charging defender, and in boxing out.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Lifting the pivot foot before dribbling** → Traveling violation; occurs most often when players are rushed and forget which foot is established as pivot
2. **Ignoring the reverse pivot** → Allowing defenders to cut off the front foot by not reverse pivoting creates charging fouls or forced poor passes
3. **Pivoting without a purpose** → Spinning without reading the defense wastes possessions; every pivot must have an offensive intention
4. **Poor balance on the pivot** → Upright posture, feet too narrow — the pivot must be executed from a strong athletic base
5. **Post players neglecting pivot work** → Post players who skip pivoting practice cannot execute drop steps or up-and-unders under defensive contact
