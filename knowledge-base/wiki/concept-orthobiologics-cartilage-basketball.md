# Orthobiologics for Cartilage Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Articular cartilage injuries are a significant source of pain and disability in basketball athletes, predisposing them to early joint degeneration and shortened athletic careers. A systematic review of 11 studies comprising 931 American basketball, football, and endurance running athletes found chondral defects in up to **36% of knees** [S10, p.605].

Basketball's specific demands — repetitive jumping, running, and cutting — place exceptionally high mechanical stresses across the knee joint. Because cartilage is aneural and avascular, its intrinsic healing potential is minimal; injured cartilage is replaced by inferior fibrocartilage rather than hyaline cartilage. Continued loading on injured cartilage accumulates degradative enzymes and cytokines, causing progressive damage [S10, pp.605-606].

Orthobiologics — primarily PRP, BMAC, and mesenchymal stem cells (MSC) — are used both as standalone non-operative treatments and as surgical adjuncts to promote cartilage healing and regeneration [S10, p.606].

## When to Use
- Symptomatic focal chondral defects in younger, more active patients (evidence strongest for lower OA grades)
- Adjunct to cartilage restoration surgery (microfracture, ACI, etc.)
- Chronic knee pain secondary to degenerative chondral lesions in masters/veteran basketball players
- **Best candidates**: Younger patients, lower body mass index, mild-to-moderate OA changes (NOT Grade IV Kellgren-Lawrence)

## Key Principles

### Why Cartilage Is Challenging
1. Cartilage is **aneural and avascular** — chondrocytes have minimal intrinsic repair potential [S10, p.605].
2. Injured cartilage is replaced by **fibrocartilage** (inferior mechanical properties vs. hyaline cartilage) [S10, p.605].
3. **Continued loading** on injured cartilage accumulates catabolic enzymes/cytokines → progressive collagen ultrastructure disruption [S10, pp.605-606].

### PRP Mechanisms for Cartilage
4. PRP releases: VEGF, PDGF, FGF, ILGF-1, interleukin-1β, interleukin-10, TNF-β [S10, p.606].
5. These mediators: anti-inflammatory effects, matrix synthesis stimulation, angiogenesis, collagen synthesis, cell proliferation and differentiation [S10, pp.606-607].
6. **LP-PRP is preferred** for cartilage — LR-PRP increases pro-inflammatory markers and catabolic cytokines, creating a less ideal environment for chondral repair [S10, p.607].

### Clinical Evidence — Knee Cartilage
7. **Kon et al. (50 OA patients, 3 PRP injections vs. HA)**: PRP showed improved outcomes and longer duration of efficacy vs. HA based on VAS and IKDC scores. Superior results in **younger and more active patients with lower degrees of cartilage degeneration** [S10, p.607].
8. **Cole et al. (double-blind RCT, LP-PRP vs. HA)**: Patients with mild OA and lower BMI experienced significantly better outcomes with LP-PRP [S10, p.607].
9. **Campbell et al. (systematic review, 3 meta-analyses, PRP vs. corticosteroids/HA/NSAIDs/placebo)**: Increased benefit for PRP — reduced pain, improved range of motion and quality of life — in patients with **focal chondral defects and early mild-to-moderate OA** [S10, p.607].
10. **Papalia et al. (48 professional soccer athletes, 3 injections PRP vs. hybrid HA)**: HHA superior at 3-6 months; NO significant differences at 12 months — long-term outcomes converge [S10, p.607].

### Clinical Evidence — Hip Cartilage
11. **Dallari et al. (111 patients, PRP vs. PRP+HA vs. HA)**: PRP alone had lower VAS at 2, 6, and 12 months; significantly better WOMAC at 2 and 6 months [S10, p.607].
12. **Battaglia et al. (100 patients, PRP vs. HA in hip OA)**: Both groups improved at 1-3 months; both progressively worsened 6-12 months (both remained improved vs. preoperative). No significant differences between groups. Higher Kellgren-Lawrence OA grade associated with worse VAS scores [S10, p.608].
13. **Hip cartilage conclusion**: PRP use for chondral lesions of the hip in the athlete remains largely unknown; warrants further investigation.

### The Standardization Problem
14. **Major limitation**: Wide variability in PRP preparation techniques across commercial systems → dramatically different platelet, leukocyte, and growth factor concentrations in final products [S10, p.608].
15. Multiple PRP classification systems exist (Dohan Ehrenfest, Mishra, PAW) — see [[concept-prp-classification-systems]].
16. Further questions unresolved: number of injections, timing, LP-PRP vs. LR-PRP, injection volume [S10, p.608].

## Evidence Summary

| Population | PRP Outcome | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Younger patients, lower OA grade | Positive | Best PRP candidates |
| Older patients, Grade IV OA | Neutral/Poor | Less benefit |
| Lower BMI athletes | Positive | Cole et al. |
| Professional athletes (career-end OA) | Neutral at 12 mo | Papalia et al. |
| Hip OA | Mixed | Dallari positive; Battaglia neutral |

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using LR-PRP for chondral injuries** → LR-PRP is pro-inflammatory and catabolic to cartilage; LP-PRP is preferred [S10, p.607].
2. **Injecting into high-grade OA (Grade IV)** → evidence shows minimal benefit; realistic expectations are critical [S10, p.608].
3. **Expecting a single PRP injection to provide permanent relief** → most protocols use 3 injections; effects last longer than HA but still temporary [S10, p.607].
4. **Ignoring BMI and OA grade** → outcomes are significantly better in younger, lower-BMI patients with lower OA grades [S10, p.607].
