# Front Handspring (Front Flip Chain — Step 6)

## Objective
Execute a complete forward handspring off one leg, landing upright on both feet — demonstrating and developing mastery of running, blocking, explosive upper-body pressing, rotational tolerance, and athletic landing mechanics.

## Setup
- 1 athlete
- Clear space (20–30 feet of runway)
- Soft/padded surface initially; can progress to hardcourt as confidence builds

## Execution
1. Take a run-up to build momentum.
2. **Block** — punch the strongest foot down forcefully (converting horizontal momentum to vertical). Simultaneously swing the rear leg up.
3. Kick up hard through the strongest leg, pushing off the ground; allow the torso to fall as hands drop to the floor. Other foot swings up behind, assisting rotation.
4. Land on palms with arms slightly bent — feet leave the floor as palms contact. "Catching yourself with your palms."
5. Momentum carries legs above the torso into a **bent-leg handstand position.**
6. Do NOT hold the handstand. Allow forward momentum to turn you over.
7. **Extend through arms and hands to gain extra height** — this is the explosive arm push.
8. Continue pulling the torso forward; land on both feet. Step forward to dissipate excess momentum if needed.

## Coaching Points
- **"Block" is key** — The block is the conversion of horizontal running speed into vertical rotation; it requires a sharp, forceful foot punch rather than a slide or leap [S9, p.16]
- **Arms extend for height** — "Extend through your arms and hands" at push-off is what creates height and rotation, not just the leg kick [S9, p.16]
- **Land extended, not in squat** — A deep squat landing means under-rotation; goal is to land with an increasingly extended body [S9, p.17]
- **No adjustment steps** — Once able to complete the handspring, work toward landing without forward steps — clean rotation mastery
- **Phenomenal drill in its own right** — "The athlete who can perform this correctly has reached an excellent level in: running, blocking, jumping, explosive pressing, rotational tolerance, and landing" [S9, p.16]
- **Regression**: Landing in a low squat requires less rotation — acceptable early in learning; may fall back onto glutes initially

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Land in a deep squat; may fall back onto glutes; use padding; consistency over perfection
2. **Intermediate**: Land in a partial squat; no fall-back; absorb momentum cleanly
3. **Advanced**: Land in an extended position without any adjustment steps — body mostly upright
