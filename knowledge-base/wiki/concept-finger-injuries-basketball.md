# Isolated Finger Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Finger injuries are very common in basketball. More than 90% involve sprains and volar plate injuries of the PIP and MCP joints. Direct ball contact with axial load is the most common mechanism. Missed or misdiagnosed injuries can result in permanent deformity and loss of function. Radiographs are required in nearly all cases. Most soft tissue injuries can be treated conservatively, but several conditions require definitive surgical care. The overarching goal for long fingers is preservation of motion. [S10, Ch.27]

## When to Use
- Any player presenting with finger pain, deformity, or limited range of motion after ball contact, a fall, or jersey-grabbing
- Assessing return-to-play eligibility for finger injuries
- Differentiating soft tissue injuries from fractures or tendon disruptions

## Key Principles
1. **Radiograph nearly all finger injuries** — fracture and joint incongruity must be excluded before reduction attempts
2. **The goal is motion** — immobilization should be minimized to prevent stiffness in long fingers
3. **Most soft tissue injuries do not require time out of play** — buddy taping often sufficient for return
4. **Early recognition is critical for boutonnière deformity** — delayed diagnosis leads to fixed deformity
5. **Bilateral clinical examination** helps determine instability for thumb/collateral injuries
6. **Rotational deformity must be corrected** in phalangeal fractures — ≤10° angulation in any plane allowed

## Injury Types and Management

### Soft Tissue (Fingertip/Nail)
- Subungual hematoma <50% nail matrix: trephination (heated paperclip, 18-gauge needle, or cautery), soak in sterile water + peroxide, sterile dressing [S10, p.303]
- Hematoma >50% nail matrix (possible open fracture): surgical nail removal, irrigation/debridement, nail matrix repair, replacement with splinting
- Nail avulsion without matrix damage: non-adherent dressing; if germinal matrix avulsed, slide nail/gauze under eponychial fold
- Lacerations: nonabsorbable monofilament (adults) or absorbable suture (children)
- Partial amputations <1 cm² soft tissue: secondary intention healing with dressing changes
- Larger wounds with exposed bone/tendon: emergent hand surgeon referral
- **RTP**: surgically treated injuries → 5–10 days with protective taping once soft tissue healed

### PIP Joint — Dorsal Dislocations
- Most common finger joint injury in basketball; hyperextension with axial load causes volar plate rupture [S10, p.304]
- Treatment: splint in 30° flexion for 1–2 weeks, then buddy taping 4–6 weeks
- No associated fracture or ligament injury required for this protocol
- Swelling/tenderness may persist months; early motion exercises prevent stiffness

### PIP Joint — Volar Dislocations
- Less common but more complications than dorsal dislocations; involves central slip avulsion, volar plate disruption, collateral ligament tears [S10, p.304]
- Successful closed reduction: splint in full extension 3–4 weeks (protects central slip), DIP free; night splinting additional 3–4 weeks
- Failure of closed reduction: open reduction and pinning
- **Risk**: late boutonnière deformity

### PIP Subluxation
- Buttonholing of proximal phalanx condyle through extensor hood rent; lateral view shows characteristic radiographic sign [S10, p.305]
- Closed reduction + buddy taping with full ROM usually sufficient
- Open reduction if closed reduction fails

### Collateral Ligament Injuries (Fingers)
- Result from varus/valgus stress; radial collateral > ulnar; index finger most common [S10, p.305]
- Most: buddy taping with no restriction in sports
- Significant instability: dorsal splint 3–4 weeks

### MCP Joint Dislocations
- Less common than PIP injuries; usually dorsal; index and small fingers most affected [S10, p.305]
- Simple: immediate closed reduction + buddy taping + immediate active motion
- Complex (metacarpal head buttonholed between flexor tendon and lumbrical): open reduction required; 3 weeks activity restriction post-surgery

### Skier's Thumb (Gamekeeper's Thumb)
- Acute UCL rupture of thumb MCP from hyper-abduction; Stener lesion present in up to 70% of cases [S10, p.306]
- Stable injuries: thumb spica cast 4 weeks + protected splinting during athletic competition 2–4 months
- Surgical indications: >30–35° instability in flexion, any instability in extension, Stener lesion, or large bony avulsion
- **RTP**: 3 weeks out of play (ball contact allowed at 3 weeks); competitive play 4–5 weeks

### Radial Collateral Ligament Injury (Thumb)
- Less common than UCL; treated similarly; may require surgical stabilization for instability [S10, p.306]

### Mallet Finger
- Extensor mechanism insertion disruption at distal phalanx from forced DIP flexion (ball hits fingertip) [S10, p.306]
- Treatment: DIP extension splinting ≥6 weeks (PIP free) + 4 weeks nighttime splinting
- Large bony fragment: surgical fixation with reduction and pinning
- **RTP**: 4–6 weeks out of play

### Boutonnière Deformity
- Central slip avulsion at middle phalanx; lateral bands contract → DIP extension deformity (appears up to 3 weeks later) [S10, p.306]
- Conservative: PIP extension splinting 6 weeks (DIP free) + gradual PIP motion
- Elite players: early surgical intervention recommended; large bony fragments require surgery
- **RTP**: 4–6 weeks out of play

### Pseudoboutonnière Deformity
- Similar appearance to boutonnière but caused by hyperextension to DIP, not PIP central slip avulsion [S10, p.308]
- Differentiated by absence of PIP central slip tenderness and equal active/passive PIP ROM
- Treatment: splinting if PIP deformity <45°; surgical capsular reconstruction for major deformities
- **RTP**: 4–6 weeks

### Jersey Finger
- FDP tendon avulsion from distal phalanx volar insertion; most common in ring finger [S10, p.308]
- Mechanism: forced DIP extension during maximal FDP contraction (grabbing jersey or rim)
- Clinical: inability to actively flex DIP with normal passive ROM
- Classification by retraction: Type I (palm) → Type III (A4 pulley, large bony fragment)
- All types require surgery; Type I within 7–10 days; Type III may be addressed up to 2–3 months

### Phalangeal Fractures (Proximal/Middle)
- Very common in basketball; proximal phalanx → volar angulation; middle phalanx → volar or dorsal depending on fracture location [S10, p.309]
- No >10° angulation in any plane; correct rotational deformity
- Non-displaced/stable: buddy taping + early ROM
- Displaced: closed reduction + cast/splint in intrinsic-plus position with adjacent digit
- Elite players with displaced fractures: surgical treatment preferred
- Immobilization limited to 3 weeks → then hand-based therapy
- Surgical fixation: mobilization within first week to regain ROM and control edema
- **RTP**: conservative with stable fractures → rigid cast/splint/buddy taping as soon as symptoms allow (often week 1); surgical → return with protective splinting once soft tissue healed; 75% ROM regained by 4 weeks

### Distal Phalanx Fractures
- Comprise 50% of all hand fractures; thumb and middle finger most common [S10, p.309]
- Nail bed injury must be evaluated and repaired to prevent nail deformity
- Immobilization: DIP joint 3–4 weeks, then motion
- Mallet deformity: continuous DIP extension splinting ≥6 weeks + 2 weeks active ROM

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping radiograph** on finger trauma → always X-ray before reduction to rule out fracture
2. **Prolonged immobilization of long fingers** → goal is motion; limit to minimum necessary duration
3. **Missing boutonnière deformity** (appears up to 3 weeks after injury) → follow up all PIP volar dislocations for late deformity
4. **Delayed jersey finger surgery** → Type I retraction (tendon in palm) must be repaired within 7–10 days
5. **Tolerating rotational malalignment** in phalangeal fractures → scissoring deformity causes permanent functional deficit
