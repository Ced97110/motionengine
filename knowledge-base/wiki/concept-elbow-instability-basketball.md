# Elbow Instability in Basketball

## Summary
The elbow is the second most commonly dislocated joint, with an incidence of 5–6 per 100,000 people per year. Basketball accounts for approximately 287 dislocations annually in the United States. Despite its inherent stability from osseous, ligamentous, and muscular structures, 90% of dislocations occur posteriorly or posterolaterally from a fall on an outstretched hand. The classical injury pattern follows the "Circle of Horii" — progressing from LCL disruption through the anterior capsule to the MCL, resulting in complete dislocation. [S10, pp.283-285]

## Mechanism of Injury
- **Mechanism**: Fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH) with extended elbow and fully supinated forearm
- **Force pattern**: Axial load → external rotation → valgus stress at the ulnohumeral joint
- **Circle of Horii injury sequence**: LCL → anterior capsule → MCL → complete dislocation [S10, p.283]
- **Posterior/posterolateral**: 90% of all dislocations
- **Posteromedial** (rare): associated with more severe soft-tissue injury and often missed anteromedial coronoid facet fracture [S10, p.283]
- Isolated MCL injuries can occur in basketball leading to valgus instability [S10, p.283]

## Key Facts
- ~287 basketball-related elbow dislocations annually in the USA [S10, p.285]
- 45% of all elbow dislocations are sports-related [S10, p.283]
- The elbow is inherently stable — dislocation requires significant trauma [S10, p.283]

## Initial Assessment and Management

### On-Court/Sideline
- Examine entire limb — shoulder, forearm, wrist
- Evident deformity: forearm in varus and supination [S10, p.284]
- Careful neurovascular exam immediately
- Obtain standard radiographs (AP and lateral) to classify: simple (no fracture) vs. complex (with fracture) [S10, p.284]

### Reduction Technique
1. Provide adequate analgesia — may require conscious sedation in emergency department [S10, p.284]
2. Correct any medial or lateral displacement of the olecranon
3. Flex the elbow and supinate the forearm while applying longitudinal traction [S10, p.284]
4. Post-reduction: repeat neurovascular exam, take through full ROM, note arc of stable ROM
5. Immobilize the elbow

### Post-Reduction Assessment
- Varus and valgus instability
- Posterolateral (PL) instability — assessed with the **lateral pivot shift test** (Fig. 25.2): patient supine, elbow overhead and fully extended, examiner supinates forearm and externally rotates shoulder while applying valgus stress, ranging from extension to flexion; positive = pain, apprehension, or palpable radial head dislocation/reduction beyond 40° of flexion [S10, p.284]
- If MCL injury suspected: valgus stress at 30° of elbow flexion + "milking maneuver"
- Repeat radiographs to confirm reduction and rule out entrapped structures
- MRI arthrogram to assess LCL and MCL pathology [S10, p.284]

## Treatment Options

### Nonoperative
- Most simple elbow dislocations can be treated nonoperatively [S10, p.284]
- **Early active mobilization starting 2 days after injury** = improved functional outcomes vs. 3 weeks of immobilization [S10, pp.284-285]
- Isolated MCL injuries: 6 weeks rest + physical therapy; if fails → MCL reconstruction ("Tommy John Surgery") [S10, p.285]

### Surgical Indications
- Elbow requires **>45° of flexion to remain reduced** → surgical repair indicated [S10, p.285]
- Open reduction if closed reduction fails (very high-energy injuries) [S10, p.284]
- Failed MCL nonoperative management → MCL reconstruction [S10, p.285]

## Rehabilitation Protocol (Post-Reduction/Post-Surgery)

### Overhead Motion Protocol (Excellent results)
1. **Weeks 1–3**: ROM at the elbow in overhead position — supine, shoulder flexed 90°, adducted to side, neutral rotation (minimizes varus force and LCL stress); pronation/supination and flexion/extension as tolerated [S10, p.285]
2. **Weeks 4–6**: ROM in the upright position [S10, p.285]
3. **Week 6+**: Strength and endurance exercises; resumption of normal activities [S10, p.285]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Prolonged immobilization** → Early mobilization (2 days) is superior to 3-week immobilization [S10, pp.284-285]
2. **Missing associated fracture** → Always radiograph; complex dislocations require different management [S10, p.284]
3. **Missing posteromedial dislocation** → Watch for anteromedial coronoid facet fracture [S10, p.283]
4. **Returning to play with unstable elbow** → Confirm arc of stable ROM before clearance [S10, p.284]
