# Ball Drops With a Partner

## Objective
Improve visual stimulus response and first-step quickness — directly simulates the loose-ball pursuit, deflection reaction, and defensive first-step demands of basketball. [S8, p.150]

## Setup
- 2 players
- Any flat surface; partners stand **5–10 yards (4.6–9 meters) apart**
- 1 sport-specific ball (basketball preferred; tennis ball for increased difficulty)

## Execution
1. Player A (dropper) extends the ball to **shoulder height** at arm's length.
2. Player B (reactor) stands 5–10 yards away in an athletic ready position.
3. Player A **releases the ball without warning** (no verbal cue).
4. Player B reacts visually to the ball leaving Player A's hand and **sprints to catch the ball after its first bounce but before its second bounce**.
5. Reset and repeat; switch roles periodically.

## Coaching Points
- Player B must be in a true athletic stance — not leaning, not anticipating which direction
- The dropper should vary the timing of the drop — do not develop a rhythm the reactor can anticipate
- "First step is everything" — the faster the first step, the more time available to track and catch
- Watch for **false anticipation** — reactor should react to the ball, not to subtle body language of the dropper

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: 5-yard distance; dropper holds ball at eye level; consistent timing
2. **Intermediate**: Increase distance to 10 yards; vary timing and height of drop
3. **Advanced**: Dropper holds **a ball in each hand** — only one will be dropped; reactor must identify which ball is falling and sprint to catch it (adds choice reaction component) [S8, p.150]; use a **Crazy Z-ball** (irregular bounce) for unpredictable trajectory
