# pro Scouting Report

## Summary
A scouting report in the pro is compiled for each game scouted and consists of three sections: defensive tendencies, offensive tendencies, and actual play sets. The depth of each report reflects the head coach's philosophy, but the format described by Dunleavy and Eyen balances detail with practical usability — enough information to prepare, not so much that it overwhelms players [S7, pp.288-290].

The scouting process is built around the conviction that live game attendance is irreplaceable; video cannot capture play calls, coach body language, player interactions, or away-from-ball action. A scout's primary mission is to obtain the opponent's play calls and record them on a structured worksheet (see Figure 18.1).

## When to Use
- Before preparing for any upcoming opponent
- When designing opponent-specific defensive coverages
- When deciding what information to share with players (dose carefully)

## Key Principles

### Section 1: Defensive Tendencies
1. Document how the opponent attacks defensively: full-court presses, half-court traps, zone types, PnR schemes.
2. Focus on **situational usage** — which defenses does the team prefer in which situations?
3. Example: if the team traps all wing PnRs, practice wing PnR attacks in preparation.

### Section 2: Offensive Tendencies
1. Motion offense or isolation-heavy?
2. What type of PnR (side, wing, top, horns, etc.) and how effective is it?
3. Quality of the low-post game?
4. Focus on **definitive tendencies**, not general capability.
5. Most valuable for anticipating late-game plays — teams tend to go to specific actions under pressure.

### Section 3: Play Sets (Most Detailed)
1. The 8-10 most frequently run plays and most situationally important plays.
2. Includes side OOB and baseline OOB plays.
3. Specific details matter — timing, spacing, who runs what option when.
4. Some coaches have large playbooks but run only a few main sets — focus on those.
5. Plays may change across the season; scouts note evolving sets.

### Scout's Worksheet
- Each play is recorded in a grid format (see Figure 18.1: Charlotte vs. Sacramento, 3-14-07).
- Play name, situational context (e.g., "After F.T.'s," "Late Game Need," "4th Qtr ATO"), and a quick diagram.
- Scout records time on clock for missed plays, then reviews game film to fill in the action.

### Why Live Scouting Matters (vs. Video Only)
1. **Play calls** — coaches disguise signals; a scout must read lips, watch both coach and PG simultaneously.
2. **Fake calls** — some coaches use decoy calls; distinguishing real from fake requires observing live execution.
3. **Body language and bench behavior** — insight into team morale, player relationships, injury status.
4. **Away-from-ball action** — cameras follow the ball; live scouts see off-ball cuts, screens, and reads.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Scouting too far from the game** → Scout the opponent's last game before your matchup — recency is essential.
2. **Presenting everything to the players** → Filter; give players only what helps performance without confusion.
3. **Ignoring situational tendencies** → Teams use specific plays in specific situations; know those triggers.
4. **Dismissing live scouting for video** → Video cannot capture calls, decoys, or off-ball action reliably.
