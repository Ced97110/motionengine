# Shooting Hand, Grip, Elbow, Balance Hand, and Target

## Summary
Once the stance is established, the upper body mechanics of the shot determine touch, direction, and arc. The shooting hand grips the ball on the fingertips (not the palm), the elbow forms an L-shape directly under the ball, the balance hand stabilizes from the side without interfering, and the shooter locks their gaze on the center of the basket. These elements work together to produce a repeatable, accurate shot. The elbow is identified as the single most important mechanical component — its alignment determines whether a shot goes left, right, or straight. [S5, pp.12-20]

## When to Use
- Applied to every shot: catch-and-shoot, off-dribble, free throws, three-pointers
- Emphasized during form shooting drills before progressing to full-speed game shots

## Key Principles

### Shooting Hand Grip
1. **Fingertips only** — the ball rests on fingertips and fingerpads; the palm does NOT touch the ball. Palm contact prevents proper backspin. [S5, p.13]
2. **"Daylight" check** — there must be visible space between the heel of the shooting hand and the ball; check by fitting two fingers from the balance hand between the heel and the ball [S5, p.13]
3. **Seam the ball** — position the valve (air hole) between the index and middle fingers; this ensures consistent backspin and rotation. Elite shooters seam the ball every time. With practice, players feel the seams without looking. [S5, p.14]
4. **L-shaped arm** — bring the ball toward the head so the shooting arm forms the letter L; this is the starting position for the shot [S5, p.13]

### Elbow Positioning
5. **Elbow is the most important element** — it is the single most important component for consistent shot-making [S5, p.15]
6. **Elbow directly under the ball and above the shooting foot** — this keeps the shot line straight and allows full follow-through extension [S5, p.15]
7. **Be a lifter, not a slinger or a pusher** — too much elbow bend = slinging; too little elbow bend = pushing; the L maintains lifting action [S5, p.15]
8. **Wrist cocked and locked** — bend the wrist back until the skin wrinkles before initiating the shot [S5, p.15]
9. **Elbow above the eyebrow at finish** — the follow-through should end with the elbow fully extended and above the eyebrow [S5, p.16]
10. **Chicken wing = missed shots sideways** — an elbow sticking out ("chicken wing") causes left/right misses and is an alignment problem harder to fix than depth errors [S5, p.15]

### Causes of the Chicken Wing Elbow
- Young players forced to shoot at 10-foot baskets develop throwing habits that ingrain elbow-out patterns [S5, p.16]
- Players not being "shot ready" before receiving a pass fail to pre-set the elbow in the L position [S5, p.16]
- Insufficient practice with one-handed form shooting [S5, p.16]

### Shot-Ready Position
11. **Show the palm to the passer** — the shooting hand palm faces the passer; elbow is already in L-shape; wrist already wrinkled [S5, p.16]
12. **Catch and lift** — from the shot-ready position, simply catch the ball and lift the arm; elbow stays aligned over the shooting foot through the entire motion [S5, p.16]

### Balance Hand
13. **Balance hand balances and lifts — it does NOT shoot or guide** — avoid calling it a "guide hand" (implies it steers the ball) or "off-hand" / "nonshooting hand" (negative framing) [S5, p.18]
14. **Side of the ball, fingertips only** — placed along the side of the ball; palm must not touch the ball [S5, p.18]
15. **Thumbs form a T** — if shooting and balance hand thumbs were to touch, they form the letter T; the balance hand thumb forms the top bar [S5, p.18]
16. **Do NOT place balance hand in front of or on top of the ball** — in front blocks the target from the shooter's view; on top adds unnecessary movement and slows release [S5, p.18]
17. **Shooting hand stays centered** — when adding the balance hand, many players accidentally shift the shooting hand to the side; the shooting hand must always remain under the center of the ball [S5, p.18]

### Hitting the Target
18. **Always aim for the center of the basket** — not the front rim, back rim, or hooks; the center of the basket is a universal target from any spot on the floor [S5, p.19]
19. **Consistent target = consistent shooter** — the center of the basket is always the center regardless of shooting angle, unlike the front rim which disappears from corner shooting angles [S5, p.19]
20. **Eyes on the target, never follow the ball's flight** — tracking the ball mid-flight breaks focus and reduces consistency [S5, p.20]
21. **Aiming center allows margin for error** — slight misses long, short, left, or right can still find the basket [S5, p.20]
22. **Bank shot exception** — when shooting a bank shot, the target changes to the top corner of the small square on the backboard nearest the shooter [S5, p.20]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: These mechanics apply universally. Post players shooting from close range use the same grip, elbow, and balance hand principles as perimeter shooters.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Palm on the ball** → Prevents backspin, kills touch; rest ball on fingertips only
2. **No "daylight" between heel and ball** → Ball is too deep in the hand; use two-finger check
3. **Not seaming the ball** → Inconsistent rotation; practice finding valve by feel
4. **Elbow sticking out (chicken wing)** → Left/right misses; tuck elbow directly under ball above shooting foot
5. **Balance hand in front of ball** → Blocks target view; move to side of ball
6. **Balance hand on top of ball** → Forces premature release of balance hand, adds time and motion; move to side of ball
7. **Shooting hand drifting to side when adding balance hand** → Creates two-handed shot; keep shooting hand centered under ball
8. **Looking at front rim or following ball flight** → Inconsistent targeting; lock eyes on center of basket through release
9. **Not being shot-ready before the catch** → Elbow is out of position when ball arrives; pre-set L-shape and wrist before the pass arrives
