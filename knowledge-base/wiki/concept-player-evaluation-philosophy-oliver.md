# Player Evaluation Philosophy (Oliver)

## Summary
Dean Oliver argues that ranking players from best to worst is ultimately less useful for coaches than answering specific first-order questions about player fit and role fulfillment. No Holy Grail statistic exists that can rank all NBA players definitively — player value is inherently context-dependent, team-dependent, and role-dependent. Basketball analytics tools add dimension to debates without resolving them absolutely [S16, pp.261-262].

For coaching purposes — an activity that looks forward in time — understanding the difference between the 21st and 28th best players in history matters far less than matching available players to team needs. In a world of uncertain player value, the first-order decision rule dominates: fill your need with the best player available for that need [S16, p.262].

## When to Use
- Making roster and free agency decisions
- Evaluating trade proposals
- Determining playing time and role assignments
- Designing offensive systems around personnel strengths

## Key Principles

1. **No Holy Grail statistic exists.** There is no single number that definitively ranks all basketball players. Multiple reasonable methods produce dramatically different rankings, and none can be validated against a ground truth [S16, p.261].
2. **Role fulfillment ≠ absolute quality.** Game-by-game win-loss records and other efficiency metrics measure how well a player fills their current role, not their absolute talent ceiling [S16, p.245].
3. **First-order thinking beats second-order thinking when player value is uncertain.** If you need a point guard and the 21st-best player is a forward, take the 28th-best player who is a point guard. Individual ratings aren't precise enough to justify ignoring obvious team needs [S16, p.262].
4. **Player value is inherently contextual.** The same player will have different statistical profiles and win contributions on different teams with different coaches, systems, and supporting casts [S16, p.254].
5. **Leaders' records should track their teams'.** The strongest indicator that a player is truly a team leader is when their individual win% closely parallels their team's win% — not when it consistently exceeds it [S16, p.247].
6. **Chemistry is partially measurable.** The meshing of players willing to play specific roles — each performing their role well regardless of hierarchy — is the "chemistry" that produces winning teams, whether they have one star (Bulls) or many role players (2002 Nets) [S16, p.245].

## Player Evaluation Framework

### Questions Worth Asking
- Is this player fulfilling their role well, or just performing well on a team that needs more from them?
- Does this player's individual record track their team's record (indicator of leadership)?
- What would this team look like if this player left?
- Is this player's value context-dependent (good only with a great coach/teammates) or portable?

### Player Type Categories Discussed
- **Great ones:** Jordan, Magic, Bird, Russell, Chamberlain, Malone, Stockton, Duncan — historical debates with statistical dimensionality [S16, Ch.21]
- **Specialists:** Shooting specialists (Reggie Miller), defensive specialists (Bruce Bowen) — how valuable can role players be?
- **"Freaks":** Players too short or skinny for their position but who succeed anyway
- **Underperformers:** Players labeled with high "potential" who didn't fulfill it
- **Longevity players:** Players who played long careers but never at an exceptional level
- **WNBA stars:** Historical comparison within the WNBA [S16, p.262]

### The Leadership Identification Problem
Identifying team leaders purely from statistics is difficult. Leading in possessions used (per-game) is one operational definition, but it doesn't always fit. The key empirical test: does the team's win-loss record approximate the player's individual win-loss record? If yes, that player is driving team outcomes [S16, p.247].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Assuming the highest scorer is the leader** → Leaders are defined by whose individual record most closely matches the team's record, not by scoring totals.
2. **Using analytical rankings to override obvious positional/need-based decisions** → Individual ratings aren't precise enough to trust over first-order fit decisions.
3. **Expecting a player to replicate their stats in a different role** → Role context is everything; a great supporting player may not perform the same as a primary offensive option.
4. **Treating validation failure as proof a method is wrong** → "Jordan isn't ranked #1 in this system, so it must be wrong" is scientifically inappropriate [S16, p.256].
