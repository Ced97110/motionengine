# SAQ Movement Competencies and Techniques

## Summary
Before prescribing SAQ drills, coaches must evaluate whether athletes possess the foundational movement competencies required for the sport: sprinting efficiency, agility (direction change mechanics), and the ability to decelerate safely. These competencies are assessed through observation of technique during controlled drills, with corrections applied before advancing to higher-intensity work. [S8, pp. 12–13]

For basketball players, the most critical movement competencies are: acceleration mechanics (first 3–5 steps), lateral change of direction, backpedal-to-sprint transition, and deceleration/braking.

## When to Use
- During the athlete assessment phase at the start of each training year.
- When observing a player's court movements for inefficiencies ("sitting," bouncing, wide-arm swing).
- When deciding which SAQ chapter drills to prioritize for a specific player.

## Key Principles

### Sprinting Technique Variants
Three variants, each requiring distinct coaching cues [S8, p. 12]:
1. **Acceleration**: Low body angle, powerful push-off, explosive arm drive — first 10–20 meters.
2. **Transition**: Rising body position, stride lengthening, maintained arm rhythm — middle phase.
3. **Maximum velocity**: Upright posture, high knee lift, minimal ground contact time — top speed.

**Three aspects assessed in each variant**:
- **Visual focus**: Athlete looks in the direction of travel — not down or to the side.
- **Arm action**: Aggressive hand and knee "hammering" motions; arms facilitate leg action. Arms that swing transversely, excessively backward, or with hunched shoulders lose speed. [S8, p. 13]
- **Leg action**: Explosive leg drive, minimized ground support time; feet should not turn excessively outward. "Sitting" (insufficient leg extension at push-off) indicates inadequate power transmission.

### Agility Techniques and Mechanics
Agility requires dynamic balance, coordination, and explosiveness during direction changes [S8, pp. 12–13]:
- **Directional changes** (cutting left/right): Initiate with the head/eyes turning first, then shoulders and hips. Turning shoulders before eyes causes rounding off the cut or weaving off the desired path.
- **Transition turns** (backpedal-to-sprint "turn-and-run"): Head turns first to the new target, then use an open or crossover step to drive into the new direction.
- **Arm action in agility**: Explosive arm action is equally critical in direction changes as in linear sprinting. Use arms to accelerate into the new movement pattern.

### Common Running Technique Errors (Correction Checklist)
| Error | Cause | Correction |
|-------|-------|------------|
| Insufficient leg extension at push-off ("sitting") | Inadequate power transmission | Ankle joint work, running/hopping, special strengthening |
| Feet turned excessively outward | Faulty running form | Run in lane with feet slightly inward; walking/jogging drills |
| "Bouncing" with vertical swaying | Push-off too vertical | Longer push-off, chalk mark drills, increased stride rate |
| Lead leg swing too wide; flat foot plant | Trunk/thigh weakness, fatigue | High-knee lifts; snatching thigh in diagonal support; strengthening |
| Ineffective arm movements (transverse, hunched shoulders) | Excessive shoulder movement; insufficient flexibility | Practice proper arm movements during easy stride/side-straddle jogging |
| Head/neck hyperextended or hyperflexed | Fatigue; technique misunderstanding | Normal erect head carriage, eyes focused ahead |

[S8, p. 13, adapted from Schmolinsky 1993]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG**: Prioritize acceleration, backpedal-to-sprint transition, and lateral change of direction for on-ball defense and fast-break execution.
- **SF/PF/C**: Prioritize deceleration mechanics, single-leg squat competency, and lateral shuffle footwork for post defense and rebounding position.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Starting direction change with shoulders/hips** → Causes path deviation ("rounding"). Cue: "eyes first, head first, then body."
2. **Passive arm action during cuts** → Loses 15–20% of potential acceleration speed. Cue: "punch the arms into the new direction."
3. **Looking down during sprints** → Disrupts body position and balance. Cue: "eyes on target, chin level."
4. **Flat-foot landing on direction changes** → Reduces ground-reaction force and increases injury risk. Cue: "land on the ball of the foot, not the heel."
