# Shoulder Assessment in Basketball

## Summary
Shoulder injuries in basketball are relatively uncommon — accounting for 1–3% of players annually in the NBA — yet even minor injuries can significantly impair shooting, dribbling, blocking, and rebounding. NBA data (2013/14–2018/19) shows the primary shoulder injuries are instabilities, rotator cuff injury, acromioclavicular sprains, and strains. Unlike ACL or Achilles injuries, players returning from shoulder stabilization procedures show significantly smaller declines in player efficiency ratings (PER) [S10, p.627].

The shoulder joint relies heavily on the sensorimotor system (SMS) for dynamic stability. Pain and tissue pathology impair the SMS's ability to control proper shoulder movement. A comprehensive assessment must establish: (1) nature and site of tissue pathology, (2) cause of pain, and (3) resultant neuromuscular control and proprioception impact. Critically, **the entire kinetic chain must be assessed** — not just the shoulder — as local and distant nonovert alterations (lumbar, hip, knee inflexibility; strength imbalances; scapular dyskinesis) frequently contribute to shoulder symptoms [S10, pp.627-628].

## When to Use
- Any basketball player presenting with shoulder pain, instability, or performance decline
- Preseason screening for shoulder injury risk
- Return-to-play assessment following shoulder injury
- Performance optimization — even minor shoulder dysfunction affects shooting mechanics

## Key Principles

### 1. Kinetic Chain Assessment
1. Standard diagnostic tests establish **overt causes** (tendon injury, instability)
2. **Nonovert alterations** — local or distant — frequently delay return to function if missed [S10, p.628]
3. **Local nonovert:** loss of shoulder internal rotation, strength imbalances, scapular dyskinesis
4. **Distant nonovert:** lumbar, hip, and/or knee inflexibility
5. Wheelchair athlete research supports this: better trunk control = fewer overuse shoulder injuries; unstable trunk base → upper extremity compensation → overuse injury [S10, p.628]

### 2. Standing Posture Assessment
- Evaluate legs, lumbar, thoracic, and cervical spine
- Forward head positioning has been found significantly more common in subjects with symptomatic shoulders
- Thoracic kyphosis: reducing it can increase shoulder ROM, but no direct evidence links kyphosis to shoulder pain
- **Symptom alteration tests**: assess whether shoulder symptoms ease immediately following correction of observed postural inconsistency — this establishes a functional link [S10, p.628]

### 3. Scapular Assessment — The Three-Component Approach [S10, pp.628-629]
The scapula requires both stability and mobility. Its 3D motion during arm elevation involves: upward rotation, posterior tilting, external rotation + clavicle elevation and protraction.

**A. Visual Observation (Static)**
- Player standing, shirt off, viewed from behind
- Identify three common prominences:
  1. **Inferomedial border** — anteriorly rotated coracoid and acromion
  2. **Medial border** — classic "winging"
  3. **Superior medial border** — superior scapula translation

**B. PALM Measurement (Palpation Meter)**
- Measures: scapula upward/downward rotation, protraction/retraction, anterior/posterior tipping, internal/external tipping, elevation/depression, pectoralis minor length, coracoid height
- Two positions: arm at 0° (resting) and arm at 90° abduction (holding 3% body weight), 15-second rest between measurements
- Key landmarks: T3 spinous process, scapula spine root, acromion angle, medial scapula border, scapula spine, coracoid process, fourth intercostal space
- Useful in assessing overhead athletes with shoulder "impingement" [S10, p.629]
- **Pectoralis minor length** measured from medial coracoid process to fourth intercostal space at sternum — may be shortened in players with forward head/rounded shoulder posture, linked to NTOS

**C. Dynamic Scapular Motion (Modified Scapula Dyskinesia Test — SDT)**
- Described by Uhl et al.
- Any abnormality in scapular motion recorded as "yes"; normal scapulothoracic motion recorded as "no"

### 4. Symptom Alteration Tests
Used to establish whether observed postural or scapular findings are functionally linked to shoulder symptoms:
- Symptoms present during a provocative test that immediately ease following correction = confirmed functional link
- Applied for: thoracic kyphosis, scapular dyskinesis, and kinetic chain deficits

### 5. SICK Scapula Syndrome [S10, p.629]
**SICK** = Scapula malposition, Inferior medial border prominence, Coracoid pain and malposition, and scapula dysKinesia
- Overuse muscle fatigue syndrome
- Symptoms: anterior shoulder pain, posterosuperior scapula pain, superior shoulder pain, proximal lateral pain, or a combination
- Frequently **misdiagnosed** as:
  - Anterior instability (anterior symptoms)
  - Cervical spine referral (posterosuperior symptoms)
  - Subacromial impingement (proximal lateral arm pain)
  - AC joint pathology (superior shoulder pain)
- Pectoralis minor length measurement is clinically useful in SICK scapula diagnosis

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions:** Report any shoulder pain early — even "minor" symptoms affect shooting mechanics and motor patterns
- **Guards/Wings:** Shooting, dribbling, and defensive positions place high demands on shoulder SMS; subtle dysfunction may precede overt injury
- **Bigs (PF/C):** Rebounding and blocking place high overhead and contact loading demands on shoulder; thoracic kyphosis and trunk stability are especially important

## Common Mistakes
1. **Assessing only the shoulder joint in isolation** → Always evaluate the full kinetic chain (lumbar, hip, knee)
2. **Relying on a single diagnostic test** → Use a battery of tests including provocative, symptom alteration, and strength tests
3. **Missing SICK scapula** → Its variable symptoms mimic multiple other diagnoses; palpate coracoid and measure pectoralis minor length
4. **Overlooking dynamic scapular motion** → Static assessment alone misses motion-dependent dyskinesis that only appears during arm elevation
5. **Ignoring forward head posture in shooters** → Cervical dysfunction from poor posture can alter shoulder SMS function
