# Achilles Tendon Rupture Rehabilitation in Basketball

## Summary
Basketball is a **common mechanism of Achilles tendon rupture** — the forceful eccentric plantar flexor contraction during landing a jump or making a cut. In the NBA, **39% of players are unable to return to play** after Achilles rupture, and those who do return show decreased performance based on Player Efficiency Rating (PER) [S10, p.743]. Anecdotally, this injury is more common in the later portions of games when players are fatigued. Even when players do return, plantar flexor deficits in strength and power can persist long-term, affecting running and jumping performance for years.

## When to Use
- Player presents with sudden onset posterior ankle/lower leg pain during jumping or cutting
- Matles sign positive
- Palpable gap in the Achilles tendon
- Imaging (ultrasound) confirms tendon discontinuity

## Key Principles

### Pathophysiology
1. There is likely **pre-existing tendon degeneration** prior to rupture — rarely preceded by pain or discomfort
2. Rupture occurs with **forceful eccentric contraction** of plantar flexors (landing, cutting)
3. Anecdotally more common in **late-game fatigue** situations
4. Results in significant deficits in plantar flexor structure and function — running and jumping performance deficits **persist into the long term** [S10, pp.743–744]

### Surgical vs. Non-Surgical Management
1. Both surgical and nonsurgical management are valid initial treatments
2. **Ultrasound imaging** of distance between tendon ends informs surgical decision:
   - Large gap → higher risk of poor outcomes/rerupture → favors surgery
3. With **comparable high-quality rehabilitation**, rerupture risk is similar between surgical and non-surgical management
4. Without standardized rehab, **surgical management has lower rerupture risk**
5. Key evidence: with comparable rehab, the surgical vs. non-surgical choice matters less than rehabilitation quality

### Rehabilitation Framework

**Basic Premise:** Expose the tendon to progressively increasing loads to stimulate repair and remodeling, while protecting from high loads and **avoiding tendon elongation**

**Early Phase**
1. Progressive weight-bearing with **plantar flexion immobilization**
2. Exercises begin with ROM — **avoiding dorsiflexion** initially
3. Progress to plantar flexor strengthening exercises
4. Maintain general conditioning throughout (upper body, contralateral limb, pool/supported cardio)

**Middle Phase**
1. Progress plantar flexor strength — bilateral → unilateral exercises
2. Heel raises: key exercise for building plantar flexor function
3. Add closed-chain loading with increasing demand
4. Balance and proprioception work

**Late Phase / Return to Sport**
1. Running progression initiated when player achieves **5 unilateral heel rises at 90% of available height** on the ruptured side
2. If unable to meet by 16 weeks: initiate running once player can raise **≥70% of bodyweight in a unilateral heel rise**
3. **Important**: An individual may never fully recover heel-rise height comparable to the uninjured side — use maximum available height on ruptured side, not the uninjured side, as the comparator
4. Tendon tissue requires **≥36 hours** to recover after heavy loading (sprinting, jumping) → basketball players may need extra rest between practices/games during return phase

### Return to Play
- General population: ~80% return to some level of sporting activity; average return at **~6 months**
- Elite athletes: **significantly lower** return rates than general population
- NBA: 39% unable to return to play [S10, p.743]
- Performance substantially affected even at 6 months — communicate realistic expectations

### Heel-Rise RTP Criterion (Key Protocol)

| Criterion | Standard |
|---|---|
| Primary goal | 5 unilateral heel rises at 90% of available height on ruptured side |
| Alternative (if unmet by 16 weeks) | ≥70% bodyweight in unilateral heel rise |
| Comparison limb | Maximum available on ruptured side (not uninjured side) |
| Recovery between high-load sessions | ≥36 hours |

[S10, p.744]

### Prognostic Indicators (Being Investigated)
- Non-modifiable: age, BMI, sex
- Acute imaging: distance between tendon ends (ultrasound)
- At 1-year post-injury: **heel-rise height**

## Common Mistakes
1. **Allowing dorsiflexion too early** → risks tendon elongation, the primary structural complication
2. **Using uninjured limb as benchmark for RTP** → unrealistic; the ruptured tendon may never fully match; use ruptured side's maximum available as the target
3. **Returning to full game schedule immediately** → tendon needs ≥36 hr recovery after heavy loading; maintain rest intervals between heavy practices/games
4. **Stopping conditioning during rehab** → substantial deconditioning occurs without active maintenance programming
5. **Setting unrealistic performance expectations** → NBA data show 39% non-return rate; prepare players and coaches for long-term reality
