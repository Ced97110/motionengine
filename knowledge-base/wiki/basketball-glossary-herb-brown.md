# Basketball Glossary — Herb Brown's Terminology

## Summary
Basketball coaches and teams use specialized terminology to teach players and confuse opponents. Herb Brown's glossary from *Let's Talk Defense* defines the specific terms and acronyms used throughout his defensive and offensive system. Understanding this vocabulary is essential for reading his plays, concepts, and coaching cues in context.

This page catalogs terms from the glossary (pp.233–246) organized by category, with Herb Brown's exact definitions preserved. [S1, pp.233-246]

| **ATW** | *All the way* — running a set play through all options before shooting; familiarizes players with all possibilities |

## Defensive Terms

**Ball-you-man**: Defensive position where a player always keeps his body between the man he is guarding and the basketball.

**Belly up / Body up**: Defensive player makes physical contact with the offensive post man (with or without ball), forcing him toward the trapping defender to stop easy ball reversal.

**Blitz the pick and roll**: The screener's defender traps the dribbler while the dribbler's defender chases over the screen to double-team and stop penetration. Teams may use a color or acronym to signal this.

**Bump, chuck, body, or ride**: Defender makes contact with an offensive player without the ball to slow, stop, influence, or impede a cut. Used against screeners and cutters.

**Centerfield**: Area above the circle or 3-point line in the middle of the offensive end where a player receives outlet passes when teammates are in trouble. Also describes the area above the nail at the free-throw line for weak-side defensive help.

**Cherry pick / Tighten / Leak out**: Defender contesting a jump shot leaves early for the offensive end hoping for an easy basket after a defensive rebound — also used to distract the shooter and break concentration.

**Close out**: Method by which a player recovers to an offensive player who has received the ball and is ready to shoot after providing weak-side help. "Defender must close out quickly but under control to limit dribble penetration."

**Contact show**: Defending the pick and roll by keeping contact with the screener while jumping out to stop the dribbler.

**Cover down**: When a player is beaten on a baseline drive, the nearest weak-side player slides down and crosses the lane to stop the drive and protect the goal.

**Deny and overplay**: Ball-you-man principle applied to a player one pass away. Defender keeps body between man and ball to discourage the penetrating pass — forcing the opponent backdoor or farther out on the perimeter.

**Digging**: Defender on the strong side shrinks off his man when the post man has the ball, trying to disconcert the post player or force a pass back outside.

**Double-team**: Two defensive players trap the offensive player with the ball.

**Dribble used**: Pressuring a big man on the perimeter as if he has already used his dribble — limits his ability to feed the post or reverse the ball.

**Full-body show**: Defensive player off the ball gets his entire body in front of a penetrating ball-handler to force a pass uphill or across court.

**Hand check**: Defensive act of controlling and channeling the dribbler or post man. In the pro, the forearm must be used (not the palm) to avoid a personal foul. Legal below the free-throw line extended when the post has the ball with his back to the goal.

**Help**: Leaving an opponent to stop penetration of a teammate's opponent.

**I (formation)**: Weak-side defensive positioning — one defender on the elbow, one on the low block/box. Signals readiness to help. Can also be set on the strong side.

**Ice / ISO (defensively)**: Isolation — clearing the area to permit one-on-one defense against the ball-handler.

**Inverting**: Keeping big defenders inside and perimeter defenders outside by switching defensive positions on the weak side regardless of opponent positioning.

**Level with the ball**: Defender whose man is trailing the play must get back to the ball to stop penetration. Used in full-court, transition, and half-court defense.

**Lock and trail**: Defender on a player receiving a down screen forces the offensive player to curl the screen by staying on his hip and outside shoulder.

**Lock in / Marry**: Defender completely denies a good shooter the basketball without helping teammates — "gloves or blankets" the offensive player. Used when the shooter is in a prime scoring position on the strong/ball side.

**One and done**: Trap the ball once in a full- or half-court press. If the pass escapes or the trap fails, defense sprints back to its normal defensive scheme.

**Open and through**: Technique to defend the weak-side pin-down — the player guarding the screener opens up to let the teammate guarding the cutter go inside, ball-side of the screen.

**Playing topside**: On a pin-down screen, defender stays attached to his man but tries to force him *away* from the screen and ball by playing above him on his outside shoulder from the start of the play.

## Offensive Terms

**Ahead**: Ball passed from the wing to the corner up the court in early offense or during transition.

**Backpick / Rip / Step-up / Blind screen**: Player without the ball comes from behind a player (with or without ball) and sets a vertical or diagonal screen from behind.

**Back or butt to the baseline**: Defensive position used to guard a post feeder once the ball has been entered — the defender's stance dictates which direction the cutter can go.

**Blast**: Two-one-two high-post offensive set; also describes the first diagonal cut from high to low post on the strong side.

**Blind pig**: Flash post from weak-side baseline to elbow/pinch post when the offensive wing is overplayed — sets up the wing for a backdoor cut once the pass is made to the flashing post player. A classic pressure release when a player is overplayed anywhere on the court.

**Box set**: Offensive alignment with four players (without the ball) in a box/square approximately 15–17 feet apart, usually on or above the elbows and boxes.

**Brush screen**: Cutter without the ball cuts inside and in front of the ball-handler toward the corner, enabling the ball-handler to drive to the basket.

**Bump or bump back**: After a player sets a screen and the offensive player uses it, the screener pops back (screens, flares, or comes back to the ball). Free yourself when your defensive player helps.

**Bury**: Guard passing to a wing cuts in front of the wing to the strong-side corner.

**Buttonhook**: Player fakes a cross screen, stops quickly, comes back to the ball, and seals his defender on his back — usually in the lane.

**Chase**: (1) Low-post player goes across the lane in the same direction as the dribbler in a pick-and-roll. (2) Player passes the ball across court or out of a double-team and follows the pass to get free for a return pass.

**Circle**: Post man without the ball circles up or down the lane toward the ball to create a passing lane when being doubled — curling to establish an outlet. Also describes the action of the second big man in the pick and roll.

**Corner**: (1) Spacing in the overloaded offensive set of the triangle offense. (2) The physical area where baseline and sideline meet.

**Counter**: Offensive player comes from baseline or over a screener to the weak side using a back screen to free himself from his defender. Also called "flaring off a back or jam screen."

**Crossing**: Two offensive players run from one side to the other with or without screening, forcing defenders to switch or chase.

**Curl**: Offensive player running a man off a screen by a post above the block curls tightly around the screen (practically shoulder-to-shoulder with the screener) to lose his defender.

**DHO**: Dribble handoff between two offensive players.

**Diagonal cut**: Cut from weak side to strong side crossing the defender's face — "possibly one of the toughest cuts a defensive player has to guard."

**Diagonal down screen**: Angled down screen usually set from the high post for a player on the opposite low block.

**Direct or drop**: Pass from the point guard to a player on or above the elbow, who then makes a straight pass to a player posting up on the same side.

**Dive**: Ball-handler drives directly at a teammate's overplayed defender, causing the defender to turn his head — enabling the overplayed teammate to go backdoor for a layup.

**Double-doubles**: Successive double screens for players trying to receive the ball — frequently staggered, difficult to defend. Second double usually surprises the defense.

**Double screen**: Two-man shoulder-to-shoulder screen.

**Drag**: High or wing pick-and-roll in transition or early offense that catches the defense by surprise.

**Duck in**: Weak-side low-post flash across the near leg of the defender and up toward the ball — sealing the defender on the back to receive a pass in scoring position.

**Early offense**: Secondary break when fast break is not available but defense is not yet set.

**Elbow area / Pinch post**: Area parallel to or a step above/below and outside the free-throw lane — an offensive player can post with his back to the basket here.

**Empty / Clear out**: Signal that the lane and side is clear for an isolated offensive player.

**Fist**: Hand signal for a specific play or tactic (pick and roll, post-up, double-stack, or defensive trap). Teams add "up," "down," "out," "right," or "left" to specify.

**Fist to palm / Punch**: Indicates a direct post-up on the low block.

**Flash / Weak-side cut**: Quick, hard cut to the ball from weak to strong side of the floor, mainly through the paint.

**Flat**: Offensive set with four players spread across the baseline on either side of the free-throw lane.

**Flex cut**: Horizontal baseline cut over or under a post screener, usually away from the ball to the strong side through the free-throw lane.

**Floppy**: Wings cross on the baseline off down screens set by big men in a scissors action.

**Get**: Player passes to a wing/perimeter and follows the pass to get it back. Some pro teams use this term for a side pick-and-roll.

**Go**: Dribble drive from one side to the other on a high pick-and-roll.

**Guard-to-guard**: Horizontal ball-swing pass; initiates two-guard-front sets (blast, Continuity-style offenses).

**Hawk cut**: Diagonal cut from above the weak-side elbow to the strong-side post area or between the baseline and sideline. Named for the pro team' use in the 1970s–80s.

**Head hunt**: Screener screens the *defender* of a specific offensive player to free that player to receive the ball (also called "hunting the man").

**High post**: Area from the free-throw line to above the top of the key/3-point line.

**Hold**: Offensive player cuts and instead of clearing out or cross-screening, he maintains position and posts his defender on the low block.

**Horns**: Offensive signal/term for a particular play — teams use it to describe different actions.

**Ice / ISO (offensively)**: Isolating an offensive player by clearing other players and defenders away to allow a one-on-one opportunity.

**Improve position to gain possession**: The receiver's responsibility — step out and create a passing lane when your teammate is being pressured. Don't force the pass; establish the angle first.

**Inside out**: Initiate offense by going inside first to collapse or trap the defense, then kick the ball out to a shooter.

**Inverting**: Keeping big players inside and perimeter players outside by switching defensive positions on the weak side.

**Jam screen**: Back pick/screen set after a player has passed and cut away — the screen allows the cutter to flare off the back screen.

**Kickback**: Ball-handler drives to the middle, draws the defense, then passes back to an open offensive player for a shot or a post feed to a sealed inside player.

**Level with the ball**: Defender whose man is trailing must get back to the ball-side to stop penetration.

**Lift**: In drop/direct post-up actions, two or three offensive players not involved in the play step out above the 3-point line on the weak side to occupy their defenders and prevent help on the post feed.

**Lob**: Pass to a post or perimeter player being fronted/overplayed — the offensive player turns, seals his defender, and receives a pass aimed toward the front of the rim (or fakes high and goes backdoor).

**Lock and lob**: High-low action when the post is being fronted or aggressively played on his inside shoulder. Post man turns and seals the defender; wing or high post throws a lob to the low-post player.

**Loop / Split**: Offensive player without the ball splits two defenders laterally to open a clear side for the dribbler or provide a throwback option when the dribbler is trapped.

**Midpost**: The area outside the lane halfway between the foul line and the low block.

**Misdirection screen**: Back screen or pick set by the player *farthest* from the ball for the player *nearest* the ball — often a surprise; can be a cross or flare screen.

**Mismatch**: A taller player guarded by a smaller or weaker defender (or a defender in foul trouble) — offense directs the ball to exploit the mismatch.

**Misses/Makes**: Whether teams fast break on misses only or on both misses and makes — affects how the defense must transition back.

**Motion**: Structured freelance offense — five players passing, cutting, screening, and reversing the ball, with all cuts dictated by reading the defense.

**Multiple screens**: Successive screens to free an offensive player for a scoring opportunity or to occupy the defense.

**Multiply**: After a steal and layup, team immediately applies full-court pressure to create another scoring opportunity before the offense can recover.

**Need / Crunch**: A play used when a basket is absolutely required — end of game or critical situation. Should be emphasized in scouting reports.

**Open**: Similar to motion — a passing game without a post player that emphasizes passing and cutting by all five players.

**Penetrate and pitch / Draw and kick**: Dribbler drives toward the goal and, if stopped by a help defender, passes to a teammate for an open shot. Effective against both zone and man-to-man.

**Pick and roll**: On-the-ball screen set by a big man on the dribbler's defender — designed to force a switch or help, creating a scoring opportunity before the defense recovers. "One of the most difficult offensive plays a defense must learn to guard."

**Pick the picker / Screen the screener**: The screener for one player receives a screen from another teammate — freeing the screener for a catch-and-shoot.

**Pin down**: Screen from high to low — the high man screens the low man's defender. Can be diagonal or vertical.

**Pistol**: Signal for a specific play — typically a flare or jam screen by an inside player for a perimeter player who has just passed. Difficult to defend because it forces a quick decision between a big and small defender; an offensive slip can also be used after the back screen.

**Pop**: Offensive player fakes a cross screen or inside cut, then pops back toward the ball to receive a pass.

**Power / Punch**: Offense signals a pass inside to a player on the low post.
