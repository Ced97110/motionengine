# Tuck Jump

## Objective
Build explosive vertical jumping power and reactive landing mechanics through repeated bilateral tuck jumps, improving players' ability to jump for rebounds, block shots, and perform layups.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Open floor space (no equipment required)
- Flat, non-slip surface
- Establish squat strength before attempting (can squat with good form)

## Execution
1. Stand erect with feet **slightly wider than shoulder-width apart**. Arms at sides.
2. Flex the knees, hips, and trunk — slowly descend toward the floor — then **immediately jump vertically** by quickly extending the body while **simultaneously throwing the arms upward**.
3. At the peak of the jump, **bring both knees up toward the chest** (hips and knees should be at 90-degree angles). Wrap both arms around the knees at the top.
4. **Release the arms** as the body extends for landing. **Land softly** — give way by flexing the knees, hips, and trunk to reduce impact forces.
5. **Immediately repeat** the jump by flexing the knees, hips, and trunk again — minimize time on the ground.
6. Perform for the prescribed time or number of repetitions. [S2, p.108]

## Coaching Points
- **"Soft landing, quick takeoff"** — absorb the landing through bent knees, then explode back up
- Knees should be at **90 degrees** at the top of the jump — partial tucks reduce the training stimulus
- The arm swing is part of the exercise — arms help generate upward momentum
- Do NOT allow stiff-legged landings — watch for locked knees on ground contact
- Minimize time on the ground — the amortization phase should be as brief as possible
- Beginners: allow 1–2 seconds between jumps before progressing to reactive (immediate) repetitions

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Tuck jumps with a 2-second pause on landing — focus on landing mechanics and knee height
2. **Intermediate**: Reactive tuck jumps (immediate takeoff on landing) — minimize ground contact time
3. **Advanced**: Add a reactive element — e.g., tuck jump off a box landing, or tuck jump into a basketball action (pivot, catch, or shot)

## Muscles Involved
**Primary at takeoff**: Gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, quadriceps (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus intermedius, vastus medius)

**Primary at landing**: Gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, quadriceps (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus intermedius, vastus medius), biceps femoris, semimembranosus, semitendinosus [S2, p.109]

## Basketball Focus
The tuck jump directly improves the ability to **jump higher and react more quickly** when contesting for a rebound. Jumping ability is critical in multiple basketball scenarios: going for a layup, blocking a shot, slam dunks. Strong quadriceps, hamstrings, and gastrocnemius muscles = higher jumps. The basic tuck jump builds both the strength and the reactive capacity to perform repeatedly throughout a game. [S2, p.109]
