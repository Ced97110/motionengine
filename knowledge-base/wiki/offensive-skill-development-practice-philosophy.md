# Offensive Skill Development Practice Philosophy

## Summary
Coach Shawn Nelson's core practice philosophy holds that *practicing with a purpose* at game speed is the single most effective way to develop offensive skills. Many players spend hours in the gym without improving because they lack intentionality. Once purposeful practice becomes an ingrained habit, a player's skills will reach another level. Nelson couples this with a goal-setting system — players chart every workout to monitor progress and continually raise their targets.

This philosophy is the foundation for all 100+ workouts in Nelson's system. Without it, the technical drills are just random repetition. With it, every rep becomes a purposeful step toward a measurable goal. [S3, pp.8-10]

## When to Use
- Every individual workout session — beginner through elite
- When introducing a new skill to a player who struggles to translate practice reps into game performance
- When a player has plateaued and doesn't understand why they are not improving
- At the start of an off-season development program to establish habits and goals

## Key Principles
1. **Practice with a purpose** — Every rep must have a defined reason; aimless shooting or dribbling produces minimal gains.
2. **Game speed always** — "The best way to improve skills is to practice at game speed all the time." Practicing below game speed prepares you for a game that never happens. [S3, p.9]
3. **No shortcuts** — Success must be earned through hard work and sound practice concepts; players who make excuses do not improve.
4. **Video feedback** — Players should record their live-ball moves and compare them to a model to get a visual read on where they are in the learning process.
5. **Set realistic, progressive goals** — Use workout charts to track FGA, FGM, 3PTA, 3PTM, FTA, and FTM. Once a goal is reached, raise the standard.
6. **Identify motivation** — A player's reason for practicing (earning a starting spot, making the team, earning a scholarship) must be channeled to maintain focus through difficult workouts.
7. **Modify and personalize** — Players should choose workouts specific to their individual needs and feel free to modify them.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Arrive with a defined goal for the session. Track makes and attempts. Execute every rep at game speed. Review video of your moves. Re-evaluate goals after each training block.

## Variations
### Coach-Monitored Tracking
The coach uses the workout charts to monitor each player's progress across the season, identifying who is improving and who is plateauing.

### Player-Owned Tracking
Each player maintains their own workout log, tracking shooting percentages and comparing them to Nelson's target benchmarks (e.g., 60% FG, 85% FT).

## Common Mistakes
1. **Shooting at half-speed** → Force full game-speed execution on every rep; slow practice creates slow habits.
2. **No targets or goals** → Set specific numerical goals (e.g., "60% from the field this workout") before starting.
3. **Ignoring the charts** → Tracking performance is not optional — data reveals trends and keeps players accountable.
4. **Same workout every day** → Rotate and progress workouts to address all skill areas, not just comfortable ones.
