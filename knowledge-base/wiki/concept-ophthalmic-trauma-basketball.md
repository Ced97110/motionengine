# Ophthalmic Trauma in Basketball

## Summary
Basketball is one of the most common causes of sports-related ocular injury in the United States, accounting for more than 20% of sports-related eye injuries evaluated in emergency departments. Men's basketball has the highest event rate per minutes played for ocular injury of any collegiate sport. The vast majority (>97%) of basketball-related ocular injuries result in no permanent visual impairment — most are benign — but a systematic sideline assessment is essential to identify the minority of cases requiring urgent ophthalmologist consultation.

The most important coaching/training staff takeaway: when in doubt about any ocular injury, defer to an ophthalmologist. Protective eyewear is highly effective but dramatically underutilized. [S10, pp.239-249]

## Epidemiology
- Basketball accounts for >20% of all sports-related ocular injuries evaluated in US EDs [S10, p.239]
- Men's basketball has the highest ocular injury event rate per minutes played of any collegiate sport; women's basketball is second [S10, p.239]
- Ocular injury is ~3.5× more likely in competition than in practice [S10, p.240]
- >75% of injuries are sustained by males; most occur in individuals under 25 [S10, p.239]
- In professional basketball (NBA), eye injuries represent >5% of all injuries; eyelid abrasions/lacerations = >50% of ocular injuries; corneal abrasion = most common injury to the eye itself [S10, p.240]
- Most common injury mechanism: inadvertent player-to-player contact (finger poke to eye) [S10, p.239]
- Most common injuries: corneal abrasion and subconjunctival hemorrhage; followed by lacerations, hyphema, traumatic iritis, and ocular surface foreign body [S10, p.240]
- >96% of NBA ocular injuries occur in players NOT wearing protective eyewear [S10, p.240]

## 5-Point Sideline Ocular Assessment

All sideline ocular assessments by non-ophthalmologist staff should include these five components. **Abnormalities on any component require ophthalmologist consultation.** If all five are normal, most athletes can return to play immediately. [S10, p.241]

1. **History**
   - Pre-existing ocular conditions, prior injuries or surgeries, current eye medications
   - Present illness: mechanism (finger/elbow to eye), visual acuity changes, eye pain, double vision, flashes of light or floaters, loss of visual field ("curtain" over vision)

2. **Visual acuity testing**
   - Snellen chart at distance or hand-held near card
   - Test each eye separately with the other eye occluded

3. **Pupillary light response**
   - Use penlight or flashlight
   - Assess: round pupil shape, direct and consensual responses, relative afferent pupillary defects
   - *Abnormal pupils → suspect optic nerve injury*

4. **Gross inspection of ocular surface and eyelids**
   - Look for lacerations or globe deformity
   - Topical anesthetic drops (proparacaine or tetracaine) may facilitate exam in uncomfortable patients

5. **Confrontational visual fields**
   - Each eye separately, other occluded
   - Compare to any pre-existing deficits
   - *Deficits → suspect retinal or optic nerve injury*

## General Return-to-Play Framework
Four questions to assess for ophthalmic injuries [S10, p.241]:
1. **Has an accurate diagnosis been established?** Courtside diagnosis often insufficient — slit-lamp not available sideline. Involve ophthalmologist for visual changes or abnormal exam.
2. **How high is the re-injury risk?** Hyphema and globe rupture = high risk; subconjunctival hemorrhage = minimal risk.
3. **Is protective equipment available?** Polycarbonate eyewear reliably prevents most common injuries from worsening.
4. **Does the athlete feel confident returning?** Common injuries may temporarily affect vision and performance confidence.

## Common Eye Injuries — Summary Table

| Injury | Key Features | Return to Play |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Corneal Abrasion | Epithelium scraped off; intense pain; blurred vision possible | Immediate if comfortable/acceptable acuity; topical anesthetic short-term only |
| Subconjunctival Hemorrhage | Blood between conjunctiva and sclera; benign; self-resolving in weeks | Immediate (unless 360° hemorrhage obscures deeper injury — then await ophthalmologist) |
| Ocular Surface Foreign Body | Eyelashes, contact lenses; acute irritation | Immediate if asymptomatic after removal; embedded corneal FB = contraindication |
| Eyelid Laceration | If eyelid involved → ophthalmologist consult; medial lacerations may involve tear drainage | Immediate after primary closure if superficial; case-specific for complex lacerations |
| Hyphema | Blood in anterior chamber; risk of glaucoma, corneal staining, rebleed | Minimum 10 days; rigid eye shield during that period; protective eyewear for 1 month |
| Traumatic Iritis | WBCs in anterior chamber; pain, redness, photophobia; delayed onset (up to 3 days) | Return once symptoms improve with topical steroids (~1–2 days) |
| Commotio Retinae | Retinal whitening after blunt trauma; peripheral = no symptoms; macular = blurred/permanent loss | Return if no visual impairment after thorough dilated exam |
| Ruptured Globe | Blunt or sharp; 360° subconjunctival hemorrhage, shallowed anterior chamber, peaked pupil | Absolute contraindication; prolonged recovery post-operative |
| Retinal Tear/Detachment | Floaters, flashes, "curtain" over vision; high myopia = risk factor | Absolute contraindication until ophthalmologist exam; retinal tear only → few days after laser; detachment → months |
| Orbital Fracture | Inferior/medial wall most common; pain with eye movement, double vision, cheek numbness | Most wait until healed: typically ≥1 month |

## Detailed Injury Notes

### Corneal Abrasion
- Fluorescein dye + blue light for diagnosis (stains denuded epithelium)
- Topical antibiotic drops until epithelium regrows (1–2 days)
- Topical anesthetics relieve pain but are toxic to epithelium — do not use chronically [S10, p.243]
- Bandage contact lenses decrease pain and can facilitate expedited RTP

### Hyphema
- Vision-threatening complications: trabecular meshwork clogging → elevated IOP → glaucoma; corneal blood staining; uveitis; rebleeding (risk persists up to 10 days)
- Management: rigid eye shield, topical steroids + cycloplegics, head-of-bed elevation, daily ophthalmologist exams with IOP checks
- 8-ball hyphema (entire anterior chamber filled with blood) = highest risk for endothelial staining and IOP spikes [S10, p.245]

### Ruptured Globe
- Do NOT manipulate the eye further — risk of prolapsing ocular contents
- Place rigid eye shield; aggressive pain/nausea control (avoid Valsalva)
- Orbital CT to rule out intraocular/intraorbital foreign body
- Operative repair within 24 hours; broad-spectrum antibiotics
- Endophthalmitis risk: ~5–12% in open globe injuries [S10, p.247]

### Retinal Tear vs Detachment
- Tear without detachment: laser retinopexy within 24–72 hours
- Retinal detachment: surgical repair (vitrectomy, scleral buckle, gas bubble, laser/cryo)
- High myopia is the primary predisposing factor for basketball-related retinal detachment [S10, p.248]

### Orbital Fracture
- Non-contrast CT with thin orbital cuts for diagnosis
- Muscle entrapment = urgent surgery (24–48 h) to prevent ischemia
- Do NOT blow nose (risk of orbital content prolapse through fracture)
- Oral antibiotics if concurrent sinus disease; tetanus prophylaxis for open wounds

## Prevention
- **Polycarbonate protective eyewear** is highly effective and endorsed by the American Academy of Pediatrics and American Academy of Ophthalmology for all youth basketball players, especially functionally monocular athletes
- Despite recommendations, use remains uncommon at both professional and amateur levels
- >96% of NBA ocular injuries occur without protective eyewear [S10, p.240]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Returning an athlete immediately after hyphema** → Wait minimum 10 days; rebleed risk is vision-threatening.
2. **Using topical anesthetics chronically for corneal abrasion pain** → Toxic to corneal epithelium; delays healing.
3. **Ignoring 360° subconjunctival hemorrhage** → May obscure underlying scleral rupture or open globe injury.
4. **Not everting the upper eyelid during foreign body removal** → Hidden FBs are common under the upper lid.
5. **Returning athlete to play with suspected globe rupture or retinal detachment** → Absolute contraindications.
