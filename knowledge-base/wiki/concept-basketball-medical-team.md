# Constructing a Basketball Medical Team

## Summary
A basketball medical team requires a multidisciplinary approach integrating physicians, athletic trainers, physical therapists, and medical specialists. The composition scales with team level and resources, but all programs should maintain minimum personnel and equipment to address acute and chronic injuries, emergencies, and pre-participation screening. Foot and ankle injuries alone account for ~40% of all basketball injuries, making specialist access essential [S10, pp.85-90].

## When to Use
- Pre-season: assemble and orient the full medical team
- Daily: athletic trainers and physical therapists provide continuous coverage
- Game day: team physician and ATC present for all practices and competitions
- Post-injury: specialist referral as indicated by injury type and severity

## Key Principles
1. **Multidisciplinary structure**: PCSM physicians, orthopedic surgeons, ATCs, physical therapists, and subspecialists must all have defined roles and clear communication channels
2. **Injury-site awareness**: Foot/ankle (~40%), head/neck/face (~13.6%), knee (~15%), and arm/hand (~9.6%) are the primary injury regions — staff the specialists accordingly [S10, p.85]
3. **Knee injuries = most lost time**: Although knee injuries (15%) are less frequent than ankle injuries, they account for the largest amount of lost playing time, especially surgical cases [S10, p.85]
4. **ACL risk reduction**: Improper landing mechanics increase ACL tear risk; a physical therapist or ATC can modify these mechanics without compromising performance [S10, p.85]
5. **Universal precautions**: All medical personnel must practice universal precautions against biohazardous material exposure at all times
6. **AED readiness**: Every provider must be trained on AED use and maintain annual refresher training — early defibrillation is the single most important factor in SCD survival [S10, p.88]
7. **Emergency Action Plan (EAP)**: Every venue must have a venue-specific EAP reviewed by all personnel before the season begins [S10, p.89]
8. **Travel preparedness**: Medical equipment must be carried in the cabin during team travel — not stored in luggage [S10, p.88]

## Personnel Roles

### Primary Care Sports Medicine (PCSM) Physician
- MD or DO with residency + 1-year PCSM fellowship
- Specializes in nonoperative orthopedic conditions and general athlete health
- Manages sprains, dislocations, fractures (acute), overuse injuries, concussions, nutrition, return-to-play decisions

### Orthopedic Sports Medicine Surgeon
- MD or DO with orthopedic surgery residency + 1-year sports medicine fellowship
- Manages surgical cases; most care is still nonoperative

### Certified Athletic Trainer (ATC)
- Bachelor's or master's from accredited ATC program
- Focus: prevention, examination, diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation of acute, subacute, and chronic conditions
- Administers and manages facilities; serves as the day-to-day point of contact for athletes

### Physical Therapist (PT)
- 3-year Doctor of Physical Therapy (DPT) program
- Musculoskeletal movement expert; prescribes exercise, hands-on treatment, education
- Crucial for recovery from surgeries and significant injuries and for maintaining peak in-season performance

### Subspecialists (as needed)
- Foot & ankle specialist (highest priority given 40% injury rate)
- Cardiologist (SCD screening, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy)
- Neurologist (concussion with prolonged symptoms)
- Ophthalmologist (eye injuries — 7,500/year in the US)
- Otorhinolaryngologist (nose, ear, throat injuries)
- Hand surgeon (complex wrist/hand/finger injuries)
- Dentist (dental trauma — 1-14% injury rate)
- Sports psychologist, nutritionist, chiropractor, massage therapist as resources allow

## Injury Epidemiology by Body Region
| Body Region | % of All Injuries | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Foot & Ankle | ~40% | Most common; orthopedic foot/ankle specialist recommended |
| Knee | ~15% | Least frequent but most lost playing time; ACL most severe |
| Head/Neck/Face | ~13.6% | Concussions, lacerations, eye, nose, dental injuries |
| Arm & Hand | ~9.6% | Finger PIP joint sprains/dislocations most common |

## Sideline and Event Equipment
- AED (mandatory at all practices and games)
- Spinal immobilization equipment
- Suture kits, skin glue, Steri-Strips, topical pro-coagulants/vasoconstrictors
- Avulsed tooth transport solution (Viaspan or Hank's Balanced Salt Solution)
- Eye protection supplies
- Basic imaging (X-ray at professional venues; portable ultrasound growing)

## Common Mistakes
1. **Storing medical equipment in team luggage** → Keep essential supplies in the cabin during travel
2. **Failing to practice the EAP before the season** → Orient all personnel to venue-specific EAP protocols before any competition
3. **No refresher AED/CPR training** → Skill retention deteriorates within months; annual refreshers are mandatory
4. **Inadequate dental injury response** → Delays in dental trauma treatment reduce tooth viability; avulsed teeth must be transported in specialized solution immediately
