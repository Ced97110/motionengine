# Underdog Risky Strategies

## Summary
When a team is an underdog, a consistent game plan is self-defeating — a consistently inferior team consistently loses. The [[concept-basketball-bell-curve-model]] proves mathematically that **increasing game variance** (the point spread standard deviation) improves an underdog's winning probability. Every tactic that widens the distribution of possible scores gives the underdog a better chance.

Conversely, when a team is a favorite, it wants to *reduce* variance by playing consistently. The same math that helps underdogs hurts consistent favorites [S16, pp.127-128].

## When to Use
- When facing a significantly better opponent in a single-elimination game or a series
- When losing at the end of games (press, shoot threes)
- When getting an *early lead* on a better team (slow the pace)
- After a time-out when choosing between defensive schemes without a clear preference

## Key Principles
1. **Variance is the underdog's friend.** A 9-point underdog can raise win probability from 15% to over 30% by shifting from a consistent strategy to high-risk/high-variance strategies [S16, p.132].
2. **Three strategies mechanically increase variance:**
   - **Pressing** → raises variance of points allowed AND removes offense-defense correlation (scores off turnovers) [S16, p.128]
   - **Shooting many threes** → raises variance of points scored (feast-or-famine) [S16, p.127]
   - **Slowing the pace** (when ahead) → raises variance of *both* ratings by reducing possessions [S16, p.128]
3. **"Fouls balance out" is the wrong frame.** Even without favoritism, random referee inconsistency increases variance and helps the underdog — underdogs actually benefit from imperfect officiating [S16, p.136].
4. **Don't use risky strategies blindly.** If the opponent has a clear advantage in the risky domain (e.g., elite outside shooters), giving them open threes is not a good risky strategy. Choose risks where the payoff is plausible [S16, p.129].
5. **Secondary risky strategies force the opponent to take risks.** Playing zone defense often causes opponents to shoot more threes and slow down — both increase variance without the underdog bearing the full risk directly [S16, p.128].
6. **Home/road rule of thumb:** At the end of a tight game, go for the *win* if on the road (underdog mindset regardless of score), go for the *tie* if at home [S16, p.129].

## Additional Risky Tactics
- Fronting the post (fishing for steals)
- Releasing guards early on defensive rebounds (seeking quick outlet)
- Sending guards to the offensive boards (second-chance opportunities)
- Playing oversized or undersized lineups

## Case Study: Florida State at North Carolina, 1992
Seminoles grabbed an early lead via Cassell/Ward threes, then:
1. Sat in a zone despite UNC having elite shooters (Donald Williams, Henrik Rodl)
2. Put ball in Cassell's hands every possession to drain the shot clock
3. Scored heavily off three-pointers
Result: one of only four wins against that UNC team all season [S16, p.128].

## Case Study: Cleveland Cavaliers vs. Chicago Bulls, 1997
Cavaliers slowed pace from 95 possessions to 78 possessions. Win probability improvement:
- Without slowdown: ~28%
- With slowdown: ~34%
- **+6 percentage points** from pace reduction alone [S16, p.128].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Being consistent as an underdog** → correction: consistency locks in the probability of losing; increase variance to create upsets
2. **Using press or three-heavy strategy as a 20-point favorite** → correction: high-risk strategies hurt favorites almost as much as they help underdogs; stay consistent and win the expected game
3. **Slowing pace when losing late** → correction: the pace-reduction strategy only works when you have an *early lead*; slowing while trailing produces fewer possessions to close the gap
