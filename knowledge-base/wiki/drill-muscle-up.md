# The Muscle-Up (Master Step)

## Objective
Demonstrate complete upper-body explosive power by transitioning from a hanging pull below the bar to a fully locked-out dip-press above it — combining strength, power, speed, balance, timing, and total-body coordination in a single movement.

## Setup
- 1 athlete
- Overhead horizontal bar
- **False grip required**: thumbs over the bar
- No assistance or momentum aids

## Execution
1. Grab the overhead bar with approximately shoulder-width grip, **shoulders pulled down tight** into the armpits.
2. Use a **false grip** — thumbs over (not under) the bar.
3. Build a **powerful swing kip** — the backswing must carry you significantly higher than a standard pull-up.
4. As you approach the peak of the backswing, **snap backwards with your arms**.
5. Simultaneously **clench glutes and posterior chain** to thrust your upper abs into the bar.
6. Keep **pulling back hard with the elbows** as you hurl your torso forward over the bar. **Keep legs raised** to assist the rotation.
7. **Rotate your wrists** as you reach the pullover position. The bar should be under your upper abs, head and chest over the bar, legs raised, **forearms as vertical struts**.
8. From this pullover position, **press yourself up by straightening your arms** (the dip-press). Look forwards with the head up as you press.
9. **Pause at the top** in the fully locked-out position above the bar.

## Coaching Points
- **"Unlike the vast majority of strength exercises it features a strong pull component, plus a strong push — add to these the need for speed, power, balance, timing, total-body coordination and a midsection of steel, and is it any wonder the muscle-up is one of the most jealously admired skills in all bodyweight training?"** [S9, p.231]
- **False grip is non-negotiable**: Without thumbs over the bar, the wrist rotation cannot occur and the torso cannot transition past the bar.
- **The dip-press is the sticking point**: Most athletes who can do bar pullovers can do a muscle-up — the only additional demand is pressing up about half as far as a regular dip. If this is the limiting factor, supplement with dip progressions [S9, p.232].
- **Relax grip at transition**: The grip must be powerful during the pull, then partially released at the top to allow forearm rotation. This is a paradox — train it consciously.
- **Head up on the press**: Looking forward helps the pressing phase by keeping the chest up and engaging the shoulder girdle properly.
- **Full prerequisite chain**: Athletes should master Steps 1–9 (swing kip → kipping pull-up → clap pull-up → chest pull-up → hip pull-up → jumping pullover → bar pullover) before attempting the full muscle-up.

## Progressions
1. **Prerequisite (Step 7)**: Hip pull-up — achieves the pulling height needed.
2. **Prerequisite (Step 8)**: Jumping pullover — learns the over-bar technique with leg assist.
3. **Prerequisite (Step 9)**: Bar pullover — full unassisted version.
4. **Master Step**: Full muscle-up with pause at the top.
5. **Beyond**: Strict muscle-up (no kip/swing), weighted muscle-up, consecutive reps.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using a regular grip** (thumbs under) → wrist rotation impossible at the top; use false grip exclusively during training.
2. **Trying to go straight over the bar vertically** → swing in a curve (behind the bar), not straight up.
3. **Weak dip-press** → build horizontal bar dip strength through the 10-step dip progression chain [S9, pp.238-249].
4. **Dropping legs during the pullover** → keep legs raised/tilted forward to counterbalance the torso rotating over.
5. **Gripping too tight at transition** → consciously relax forearm grip at the peak to enable wrist rotation.
