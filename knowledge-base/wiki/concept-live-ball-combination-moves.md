# Live-Ball Combination Offensive Moves

## Summary
Live-ball combination moves are sequences where a player uses 1–2 dribbles combined with a finishing footwork action — stride stop, step-through, big hop, hop back, or hop-back/roll-out — to create a shot off the live dribble. The word "combination" (Combos) refers to the fact that the move chains a dribble action with a specific footwork-and-shot finish, making these sequences harder to guard than simple pull-ups.

Mastering these moves bilaterally (both left and right) is essential for perimeter scorers and ball-handlers at every level. The structured workout system in this source escalates volume from 80 to 240 total shots per session and alternates free-throw clusters between sets to build game-speed conditioning and routine. [S3, pp.101–120]

## When to Use
- When driving off the live dribble and reading a defender's momentum to stop and shoot
- When the defense over-commits on dribble penetration (stride stop / big hop fires before help arrives)
- When a defender's feet are set and a step-through creates a lane to the rim
- When retreating to create space (hop back → three-pointer or midrange pull-up)
- When hop-back is contested and the player needs to roll out into a secondary dribble attack (roll-out counter)
- When the defense closes out hard on a hop back (crossover counter)

## Key Principles
1. **Every combo is bilateral** — practice left-hand and right-hand dribble initiation equally to avoid scouting tendencies.
2. **Progression by volume** — begin with 80-shot workouts, advance to 120 and then 240 as the moves become automatic.
3. **Intersperse free throws** — shooting FTs between combo sets builds composure and simulates game pressure; goal is 80–85% FT rate.
4. **Separation = shot** — do not attempt the shot until the footwork action has created genuine body separation from the imagined/real defender.
5. **Field goal standard** — target 60% FG on all combo workout sets; this is the benchmark that confirms technique is sound before increasing volume.
6. **Dominant and non-dominant** — (L) and (R) designations refer to the dribble hand initiating the move, not the pivot foot; train both to equal proficiency.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: primary user; must master all five footwork finishes off both hands to run pick-and-roll, isolation, and push-transition attacks
- **SG**: must master stride stop and big hop for catch-and-shoot conversions in motion offense
- **SF**: stride stop and hop back are essential for mid-post and short-corner scoring
- **PF**: big hop and step-through are critical for catching in the high post and driving baseline

## Variations

### Stride Stop Shot
Player takes 1 or 2 dribbles and performs a stride stop (two-foot simultaneous landing) to create a balanced, set-feet jump shot. Attacked in both directions. [S3, pp.101–106]

### Step-Through
After the stride stop, if the defender contests the shot, the player steps through (lead foot crosses the defender's body) for a layup or floater. Combines the stride stop read with a secondary finishing move. [S3, pp.107–115]

### Big Hop
Player gathers off 1 or 2 dribbles into a wide two-foot hop landing — wider than a stride stop — creating maximum body separation and a low, stable shooting base. Especially effective in the midrange. [S3, pp.116–121]

### Hop Back
Player hops backward off 1 dribble (to create space) and catches the ball behind the three-point line for a catch-and-shoot or midrange pull-up. The 1-dribble hop-back is primarily used for 3-point shots; the 2-dribble version is typically a midrange action. [S3, pp.122–132]

### Hop Back/Roll Out
The hop back is combined with a "roll out" dribble — if the defender stays with the hop back, the player rolls the ball forward (roll-out dribble) and attacks again off the live dribble. This creates a two-read sequence within one possession. [S3, pp.133–143]

### Hop Back/Roll Out/Crossover
An additional counter to the hop back: instead of rolling out straight, the player crosses the ball to the opposite hand mid-roll-out to attack the defender's new position. The most advanced of the live-ball combo series. [S3, pp.144+]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Same-hand dominance** → always train both (L) and (R) in equal reps in every session
2. **Shot before separation** → cue: "earn the shot with the footwork" — the stop or hop must be complete before the shooting motion begins
3. **Rushing through FT clusters** → FTs between sets are not a break; maintain full shooting routine on every free throw
4. **Skipping volume progression** → jumping directly to 240-shot workouts without building the 80- and 120-shot base leads to sloppy mechanics at full speed
5. **Flat hop back** → on the hop back, the player must gather with two feet and land in athletic position — a straight-back landing reduces shooting power and control
