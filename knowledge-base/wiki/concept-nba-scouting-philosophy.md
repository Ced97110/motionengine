# NBA Scouting Philosophy

## Summary
Professional basketball scouting is the systematic study of an opponent's offensive and defensive tendencies, play sets, and signals. The NBA scout's primary job is to build a comprehensive file on every opposing team's offensive actions and to decipher the specific calls (verbal and hand-signal) coaches use to communicate those actions to players on the floor.

According to the *Basketball Playbook 2* introduction, pro scouts typically know opponents' plays better than their own team's plays — with files containing thousands of cataloged actions. Coaches protect their calls by covering their faces on verbal signals and holding out suit jackets to obscure hand signals. The best scouts crack these anyway [S14, pp.VII–IX].

The philosophy of this scouting system is that **there are no secret plays**: "Every team knows the basic actions and sets of every other team" (Alvin Gentry, quoted in [S14, p.VIII]). Success comes from execution, adjustment, and fitting plays to personnel — not from secrecy.

## When to Use
- Pre-game preparation: organizing opponent tendencies into a usable scouting report
- In-game adjustment: identifying which play is being called from the bench and communicating to defenders
- Play design: reverse-engineering successful NBA actions for your own system
- Teaching moments: showing players why a play works by understanding the defensive problem it is designed to solve

## Key Principles
1. **Identify the primary focus player** — every NBA play is designed primarily to benefit one specific player. Knowing who that player is tells defenders where to focus help.
2. **Categorize by action type** — most teams file plays by category: post-ups, pick-and-rolls, baseline screening plays, early offense, 1-4 sets, combination plays, OOB actions. This enables rapid retrieval.
3. **Decipher the call** — the play call is the signal the coach uses (verbal or hand). Top scouts identify these; coaches protect them by covering faces and suit-jacket obscuring. Each team should assign their own calls to plays they adopt.
4. **No magic plays** — a good defense can stop any specific play. The art is constant adjustment: "The great coach is constantly tweaking plays to outmaneuver the defense" [S14, p.VIII].
5. **Apply at any level** — a team without a Michael Jordan still has someone who plays like Michael Jordan at their level. Pro plays translate when matched to relative personnel strengths.

## Structure of an NBA Scouting Report
1. **Offensive and defensive notes** — tendencies of the opponent
2. **Depth chart** — roster by position
3. **Call sheet** — signals for play actions (verbal and hand signals)
4. **Personnel section** — individual player strengths and weaknesses
5. **Playbook** — diagrams of each play, with primary focus player noted

## Common Mistakes
1. **Categorizing plays by name only** → Organize by action type (PnR, post, screen) — this is how defenses need to prepare.
2. **Ignoring the primary focus player** → A play without a noted target is harder to scout and defend.
3. **Treating plays as fixed** → Every play has multiple options; defenses must prepare for all reads, not just the primary action.
