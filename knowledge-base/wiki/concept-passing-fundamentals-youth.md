# Passing Fundamentals (Youth)

## Summary
Passing is identified as the most important basketball fundamental — Coach Adolph Rupp wrote this in 1948, the year he won both the NCAA championship and the Olympic gold medal. Despite its importance, passing has become a lost art in modern basketball. Coaches prize great passers because they elevate every teammate around them — John Stockton and Allen Iverson are cited as examples of elite passers who raised team quality through their distribution skills.

There are three basic passes: the **bounce pass**, the **chest pass**, and the **overhead pass**. Beyond these are the one-handed chest pass, one-handed bounce pass, overhead outlet pass, and baseball pass. Developing passing skill requires not only mechanical accuracy but also court vision (seeing the whole court at all times), touch (barely receiving before releasing), and timing (hitting the right teammate at exactly the right moment).

## When to Use
- **Chest pass**: Most common pass in the half-court; used when the receiver is 10–20 feet away and the lane is clear
- **Bounce pass**: Into the post, through a defender's hands, or whenever a chest pass is deflectable
- **Overhead pass**: When a defender is directly in front of the passer, or for skip passes
- **One-handed chest/bounce**: In traffic or when one arm is occupied
- **Overhead outlet pass**: After a defensive rebound to initiate the fast break
- **Baseball pass**: Long passes the full length of the court to start the fast break

## Key Principles
1. See the whole court at all times — develop full-court vision, not tunnel vision
2. Barely touch the reception before zipping off another pass — the best passers are in constant motion with the ball
3. Hit the correct teammate at exactly the right time — timing is as important as accuracy
4. Look one direction and pass another — deception is part of the art of passing
5. Fake a pass in one direction before tossing it another
6. Step toward the pass as a receiver — "step to the ball, watch it all the way into your hands"

## Chest Pass Mechanics
- Cup hands on both sides of the basketball using the pads (not palms)
- Thumbs point upward, palms face the ball; ball held at chest level
- Step forward and pass the ball toward the receiver's chest, flipping both wrists
- Thumbs finish pointing downward, palms face outward
- Receiver steps toward the pass to receive it, watching the ball all the way into the hands [S15, p.94]

## Bounce Pass Mechanics
- Same hand position and delivery as the chest pass
- Aim the ball at a point on the floor between passer and receiver
- From the bounce spot, the ball should hit the receiver between the beltline and the chest [S15, p.94]

## Overhead Pass Mechanics
- Hold the ball slightly overhead with elbows flexed, forearms perpendicular to the floor, upper arms parallel to the floor
- Cup both hands on each side but slightly behind the ball; both thumbs well behind the ball
- Flip with a slight wrist action — the ball is not thrown but flipped; arms move forward as the pass is delivered
- Thumbs finish pointing toward the receiver, palms pointing outward [S15, p.94]

## Overhead Outlet Pass Mechanics
- Differs from the overhead flip pass: hold ball completely overhead with almost no elbow flex
- Bring ball back toward the head as you prepare to deliver
- Make the pass strong by bringing arms violently forward; distance determines force needed
- Flip wrists as the throw is completed [S15, p.95]

## One-Handed Chest Pass Mechanics
- Place one hand on the side of the ball, the other behind the ball with a loose grip using the pads of the fingers
- With elbows flexed, push the ball with the hand behind the ball
- Follow through so both arms finish fully extended [S15, p.95]

## One-Handed Bounce Pass Mechanics
- Delivered exactly as the one-handed chest pass
- Pick a point on the floor so the carom reaches the receiver between the belt and the chest [S15, p.95]

## Baseball Pass Mechanics
- Turn sideways to the receiver; keep both hands on the ball as you pull it back beside the ear
- Non-throwing hand (left) below the ball for balance; throwing hand (right) behind the ball for thrust
- As the pass is delivered, the left hand drops; the ball is thrown like a baseball — straight forward, no wrist twist (twist creates a curveball)
- Follow through by stepping forward with the left foot as the ball is released [S15, p.95]

## Common Mistakes
1. Using the palms instead of the pads → loss of control and accuracy; cue "fingerpads, not palms"
2. Floating the bounce pass too deep → receiver gets it at the knees; cue "bounce spot closer to the receiver than to yourself"
3. Throwing overhead pass instead of flipping it → sailing pass; cue "wrist flip, not arm throw"
4. Baseball pass with a wrist twist → curveball pass; cue "straight through, no twist"
5. Receiver not stepping to the ball → easy deflection; cue "step to every pass"
