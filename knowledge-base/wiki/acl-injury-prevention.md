# ACL Injury Prevention for Basketball

## Summary
Basketball has the highest rate of ACL tears of any sport [S2, p.163]. The ACL (anterior cruciate ligament) connects the medial aspect of the lateral femoral condyle to the anterior medial tibial spine and limits internal rotation and forward translation of the tibia relative to the femur. ACL injuries are among the most debilitating in basketball — they typically cause at least one full lost season, carry a ~23% re-injury rate, and are associated with a 100% probability of developing knee osteoarthritis within 20 years regardless of surgical reconstruction. Prevention — or more accurately, **injury reduction** — is the best treatment.

Female players are 4-6× more likely to sustain ACL injuries than male players. High school athletes are more susceptible than college or professional players [S2, p.163]. The majority of ACL tears are **noncontact injuries** related to how an athlete moves, which means targeted training can meaningfully reduce risk.

## When to Use
- Year-round as part of every athlete's training program, not just during pre-season
- Especially critical for female players and high school athletes
- After reviewing movement patterns — coaches should watch for signs of the four neuromuscular imbalance patterns during practice
- As a bridge between physical therapy and return-to-sport training after any knee injury

## Key Principles

### ACL Injury Mechanism
1. ACL injuries most often result from a **combination of three simultaneous motions**: increased tibial external rotation + increased hip internal rotation/adduction + **dynamic knee valgus** [S2, p.164]
2. Any one motion in isolation typically would not injure the ACL — the combination is lethal
3. Dynamic valgus (knock-knee position during landing, cutting, or pivoting) is the primary visible danger signal
4. The highest-risk moment is **deceleration during a cut** — especially when unanticipated [S2, p.164]
5. "Think of this as injury reduction rather than prevention — you cannot prevent all tears, but you can reduce the number" [S2, p.164]

### Four Neuromuscular Imbalance Patterns
Research by Hewett, Myer, and Ford at Cincinnati Children's Hospital identified four patterns that predispose athletes to ACL injury [S2, p.165]:

| Pattern | Description | Danger Sign |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Ligament Dominance** | Relies on the ligament rather than muscles to stabilize the knee | Dynamic valgus (knock-knee on landing) |
| **Quadriceps Dominance** | Quads overpower hamstrings; poor posterior chain force absorption | Landing with stiff knees, no deep knee flexion |
| **Leg Dominance** | One leg significantly stronger/more coordinated than the other | Asymmetrical landings, favoring one side |
| **Trunk Dominance** | Poor trunk/core control leads to lateral trunk lean, increasing hip adduction forces | Trunk dropping to one side on single-leg tasks |

### Training Progression Principle
- Exercises progress from **stationary strength** → **dynamic power with technique emphasis**
- No one-size-fits-all program; advance based on individual athlete performance [S2, p.165]
- A qualified professional should provide critical feedback, especially for high-risk athletes

## Exercise Map by Imbalance Pattern

### Pattern I — Ligament Dominance (Dynamic Valgus)
Goal: Strengthen lateral hip muscles to prevent knee collapse
- [[drill-band-squat]] — Band squat activating gluteus medius/minimus
- [[drill-band-defensive-slide]] — Band defensive slide mimicking on-court lateral movement
- [[drill-side-lying-clam]] — Side-lying clam for gluteal and hip external rotator strength

### Pattern II — Quadriceps Dominance
Goal: Build posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, low back) to absorb landing forces
- [[drill-physioball-hamstring-curl]] — Bridge + hamstring curl on Physioball

### Pattern III — Leg Dominance
Goal: Equalize bilateral leg strength and coordination
- Single-leg squat off box
- Bulgarian split squat

### Pattern IV — Trunk Dominance
Goal: Improve core stability to control hip/trunk alignment during dynamic movement
- BOSU round double crunch
- Physioball lateral crunch

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: All players should perform ACL prehabilitation. The program is especially important for female players and any player returning from a lower extremity injury.
- **Coaches**: Watch for dynamic valgus (knees caving in) during rebounding landings, cuts, and change-of-direction drills. This is the primary visual indicator of ACL risk.

## Common Mistakes
1. Assuming only female players need ACL training → all players benefit; females just have amplified risk
2. Skipping injury prevention work when no injury exists → the program must be done before injury to be effective
3. Doing prevention exercises once pre-season and stopping → must be maintained year-round; the neuromuscular patterns require consistent reinforcement
4. Advancing too quickly from stationary to dynamic exercises → master static alignment before adding dynamic loading
5. Treating injury prevention as optional → "the best way to treat an injury is to never get injured in the first place" [S2, p.163]
