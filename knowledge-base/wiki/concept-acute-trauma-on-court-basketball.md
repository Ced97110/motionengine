# Immediate On-Court Acute Trauma Management in Basketball

## Summary
More than 500,000 basketball injuries are treated in US emergency rooms annually. Basketball is an inherently vertical, multidirectional sport requiring 70–250 jumps and direction changes every 2–3 seconds per game — 2–4 times more than soccer or volleyball — making on-court injury management a critical competency for all team health professionals. Injury rates are 8.5–1.1 per 1000 hours of athletic participation [S10, p.193].

The game has evolved from semi-contact to high contact, increasing injury severity. Lower limb injuries account for 46–68% of all basketball injuries, followed by head/neck (5.8–23.7%), upper limb (5.6–23.2%), and spine/pelvis (6–14.9%). Management must always prioritize: (1) acute life-saving intervention, (2) minimizing injury zone, (3) preventing further injury, (4) decreasing pain, (5) promoting healing, and (6) enabling safe return to competition.

FIBA rules allow a team doctor to enter the court without official permission when, in the doctor's judgment, the injured player requires immediate medical attention.

## When to Use
- Any acute trauma occurring during a basketball game or practice
- Applies to all healthcare professionals present at events regardless of experience level
- Decision framework applicable to high school, college, and professional settings

## Key Principles

### General Triage Principles
1. **Three-tier triage** — determine injuries requiring (a) return to play, (b) removal and stabilization, or (c) emergency hospital transfer [S10, p.194]
2. **ABCD primary survey** — always begin with Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability assessment regardless of injury site [S10, p.196]
3. **When in doubt, remove from play** — athlete health is paramount; case-by-case judgment for moderate injuries [S10, p.196]
4. **Emergency transfer indicators** — long bone fractures, open fractures, major joint dislocations, neurovascular compromise [S10, p.194-195]

### Extremity Trauma Assessment
1. **History first** — mechanism of trauma, severity of pain, ability to bear weight, functional deficits, instability, numbness, weakness [S10, p.194]
2. **Survey the whole extremity** — open injuries, gross deformities, major soft tissue swelling [S10, p.194]
3. **Active and passive ROM** — carefully evaluate range of motion at all adjacent joints [S10, p.194]
4. **Palpation for pain localization** — note crepitation and pathological motion [S10, p.194]
5. **Neurovascular exam** — pallor, paresthesia, pain, cold, diminished pulses = ischemia emergency [S10, p.194]
6. **Compartment syndrome awareness** — especially lower extremity after severe contusions or tibia fractures; key sign is pain out of proportion to injury and marked increase in pain during passive stretching [S10, p.194]
7. **Ottawa Rules** — use to determine need for radiography in knee and ankle injuries [S10, p.194]

### Ottawa Knee Rules (any one criterion = get X-ray)
- Patient ≥55 years old
- Isolated tenderness on patella
- Tenderness at head of fibula
- Inability to flex knee to 90°
- Inability to bear weight for four steps [S10, p.194]

### Ottawa Foot-Ankle Rules (any one criterion = get X-ray)
- Pain over lateral malleolus
- Pain over medial malleolus
- Pain over navicular bone
- Pain over base of fifth metatarsal
- Inability to bear weight for four steps [S10, p.194]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Players should communicate immediately with medical staff when injured — describe pain location, mechanism, whether they can bear weight, any numbness or weakness

## Variations

### Open Injuries and Lacerations
- Evaluate for extension to bone, joint, or neurovascular structures
- Irrigate with sterile saline to clean debris; mechanical debridement with sterile sponge if needed
- Apply antiseptic; small lacerations: tissue glue or sterile strips; larger: sutures
- Quick sideline management with stapler possible to keep player in game; revise wound after game [S10, p.195]

### Closed Fractures (Sideline Management)
- Focus: relieve pain by stabilizing, prevent further neurovascular injury
- Gentle longitudinal traction only if markedly displaced — align tissues only, do not aggressively reduce
- Apply splint including fracture site and adjacent joints
- Post-reduction neurovascular exam mandatory
- After traction/splinting: elevation and ice to decrease pain and inflammation [S10, p.195]
- Lower extremity long bone fractures: non-weight-bearing ambulation (stretcher, wheelchair, crutches) [S10, p.195]

### Joint Dislocations
- Finger and shoulder dislocations most common
- **Emergency transfer required for**: knee dislocations and hip dislocations (high incidence of neurovascular injury) [S10, p.195]
- Finger dislocations: experienced physician can perform sideline reduction; post-reduction stability assessment; buddy taping; possible return to play [S10, p.195]

### Ligament Sprains (Return-to-Play Framework)
- **Grade 1**: Stable, minimal pain → symptomatic care → return to play [S10, p.195]
- **Grade 2**: Mild to moderate laxity → challenging decision; test stability, tape/brace, court-side running/jumping/cutting drills before decision [S10, p.195]
- **Grade 3**: Marked laxity → remove from play; RICE protocol [S10, p.195]
- Special concern: Type 2 acromioclavicular sprains — difficult to protect/stabilize [S10, p.195]

### Muscle Contusions
- Ice and compression; generously pad area if player returns
- Severe quadriceps contusions: risk of heterotopic ossification; remove from play, rest in minimum muscular tension with extremity elevated [S10, p.196]

### Muscle Strains
- RICE protocol; do not allow return to play
- Lower extremity strains: crutches for weight-bearing [S10, p.196]

### Complete Tendon Ruptures
- Severe functional limitation; remove from play immediately
- Immobilize joint the tendon crosses with splint [S10, p.196]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping neurovascular exam** → Always assess pulses, sensation, and motor function after any extremity injury
2. **Attempting aggressive reduction without experience** → Only experienced physicians should attempt reduction; do it gently
3. **Missing compartment syndrome** → Key sign is pain out of proportion; passive stretch pain; does not always present with ischemia signs
4. **Missing knee/hip dislocation** → These are medical emergencies — transfer immediately regardless of how benign they appear
5. **Letting Grade 2 sprain play without bracing** → Always test stability and compare to unaffected side before return to play
