# Complete Shooting Mechanics (Wissel)

## Summary
Hal Wissel presents a six-component sequential shooting mechanics system — Sight → Balance → Leg Motion → Hand Position → Elbow-In Alignment → Follow-Through — that builds a shot from the ground up. The system emphasizes that all parts must flow in a rhythmic sequence; good mechanics should not feel mechanical. Coaches should work on one or two mechanics at a time, never overwhelming a player with every detail. [S7, pp.6-8]

The system is particularly notable for its "block and tuck" hand position concept (shooting hand behind ball, balance hand under ball), the insistence on keeping the ball high rather than lowering it for rhythm, and the specific elbow-in alignment principle as the single structural key to a straight shot.

## When to Use
- Individual shooting instruction (break down and correct one element at a time)
- Pre-season mechanics review for all players
- Diagnosing persistent shooting errors (map error to specific component)
- Teaching shooting to beginners from scratch

## Key Principles

### 1. Sight
- Eyes focus just over the **front of the rim** for all shots except bank shots
- Bank shots: aim for the **top near corner of the box** on the backboard; optimal angle is 45° ("45-degree funnel" widens with distance from the basket)
- See the target as early as possible; maintain focus until ball reaches the goal
- **Never follow the flight of the ball or the defender's hand** — concentrating on the target eliminates distractions [S7, p.6]

### 2. Balance
- Feet shoulder-width apart, toes pointing straight ahead
- **Shooting-side foot forward** (right foot for right-handed shot); toe of back foot aligns with heel of front foot (toe-to-heel)
- Head positioned over waist and feet, slightly forward
- Knees flexed to provide power — players who don't flex knees compensate by throwing from behind the head or shoving from the hip
- Shoulders relaxed, inclined toward the basket [S7, p.7]

### 3. Leg Motion
- Start with knees slightly flexed; use a **down-and-up motion** — bend then fully extend
- Legs and shooting arm move together: "as the legs go up, the arm goes up"
- Say "down and up" as a trigger cue to synchronize the motion
- **Do not lower the ball for rhythm** — use leg motion instead; keeping ball high = quick release + fewer errors
- On catch: "down" comes just before the catch; shot goes up as legs go up for a one-motion quick release [S7, p.7]

### 4. Hand Position — The Block and Tuck
- **Shooting hand** directly behind the ball, high, facing the rim; index finger at the midpoint of the ball
- **Balance (nonshooting) hand** slightly under the ball; weight rests on ring finger and little finger; elbow points slightly back and to the side
- Ball contacts **finger pads, not the palm** — relaxed hand forms a natural cup
- Thumb of shooting hand is relaxed, not spread wide (prevents forearm tension)
- Hold the ball between the ear and shoulder on the shooting-side
- This "block and tuck" frees the shooting hand to shoot rather than balance-and-shoot simultaneously [S7, p.7]

### 5. Elbow-In Alignment
- Keep the shooting elbow tucked in and aligned under the ball toward the basket
- When elbow is in, ball is aligned with the basket — this is the structural key to a straight shot
- Players with limited flexibility: first position shooting hand behind ball facing front, then move elbow in as far as flexibility allows [S7, p.8]

### 6. Follow-Through
- After releasing off the index finger: keep shooting arm **up and fully extended**, index finger pointing straight to target
- Palm of shooting hand faces slightly down; palm of balance hand faces slightly up
- Keep eyes on the target
- **Hold the arm up** until the ball reaches the basket, then react (rebound or defense) [S7, p.8]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Own the complete mechanics sequence. In practice, isolate one component at a time. In games, let mechanics be automatic and focus only on target and rhythm.

## Variations
### Inside Jump Shot
- Jump first, then shoot at the **top of the jump** — arm, wrist, and fingers provide most of the force
- When closely guarded: generate enough leg power to jump higher than the defender; align ball slightly higher

### Outside / Three-Point Jump Shot
- Lift ball **simultaneously** with upward leg extension — feel like you're shooting *as* you jump, not at the top
- More force from legs, back, and shoulder than an inside shot
- Set up behind the line; land in balance in the same spot
- Balance and control are more important than maximum jump height on 3PT shots [S7, p.11]

### Shooting Off the Catch
- **Pass from in front**: shooting hand behind ball facing rim, balance hand under ball
- **Pass from strong-hand side**: balance hand blocks, then shooting hand set behind; reset balance hand under ball
- **Pass from weak-hand side**: shooting hand blocks, balance hand tucked under; reset shooting hand behind
- Block-and-tuck on the catch — never rotate ball in from the sides (causes sidespin) [S7, p.12]

### Shooting Off the Dribble
- Dribble to front of shooting knee; pick ball up with **shooting hand on top, balance hand under**
- Jump behind the ball on the last dribble
- Never reach to the side for the ball
- Strong-hand side: long dribble with outside hand past defender, push off pivot foot, pick up in front of shooting knee
- Weak-hand side: crossover dribble to front of shooting-side knee, then same mechanics [S7, pp.12-13]

### Step-Back Jump Shot
- Read the defender; fake a drive, take a quick step back with outside foot while dribbling back with outside hand
- Jump behind the ball; pick up in front of shooting knee
- Exaggerate follow-through of shoulders, head, and shooting hand toward the basket to counter backward lean [S7, p.13]

### Shot Fake into Step-Through Jump Shot
- Make aggressive shot fake — "a shot fake is a shot not taken"
- Aggressively bring ball up over the forehead, knees stay flexed
- Read defender's hand; if hand is up on the ball side, step through with inside foot past defender's lead foot
- Move head and shoulders under the defender's arm; protect ball; expect contact and complete the shot [S7, pp.11, 14]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Lowering ball for rhythm** → Use down-and-up leg motion; keep ball high between ear and shoulder
2. **Elbow out** → Causes follow-through to go right (for right-handers); tuck elbow in under ball
3. **Sidespin on ball** → Caused by rotating ball in from sides at catch or pickup, or balance hand thumbing ball; use block-and-tuck method
4. **Floating forward/backward off the dribble** → Pick ball up in front of shooting knee with knees flexed to redirect momentum upward
5. **Eyes follow the ball flight** → Keep eyes locked on front of rim until ball reaches basket
6. **Shooting off palm** → Relax thumb; set ball on finger pads for backspin, control, and soft touch
7. **Shots hit right rim (right-hander)** → Elbow out or not square to basket; square up, elbow in
8. **Shots hit left rim (right-hander)** → Ball set too far right (hip); not enough leg power; shoving from right to left; square up and use legs
