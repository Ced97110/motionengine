# Fast-Break Execution

## Summary
Given the freedom-based approach of Karl and Moe's fast-break system, execution is governed by habits rather than rigid patterns. Two foundational habits — an instant mental switch from defense to offense, and proper spacing on the court — underpin every scoring opportunity created in transition. Players must react instinctively to each unique situation; pre-programmed movement patterns make life easier for the defense and reduce scoring chances. [S7, pp.153-156]

## When to Use
- Immediately after any change of possession: defensive rebound, steal, made basket by opponent (inbound), or turnover
- Any time the offense has a numerical advantage over the defense during transition

## Key Principles

### The Mental Switch
1. The first ingredient of an effective fast break is the **offensive transition mind-set** — rapidly switching from defense to offense within a split second
2. This switch must happen after: a basket, a defensive rebound, a steal, a turnover, a sideline/baseline inbounds play, or a made free throw
3. A slow inbound or a delayed outlet pass nearly always kills the fast break [S7, p.156]

### Proper Spacing
- Players must maintain proper spacing at all times during the break
- In a 2-on-1, if both offensive players are too close together, one defender can cover them both — the advantage is eliminated
- Wings must stay **as wide as possible** (near the sideline) until reaching the free-throw line extended on the offensive end
- The two trailers must be spread far enough apart that one defender cannot cover both simultaneously [S7, p.156]

## Player Responsibilities

### PG (1) — Starting Point
- Receives the outlet pass from the rebounder **as far from the defensive basket as possible**
- If heavy defensive pressure makes the long outlet impossible, the PG comes closer to the rebounder to create a clear passing lane
- Once the ball is in hand: dribble up the **middle** of the court (not the sideline — the sideline enables defenders to trap)
- Four options: penetrate to the basket, pass to an open wing, shoot a pull-up jumper, or launch a three-pointer on occasion
- The PG is the key decision-maker for the first scoring choice in the frontcourt [S7, p.153-154]

### Wings (2 and 3) — Winging It
- The two players closest to the wings when possession is gained sprint immediately to the free-throw line extended positions on opposite sides of the court
- Stay **wide** along the sideline — wider spacing = better passing lanes
- After reaching the free-throw line extended on the offensive end, read and react:
  - Cross in the lane (attacking the rim)
  - Stop at the free-throw line extended (set up for a jumper)
  - Play give-and-go with the PG
  - Take any other option the defense allows
- Ideally the 2 and 3 fill the wings, but the 4 or 5 can fill if they are faster/closer [S7, p.154]

### Trailers (4 and 5) — Support Troops
- Assuming 4 and 5 grab the majority of defensive rebounds, they are the last two players up the court
- 4 (rebounder): fills the lane between the PG and a wing on one side of the court
- 5 (deeper rebounder or second big): trails the PG, moving to a position near the top of the key
- Both trailers must be spread apart — one defender cannot cover both
- Stay alert and ready to alter their paths based on what develops in front of them [S7, p.155]

## Primary Break Decision-Making
- When the offense has a real numerical advantage (2v1, 3v2, etc.), defenders are outmanned and off balance
- The offense must exploit mismatches instinctively — no pre-programmed "always go here" rules
- The PG's read of the front defender determines whether to attack the lane, pass to a cutting wing, or hold for the trailer
- Trailers fill behind the break and present secondary scoring opportunities if the primary break is stopped

## Common Mistakes
1. **Delayed mental switch** → Practice the trigger situations (rebound, steal, etc.) so the reaction is automatic
2. **Wings pinching toward the middle** → "Stay wide" must be a daily coaching emphasis
3. **PG dribbling up the sideline** → The sideline is a trap; always attack the middle of the court
4. **Trailers stacking on the same side** → 4 and 5 must consciously spread; check their spacing as they run
5. **PG holding for the perfect pass** → React to the defense; if a wing is open early, throw it immediately
