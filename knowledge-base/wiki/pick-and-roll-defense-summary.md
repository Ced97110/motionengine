# Pick-and-Roll Defense — Summary of Strategy

## Summary
Herb Brown's foundational pick-and-roll defensive philosophy centers on one core principle: **force the dribbler away from the goal and stop penetration**. The screener's defender carries enormous responsibility in every pick-and-roll situation. The dribbler's defender must be physical and fight over every screen while the screener's defender contacts, shows, and steps out to prevent ball reversal.

Defending the two-man pick and roll is described as "probably the most difficult defensive maneuver your players have to contend with" and requires constant daily practice to master. [S1, pp.127-128]

## When to Use
- Every possession where an on-ball screen is set
- Wing, high, elbow, transition drag, and step-up pick-and-roll locations
- Staggered double-high and elbow-staggered screen situations
- Short-clock and late-game situations (switch is preferred)

## Key Principles
1. **Force the dribbler away from the goal** — the entire defense is designed to push the ball handler off their preferred driving lane
2. **Stop penetration first** — the team's collective mindset must be penetration prevention above all else
3. **Dribbler's defender: belly/body up** — make yourself thin, fight over the screen physically
4. **Screener's defender: contact show and step out** — stop the ball from being reversed to the other side of the floor
5. **Rotate by ball flight** — if ball is kicked ahead to the weak side, recover to your own man; if ball stays on strong side or returns to screener, rotate early and aggressively from the closest weak-side big man
6. **Wing/side PnR: aggressively show or soft double-team** — sustain until dribble penetration is stopped, then rotate
7. **Staggered drag PnR: second screener stays connected** — must be able to stop or trap dribble penetration; first screener can let defender through and shrink to middle to pick up the roll man

## Player Responsibilities
- **Ball-handler's defender (X1/X2)**: Be physical, belly up to the ball carrier, fight to get over the screen by making yourself thinner. Force dribbler away from the basket.
- **Screener's defender (X4/X5)**: Contact show and step out to stop ball reversal. In staggered situations, the second screener's defender stays connected to stop/trap penetration. The first screener's defender can let the ball-handler's defender through, then shrink to the lane for the roll man.
- **Weak-side big (closest)**: When ball stays strong side or returns to screener, rotate early and aggressively.
- **Perimeter help defenders**: Rotate according to ball flight — recover to own men on skip passes to weak side.

## Variations
### Wing/Side PnR — Aggressive Show or Soft Double
Many teams aggressively show or softly double-team the ball until penetration is stopped, then rotate by ball flight. [S1, p.127]

### Force Sideline — Baseline Trap Rotation
Force the wing pick-and-roll toward the sideline to initiate a baseline trap with rotations (especially when the screener pops). Defenders rotate on skipped passes. [S1, p.127; Figures 8.33-8.34]

### Transition Drag Screen
Big defending a small guard fights over the screen. [S1, p.128; Figure 8.37]

### Step-Up PnR
Switch similar-sized players (e.g., 3 and 1) in step-up situations. [S1, p.128; Figure 8.38]

### High PnR with Strong Side Empty
If X4 cannot get back to 4 popping out, X5 must sprint and pre-rotate. Can also immediately tilt or shadow X5. [S1, p.128; Figure 8.36]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Screener's defender chasing the roll man too early** → stay connected until the dribble is contained, then react
2. **Ball-handler's defender going around the screen** → must fight over the top; "make yourself thinner"
3. **Rotating to weak side too soon on a kick pass** → wait to see ball flight — recover to own man on a true skip
4. **Screener's defender failing to contact show** → ball reversal opens the floor; must step out physically
