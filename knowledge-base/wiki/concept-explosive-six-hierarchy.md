# The Explosive Six Hierarchy

## Summary
Paul Wade's *Explosive Calisthenics* organizes all explosive bodyweight training around six movement families, arranged in a two-tier hierarchy. The bottom tier — **jumps** and **power push-ups** — are foundational *power* movements that every athlete must develop before attempting the upper tier. The upper tier — **kip-ups**, **front flips**, **back flips**, and **muscle-ups** — are *skill-agility* movements that require the raw power built in the lower tier as a prerequisite [S9, pp.1-3].

For basketball athletes, the Explosive Six provides a complete bodyweight framework for developing the leg spring, midsection snap, and upper-body power needed for elite court athleticism.

## When to Use
- All athletes should begin with the power tier (jumps + power push-ups) and only progress to the skill tier once adequate power is established
- Basketball players can treat jumps and power push-ups as year-round power maintenance tools
- Skill-tier movements (kip-ups, flips) are relevant for advanced athletes seeking total-body agility and neuromuscular coordination

## Key Principles

### The Two Tiers

**Power Tier (Foundation)**
1. **Jumps** — Build leg spring, vertical power, ankle/knee conditioning. The cornerstone of lower-body explosiveness. Basketball superstars work jump progressions daily for years [S9, p.3]
2. **Power Push-Ups** — Build shoulder, arm, and upper-body explosive power. Also condition the bones and soft tissues of the upper body to handle high-velocity forces [S9, p.4]

**Skill Tier (Built on Power Foundation)**
3. **Kip-Ups** — Require leg spring, midsection snap, and shoulder/arm power. A pure skill movement requiring significant neural coordination
4. **Front Flips** — Require leg launch power, midsection tuck, and rotational skill
5. **Back Flips** — Same power requirements as front flips; typically considered highest agility demand
6. **Muscle-Ups** — Require pulling power and shoulder coordination; sits somewhere between pure power and skill [S9, p.1]

### The Fuel Tank Analogy
"If the skill-agility exercises are your racing vehicles, think of the basic power moves as your fuel tank. The more consistently you train with 'em, the bigger and more turbocharged your vehicle." [S9, p.3]

### Three Power Prerequisites for Skill Work
To safely and effectively attempt kip-ups, flips, and muscle-ups, an athlete must first develop:
1. **Leg spring** — the ability to launch the body high enough
2. **Midsection snap** — core power to tuck the legs
3. **Shoulder and arm power** — to increase momentum and push off the floor [S9, p.3]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Train jump progressions and power push-up progressions consistently before attempting kip-ups or flips. Treat these as career-long tools, not steps to check off.

## Variations
### Within Each Chain
Each of the six movement families has 10 progressive steps from beginner to master. Athletes work through steps gradually, seeking linear improvement at each level rather than rushing to the top step.

### Combined Training
Most sessions should include both tiers: power work (Rule of Three/Six) first, then skill work (time surfing/consolidation training). Power work is the backbone; skill work expresses that power in coordinated patterns.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Attempting flips or kip-ups without power base** → Leads to injury (twisted knees, sprained ankles, broken forearms) because bones and soft tissues aren't conditioned [S9, p.4]
2. **"Beating" a step by performing it once** → True mastery means continually improving at each level, not just crossing it off a list [S9, p.3]
3. **Neglecting the upper-body power tier** → Power push-up progressions are equally important to jump progressions for total explosive development
