# Agility Training

## Summary
Agility is the ability to change direction rapidly and accurately, maintaining control of body position throughout. In basketball, agility is expressed in every defensive slide, every cut to the basket, every closeout, and every change-of-direction drive off the dribble. Agility training ranges from **planned** (predetermined movement patterns like agility ladder drills) to **reactive** (responding to unpredictable stimuli like defender movement or coach commands). Both types must be trained for basketball players. [S8, pp.214-233]

Agility depends on: (1) speed of movement, (2) deceleration ability, (3) re-acceleration ability, (4) body control during direction change, and (5) reaction time to stimuli. Strength and plyometric training build the physical capacity; agility drills refine the coordination and movement skill.

## When to Use
- During the body of every SAQ training session
- After dynamic warm-up is complete and athlete is physiologically ready for high-intensity work
- Progress from simple single-direction patterns → complex multi-direction → reactive agility with unpredictable stimuli
- Year-round with intensity/volume periodized across the training cycle

## Key Principles
1. **Master planned patterns before adding reaction**: Athlete must execute the movement correctly before adding a cognitive load [S8, p.224]
2. **Specificity**: Basketball agility patterns — lateral slides, drop steps, 45° cuts, V-cuts — should dominate the drill menu; not just linear speed drills [S8, p.224]
3. **Deceleration = the other half of agility**: Training acceleration without deceleration creates injury risk and poor direction-change mechanics; "stop fast to go fast"
4. **Work-to-rest**: Use 1:3 to 1:4 work-to-rest ratio for quality agility work; reduce sets rather than reducing rest to maintain quality [S8, p.225]
5. **Progressive overload**: Move from simple to complex, slow to fast, low to high intensity across the mesocycle
6. **Combine with sport-specific skills**: Catching a pass, taking a dribble, or shooting at the end of an agility pattern forces basketball-specific transfer [S8, p.233]

## Movement Pattern Progression

### Level 1: Linear Patterns
- Straight-line sprints, backpedals, A-March walks, A-skips

### Level 2: Basic Direction Changes
- 20-yard shuttle, T-drill, lateral shuffles, carioca

### Level 3: Complex Multi-Direction
- Zigzag runs, Z-pattern cuts, X-pattern multiskill, hexagon drill

### Level 4: Reactive Agility
- All ladder patterns with coach visual/auditory commands [S8, pp.216-222]
- Sprint and Cut on Command
- Backward Icky Shuffle with Reaction

### Level 5: Integrated Sport Skill
- Hop-Scotch drill to catch a pass
- One-Leg Hop to dribble and lay-up
- Sprint-to-Vertical Jump [S8, p.233]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using agility drills as conditioning** → sloppiness during fatigue reinforces poor movement patterns; use for skill development when fresh
2. **Only training in one plane** → basketball requires lateral, diagonal, and rotational agility — train all planes
3. **Skipping deceleration training** → ACL risk increases when athletes can accelerate but cannot decelerate safely
4. **No reaction component** → game agility is always reactive; pre-planned patterns alone don't fully transfer
