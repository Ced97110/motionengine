# The Sikma Inside Pivot (and Square-Up) Move

## Summary
The Sikma inside pivot move — named for an elite post player, who developed it during his pro career with the Seattle Supersonics — is a post technique where the free foot makes a 180° **reverse pivot toward the basket and the defender**, squaring the post player up to face both simultaneously. Unlike the [[concept-post-front-outside-pivot|front outside pivot]], which pivots away from the basket first, the reverse-pivot post move goes directly toward the basket and the defender, creating separation as the post player spins toward the defense [S6, pp.197-199].

This move is also used by perimeter players to create separation between the ball handler and the ball defender [S6, p.198].

## When to Use
- Same situations as the front outside pivot: when the defender is not making physical contact and cannot be clearly read by feel
- When the post player prefers to create immediate separation toward the defender's body
- On both sides of the court, with either foot as the initial pivot foot
- Used by both post players and perimeter players to create separation in general

## Key Principles
1. **Same universal catch routine** — Two-foot hop, chin the ball, elbows extended, look over top shoulder [S6, p.198].
2. **Reverse pivot toward the basket** — With back originally toward the basket, a reverse pivot (swinging the free foot toward the basket) sends the free foot both toward the basket AND toward the defender simultaneously [S6, p.198].
3. **Free foot steps toward the basket, not laterally** — The swing creates space/separation while landing the post player in a direct face-up position [S6, p.198].
4. **Either foot can be the pivot foot** — The choice determines whether the square-up is above or below the block:
   - **Top (high) foot as pivot**: free foot (lower) swings to face up above the block — preferred position
   - **Lower (baseline) foot as pivot**: free foot (high) swings to face up — may result in squaring up below the block, reducing scoring angles [S6, p.199]
5. **Correct for below-the-block positioning** — If the reverse-pivot post move consistently places the post player below the block, simply move the initial post-up position higher on the lane line [S6, p.199].
6. **Dribble not yet used** — After the square-up, the post player has a full live dribble and can use the same attack options as the front outside pivot: front crossover pivot, blast move, jump shot, or drive.

## Footwork Detail — Top Foot as Pivot (Left Side of Lane)
- Post player on left side of lane, back to basket, back to X4
- Pivot foot = top (left) foot
- Free foot = right foot
- 180° reverse pivot: right foot swings toward the basket AND the defender
- End position: square-up facing basket, left foot now the baseline foot (pivot), right foot is the new high foot

## Footwork Detail — Lower Foot as Pivot (Left Side of Lane)
- Same starting position
- Pivot foot = lower (right) foot
- Free foot = left foot
- 180° reverse pivot: left foot swings toward the basket and defender
- End position: square-up facing basket, right foot (now baseline foot) as the new lower foot
- Caution: may result in squaring up below the block; adjust initial post-up depth accordingly

## Comparison to Front Outside Pivot
| Feature | Front Outside Pivot | Sikma Inside Pivot |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Initial pivot direction | Away from basket | Toward basket |
| Pivot type | Front pivot | Reverse pivot |
| Key benefit | Simpler rotation | Creates separation from defender |
| End position | Square-up facing basket | Square-up facing basket |
| Dribble used? | No | No |

## Historical Note
an elite post player played college basketball at Illinois Wesleyan University under Coach Dennis Bridges and started his pro career with the Seattle Supersonics. He effectively created this specific footwork technique, which became one of the most taught post-facing moves in basketball [S6, p.197].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Squaring up below the block** → Reduces scoring angles; correction: start the initial post-up position higher on the free-throw lane line.
2. **Confusing with the front outside pivot** → Coaches must clearly designate which direction the initial pivot goes; use "reverse toward the basket" as the cue for the reverse-pivot post move.
3. **Not completing a full 180°** → Post player ends up sideways; correction: free foot must complete the full swing to a true square-up.
