# Youth Basketball Practice Organization

## Summary
Before the season begins, the head coach must make two foundational decisions: (1) which parts of the motion offense to run, and (2) how much man-to-man defense to teach. Both decisions must be calibrated to the players — their age, experience, and athleticism. Once those decisions are made, the appropriate drills are selected and practice rules established. This framework, from Burrall and Patrick Paye's *Youth Basketball Drills*, is designed specifically for youth and middle/high school programs where maximizing active repetition time is the primary goal [S15, pp.337-339].

The system emphasizes: short drills (≤10 minutes each), small groups for maximum reps, competitive and fun formats, progressive complexity across the season, and clear coach-to-player communication protocols. The goal is that every player is always active and improving.

## When to Use
- Before the first practice of any season to establish the practice framework
- When adding assistant coaches (parent volunteers or sandlot coaches) who need clear roles
- When diagnosing practice inefficiency — players standing around, too many points per drill
- When planning weekly/monthly fundamental coverage to avoid gaps

## Key Principles
1. **Decide your system first** — Choose how many motion offense rules to implement based on player age and experience. Minimum of two rules (allows 1-on-1). Add cutting maneuvers, then screening maneuvers as the season progresses [S15, p.337].
2. **Decide your defense second** — Choose the depth of man-to-man defense: stance/footwork only → add help-side → add trapping as players improve [S15, p.337].
3. **Establish practice rules from Day 1** — Rule 1: one whistle = stop and stay in place; two whistles = meet at the predetermined end-court basket. Rule 2: no talking for players — only coaches talk [S15, p.337].
4. **Write a daily practice plan** — Every practice must be scripted in advance. Distribute copies to all assistants [S15, p.338].
5. **Keep drills short** — No drill should exceed 10 minutes; most should be shorter. Young minds wander when held too long to one task [S15, p.338].
6. **Minimize players per drill** — Fewer players per station = more repetitions per player = faster muscle memory development. Use assistant coaches to supervise multiple stations [S15, p.338].
7. **Make drills fun and competitive** — Balance: do a fun drill after every two learning drills. Winning itself becomes its own motivator once the team starts winning [S15, p.338].
8. **One or two teaching points per drill** — Players retain a few cues far better than many. Demonstrate every technique before execution [S15, p.338].
9. **Track fundamentals weekly/monthly** — Use a master checklist board with all 19 chapter/skill areas. If something was missed in a week, add it to the next plan [S15, p.338].
10. **Be enthusiastic** — "Like a common cold, enthusiasm is contagious. Spread it among your players" [S15, p.338].

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Follow the two-whistle communication system. No talking while coach is explaining.
- **Coaches/Assistants**: Each receives a daily practice plan copy. Parent or sandlot volunteers assigned to station supervision.

## Variations
### Youth League (< Middle School)
Recruit parent/guardian volunteers as on-floor assistant coaches. Coach explains all drills and assistants supervise station execution. Keep groups of 3-4 players maximum.

### Middle/High School
Use paid or experienced assistant coaches. Break the squad into small groups and run simultaneous stations. Cross-reference sandlot coaches who know the book's system.

### Short-Season or Limited Practice Time
Use the 30-minute sample schedules. Prioritize motion offense cuts and basic 1-on-1 defense in early season; add screening and help defense as time allows.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Too many players per drill** → station overcrowding reduces reps; split into smaller groups with more coaches
2. **Too many teaching points at once** → players remember none; limit to 1-2 cues per drill, demonstrate before executing
3. **Drills run too long** → young players lose focus; cap at 10 minutes, rotate to a related drill covering the same fundamental
4. **No written plan** → practice loses direction; always script in advance and distribute to all assistants
5. **Skipping a fundamental for weeks** → use the master checklist board to catch gaps before they become game-day problems
6. **Coach talks too much, players drill too little** → "Keep your talking to a minimum and the drilling to a maximum. People learn best by doing" [S15, p.338]
