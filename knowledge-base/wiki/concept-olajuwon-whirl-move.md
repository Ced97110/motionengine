# The Olajuwon Whirl Move

## Summary
The Olajuwon Whirl Move (named for Hakeem Olajuwon) is an advanced post scoring technique where, after catching the ball with a two-foot hop, the post player uses the **contact foot** (the foot nearest the defender) as the **free foot** and makes a 180° **front pivot** away from the defender toward the basket. This seals the defender with the post player's chest and side — rather than the back and legs as in the drop-step — and the player is immediately facing the basket after the pivot [S6, pp.191-193].

The key distinction from the drop-step is the pivot type and the sealing surface: the drop-step uses a reverse pivot and seals with the backside/legs; the Olajuwon move uses a front pivot and seals with the chest and side. Both moves begin from the same catch routine and both can attack the baseline or the middle [S6, p.192].

## When to Use
- When the defender is shading the post player on the high side (same read as a baseline drop-step, but executed as a front pivot)
- When the post player wants to face the basket immediately after the first pivot, rather than after a second square-up step
- When a quicker, more direct path to the basket is needed
- Can be used on either side of the lane, attacking the baseline or the middle

## Key Principles
1. **Same universal catch routine** — Two-foot simultaneous hop, chin the ball, look over the top shoulder to read the defender and scan for double-down defenders [S6, p.192].
2. **Contact foot is the FREE foot** — Opposite of the drop-step: in the Olajuwon move, the foot nearest the defender becomes the free foot that swings forward toward the basket [S6, p.192].
3. **Front pivot away from the defender** — Since the post player's back is toward the basket and the defender is also behind the post player, a "front pivot" (away from the basket in relation to the post player's facing direction) actually swings the player toward the basket and the defender simultaneously [S6, p.192].
4. **Step toward the basket, not laterally** — The free foot must step toward the basket, not sideways. The post player's body screens the defender, sealing with the chest and side [S6, p.192].
5. **Power dribble before lifting the pivot foot** — Advance the ball with a low, hard dribble landing just outside the free foot; determine whether to continue dribbling or kill the dribble [S6, p.193].
6. **Kill the dribble with a bunny hop** — If ending the dribble, execute a two-foot simultaneous landing (bunny hop) to again have the freedom of choosing either foot as the new pivot foot [S6, p.193].
7. **Finish at 45 degrees to the basket** — Same as the drop-step: the post player must shoot at a 45° angle (NOT squared up) to protect the ball and shot from helpside defenders rotating over [S6, p.193].

## Footwork Detail — Olajuwon Move Toward the Baseline (Defender High Side)
- Post player is on the right side of the lane, back to basket
- X4 is on the high side of 04
- Contact (right) foot becomes the free foot
- 180° front pivot: right foot swings toward the baseline and toward the basket
- 04 is now facing the basket with the baseline foot as the new pivot foot
- Finish: lay-up, baby hook, or turn-around jump shot

## Footwork Detail — Olajuwon Move Toward the Middle (Defender Low Side)
- Post player on the right side of the lane, back to basket
- X4 is on the low (baseline) side of 04
- Free foot = baseline foot (left foot on the right side of the lane)
- Front pivot: left foot swings completely around toward the middle of the lane and basket
- Pivot foot = right foot (inside/high foot)
- Finish: lay-up, baby hook, or front-pivot turn-around jump shot in the middle of the free-throw lane

## Comparison to Drop-Step
| Feature | Drop-Step | Olajuwon Move |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| Pivot type | Reverse pivot | Front pivot |
| Pivot foot | Top (high) foot | Opposite of contact foot |
| Free foot | Lower (baseline) foot | Contact (nearest defender) foot |
| Seal surface | Backside, back, and legs | Chest and side |
| Facing direction post-pivot | Back to basket initially | Facing basket immediately |
| Path to basket | Through defender's back | Around defender's front |

## Squaring Up After Arrival in the Lane
After either the drop-step or the Olajuwon move brings the post player to the middle of the free-throw lane, the post player may use a **square-up front pivot**: front pivot off the inside (right) heel and swing the left foot around to face the basket for a short jump shot. This is the same footwork a perimeter player uses after a dribble drive [S6, p.194].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stepping laterally instead of toward the basket** → The defender is not sealed and can recover; correction: the free foot must step toward the basket, not sideways.
2. **Squaring up on the power shot** → Exposes the ball to helpside defenders; correction: finish at 45° on all post power shots.
3. **Dribbling before reading** → Wastes the pivot foot options; correction: complete the chin-the-ball and read sequence before using the dribble.
4. **Dribble landing outside the free foot** → Risk of fumble or travel; correction: first power dribble should land just outside the free foot.
