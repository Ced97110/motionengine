# Leg Strength for Basketball

## Summary
Leg strength is the physical foundation of basketball performance. Every explosive action on the court — accelerating past a defender, jumping for a rebound, cutting to the basket, maintaining a defensive stance — depends on the ability of the legs, hips, and lower back to produce and absorb force. *Basketball Anatomy* presents a comprehensive leg-strength programme built around compound lifts (squats, deadlifts), unilateral exercises (lunges, step-downs), and resistance-band work (backward/lateral walks), each explicitly linked to on-court demands. [S2, pp.11-29]

The key insight is that the basketball court creates a continuous demand for force production *and* deceleration across multiple planes of movement. Training must therefore develop not just vertical (jumping) strength but also horizontal (acceleration) and lateral (sliding/cutting) strength, as well as the eccentric capacity to stop and change direction safely.

## When to Use
- **Off-season:** Foundation phase; build maximal strength through compound lifts at higher loads.
- **Pre-season:** Convert strength to power; pair lifts with plyometrics (contrast training).
- **In-season:** Maintenance phase; reduce volume but preserve intensity to maintain strength.
- **Injury prevention:** Band walks and unilateral exercises can be used year-round as activation and prehab work.

## Key Principles
1. **Squat as the foundation** — the front and back squat develop the full lower-extremity musculature for force application into the floor [S2, p.11].
2. **Posterior-chain priority** — deadlift and RDL build glutes and hamstrings for jumping power and hamstring injury prevention [S2, pp.14-17].
3. **Glute specificity** — the hip thrust directly loads the gluteal muscles for horizontal and vertical propulsion and safe landing mechanics [S2, pp.18-19].
4. **Unilateral work** — reverse lunge and reverse step-down from box address the single-leg nature of basketball movements (layups, cuts, defensive slides) and improve proprioception [S2, pp.20-23].
5. **Multi-plane strength** — band backward walk and band lateral walk develop quad/hip endurance and lateral hip abductor strength respectively, mirroring defensive positioning demands [S2, pp.24-27].
6. **Achilles complex** — calf pop-ups strengthen the gastrocnemius/soleus/Achilles unit for second-jump advantage in rebounding situations [S2, pp.28-29].
7. **Neuromuscular control** — knee alignment (no buckling) must be monitored throughout all lower-body exercises to prevent ACL and patellar injury.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions:** All five positions share the same fundamental leg-strength needs. Post players (PF, C) may emphasize hip thrust and squat for boxing-out and rebounding; guards (PG, SG) may prioritize band lateral walks and reverse step-down for lateral quickness and layup mechanics; wings (SF) benefit from all exercises equally.

## Variations
### Power-Focused (Jumping)
Emphasize: front squat, deadlift, hip thrust, calf pop-up. These develop the vertical force output chain.

### Lateral/Defensive Focused
Emphasize: band lateral walk, band backward walk, reverse lunge, reverse step-down. These develop lateral hip strength, defensive-stance endurance, and directional change.

### Injury Prevention / Prehab
Emphasize: Romanian deadlift (hamstring strength ratio), reverse step-down (single-leg control), band lateral walk (gluteus medius activation). Used as warm-up activation or supplemental volume.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Neglecting posterior chain** → prioritize deadlift and RDL alongside squats to balance anterior/posterior thigh strength ratios and reduce hamstring/ACL injury risk [S2, p.17].
2. **Skipping unilateral work** → bilateral lifts do not fully replicate single-leg demands of cutting and layups; add reverse lunge and step-down work [S2, pp.20-23].
3. **Ignoring lateral plane** → forward/backward strength without lateral hip strength leaves defensive slide mechanics undertrained; include band lateral walks [S2, pp.26-27].
4. **Rounding the back in deadlift/RDL** → ensure lumbar neutral; reduce load and coach the hinge pattern before progressing.
5. **Standing up in band walks** → the training stimulus depends on maintaining the quarter-squat position throughout.
