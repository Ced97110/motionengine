# Basketball Preseason Conditioning Program (Lee Rose)

## Summary
Lee Rose's preseason conditioning program is composed of three distinct phases: base building through interval training, agility and power training, and performance training. Developed from five years of personal statistical data on players by time and position, this system creates basketball-specific fitness through fast-twitch emphasis and anaerobic conditioning rather than the traditional long-distance running that characterized earlier basketball conditioning.

The system is grounded in the principle that basketball demands short, high-intensity bursts (anaerobic work) rather than sustained aerobic effort. Per the American Sports Medicine Institute (ASMI), anaerobic training is "activity that lasts from 30 seconds to 2 minutes" — this is the physiological window that must be trained [S13, pp.2-3].

## When to Use
- The 10 days before practice officially begins
- Preseason camp before organized team activities
- As a benchmark for measuring player fitness improvement day-to-day
- To identify players who are not in condition to practice without injury risk

## Key Principles
1. **Reduce distance, increase speed** — Long-distance running maintains aerobic fitness but does not develop the fast-twitch fibers basketball demands. Reduce the distance and push the pace.
2. **Interval training is the primary method** — Work-to-rest ratio of 1:2 (e.g., 1 minute work : 2 minutes rest). This trains anaerobic capacity, builds lung capacity, and speeds recovery without causing injury-producing fatigue.
3. **Three-phase structure** — Phase 1: base building via interval runs (M/W/F eighths, T/Th quarters). Phase 2: agility/power stations (defensive slides, sit-ups, burpees, speed jumping, rope jumping, rebound jumping). Phase 3: plyometrics depth jumping (T/Th).
4. **Position-graded time standards** — Guards and forwards begin at 44 seconds per 200m interval; centers begin at 45 seconds. Times decrease by 1 second each day across the 10-day program.
5. **Clock as motivator** — Use a visible clock set to 24 minutes; players race the clock, not teammates. The clock removes coach as disciplinarian.
6. **Accountability with penalty structure** — Missed times require makeup intervals with added time allowances (5 seconds for first makeup, 10 seconds for third attempt).
7. **Hydration is mandatory** — Players must have water available at all workouts, practices, and games.

## Program Structure

### Phase 1: Interval Base Building (10 days)
- **Monday, Wednesday, Friday**: Eight 200m (⅛ mile) intervals per player
- **Tuesday, Thursday**: Four 400m (¼ mile) intervals per player
- Groups: Guards → Forwards → Centers, staggered start times
- Clock begins at 24:00 and runs continuously; assistant coaches record all times
- Eighth-mile day stagger: guards at 24:00, forwards at 23:00, centers at 22:00
- Quarter-mile day stagger: guards at 24:00, forwards at 22:00, centers at 20:00

### Phase 1 Time Standards

**Week 1:**
| Position | Mon (200m) | Tue (400m) | Wed (200m) | Thu (400m) | Fri (200m) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Guards | :44 | 1:38 | :43 | 1:35 | :42 |
| Forwards | :44 | 1:38 | :43 | 1:35 | :43 |
| Centers | :45 | 1:42 | :44 | 1:41 | :44 |

**Week 2:**
| Position | Mon (200m) | Tue (400m) | Wed (200m) | Thu (400m) | Fri (200m) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Guards | :41 | 1:33 | :40 | 1:33 | :39 |
| Forwards | :42 | 1:33 | :41 | 1:33 | :40 |
| Centers | :44 | 1:40 | :43 | 1:40 | :42 |

### Phase 2: Agility and Power Stations (M/W/F, after intervals)
Six stations, 45 seconds each, 2-minute rest between stations:
1. Defensive slides (agility, lateral movement)
2. Sit-ups (core strength)
3. Burpees (full-body agility and conditioning)
4. Speed jumping (plyometric — rate of jump cycles)
5. Rope jumping (plyometric — ankle elasticity, timing)
6. Rebound jumping (plyometric — vertical jump endurance)

Team splits by position group (5 groups; one station always open per rotation). All groups start on the same whistle. Goal is set by the previous day's average repetitions.

### Phase 3: Plyometrics Depth Jumping (T/Th, after interval runs)
The entire Tuesday/Thursday post-run workout consists of depth jumping — stepping off a box and immediately jumping vertically upon ground contact, exploiting the natural elasticity of muscle tissue per Verhoshansky's training model [S13, p.5].

## Coaching Points
- "The objective of this conditioning is to get players in sufficient condition so that they can complete a two-hour practice without pulling a muscle" — this is not a boot-camp mentality
- Use discretion when players have pushed as hard as they can; conditioning is a means, not an end
- Players must touch the end lines with both hands on all turns
- A false start costs an extra interval
- All makeups completed before Phase 2 begins
- Times for big men (centers) should be set in consultation with the players, modified for age, weight, and speed

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using long-distance running for basketball conditioning** → Switch to interval training; basketball is an anaerobic sport requiring fast-twitch emphasis.
2. **No position differentiation in time standards** → Guards and centers have different physical profiles; use position-specific standards.
3. **Skipping the plyometrics phase** → The vertical jump and reactive strength built by depth jumping directly transfer to basketball rebounding, blocking, and finishing.
4. **No accountability system** → Without recorded times and penalty makeups, conditioning becomes voluntary and inconsistent.
