# In-Season Intensity Prescription Tools for Basketball

## Summary
Prescribing the correct training intensity during the competitive basketball season is one of the most complex challenges facing strength and conditioning professionals. Three validated tools help coaches make informed, individualized load decisions throughout the season: **Prilepin's Chart**, the **Relative Intensity Scale**, and the **Resistance Training-Specific RPE Scale**. Each tool addresses a different aspect of the intensity prescription problem — optimal rep ranges, volume-adjusted loading, and self-regulated daily readiness [S11, Ch.12].

Strength levels can fluctuate up to 18% day to day during a competitive season, making rigid percentage-based programming unreliable. These tools provide flexible frameworks that account for accumulated fatigue, playing time, travel, and recovery status.

## When to Use
- **Prilepin's Chart**: When programming power and explosive primary exercises; helps select optimal rep ranges for any given %1RM
- **Relative Intensity Scale**: When differentiated loading is needed within the same session (starters vs. bench players after a game)
- **RPE Scale**: In the later stages of the season when 1RM data is stale (determined 2-3 months earlier); also useful for velocity-based autoregulation

## Key Principles
1. **No single tool is sufficient**: Use all three in combination — Prilepin for rep range guidance, Relative Intensity for volume-load adjustment, RPE for daily autoregulation
2. **Familiarity is required for accurate RPE**: High school athletes with low training age may over- or under-rate effort; RPE is most reliable for college and professional athletes
3. **RPE is most accurate at heavier loads (>70% 1RM)**: At lighter loads (e.g., 15 reps @ 50%), athletes paradoxically report less difficulty than at 5 reps @ 90%, even though total work is higher
4. **Record keeping builds RPE accuracy**: Athletes who log resistance used and RPE for each session become more accurate self-assessors over time
5. **Velocity-based training complements all three tools**: Real-time bar velocity data provides external feedback that validates RPE and guides load selection throughout the in-season phase

## Prilepin's Chart

Original source: Laputin and Oleshko (1982). Provides guidance for power and explosive exercises specifically.

| % of 1RM | Optimal Reps/Set | Optimal Total Reps | Range of Total Reps |
|----------|------------------|--------------------|---------------------|
| 55-65% | 3-6 | 24 | 18-30 |
| 70-80% | 3-6 | 18 | 12-24 |
| 80-90% | 2-4 | 15 | 10-20 |
| >90% | 1-2 | 4 | 4-10 |

**Coaching Application**: At 76-85% (common preseason/in-season load), prescribe 3-4 reps per set. Total volume should stay at 15-18 reps across all sets. Example: 5×3 or 4×4.

Note: Total reps = sets × reps. At 55-65%: 8×3, 4×6, or 6×4 are all valid combinations.

## Relative Intensity Scale

Adjusts absolute load based on volume, producing a "difficulty level" rating. Especially useful in-season when starters and bench players are training together.

**Selected values from Table 12.2:**

| Difficulty Level | Relative Intensity | Example (5×2) |
|------------------|--------------------|----------------|
| Maximum | 100% | 100% 1RM for 1 rep |
| Heavy+ | 97.5% | — |
| Heavy | 90-92.5% | 88-88% 1RM |
| Moderate+ | 85-87.5% | 80.8-83.3% 1RM |
| Moderate | 80-82.5% | 76-78.5% 1RM |
| Light+ | 75-77.5% | 71.3-73.8% 1RM |
| Light | 70-72.5% | 66.5-69% 1RM |
| Too Light | 65-67.5% | 61.8-64.3% 1RM |

**Practical Example** (5×2 back squat, 48 hours post-game):
- **High-minute starter** (fatigued): 69% absolute → "Light" difficulty (72.5% relative)
- **Low-minute player** (less fatigued): 78.5% absolute → "Moderate" difficulty (82.5% relative)
- **DNP athlete** (fully fresh): 85.5% absolute → "Heavy" difficulty (90% relative)

This allows one unified session template with individualized load assignments based on player readiness.

## Resistance Training–Specific RPE Scale

Based on Zourdos et al. (2016). Rates difficulty in terms of **repetitions remaining** (reps in reserve — RIR), not just effort perception.

| RPE Rating | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| 10 | Maximum effort — no reps remaining |
| 9.5 | Could increase the load, no more reps |
| 9 | 1 repetition remaining |
| 8.5 | 1-2 repetitions remaining |
| 8 | 2 repetitions remaining |
| 7.5 | 2-3 repetitions remaining |
| 7 | 3 repetitions remaining |
| 5-6 | 4-6 repetitions remaining |
| 3-4 | Light effort |
| 1-2 | Little to no effort |

**Key calibration rule**: An RPE of 10 should correspond to ~100% on the Relative Intensity Scale for the given rep scheme. Example: athlete rates 8 to 10 reps → working at approximately 72.5-80% absolute intensity.

**Limitations**:
- Unreliable for novice/high school athletes with low training age
- Strength levels fluctuate up to 18% day to day — even experienced athletes misread effort [S11, Ch.12]
- Most accurate when loads exceed 70% 1RM
- Athletes should log workouts with resistance used + RPE to improve accuracy over time

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using Prilepin's Chart for hypertrophy work** → Prilepin is designed for power/explosive exercises; assistance exercises use different rep/volume schemes (2-3 sets of 8-12)
2. **Applying RPE scale without athlete education** → Athletes must understand the RIR language and concept before self-selecting loads; build this during preseason
3. **Ignoring game minutes when prescribing intensity** → A 38-minute starter and a DNP player should never get the same load on a post-game day
4. **Letting 1RM data become stale** → Testing 1RM in October and using those numbers in February is unreliable; shift to velocity-based or RPE-based autoregulation by mid-season
5. **Over-prescribing total reps above Prilepin's range** → Exceeding optimal total rep ranges at high intensities (e.g., >20 reps at 80-90%) leads to excessive fatigue and injury risk

## Variations
### Velocity-Based Training Integration
When velocity tracking devices are available, target velocity zones replace percentage-based loads entirely:
- Strength-speed zone: 0.3-0.6 m/s (competitive phase I primary exercises)
- Power zone: 0.75-1.0 m/s (squat/bench press)
- Olympic lift zones: 1.2-1.9+ m/s (position-specific)

Velocity data also provides motivational feedback, creating a competitive environment when athletes compare bar speed with teammates.
