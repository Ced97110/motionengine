# The Role of the Team Physician in Basketball

## Summary
The team physician is the head of a basketball program's healthcare team, responsible for treating and coordinating medical care for all athletic team members. Per the ACSM Team Physician Consensus Statement 2013, the team physician must hold an unrestricted medical license (MD or DO), possess special proficiency in musculoskeletal injuries encountered in sports, and ultimately bear responsibility within the team structure for medical decisions affecting athletes' safe participation [S10, p.121].

Beyond clinical expertise, the team physician must understand basketball itself — its injury mechanisms, physical demands, and the competitive environment — in order to immediately respond to sport-specific emergencies while balancing return-to-play decisions against long-term player health [S10, p.122].

## When to Use
- Defining roles within a basketball sports medicine department
- Onboarding a new team physician at any level (high school, college, professional)
- Designing an emergency action plan (EAP) for a facility
- Establishing pre-participation physical (PPE) protocols
- Planning for away-game medical coverage

## Key Principles

### 1. Qualifications and Training Background
1. **Core specialties**: Team physicians typically train in family medicine, internal medicine, pediatrics, physical medicine/rehabilitation, emergency medicine, orthopedics, neurology, or cardiology [S10, p.121].
2. **Primary care sports medicine subspecialty**: Physicians with primary care + sports medicine training undergo a post-residency fellowship that refines musculoskeletal exam skills, differential diagnosis for sports injuries, and healthcare team role understanding across levels [S10, p.121].
3. **Emergency competencies are mandatory**: Beyond musculoskeletal expertise, team physicians must have fundamental knowledge of cardiopulmonary resuscitation, cervical neck injury management, spine boarding, and on-court emergency response [S10, p.121].

### 2. The Athletic Trainer (ATC) Partnership
1. **The ATC is the front-line healthcare provider**: The certified athletic trainer (ATC) is typically the first medical responder to acute injuries and assists in managing chronic conditions. The ATC operates at the direction of the team physician [S10, p.122].
2. **ATC coordinates referrals and follow-up**: The ATC arranges specialist referrals, assists with diagnostic testing, and ensures athletes follow through on care [S10, p.122].
3. **Rehabilitation is primarily the ATC's domain**: ATCs are extensively trained in non-operative and post-operative rehab protocols, ensuring safe and timely recovery [S10, p.122].
4. **The ATC-physician relationship must be built on trust**: This partnership is foundational — together they bridge the gap between athletes, coaches, family, and the organization. Respect, trust, and communication are the non-negotiable foundations [S10, p.122].

### 3. Pre-Participation Physical Examination (PPE)
1. **First interaction with the athlete**: The PPE is typically the team physician's first formal contact with each player [S10, p.122].
2. **PPE components**: Thorough personal and family history review plus a detailed physical examination. If the physician cannot personally conduct the PPE, they must personally review all records [S10, p.122].
3. **PPE identifies risk factors**: The PPE flags potential injuries/illnesses and determines need for additional testing or specialty referral before the season begins [S10, p.122].

### 4. Cardiac Screening in Basketball
1. **Basketball players face elevated sudden cardiac death risk**: Team physicians must be vigilant due to the high prevalence of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) in African American athletes and Marfanoid characteristics common in tall, lean basketball players [S10, p.122].
2. **Marfanoid athletes at risk for aortic pathology**: Athletes with Marfan characteristics face risk from arrhythmia, aortic aneurysm, or dissection [S10, p.122].
3. **EKG interpretation competency required**: Team physicians should understand the International Criteria for EKG interpretation in athletes and know when to refer to cardiology for further workup [S10, p.122].

### 5. Game and Practice Coverage
1. **On-call responsibility is constant**: Even if not present at all practices and games, the team physician is typically on-call for the ATC and coaching staff at all times [S10, p.122].
2. **Monitor the full environment during events**: Team physicians must watch not just players but the entire environment — coaches, staff, referees, cheerleaders — as all may require medical attention [S10, p.122].
3. **Institutional policies vary**: The expectation for game/practice attendance varies by institution. Understand the contractual arrangements clearly.

### 6. Travel Medicine Responsibilities
1. **Prepare a travel medical bag**: Supplies and medications must be prepared for away games. Understand local, state, and national policies for transporting and dispensing medications [S10, p.122].
2. **Know medical liability at away venues**: Verify malpractice/liability coverage at venues outside the team's home facility and state [S10, p.122].
3. **Meet opponent's medical staff at each venue**: Discuss the arena emergency action plan (EAP), locate emergency medical centers, radiology services, and pharmacies. Be prepared to evaluate members of either team [S10, p.123].

### 7. Emergency Action Plan (EAP)
1. **Develop the EAP for all practice and game venues**: The EAP must ensure urgent situations are handled comprehensively and consistently [S10, p.123].
2. **Rehearse the EAP with all personnel**: All facility staff and support staff must practice the EAP under physician and ATC guidance [S10, p.123].
3. **Additional protocols required**: Concussion assessment and management, drug-testing procedures, and return-to-play guidelines must all be formally established and implemented [S10, p.123].

### 8. Administrative and Media Responsibilities
1. **Develop administrative policy and procedures**: Team physicians must be involved in creating institutional protocols beyond clinical care [S10, p.123].
2. **HIPAA compliance is mandatory**: Medical information about athletes must remain private. A signed release from the athlete (or family if a minor) is required before any health status statements can be made [S10, p.123].
3. **Effective communication is a clinical skill**: Team physicians must be clear, precise communicators — especially in high-visibility situations when media attention on injured players is intense [S10, p.123].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Delegating PPE review entirely to the ATC** → the team physician must personally review all PPE records even if they didn't conduct the examination.
2. **Missing cardiac red flags in tall, lean basketball players** → Marfanoid features warrant cardiology referral; HCM risk is elevated in African American athletes.
3. **Unprepared travel bag** → medications and supplies, along with knowledge of local dispensing regulations, must be arranged before every away trip.
4. **No formalized EAP rehearsal** → an unrehersed EAP fails in real emergencies; all facility personnel must practice it.
5. **Speaking to media without a signed release** → any health information disclosure without athlete consent is a HIPAA violation.
