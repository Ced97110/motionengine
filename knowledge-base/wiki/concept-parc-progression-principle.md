# The PARC Progression Principle

## Summary
The PARC Principle is a four-component framework for determining when an athlete is ready to advance from one level of explosive training to the next. Developed in the context of progressive calisthenics (Explosive Calisthenics, S9, Ch.10), PARC replaces the traditional rep-counting progression model with a qualitative mastery standard. The acronym stands for: **P**roficiency, **A**daptation, **R**egularity, **C**onfidence.

Unlike strength training — where you progress by adding reps until you hit a target number — explosive and power training must be governed by *quality* of movement, not quantity. Reps build exhaustion, and exhaustion is the enemy of power. PARC gives coaches and athletes a systematic, self-coaching tool to decide when to advance through progressions safely and effectively.

## When to Use
- Governing progression through any explosive exercise chain (jumps, plyometrics, kip-ups, muscle-ups, flips)
- Evaluating a player's readiness to advance to a harder movement or drill level
- Replacing arbitrary rep-count benchmarks in power and agility training
- Self-assessment during individual athletic development programs

## Key Principles

### Why Rep Targets Fail for Explosive Training
1. **Reps build exhaustion, not power** — Power training demands super-quick, snappy movements. Crisp speed and exhaustion are mutually exclusive. Building reps runs counter to the goal. [S9, p.10]
2. **Adding reps increases injury risk** — Explosive work is ballistic; muscles are not in full control for large portions of movement. As reps accumulate, fatigue dilutes concentration and self-correction ability, magnifying injury risk. [S9, pp.10-11]
3. **Reps encourage the wrong psychology** — When you're thinking "reps," you're not thinking "clean." Explosive training requires monitoring the *efficiency, speed, power, and complexity* of movement — not counting repetitions. [S9, p.11]

### The PARC Rule
An athlete may advance to the next step in a progression chain **only** when all four PARC components are satisfied: [S9, pp.12-16]

#### P — Proficiency
The ability to perform the movement *correctly* — not perfectly, but cleanly and efficiently. Proficiency means ticking all the basic boxes of technique. It does not demand world-class aesthetic form; it demands that the job gets done safely and with speed and power. "Strive for excellence, but never lose your freedom." [S9, p.13]
- *Example:* In a kip-up, the athlete must be rock-solid at the end of the movement. If they fall over backwards, they are not yet proficient.

#### A — Adaptation
The *body's* capacity to cope with the skill — not just the nervous system's. Some athletes can technically perform a movement but suffer pain, strains, or joint stress doing so. If the body is screaming, it has not adapted. Regression to earlier steps or supplementary conditioning work is required before advancing. [S9, p.14]
- *Example:* A player achieves a front handspring every few days but suffers strained abs and shoulder pain each time — the body has not adapted. Move back a step.

#### R — Regularity
The ability to perform the technique *consistently* — not just once. As a rule of thumb: **9 out of 10 attempts** (with rest between) must be successful before advancing. One lucky rep does not qualify advancement. [S9, p.14]
- *Example:* If a player can only clear a suicide jump correctly every other attempt, they lack regularity and are not ready to advance.

#### C — Confidence
The mental sense of self-assurance built through repeated successful, safe performance. A player who is anxious or terrified before each attempt has not yet achieved competence in that move. Confidence is not bravado — it is earned through consistent successful execution with room to spare. [S9, pp.14-16]
- *Example:* If a player psyches themselves up for a back handspring but is terrified of injury, they lack confidence. Never advance from this psychological state.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Apply PARC self-assessment during individual athletic development. Do not advance to harder exercises until all four components are satisfied. [S9, p.16]

## Variations
### Applying PARC as a Coach
Coaches can use PARC to evaluate players on any athletic movement progression — from box jumps to lateral cuts to depth jumps. Watch for: technique cleanliness (P), complaint-free training (A), consistent success rate (R), and willingness to attempt without hesitation (C).

### Regression Protocol
If any PARC component fails — especially Adaptation — the athlete should move *back* two or more steps, not simply stay at the current level. Supplementary conditioning exercises may be needed to close the gap.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Advancing on one lucky rep** → Regularity requires 9/10 consistent success. One rep doesn't count.
2. **Ignoring body signals** → If the athlete is sore or strained after each attempt, Adaptation has not been achieved. Regression is required.
3. **Rushing past Confidence** → Anxiety before every rep is a red flag. Never advance from fear.
4. **Using rep targets for explosive work** → The double-progression rep system works for strength, not power. "Always push for greater perfection, never for more reps." [S9, p.11]
5. **Skipping steps** → Random progression without establishing baselines leads to injury. Begin at the beginning, use PARC at every step. [S9, p.17]
