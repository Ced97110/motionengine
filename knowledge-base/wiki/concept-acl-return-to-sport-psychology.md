# ACL Reconstruction — Psychological Aspects of Return to Sport

## Summary
Return to sport (RTS) after ACL reconstruction (ACLR) is not solely a physical challenge. A growing body of evidence demonstrates that psychological factors — including kinesiophobia (fear of movement/reinjury), psychological readiness, self-efficacy, and pain catastrophizing — significantly influence functional outcomes, movement quality, and the risk of sustaining a second ACL injury. Clinicians using a biopsychosocial model should monitor and address these factors throughout rehabilitation, not just at the final clearance decision.

Early rehabilitation success (restoring full ROM, improving quadriceps strength) is linked to a better psychological outlook, setting the stage for later phases and ultimately return to sport. Psychological readiness and fear measures are now considered standard outcome tools alongside physical performance tests. [S10, pp.1007-1013]

## When to Use
- Throughout the full ACLR rehabilitation continuum (early, middle, and late phases)
- At return-to-sport clearance decision points
- When an athlete demonstrates movement asymmetry despite adequate physical strength
- When athletes are psychologically reluctant to progress rehabilitation intensity
- When screening for second-injury risk after a previous ACLR

## Key Principles

### 1. Psychological Readiness (ACL-RSI)
- The ACL-RSI is the gold-standard tool for measuring psychological readiness to return to sport after ACLR [S10, p.1008]
- It measures three constructs: **emotions**, **confidence**, and **risk appraisal** — scored 0–100 (higher = more positive)
- Administer infrequently (early, middle, and end of rehab); use a single confidence question between administrations
- Critical cut-off scores:
  - < 56 points at 4 months post-surgery = suboptimal psychological outlook [S10, p.1009]
  - < 62 points at 6 months = at-risk for non-return to sport
  - ACL-RSI scores < 76.7 at RTS = 90% sensitivity for identifying second-injury risk (in athletes ≤20 years)
- A 6-question short form (ACL-RSI Short Form) is also validated with the same 0–100 scale [S10, p.1009]

### 2. Kinesiophobia (TSK-11)
- The TSK-11 measures fear of movement/reinjury across three constructs: fear of pain, fear of reinjury, and somatic focus [S10, p.1009]
- Scored 11–44; higher scores = greater kinesiophobia
- MCID: 3.7 points; scores > 18 at RTS = 13× more likely to sustain a second ACL injury within 2 years
- Greater kinesiophobia → more stiffened movement patterns: less knee flexion, less hip flexion, less trunk flexion, greater hip adduction, and greater gluteus maximus pre-activation during drop vertical jump tasks

### 3. Self-Efficacy (K-SES)
- The K-SES (Knee Self-Efficacy Scale) measures confidence in performing tasks requiring knee function [S10, p.1009]
- 22 items across four sections: daily activities, sports/leisure, physical activities, and future knee function
- Scored 0–10 per item; ≥ 7 = acceptable level of self-efficacy

### 4. Psychological Factors Affect Movement
- Athletes with lower ACL-RSI scores demonstrate less knee flexion at initial contact and at peak during over-ground walking [S10, p.1007]
- Athletes with greater kinesiophobia show a stiffened sagittal-plane jump-landing pattern with less knee/hip flexion, greater hip adduction, and altered muscle pre-activation [S10, p.1008]
- Movement asymmetry during rehabilitation should be evaluated with psychological factors as a contributing variable, not only physical strength

### 5. Psychological Readiness and Reinjury Risk
- Athletes who sustain a second ACL injury within 2 years did NOT show improvement in psychological readiness from pre-surgery to 1 year post-surgery, while those who avoided re-injury DID improve [S10, p.1007]
- Paradoxically, high confidence (not low) combined with good physical performance test scores may also increase reinjury risk — athletes may return to sport sooner and take greater risks [S10, p.1007]
- Fear (kinesiophobia) at RTS clearance correlates with higher rates of ipsilateral graft rupture within 1 year

### 6. Psychologically Informed Practice (PIP)
- PIP is the integration of the biopsychosocial model into clinical rehabilitation [S10, p.1008]
- Clinicians evaluate and address psychological barriers alongside physical impairments
- Three contraindications for PT-delivered psychological intervention (refer to mental health professional): (1) identified current mental disorder, (2) patient unable to participate meaningfully in treatment, (3) patient unwilling to participate [S10, p.1011]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Complete psychological screening tools (ACL-RSI, TSK-11) at structured intervals during ACLR rehabilitation
- **Athletes ≤20 years old**: Extra vigilance on psychological readiness monitoring due to heightened second-injury risk

## Psychological Screening Tools Summary

| Tool | Construct | Scale | Key Cut-Off |
|------|-----------|-------|-------------|
| ACL-RSI | Psychological readiness (emotions, confidence, risk appraisal) | 0–100 (higher = better) | <56 at 4 mo, <62 at 6 mo |
| ACL-RSI Short Form | Same as ACL-RSI (6 questions) | 0–100 | <60 at 6 months |
| TSK-11 | Kinesiophobia (fear of movement/reinjury) | 11–44 (higher = more fear) | >18 at RTS = 13× reinjury risk |
| K-SES | Self-efficacy for knee function tasks | 0–10 (higher = more certain) | ≥7 = acceptable |

## Variations

### Graded Exercise and Graded Exposure
Graded exercise uses a quota-based progression system where exercise demands are systematically increased and positive reinforcement is given when quotas are met. Graded exposure identifies feared movements through questionnaire or interview, then incorporates them into the rehab program with progressively increasing fear intensity until fear declines. Plyometric-based graded exercise programs significantly improved knee self-efficacy, self-reported function, vertical jump height, and quadriceps strength in ACLR patients. The proportion of patients classified as "psychologically ready" also significantly increased after progressive plyometric + agility + strengthening programs. [S10, p.1010]

### Mindfulness Meditation (MM)
MM involves refocusing attention on present-moment awareness of inner feelings, body sensations, and surroundings — without judgment. Effective for increasing subjective well-being and reducing fear responses and emotional reactivity in musculoskeletal pain populations. May be useful for ACLR athletes with negative psychological factors or persistent pain. Techniques include: breathing methods, body scan, guided imagery, observer meditation. [S10, p.1010]

### Other Cognitive-Behavioral Interventions
- Guided imagery, relaxation training, coping skill development, and modeling have been studied after ACLR
- These interventions improved self-reported knee function, reduced fear/anxiety, and resolved knee impairments
- No studies to date (at time of publication) have examined cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) on RTS outcomes specifically, though a clinical trial (NCT03959215) using a smartphone-based CBT app was underway [S10, p.1010]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Clearing athletes based only on physical criteria** → Always include psychological screening (ACL-RSI, TSK-11) as part of the RTS decision
2. **Ignoring movement asymmetry's psychological component** → When athletes demonstrate altered movement patterns despite adequate strength, assess kinesiophobia as a contributing factor
3. **One-time psychological assessment** → Administer tools at early, middle, and late rehab phases; use simple confidence questions between formal assessments
4. **Assuming low confidence always = high reinjury risk** → High confidence + good physical performance can also indicate premature return, as athletes may not perceive their own risk
5. **PT managing psychological disorders beyond their scope** → Refer to mental health professional when indicated (mental disorder, non-participation, refusal)
