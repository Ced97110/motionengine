# Quick Feet (in All Directions)

## Objective
Enhance stride frequency on the first step by running through a half agility ladder using a "one foot per rung" pattern, focusing on foot speed rather than linear running speed. [S8, p.180]

## Setup
- 1 player (coach or partner optional for reaction variation)
- **Half agility ladder** (to reduce neural fatigue and isolate first-step quickness rather than prolonged running speed)
- Optional: 2 cones placed 5–10 yards on each side of the ladder end for reaction command targets

## Execution
1. Run through the agility ladder placing **one foot down between each rung** (one foot per square).
2. Concentrate on **foot speed**, not linear running speed — the goal is rapid foot contact cycles.
3. Keep eyes up (do not look down at the ladder).
4. **Reaction variation**: On the coach's auditory or visual command given at the end of the ladder, immediately sprint to the designated cone (left or right, or laterally/backward for added difficulty). [S8, p.180]

## Coaching Points
- "Fast feet, not fast body" — emphasize the rate of foot turnover, not forward lean
- Every drill can be performed **laterally or backward** for increased difficulty
- The coach should vary which cone they signal to prevent anticipation
- Short ground contact time on each step is the performance indicator
- Using only half the ladder prevents neural fatigue from becoming a limiting factor

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Walk through the pattern, one foot per rung, to establish the footstrike sequence
2. **Intermediate**: Maximum foot speed through the full half-ladder
3. **Advanced**: Add a reaction command at the exit — lateral sprint, backward sprint, or sprint to a named cone

## Concepts Taught
- [[basketball-acceleration-and-deceleration]] — first-step stride frequency is a critical component of on-court quickness
- [[rate-of-force-development-basketball]] — rapid foot turnover requires high RFD from the calves and ankles
- [[ankle-proprioception-basketball]] — precision foot placement at speed trains ankle neuromuscular control
