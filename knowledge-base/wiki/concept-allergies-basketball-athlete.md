# Allergies in Basketball Athletes

## Summary
Basketball athletes are exposed to the same allergens as the general population but face added challenges due to frequent travel, multiple practice venues, and different environmental conditions across arenas. Allergic conditions ranging from rhinitis to anaphylaxis can significantly impair athletic performance and, in severe cases, be life-threatening. Up to 49% of Olympic athletes have been found to have sensitization to inhalant allergens — a rate that underscores the importance of identifying and managing allergies in competitive basketball players.

## When to Use
- Pre-participation screening for any athlete with history of allergic reactions
- When an athlete presents with performance decline, breathing abnormalities, or fatigue without clear training explanation
- When a player is traveling to a new geographic region with different allergens
- When prescribing any medication that may have WADA implications

## Key Principles

### Epidemiology
- Up to 30% of individuals in Europe and the US suffer from allergic rhinitis [S10, p.911]
- Olympic athletes: positive skin prick testing rates up to 41%
- Olympic athletes: up to 49% sensitized to inhalant allergens
- Food allergies: ~7%, drug allergies: ~5%, venom: ~2% in Olympic athletes [S10, p.911]

### Pathophysiology
- Most common mechanism: **IgE-mediated histamine and leukotriene release** from mast cells and basophils
- Early and late phase components both impact athlete well-being
- Severe reactions can progress to anaphylaxis (IgE-mediated or IgE-independent)

### Clinical Presentation
**Allergic rhinitis** (most common):
- Nasal congestion, runny nose, ocular involvement
- May present as decreased energy, breathing abnormalities, or poor athletic performance

**Severe / anaphylaxis**:
- Angioedema, stridor, hypotension, cardiac manifestations, gastrointestinal symptoms

### Diagnosis Tools
- **AQUA** (Allergy Questionnaire for Athletes): validated screening tool specific to athletic population; available in 10+ European languages; highly specific [S10, p.911]
- **Nasal allergen provocation test**: for allergic rhinitis investigation
- **Skin prick test**: gold standard for confirming diagnosis
- **Serum-specific IgE test**: alternative to skin prick
- **Basophil activation test**: for subset of local allergic rhinitis

### Treatment Options (Table 71.3)
| Class | Delivery | WADA Status |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Anti-histamine | PO, IN, Opt | Permitted |
| Decongestant | PO, IN | Varies — some need TUE |
| Anticholinergics | IN | Permitted |
| Leukotriene inhibitors | PO | Permitted |
| Mast cell stabilizers | IN, Opt, PO | Permitted |
| Corticosteroid | PO, IN, IV, IM, Opt | Requires TUE |
| Allergen immunotherapy | PO, IM, SC | Permitted |
| Epinephrine | IM | Requires TUE |

**Most important intervention**: Avoidance of known triggers

**WADA Caution**: Systemic steroids and some decongestants are on the TUE list — always check league-specific TUE lists before prescribing

### Special Circumstances

**Food Allergies**:
- Increasing in prevalence across all demographics
- Travel and inconsistent dining options create challenges for basketball athletes
- Athletes must know ingredient lists; desensitization/oral immunotherapy options exist
- Allergy specialist consultation recommended for oral immunotherapy

**Exercise-Induced Anaphylaxis (EIAn)**:
- Rare condition; anaphylactic reaction triggered by exertion itself
- Can present after years of symptom-free exercise
- Subtype: **Food-Dependent Exercise-Induced Anaphylaxis (FDEIAn)** — anaphylaxis when athlete exerts within hours of eating
- Diagnosis: usually clinical, after excluding mastocytosis; exercise challenge test may assist
- **Management**: Athletes with history of EIAn must carry epinephrine at all times and have a detailed action plan

## Common Mistakes
1. **Attributing poor performance to fatigue without screening for allergies** → allergic rhinitis commonly presents as decreased energy and poor performance rather than classic nasal symptoms.
2. **Prescribing systemic steroids without checking WADA/league TUE requirements** → systemic corticosteroids require a TUE in competition.
3. **Not having epinephrine available** → any athlete with a known anaphylaxis-inducing allergy must have epinephrine accessible at all practices and games.
4. **Failing to account for new allergen exposure from travel** → basketball's travel schedule exposes athletes to different regional allergens; proactive planning is required.
