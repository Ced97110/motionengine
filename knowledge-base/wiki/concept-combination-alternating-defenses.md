# Combination and Alternating Defenses

## Summary
Combination and alternating defenses are tactical weapons best suited for underdogs or teams whose standard defense is not working. They disrupt practiced offensive sets by presenting unexpected looks. Del Harris lists five specific approaches — box-and-one, triangle-and-two, zone-to-man, man-to-zone, and full alternating coverage — and stresses they are not starters but tactical adjustments. [S7, pp.265-266]

## When to Use
- When your basic system is not producing stops
- When an opponent has one or two dominant players the defense cannot stop 1v1
- When the opponent's offense is clicking and you need to disrupt their rhythm
- Most appropriate for underdogs; elite teams should not open a game with these schemes

## Key Principles
1. **Reserve for underdog situations**: "Such defensive tactics are more suited to the underdog role." Do not use them when you can overpower an opponent with basic defense. [S7, p.265]
2. **Verbal signals required**: Every mid-possession shift must be pre-planned and triggered by a specific verbal key — no ad-libbing.
3. **Zone attacks are easier to cover than man sets**: When you shift zone-to-man, the opponent usually continues running their zone attack, which is easier to defend.
4. **Alternating creates confusion** but also risks your own team's confusion — limit to 2-3 triggers that all players know.

## The Five Combinations

### 1. Box-and-One
- Four defenders in a 2-2 or 1-2-1 box formation using matchup rules
- One defender chases (denies the ball to) the opponent's most dangerous player
- **Matchbox-and-One variant**: The four box defenders use matchup zone rules while one chases the hot player — blends matchup and chasing coverage [S7, p.265]

### 2. Triangle-and-Two
- Three defenders in a triangle zone; two defenders playing man-to-man chasing the two most dangerous perimeter players
- Useful against teams with two elite shooters/scorers

### 3. Zone-to-Man Adjustment
- Show a zone defense, then shift to man-to-man on a specific trigger: ball to high post, ball swung to the weak-side wing, pass to the corner, etc.
- Advantage: The opponent keeps running their zone attack, which is generally easier to defend than their man-to-man sets

### 4. Man-to-Zone Adjustment
- Show man-to-man, then shift to zone when the ball reaches a predetermined spot or player
- Effective against PnR specialists, isolation one-on-one attackers, and certain low-post sets
- Shift is triggered by ball position, not by player movement

### 5. Alternating Defenses
- Change defenses without time-outs — mid-possession based on floor position
- Examples: right side of court = man, left side = zone; change on weak-side swing; change when ball goes to the corner; change based on last scoring possession (even score = man, odd score = zone)
- "Remember that these kinds of tactics are reserved mainly for an underdog team or for situations when your team is behind and your normal stuff is not working." [S7, p.266]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using combination defenses as a primary system** → these are tactical adjustments, not foundational schemes
2. **No clear trigger signal** → players must know exactly what event initiates the shift; ambiguity causes breakdowns
3. **Opponents adapting quickly** → "alternating defenses keeps opponents guessing" but if the pattern is obvious, a good offense will adjust; vary the triggers
4. **Forgetting zone-attack vulnerability** → when you go zone-to-man, the opponent still runs their zone attack, which benefits the defense
