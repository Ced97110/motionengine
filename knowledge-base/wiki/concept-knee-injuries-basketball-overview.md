# Knee Injuries in Basketball — Overview

## Summary
Knee injuries are among the most common and serious injuries in basketball, accounting for the most time away from sport. The knee is stressed by the sport's constant cutting, pivoting, jumping, and landing movements. Management depends on severity, anatomical structure involved, age, level of play, symptomatology, and functional demands. Prevention programs integrated into warm-up routines have proven effective in reducing knee injury rates [S10, p.348].

## When to Use
- Team physicians and athletic trainers assessing any knee injury on the court or in the training room
- S&C coaches designing injury-prevention programs for basketball rosters
- Coaches understanding return-to-play timelines for injured players

## Key Principles
1. Knee injuries in basketball are responsible for the most time away from sport — prevention programs integrated into warm-up routines are effective and easily implementable [S10, p.348].
2. Management approach (conservative vs. surgical) must account for severity, anatomic structures involved, age, expectations, and level of play [S10, p.348].
3. Adequate return-to-sport criteria and prevention methodologies are key for reducing the number of basketball injuries [S10, p.348].
4. MRI tends to overestimate ligamentous lesions — clinical examination and progression should lead treatment decisions [S10, p.340].
5. Combined injuries (e.g., ACL + lateral meniscus; LCL + PCL) are common and must be identified to avoid mismanagement [S10, pp.339-341].

## Injury Categories

### Patellar Dislocation
- Medial patellofemoral ligament (MPFL) is the primary restraint; commonly injured in first-time dislocation
- RTS rarely before 6 months after surgical reconstruction
- Return to pre-injury level of sport: 32–82% of athletes [S10, p.338]
- May be caused by pre-existing morphologic abnormality or high-energy trauma

### Knee Dislocation
- Rare but possible in basketball; ~50% reduce spontaneously
- Orthopedic emergency — immediate transfer to emergency department
- Always perform peripheral neurovascular examination; normal exam does not rule out vascular injury (popliteal artery) — serial exams mandatory [S10, p.338]

### ACL Injury
- Accounts for up to 64% of all knee injuries in basketball [S10, p.339]
- Incidence: female 0.29, male 0.08 per 1000 athletic exposures [S10, p.339]
- Common mechanism: rapid cutting, incorrect landing; often non-contact
- Ages 15–25 account for half of all ACL injuries
- 15× greater risk of second ACL injury in first 12 months after reconstruction [S10, p.339]

### PCL Injury
- Uncommon in basketball; often associated with contact
- Common mechanism: blow to front of knee while flexed and foot plantar-flexed
- Many partial tears heal conservatively; reconstruction reserved for avulsion fractures, combined ligamentous injuries, or severely symptomatic chronic instability [S10, p.339]

### MCL Injury
- Collegiate rates: 0.87/10,000 AE (female), 1.02/10,000 AE (male) [S10, p.340]
- Grade I: RICE ± hinged brace, RTS 2–3 weeks
- Grade II: brace up to 3 weeks, RTS ~6 weeks
- Grade III: minimum 4-week immobilization, RTS rarely before 10 weeks; surgery if complete medial ligamentous complex disruption or conservative treatment fails [S10, p.341]

### LCL/PLC Injury
- Isolated LCL/PLC less common than MCL; may coexist with ACL/PCL in up to 40% of cases [S10, p.341]
- Often misdiagnosed — true incidence underestimated
- Misdiagnosis can lead to chronic lateral/posterolateral laxity and jeopardize career
- Dial test: >10° side-to-side difference at 30° flexion = isolated PLC; present at both 30° and 90° = combined PCL+PLC [S10, p.341]
- Grade I: conservative; Grade III: surgical; Grade II: case-by-case [S10, p.341]

### Meniscal Injury
- Common in basketball; 59.7% lateral, 40.3% medial in NBA data [S10, p.342]
- 10.5% of WNBA athletes had meniscal injuries at combine [S10, p.342]
- Three zones: red-red (vascular, good healing), red-white (intermediate), white-white (avascular)
- Lateral meniscectomy more damaging than medial due to load-transfer role
- Meniscus preservation preferred, especially in young players; repair has higher reoperation rate but better long-term outcomes than partial meniscectomy [S10, p.345]
- RTS after isolated meniscus repair: minimum 4 months [S10, p.346]

### Knee Tendinopathies & Tendon Tears
- Patellar tendinopathy (jumper's knee): prevalent in up to 31.9% of elite basketball players [S10, p.347]
- Osgood–Schlatter syndrome in adolescents (boys 12–15, girls 8–12): self-limited, conservative treatment
- Complete patellar or quadriceps tendon rupture: surgical repair mandatory; RTS rarely before 6 months [S10, p.348]

## Common Mistakes
1. Ruling out vascular injury based on a single normal neurovascular exam after knee dislocation → perform serial examinations [S10, p.338]
2. Missing LCL/PLC injuries because they are less common → compare to contralateral knee, use dial test [S10, p.341]
3. Proceeding with ACL/PCL reconstruction without addressing lateral/posterolateral injuries → may compromise reconstruction outcome [S10, p.341]
4. Choosing meniscectomy over repair in young players → repair offers better long-term outcomes [S10, p.345]
5. Overrelying on MRI findings — MRI tends to overestimate lesions → lead management with clinical exam [S10, p.340]
