# Single-Arm Dumbbell Row (Basketball)

## Objective
Develop unilateral posterior deltoid and latissimus dorsi strength to address dominant-arm imbalances, improving shooting setup, rebounding, and non-dominant hand performance.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Flat bench
- Dumbbell (appropriate weight)

## Execution
1. From a standing position, **flex the left knee to 90 degrees** and place it on the bench for support.
2. Place the left hand on the bench to support body weight. Pick up the dumbbell with the right hand, letting it hang with the right arm fully extended.
3. Keep the back **flat** and head in a **neutral position**. Bring the dumbbell up by flexing the elbow until the elbow is **even with the side of the ribs**.
4. Return the dumbbell to the starting position (arm fully extended).
5. Perform the prescribed number of repetitions.
6. **Switch sides** — repeat with the left arm.

## Coaching Points
- "Back flat, neck neutral — don't twist the torso to row the weight."
- Elbow drives up and back — not out to the side.
- "Row to your hip pocket, not to your armpit."
- Equal reps on both sides every session — this exercise exists specifically to balance bilateral strength.
- For a sport-specific variation: **Single-Arm Band Row** — stand in a basketball defensive stance and perform the rowing motion using a cable column or elastic band.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Light dumbbell, supported on bench with both knee and hand on bench.
2. **Intermediate**: Standard single-arm dumbbell row with controlled tempo.
3. **Advanced**: Single-Arm Band Row standing in defensive stance — adds hip and core stability demand.

## Muscles Involved
- **Primary**: Posterior deltoid, latissimus dorsi
- **Secondary**: Trapezius, rhomboid major, rhomboid minor, biceps brachii

## Basketball Focus
The single-arm dumbbell row targets the **posterior deltoids** — critical for setting up a shot and winning rebounding battles. The key basketball insight: bilateral exercises (using both arms at once) allow the **dominant side to compensate**, masking weakness. Single-arm rows force each arm to work independently, directly developing the **weaker arm**.

Having **equal strength in both arms** benefits basketball players who must:
- Shoot with their **non-dominant hand** (layups, floaters, finishes in traffic)
- Rebound with both hands simultaneously against physical opponents
- Hold position and pass under defensive pressure from either side
