# Shooting Stance and Body Positioning

## Summary
Every great shot is built from the ground up. The shooting stance is the foundation — consistent foot positioning and body alignment create the repeatable mechanics that separate elite shooters from average ones. The same stance should be used for all shots: jump shots, free throws, and three-pointers. Consistency starts with the feet. [S5, pp.9-11]

## When to Use
- Every shooting situation: catch-and-shoot, off-the-dribble pull-up, free throws, three-pointers
- Applied consistently regardless of shot distance or court location

## Key Principles
1. **Build from the ground up** — foot position is the foundation of every shot, like the foundation of a house
2. **Establish the shot line** — toe, knee, hip, elbow, shoulder, wrist, and basketball must be as close to a straight line as possible pointing at the target; maintaining this line increases shooting percentage and eliminates unnecessary motion [S5, p.10]
3. **Shoulder-width base** — too narrow creates poor balance and limits range; too wide reduces quickness and explosiveness needed to get the shot off
4. **Stagger the feet** — slide the non-shooting foot (left foot for right-handers) back until its toes are even with the arch of the shooting foot; then space feet shoulder-width apart
5. **Lead foot points at the target** — the shooting-side foot (right foot for right-handers) toes point directly at the basket
6. **Bend at the knees AND hips** — bending only at the knees leaves heels on the floor and throws the body off balance; bring the head forward so shoulders are in front of feet and head is in front of shoulders [S5, p.10]
7. **"You crawl, you walk, then you run"** — master stationary form before attempting shots off the dribble or from range [S5, p.9]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: The same stance fundamentals apply regardless of position. Interior players need the same foundation as perimeter shooters.

## Variations
### Hip-Bend Drill
To practice correct hip engagement, lean forward from a stationary position and try to get as close to the basket as possible without moving the feet. The natural result is proper hip bend. Hold for a few seconds; repeat several times. [S5, p.10]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stance too narrow** → Poor balance, reduced shot range; widen to shoulder width
2. **Stance too wide** → Loss of quickness and explosiveness; narrow to shoulder width
3. **Bending only at knees, not hips** → Body becomes too upright, heels stay on floor, off-balance when catching and shooting; practice the hip-lean drill to develop proper feel
4. **Starting too far from the basket** → Most young players skip form shooting and go straight to the three-point line; start 2 feet from the rim and work outward
5. **Inconsistent stance between shot types** → The same foot position should be used for all shots; inconsistency destroys repeatability
