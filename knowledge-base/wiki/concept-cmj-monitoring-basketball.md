# Countermovement Jump (CMJ) Monitoring for In-Season Fatigue Tracking

## Summary
The countermovement jump (CMJ) is a reliable, repeatable tool for monitoring neuromuscular fatigue and training adaptation across a basketball season. Research on elite female basketball players across a 10-week in-season competition phase found that while headline performance markers (jump height) remained stable even under high training loads, **kinetic variables — particularly eccentric peak velocity, concentric mean power, and dip depth — changed meaningfully at mid-season peak load**, signaling neuromuscular fatigue before jump height declined.

For strength and conditioning coaches, this means that jump height alone is an insensitive monitor. Coaches must track eccentric variables and within-athlete movement variability to catch overreaching before it affects performance on the court. [S12, pp.92-96]

## When to Use
- **Throughout the competitive season** as a regular (pre-practice or morning) neuromuscular readiness screen
- At key load-spike periods (tournament runs, double-game weeks) when fatigue accumulation is highest
- When players show signs of sluggishness, slower first steps, or missed rebounds — but jump height tests look normal
- As part of a broader athlete monitoring system alongside RPE (session rating of perceived exertion)

## Key Principles

1. **Jump height is the last variable to decline.** During peak training load, athletes alter their jump *mechanics* (deeper dip, longer eccentric phase) to maintain jump height. Coaches who only measure height miss early warning signs of fatigue.
2. **Monitor movement variability, not just mean performance.** Within-subject coefficient of variation (%CV) in kinetic variables increased at mid-season. Higher variability = more neuromuscular noise = fatigue signal.
3. **Eccentric variables are the most sensitive indicators.** Eccentric peak velocity and dip depth changed meaningfully when concentric power and height did not. An increase in dip magnitude signals athletes are using a deeper squat strategy to compensate for reduced explosive capacity. [S12, p.93]
4. **Consistency improvement is a real adaptation.** Even when power scores don't rise, jump consistency improving across a season is evidence that motor patterns are being learned — a genuine training adaptation. [S12, p.93]
5. **Supervised training environment accelerates motor learning.** Athletes in their first year of centralized, coached strength training show jump pattern improvements simply through increased coordination, not strength gains. [S12, p.93]
6. **Peak training load compresses group variation.** When loads are highest, the best jumpers may be suppressed toward the level of lesser jumpers, reducing within-group spread. This can mask individual fatigue in group averages.
7. **Practical stakes are real.** A longer eccentric phase during a maximal jump on court means delayed push-off — which in a game context translates to a missed pass or lost rebound. [S12, p.95]

## CMJ Variables and Their Coaching Significance

| Variable | Sensitivity | What Change Signals |
|---|---|---|
| Jump Height (cm) | Low (stable under fatigue) | Gross power loss — late-stage fatigue indicator |
| Dip Depth (cm) | Moderate-High | Compensatory eccentric loading — fatigue adaptation |
| Concentric Mean Power (W) | Moderate | Reduction in explosive force output — strength-fatigue link |
| Concentric Peak Velocity (m/s) | Moderate | Altered jumping mechanics mid-season |
| Eccentric Peak Velocity (m/s) | High | Most variable measure; sensitive to neuromuscular changes |
| Within-subject %CV | High | Movement variability — use to distinguish adaptation from fatigue |

*Data from elite female basketball players: pre-season mean jump height 0.39 ± 0.05 m; concentric peak velocity 3.22 ± 0.25 m/s. [S12, p.92]*

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Perform standardized 5-jump CMJ protocols consistently (same time of day, same warm-up, arm swing restricted or permitted — choose one and keep it consistent all season)
- **C / PF**: Pay special attention to dip and eccentric variables — big players generate the most absolute power and their fatigue signatures may be most pronounced in load metrics (W, not W/kg)
- **PG / SG / SF**: Concentric peak velocity and jump height most relevant for first-step and contest-shot applications

## Implementing CMJ Monitoring in Practice

### Protocol
1. Use a standardized 5-jump CMJ protocol at each monitoring time point (pre-season, mid-season, post-season minimum; weekly or bi-weekly is preferable during competition phase)
2. Restrict arm swing OR allow free arm swing — but never mix within a season (arm-restricted values will be lower)
3. Collect: jump height, concentric mean power (W/kg), concentric peak velocity (m/s), dip depth (cm), eccentric peak velocity (m/s)
4. Calculate within-subject %CV across the 5 jumps for each variable — consistency is its own data point

### Reading the Data
- **Jump height stable + dip increasing**: Fatigue present but compensated — reduce load, monitor closely
- **Concentric mean power declining + jump height stable**: Mid-phase fatigue warning — review weekly training volume
- **%CV increasing across all variables**: Significant neuromuscular disruption — consider a deload week
- **%CV decreasing season-long**: Motor learning occurring — positive adaptation sign even without power gains

## Variations

### Unrestricted Arm Swing CMJ
Allows more natural jumping action and produces higher jump height values than restricted-arm protocols. Use when comparing to published normative data for basketball players.

### Reactive Strength Index (RSI) Monitoring
For more advanced monitoring, RSI (jump height ÷ ground contact time) is even more sensitive to eccentric fatigue than CMJ height alone.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Only tracking jump height** → Add eccentric velocity and dip depth to catch fatigue earlier
2. **Comparing across different protocols** → Standardize arm swing, warm-up, and time-of-day; never compare restricted to unrestricted values
3. **Ignoring within-set variability** → A player who averages 0.40m across 5 jumps but has 25% CV is not the same as one who averages 0.40m with 7% CV
4. **Treating group averages as individual readiness** → Group means mask individual fatigue; track each athlete's baseline and deviation from their own norm
5. **Panicking at mid-season kinetic drops** → Small declines in concentric power at peak load may be normal and self-correcting; look at trends over 3+ weeks, not single sessions
