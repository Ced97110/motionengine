# Game Preparation (pro — Dunleavy & Eyen)

## Summary
Mike Dunleavy and Jim Eyen's chapter on pro game preparation provides a complete blueprint for how a professional staff prepares for each opponent. The system covers advance scouting (live attendance), scouting reports (3 sections), video technology (edits and breakdowns), game plan construction (statistical + film + suggested plan), game-day morning routine (coaches' meeting → film → walk-through → shoot-around), and pregame ritual [S7, pp.287-296].

The core philosophy: "At the professional level, talent and skill are foremost in the quest for success, but preparation can be a great equalizer." The average pro game is decided by ±4 points, meaning efficient preparation directly impacts outcomes.

## When to Use
- Before every game at any competitive level
- When adapting opponent-specific defensive coverages (PnR traps, post doubles)
- When deciding how much information to present to players vs. holding back
- As a framework adaptable to high school and college programs with fewer resources

## Key Principles

### Advance Scouting
1. **Live attendance is irreplaceable** — body language, bench behavior, play calls, and away-from-ball action cannot be captured on video.
2. **Scout the opponent's last game** before playing them — recency matters.
3. **Obtaining play calls is the primary mission** — scouts record calls on worksheets and cross-reference with game film for any missed plays.
4. **Differentiate real calls from decoys** — coaches may use fake calls, two signs simultaneously, or verbal disguises.
5. Live scouts must watch the **coach and the point guard simultaneously**; lip-reading is a valuable acquired skill.

### Scouting Report (3 Sections)
1. **Defensive tendencies** — full-court presses, half-court traps, zone defenses, PnR schemes, situation-specific usage.
2. **Offensive tendencies** — motion vs. isolation, PnR type and effectiveness, low-post game quality; focus on definitive tendencies, not general capability.
3. **Actual play sets** — the most detailed section; 8-10 most frequently run or most situationally important plays, including OOB plays.

### Video and Technology
- Video coordinator logs every game into a digital system; games are broken down by play type (offense, defense, play sets, turnovers, offensive rebounds).
- **20-25 play edit** shown to the team the day before the game (~10-15 minutes).
- **Game-day morning edit** divided into 5 categories: transition offense, set offense, side OOB plays, baseline OOB plays, example of opponent's defense.
- **Pre-game edit** (10-15 min): recent clips with emphasis on the team's last matchup vs. this opponent.
- **Post-game**: full copies and breakdowns given to all coaches and players; film used in postgame analysis. "The film doesn't lie."

### Game Plan Construction
- Each of the 3 assistant coaches is assigned a division of the 29 opponents; each becomes an **expert on his teams** over the season.
- Statistical foundation: cumulative season stats + **5-game rolling stats** (FG%, 3P%, FT%, rebounding, turnovers) to identify hot/cold trends.
- Individual stats (cumulative + 5-game) identify turnover-prone players and hot shooters — both influence defensive decisions.
- Assistant coaches submit a **suggested game plan** (see Figure 18.2) covering: basic defense, low-post defense, PnR coverages (wing/top/elbow/corner), 1-on-1 ices, opponent depth chart, shooters list, poor FT% targets, and notes.
- Final game plan emerges from staff discussion after viewing film, practicing (if schedule permits), and consensus.

### Game-Day Morning Routine (7:30 pm games)
- **8:30 am coaches' meeting**: latest injury info, review film, finalize defensive coverages, review depth chart, finalize matchups, discuss how opponent will defend YOUR offense.
- **Players' film session**: synopsis of opponent (strengths/weaknesses), starters/matchups announced, players encouraged to contribute insight, 15-20 min edit shown, questions answered.
- **Walk-through on court**: 8-10 opponent plays demonstrated with defensive coverages; player input valued ("along with contribution comes ownership").
- **Shoot-around**: shooting drills geared to shots from the team's own play sets; split into bigs/smalls; ends with prescribed free throws.
- Coaches remain to assist players wanting additional work.

### Pregame Ritual
- Arena arrival: coaches 4:30-5:00 pm; players from 5:00 pm.
- Full-game copy of last matchup vs. opponent runs on big screen in locker room from 5:30 pm.
- Whiteboard covered until 45-minute mark (when doors close to press).
- Team meeting: another 10-15 min film edit → game plan review → whiteboard walk-through of opponent plays and defensive coverages → 20 minutes to warm up.
- Whiteboard content: basic defense with opponent-specific PnR coverages, post doubles, play diagrams, 3PT percentages, FT% outliers, depth chart.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Information overload** → Give players the dose of information that helps performance without creating confusion.
2. **Relying solely on video for scouting** → Live attendance captures calls, body language, and off-ball action that video misses.
3. **Generic game plans** → The plan must be customized for this specific opponent, not a recycled template.
4. **Not adapting coverages to elite individuals** → If facing a Steve Nash or Tim Duncan, basic coverages may need to be modified on a nightly basis.
5. **Ignoring five-game rolling stats** → Season cumulative stats can hide recent trends; five-game stats identify who is hot right now.
