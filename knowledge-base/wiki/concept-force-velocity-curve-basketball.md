# Force-Velocity Curve for Basketball Training

## Summary
The force-velocity relationship is a foundational principle for all basketball strength and conditioning programs. **Power = Force × Velocity**, meaning that improving either force or velocity increases power output. The force-velocity curve is hyperbolic — heavier loads move slower, lighter loads move faster. The primary goal of basketball-specific strength and power training is to **shift the entire curve to the right**, enabling athletes to produce more force at higher speeds — becoming more explosive [S11].

Improving the **rate of force development (RFD)** also improves power production because it reflects the ability to produce large forces in very short time windows (200-300ms), which is the critical window for most basketball explosive actions.

## The Five Zones of the Force-Velocity Curve

<!-- DIAGRAM: Figure 4.2 — Hyperbolic force-velocity curve with five labeled zones from maximum strength (high force, low velocity) to maximum velocity (low force, high velocity); peak power sits in the middle -->

| Zone | Load (% 1RM) | Emphasis |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Maximum Strength | >90% | Peak force; low velocity |
| 2. Strength-Speed | 80-90% | Strength-dominant intermediate zone |
| 3. Peak Power | 30-80% | Maximum power output |
| 4. Speed-Strength | 30-60% | Velocity-dominant intermediate zone |
| 5. Max Velocity | <30% | Maximum movement velocity |

### Zone Descriptions

1. **Maximum Strength** (>90% 1RM): Primary objective is peak force. Training here builds the force foundation that all other zones depend on.

2. **Strength-Speed** (80-90% 1RM): Intermediate zone. Does not produce peak power or peak force, but builds strength with some velocity component. Useful for athletes who need a stronger foundation.

3. **Peak Power** (30-80% 1RM): Sits in the middle of the curve. This zone produces maximum power output — the most important zone for basketball explosiveness.

4. **Speed-Strength** (30-60% 1RM): Greater velocities than peak power zone but lower forces. Allows rapid movement while maintaining some load.

5. **Maximum Velocity** (<30% 1RM): Enables maximal movement speed. Loads are very light, prioritizing velocity of movement.

## When to Use Each Zone

**Train the entire curve** — not just one zone. The whole spectrum must be developed for complete basketball athleticism.

**Position-specific examples:**
- A **center** who is very strong but slow on speed tests → shift training toward max velocity and speed-strength zones
- A **point guard** who is fast and agile but injury-prone due to lack of strength → emphasize maximum strength and strength-speed zones to build a structural foundation

**General guideline:** Basketball athletes should spend more time in the **peak power zone** (30-80% 1RM) than in maximum strength, but both must be developed in a periodized plan.

## Key Principles
1. Power = force × velocity — improving either component improves power
2. The hyperbolic curve shows an inverse relationship: heavier loads = slower movement; lighter loads = faster movement
3. The primary training goal is to shift the curve RIGHT — more force at higher velocities
4. RFD matters as much as peak force in basketball, where actions occur in <300ms
5. Train athletes on their weak side of the curve, not just the zones they're already good at
6. "Athletes love to perform tasks they are good at. Coaches need to expose athletes to their weaknesses" [S11]

## Position Applications
- **PG**: Often already velocity-dominant; may need maximum strength and strength-speed zones
- **SG/SF**: Most balanced; train across the full curve with peak power emphasis
- **PF/C**: Often force-dominant; may need speed-strength and max velocity zones

## Common Mistakes
1. Only training maximum strength → missing explosive power development in peak power zone
2. Only training with bodyweight/light loads → missing the force foundation that enables peak power
3. Applying the same program to all positions → PG and C have very different force-velocity profiles
4. Ignoring RFD → the ability to produce force quickly is more relevant than peak force in basketball
