# Trapping and Double-Teaming

## Summary
Trapping and double-teaming are Herb Brown's primary disruptive defensive tactics — used to surprise, confuse, unnerve, and distract opponents, generate deflections and steals, and take the opposition out of their set offense and comfort zones. The system has a precise role structure (trappers, interceptors, goaltender), a set of absolute pressure rules, and detailed rotation schemes for every situation where the trap may come. [S1, pp.152-165]

The framework applies at every court level — full court, three-quarter court, half court — and in both man-to-man and pressure zone contexts. Coaches must decide in advance which trigger to use, who traps, and how to rotate the remaining three defenders.

## When to Use
- Against dominant post players who cannot be stopped 1-on-1
- Isolations: when a player is getting hurt by an offensive iso
- PnR situations: to stop penetration and limit the roll pass
- First wing pass in the half court to disrupt offensive rhythm
- After made field goals, made free throws, or dead-ball situations (full court)
- Against teams with poor passers, tentative players, or slow ball movers
- When trailing late — to force turnovers and speed up possessions

## Key Principles

### Role Structure — 2-2-1 Alignment
1. **Trappers (2):** The two defenders directly pressuring the ball — apply the trap at a right angle, attacking from the blind side. [S1, p.152]
2. **Interceptors (2):** The next two defenders nearest the trappers — cut off passing lanes, read the trapped player's eyes, and look to steal. [S1, pp.152, 158]
3. **Goaltender (1):** The defender farthest from the ball — protects the basket, prevents layups and easy scores. Has two interceptors AND two trappers in front. [S1, pp.152, 158]

*"Our alignment almost always looks like a two-two-one zone defense when two of our players are trapping the ball."* [S1, p.161]

### The Buddy System
- Each defender's "buddies" are the two nearest teammates on his immediate left and right.
- Buddies are responsible for working together in any trap or double-team situation. [S1, p.161]

### Three Absolute Pressure Rules
1. **No gut passes.** The ball cannot be passed to the middle of the floor — ever. Force passes uphill, outside, or across the top. [S1, p.161]
2. **Seal all traps.** The offense cannot be permitted to split the trap with the dribble. Trappers cross their inside legs to seal the gap. [S1, p.162]
3. **No sideline/baseline beats.** The offense cannot beat the trap on the short side. Force and stop the ball — treat every dribble-beat attempt as a baseline drive. [S1, p.161]

### Trapping Mechanics
- Come at a **right angle** to the ball — attack from the blind side, not from behind. [S1, p.152]
- Force the trapped man to pass **over the trapper's outside shoulder** to eliminate gut/penetrating passes. [S1, p.152]
- Interceptors read the **eyes** of the trapped player to decide whether to contain or go for the steal. [S1, p.152]
- Interceptor goes for the steal only if **90% certain** he can get it; otherwise feint the trap. [S1, p.160]
- Front offensive post players to eliminate the most dangerous outlet pass. [S1, p.152]
- Trap aggressively with **hands up** — trace, deflect, steal; bump with chests not hands; deflections lead to steals. [S1, p.162]
- Trappers have **inside legs crossed** to prevent the offense from splitting. [S1, p.162]
- When the ball is passed out of the trap, the trapper **sprints out of the trap immediately** — moving on the pass, not the catch. [S1, pp.154, 162]

### Trapping Triggers in Man-to-Man Defense
1. Double the **first wing pass** with the nearest defender (usually the passer's defender). [S1, p.155]
2. Half-court trap: trailing defender gets level with ball, traps dribbler near mid-court after forcing sideline. [S1, p.156]
3. Three-quarter court trap: trail defender traps near sideline in the backcourt when dribbler's back is turned. [S1, p.156]
4. Full-court trap including big men; all other defenders in extended full-court shell. [S1, p.156]
5. Run-and-jump: a defender alongside the dribbler leaves his man to run at the dribbler to trap or force a pass. Especially effective against head-down dribblers. [S1, p.157]

### Trapping Areas
- **Preferred:** Corners of the court (offense has fewest passing angles). [S1, p.162]
- **Also used:** Areas on either side of the mid-court line. [S1, p.162]
- **Above FT line:** Two nearest players at a right angle — normally a guard and a wing. [S1, p.162]
- **Below FT line:** Wing and baseline player nearest the ball. [S1, p.162]

### When to Trap After Misses vs. Makes
- After **made field goals, made free throws, or dead balls**: pick up full court and trap. [S1, p.157]
- After **misses in transition**: contain and organize — no doubling in transition unless all defenders are below the ball. [S1, p.157]

### One-and-Done Principle
When trapping in the corner and a skip pass is made to the weak side, the defense automatically transitions to man-to-man. Trapper sprints out of the trap to pick up a free man; everyone matches up and sprints in the direction of the pass. [S1, p.163]

## Post Trapping — Double-Teaming the Low Post

### When to Trap the Low Post
1. Outstanding players post up and receive on the low block
2. Post player isolates defender and takes him on the dribble to the low block
3. Isolation plays getting hurt by post player
4. Pick-and-roll — to stop penetration and limit the roll pass inside [S1, p.153]

### Trap Trigger Options (decide before the game)
1. Off the passer — when he cuts or spots up
2. From the nearest off-the-ball defender
3. Off the cutter after post entry — snap back once cutter has reached the goal
4. From a predesignated nonscorer
5. From the nearest baseline big man
6. From the weak-side high defender
7. Front the post + weak-side big crosses lane to sandwich behind on lob
8. From centerfield (nail) / top of the key area
9. On the post catch from a predetermined player or specific floor area
10. When the post player dribbles (attack from predetermined area)
11. Not until the post proves he warrants a double-team [S1, pp.159-160]

### Post Trap Mechanics
- **Trappers seal the inside** to take away the penetrating pass and turn the post player back to his defender. [S1, p.160]
- **Front the post** and keep the ball from going inside when you want to stay home and pressure perimeter shooters. [S1, p.153]
- Trap and be prepared to rotate whenever the ball is passed inside to a low-post threat. [S1, p.153]
- When helping (digging) from top or side: **spread out wide, step in, give a full-bodied show** — not an arm reach. Step in front of drivers to force offensive fouls or direction changes. [S1, p.153]
- If the post player's dribble has been used: **hands up and active** to make passing difficult. [S1, p.154]
- After the post is forced out and tries to repost: defender **fronts him immediately** to deny low-post position closer to the basket. [S1, p.160]
- Against a lob to the post: body up half a man to **prevent the seal, lock, and lob**. [S1, p.160]

### Key Post Trap Rotation Scenarios

**Trap from the Strong-Side High (X2 traps):**
- X2 traps from strong-side high; X1 denies pass back to passer; tactic is to encourage a lob or skip pass to the weak side.
- X2 and X5 trap post player 5; X3 and X4 in tandem at weak-side elbow and box. [S1, p.158]

**Rotation on Pass Out of the Post:**
- On the pass from post to top of key: X3 covers that pass to 2; X4 rotates to weak-side wing for 3; X2 (original trapper) rotates and fronts 4; X5, X3, X1 rotate to nail, weak-side elbow, and box. [S1, p.158]

**Trap from the Strong-Side Low (Baseline cutter snaps back):**
- X1 takes the baseline cutter to the rim, preventing a layup pass, then snaps back to double the post. Three other defenders rotate counterclockwise to cover the backboard. [S1, p.155]

**Trapping from the Top:**
- X2 seals and traps from the top of the key on the pass to 5; X1 drops between 1 and 5 to help; X3 covers pass to the weak-side corner or top; X1 and X2 rotate accordingly. [S1, p.155]

**Rotation When Ball Is Reversed and Dive Man Appears:**
- When the trap is set and a player dives to the goal: X3 forces the diver behind him and passes off to X4; on ball reversal, X3 and X4 rotate out; X2 rotates across the lane to front the dive man. [S1, p.159]

**Rotation When Post Feeder Cuts:**
- Trap comes from the post feeder's defender after cutter moves to weak side; X2, X3, X4 stay home; on ball reversal, X1 sprints out of trap over X4 to recover to his man. [S1, p.159]

## Pressing Zone Principles
1. Shade the ball; shadow or match up with the nearest offensive player in your area.
2. Play the ball tough — pressure the player with the ball always. [S1, p.161]
3. Trap as aggressively as the situation warrants — mix slow traps, quick traps, and fakes. [S1, p.161]
4. Trapper moves on the pass, NOT on the catch. [S1, p.162]
5. Keep inside legs crossed when trapping to prevent splits. [S1, p.162]
6. Hands up — trace, deflect, steal; bump with chest not hands. [S1, p.162]
7. Shut down all gut/penetrating passes; force uphill or across the top. [S1, p.162]
8. Put pressure on the ball whenever the offensive player passes, dribbles, or shoots. [S1, p.162]
9. Defenders communicate when offensive players duck into the lane or flash to the high post. [S1, p.162]
10. Rebound defensively with all five players after every trap situation. [S1, p.162]
11. After a ball escapes a corner trap: match up and recover immediately; sprint to free men in direction of the pass. [S1, p.162]

## Weak-Side Positions Reference
| Position | Location |
|---|---|
| Nail / Plug | Center of the lane at the FT line |
| Elbow | Corner of the FT line |
| Box | Low-block area |

Weak-side help always covers nail, elbow, and box — the three off-ball help positions. [S1, p.157]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Allowing a gut pass** → Defense is split; offense gets an easy basket. Correction: force all passes over the outside shoulder; no player goes through the middle of the trap.
2. **Trapper moves on the catch instead of the pass** → Recovery is a step slow; offense has time to reset. Correction: "move on the pass, not the catch."
3. **Interceptor lunges too early** → Takes himself out of the passing lane; offense skips past for an open shot. Correction: interceptor waits until ball is in the air; only goes for steal if 90% certain.
4. **Trap without weak-side help loaded** → Skip pass produces a wide-open layup. Correction: confirm weak-side nail/elbow/box coverage before committing to the trap.
5. **Goaltender leaves the basket** → Easy layup out of the corner trap. Correction: goaltender cannot vacate until a teammate rotates back to cover the goal.
6. **Closing out aggressively on weak offensive threats** → Foul or draw-and-kick opportunity. Correction: close out short on drivers, long on shooters; gamble only when 90% certain.
