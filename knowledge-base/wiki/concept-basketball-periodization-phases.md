# Basketball Training Periodization Phases

## Summary
Basketball's long competitive season (16–30 weeks depending on level) demands a carefully periodized approach to resistance training. Strength and conditioning professionals must manage volume, intensity, and frequency across four distinct phases — off-season, preseason, in-season, and playoff — while balancing practice load, travel, and individual athlete recovery status. The in-season phase is often split into two or three microcycles to allow flexible management of the season's "ebbs and flows" and to peak athletes for the playoffs [S11, pp.1-2].

## When to Use
- **Annual planning**: designing the full training year for a basketball program
- **In-season management**: deciding when to push training intensity vs. back off
- **Playoff preparation**: fine-tuning the program for peak performance at season's end
- **Individual athlete management**: accounting for playing time, travel, and recovery differences

## Key Principles
1. **Preseason goal**: Have athletes ready to sustain the rigors of early practice/preseason games; then pivot to maximizing performance for the start of the regular season
2. **In-season mesocycle splitting**: Split the 16–30 week in-season into 2–3 microcycles to allow flexibility and a playoff push
3. **Concurrent training adjustment**: Reduce volume and intensity in resistance training sessions to achieve a proper training effect alongside practice and competition
4. **Intensity escalation in second microcycle**: Gradually pick up weight-room intensity mid-season to maintain strength and power
5. **Playoff phase**: Fine-tune (do not overhaul) the program; reintroduce slight tweaks to regain any strength/power lost during the season
6. **Post-playoff onset**: Reduce volumes and intensities; maintain frequency; prioritize recovery between games
7. **Individual variation**: Recognize signs of growth and regression in each athlete; make provisions for travel schedule and recovery status

## Program Design Variables
When designing programs for each phase, guide decisions with these variables [S11, p.2]:
- **Frequency** — workouts per week; highly dependent on competition/travel schedule, individual playing time, and individual load/recovery status
- **Exercise order** — multijoint before single-joint movements (large to small muscle groups)
- **Intensity** — training percentage of 1RM or velocity-based training
- **Volume** — sets and repetition ranges
- **Rest periods** — dependent on training goals

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: participate in periodized resistance training program; communicate wellness status to strength staff
- **Coaches/strength staff**: work closely together and with medical staff to integrate training stress management

## Variations
### Two-Microcycle In-Season Model
Split the season into two halves. First half: maintenance focus, lower intensity. Second half: gradually increase intensity for playoff readiness.

### Three-Microcycle In-Season Model
Add a transition microcycle mid-season. Useful for longer NBA-length seasons (30 weeks) where early fatigue accumulation must be managed before a mid-season recovery phase.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Overhauling the program at playoff time** → stick to the established program with minor tweaks; major changes at playoff time increase injury risk
2. **Treating all athletes identically** → needs differ athlete to athlete; monitor each player's load and recovery status individually
3. **Ignoring travel schedule** → long road trips significantly affect recovery; reduce training load around heavy travel stretches
4. **Not splitting the in-season mesocycle** → without microcycle breaks, it is impossible to manage the ebb-and-flow of a 16–30 week season appropriately
