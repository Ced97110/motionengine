# Basketball Jump and Landing Biomechanics

## Summary
Jumping and landing biomechanics are critical to basketball performance and injury prevention. Basketball-specific jumping occurs during shooting, rebounding, and shot blocking, with males averaging 41–56 jumps per game and females 19–43 jumps per game [S10, p.38]. The countermovement jump (CMJ) is the most common basketball jump pattern, and understanding its four phases — preparatory, propulsive, flight, and landing — is essential for coaches and clinicians working to improve performance or reduce injury risk [S10, pp.39-40].

The landing phase is the highest injury-risk phase, as forces are greatest and neuromuscular control errors are most consequential. The propulsive phase is the performance phase, where knee and hip extensor moments predict jump height [S10, p.40].

## When to Use
- Designing strength and conditioning programs to improve vertical jump performance
- Screening athletes for injury risk based on landing mechanics (DVJ)
- Guiding plyometric progressions from beginners through advanced athletes
- Informing return-to-play testing after lower extremity injury

## Key Principles
1. **Four phases of jumping: preparatory → propulsive → flight → landing** — Each phase has distinct biomechanical characteristics and training/injury implications [S10, p.39].
2. **The stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) powers the CMJ** — During the preparatory phase, the body center of mass decelerates downward, eccentrically loading hip extensors, knee extensors, and ankle plantarflexors. This pre-stretch amplifies the subsequent concentric propulsive phase [S10, p.39].
3. **Knee and hip extensor moments predict jump height** — Increased knee and hip extensor moments during the propulsive phase are correlated with greater vertical jump height; these muscles are the primary training targets [S10, p.40].
4. **CMJ outperforms squat jump due to SSC** — The countermovement allows greater force production than starting from a static flexed position (squat jump) — program jump training using CMJ patterns [S10, p.40].
5. **Single-leg vs. double-leg jump height context matters** — Stationary double-leg jumps are ~25% higher than single-leg; with a running approach, single-leg takeoff surpasses double-leg by ~10% with 3–5 strides [S10, p.40].
6. **Optimal drop heights for DVJ are 15–30 cm** — Optimal drop height for drop vertical jump (DVJ) training varies by sex, age, and skill level; these movements are also used in injury screening [S10, p.40].
7. **Landing phase generates forces up to 4–5× bodyweight** — The landing phase is the highest-stress phase; forces are even greater as jump height improves, increasing demand for eccentric strength and neuromuscular control [S10, p.39].

## Player Responsibilities
- **C (Center)**: Jumps most frequently per game due to proximity to basket and rebounding/shot-blocking demands — highest cumulative landing load, highest priority for eccentric landing training.
- **PF/SF**: High jump frequency in rebounding and transition play — require bilateral and unilateral landing mechanics training.
- **PG/SG**: More single-leg jump patterns off penetration and fast-break finishes — single-leg landing mechanics training is essential.

## Variations
### Drop Vertical Jump (DVJ) for Screening
DVJ movements from 15–30 cm drops are used in screening methodologies to identify injury risk based on altered landing biomechanics (e.g., knee valgus, reduced hip/knee flexion) [S10, p.40].

### Plyometric Training Progressions
Plyometric training that uses DVJ movements is advocated to increase vertical jump height and performance in explosive activities — progressing drop height carefully as neuromuscular control is established [S10, p.40].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Training jump height without addressing landing mechanics** → The landing phase carries the highest injury risk; every jumping drill must include controlled landing technique.
2. **Neglecting hip and knee extensor strength** → These are the primary determinants of jump height in the propulsive phase — squat and hip hinge patterns must be prioritized.
3. **Using only bilateral jump training** → Basketball requires both single-leg and double-leg patterns; single-leg landings and takeoffs must be specifically trained.
4. **Starting with excessive drop heights in DVJ** → Optimal range is 15–30 cm; too high before neuromuscular readiness increases injury risk rather than building performance.
