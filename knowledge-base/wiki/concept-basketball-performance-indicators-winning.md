# Key Performance Indicators for Winning Basketball

## Summary
A statistical analysis of all 156 women's basketball matches at the 2004–2016 Olympic Games (312 team datasets) identified that shooting proficiency and defensive actions are the primary determinants of match outcome. Using both linear (logistic regression) and non-linear (classification tree) statistical techniques, a specific combination of four performance indicators was identified that provides a 91.1% probability of winning [S12, pp.19–25].

These findings align with prior research on men's Olympic basketball and confirm that the same four indicators — **field-goal percentage, defensive rebounds, steals, and turnovers** — are sex-independent predictors of elite basketball success [S12, p.23].

## When to Use
- Game planning and scouting report preparation
- Team selection decisions (prioritizing defensive athletes)
- In-game adjustments when trailing
- Post-game analysis to identify performance breakdowns
- Pre-Olympic or pre-tournament preparation prioritization

## Key Principles

### The Four Critical Performance Indicators
1. **Field-Goal Percentage** — Largest single effect on match outcome (d = 1.30, large effect). Winners averaged 77.9% vs. losers' 60.6% (normalized to possessions). The primary decision-node in the classification tree: FG% > 62.2% separates wins from losses at the highest level [S12, p.21]
2. **Defensive Rebounds** — Second-largest effect (d = 1.21, large effect). Winners averaged 47.4 vs. losers' 35.9. Teams that secure the defensive glass deny second-chance points and initiate faster transitions [S12, p.21]
3. **Steals** — Third-largest effect (d = 0.90, large effect). Winners averaged 15.5 vs. losers' 10.8. Strongly associated with speed, agility, anaerobic power, and repeated sprint ability in elite female basketball players [S12, p.23]
4. **Turnovers** — Small but statistically significant effect (d = −0.35). Winners committed *fewer* turnovers (25.7) than losers (28.4). High steals + low turnovers combination dramatically increases loss probability [S12, p.21]

### The Maximum Win Probability Combination (91.1%)
When a team achieves ALL of the following (normalized to ball possessions):
- Field-goal percentage **> 62.2%**
- Defensive rebounds **> 30.8**
- Steals **> 9.3**
- Turnovers **< 36.6**

→ Probability of winning = **91.1%** [S12, p.22]

### The Maximum Loss Probability Combination (96.7%)
When a team has:
- Field-goal percentage **≤ 62.2%**
- Steals **≤ 18.8**
- Turnovers **> 18.4**

→ Probability of losing = **96.7%** [S12, p.21]

### Additional Significant Indicators
- **Assists** (d = 0.91, large): Closely related to FG% — ball movement creates higher-quality shots
- **Blocked shots** (d = 0.66, medium): A secondary defensive contribution
- **Offensive rebounds** (d = 0.55, medium): Included in the best logistic regression model
- **Free-throw percentage** (d = 0.52, medium): Less discriminating than field-goal percentage
- **Fouls committed**: NOT significantly different between winners and losers

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Minimize turnovers; create shooting opportunities for teammates through assists; pressure defenders into steals via full/half-court press situations
- **SG**: Maximize field-goal percentage through shot selection; contribute steals on guard-to-guard passes
- **SF**: Defensive rebound on every missed shot; contest perimeter shots to reduce opponent FG%
- **PF**: Defensive rebound aggressively; limit opponent offensive rebounds; shot quality over quantity
- **C**: Dominate defensive glass (primary rebounding responsibility); blocked shots; limit opponent paint attempts to reduce their FG%

## Variations
### Balanced vs. Unbalanced Games
Prior research on the Spanish Women's League found that during close games (≤12 point difference), 1-point, 3-point FG%, assists, and defensive rebounds are most important. During unbalanced games (>12 point difference), 2-point FG%, defensive rebounds, and steals dominate [S12, p.20].

### Men's vs. Women's Games
The same four indicators (FG%, defensive rebounds, steals, turnovers) predict outcome in both men's and women's Olympic basketball. However, women's teams generate more steals per game (15.5 winners) compared to men's teams (10.5 winners) — likely related to anthropometric differences [S12, p.23].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Focusing only on offense** → Defensive rebounds and steals have as large an effect on winning as shooting percentage; defensive culture must be a priority [S12, p.22]
2. **Ignoring turnover differential** → Teams that force turnovers via steals but also commit turnovers themselves undermine their defensive advantage [S12, p.21]
3. **Maximizing shot volume over shot quality** → FG% is the key variable, not total shots; reducing low-percentage attempts raises the indicator [S12, p.21]
4. **Selecting athletes only for offensive skill** → Athletes with superior speed, agility, anaerobic power, and repeated sprint ability generate more steals; these physical qualities should factor into selection [S12, p.23]
