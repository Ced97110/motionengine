# Triangle Offense (S13)

## Summary
The Triangle Offense is a continuity half-court system built around one integrated primary set from which backdoor cuts, pick-and-rolls, post splits, isolations, and ball-reversal actions all emerge through read-and-react decision-making. Unlike a playbook of 75-150 plays, the triangle team masters one core formation and its built-in escapes and releases — making it easier to teach and harder to scout.

The offense begins with a conventional two-guard front, a post player at the midpost strong side, and forwards at the free-throw line extended on opposite sides. **Passing dictates cuts, floor balance, screens, and shot opportunities.** Four perimeter positions are interchangeable, and the offense can be initiated from either a two- or three-guard front (using a ballhandling small forward). It can also begin from backcourt with speed cuts when defensive guards pressure [S13, pp.142-146]. 

*Note: This version is distinct from the Phil Jackson/Tex Winter triangle covered in [[concept-triangle-offense]] and [[concept-triangle-offense-principles]] — it shares the name and some principles but has different specific option trees.*

## When to Use
- As the primary half-court offense for teams that value ball control, interchangeable roles, and read-and-react play over memorized play sets
- Against pressure defense — the three-guard front option minimizes full-court man and front-court traps
- When the team has multi-skill players who can function at multiple positions
- Against opponents who have scouted a large playbook — the single-set system is difficult to prepare for

## Key Principles
1. **Passing dictates movement** — every pass triggers a specific and immediate action; players read which pass was made and react accordingly.
2. **Read the defense and react** — the most important player skill is reading defensive positioning before committing to a cut or screen.
3. **Four interchangeable perimeter positions** — any of the four perimeter players (O1, O2, O3, O4) can occupy any perimeter spot; isolations become just another option within the continuity.
4. **Post split rule** — the passer to the post sets the screen on the post split action.
5. **Escapes and releases built in** — every movement sequence has a built-in counter when the primary option is covered [S13, pp.142-145].

## Formation
Two-guard front (O1 at guard right, O2 at guard left), O3 at strong-side wing (free-throw line extended), O4 at weak-side wing (free-throw line extended), O5 at strong-side midpost. O1 passes to O3 to initiate, then cuts to the strong-side deep corner.

## Phases / Options

### Option 1: Corner Pass → Baseline Cut → Corner PnR
1. O3 passes back to O1 in the corner.
2. O3 takes two quick steps middle, then pivots hard on the left foot to get head and shoulders past the defender.
3. O5 steps out and screens for O3 cutting baseline — O1 looks for O3's return.
4. If no pass to O3, O3 clears to the far corner. O1 waits for O3 to clear.
5. O5 sets a corner sideline PnR for O1. O1 moves off the screen to go middle.
6. O5 reads the defense: roll to the basket or pop to the corner.
7. O1 dribble-drives middle looking for a layup, draw-and-kick, or pull-up jumper [S13, p.143].

<!-- DIAGRAM: Figures 6.18-6.19 — O3 cuts baseline off O5 screen; O5 sets corner PnR for O1 driving middle [S13, p.143]. -->

### Option 2: Post Entry → Backdoor → Post Split
1. O3 passes to O5 at the midpost (instead of corner pass).
2. **Automatic backdoor reverse for O1** out of the corner — a pass from O5 to O1 should produce a layup or foul.
3. If no pass to O1, and O5 doesn't make a quick 1-on-1 move, O3 runs a **post split** by screening for O2. (Rule: the passer to the post sets the screen.)
4. O2 makes a hard left-footed jab step then uses O3's screen and a pass from O5 for a drive or jump shot.
5. If O2 cannot get a good shot, O2 maintains the dribble and runs a sideline PnR with O5 — O5 can roll or pop to the corner [S13, p.144].

<!-- DIAGRAM: Figure 6.20 — O3 passes to O5; O1 backdoor; O3 sets screen for O2 on post split [S13, p.144]. -->

### Option 3: Weak-Side Flash Cut
1. **Timing play**: O4 on the weak side reads that there is no pass to O1 (backdoor) and no O3-O2 post split. O4 makes a hard flash cut to the top of the free-throw circle and receives a sharp pass from O3.
2. O2, beyond the three-point line, fakes a step as if catching the pass, then makes a **backdoor speed cut** for a layup.
3. If O2 doesn't get the backdoor pass, O2 immediately pivots and posts up.
4. O3 turns and sets a screen for O1 coming out of the corner toward the ball.
5. O4 reads and passes to O1, to O2 on a post-up, or takes his own shot [S13, pp.144-145].

<!-- DIAGRAM: Figures 6.21-6.22 — O4 flash cut to elbow; O2 backdoor; O3 screens for O1 coming from corner; O4 makes the play [S13, pp.144-145]. -->

### Option 4: Weak-Side Guard PnR
1. O3 passes to O2 at the top of the circle.
2. O4 has the option of setting a **back screen** or a **sideline PnR** for O2, depending on defender position. O2 reads the screen type and runs the two-player game for a good shot.
3. After passing to O2, O3 turns and sets a screen for O1 coming out of the corner — putting O1 in position as the safety back on defense [S13, p.145].

<!-- DIAGRAM: Figure 6.23 — O3 passes to O2 at top; O4 sets back screen or PnR; O3 screens for O1 [S13, p.145]. -->

## Isolation (Example: Isolating O2)
1. O2 and O3 exchange positions. O4 drops to weak-side low block. O1 passes to O2 and goes to the strong-side corner.
2. O2 reverses to O3; O2 makes a brush cut off O5 (who set up higher to free the path) and sets a down screen for O4, who relocates to the middle of the free-throw circle.
3. O3 dribbles left toward the sideline to establish a passing angle. O3 inserts the ball to O2 posting up strong.
4. O3 clears out — O2 has the entire side of the floor: quick post-up move, step-back, or face-up 1-on-1 dribble drive [S13, pp.145-146].

## Transition to Opposite Side
When O1 enters on the right but wants to run the triangle left:
1. O1 passes to O2. O2 quickly passes to O4 and relocates corner ball side.
2. O5 crosses the lane to the strong side and establishes a midpost position.
3. O1 and O3 take weak-side responsibilities. O4 now has the four passing options and the play continues [S13, p.146].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not reading which option is available** — players must scan all reads in sequence (backdoor → guard-around → flash cut → PnR) rather than defaulting to a favorite option.
2. **Post split screen not set by the passer** — the rule is explicit: whoever passes to the post sets the screen; violating this breaks the continuity.
3. **O1 not clearing the corner quickly enough** — O1 must relocate on the strong-side corner before any of O3's options can unfold.
4. **O4 not reading the timing on the flash cut** — Option 3 is a timing play; O4 must watch both Option 1 and Option 2 fail before cutting.
