# Defensive Practice Philosophy — Herb Brown

## Summary
Herb Brown's defensive practice philosophy centers on three pillars: (1) building defense progressively from 1-on-1 to 5-on-5 so every individual skill connects to team defense, (2) making drills as competitive as possible under realistic game conditions including shot and game clocks, and (3) constantly reviewing fundamentals to prevent slippage — because a breakdown in a critical game moment often traces to a concept not practiced recently enough. [S1, pp.52-53]

## When to Use
- Pre-season camp through the entire season
- Any time video review reveals defensive breakdowns
- As a framework for designing daily practice plans

## Key Principles
1. **Never take for granted that a defensive tactic has been adequately covered.** Constantly review schemes to prevent slippage. A forgotten assignment in a critical game moment can cost a win. [S1, p.52]
2. **Build defense progressively:** start 1-on-1 → 2-on-2 → 3-on-3 → 4-on-4 on both half- and full court. [S1, p.52]
3. **No switching at the start.** Insist players fight through screens by talking and helping each other. Switching is only taught after communication and fight-through habits are established. [S1, p.52]
4. **Pressure the ball and change direction.** Want to control and slow the opponent's advance up the court by pressuring and constantly making the dribbler change direction. [S1, p.52]
5. **Make drills competitive.** Limit dribbles to add constraints; also allow free-lance play. The offense tries to beat the defense as efficiently as possible while the defense tries to prevent it. [S1, p.52]
6. **Re-create actual game situations.** Break down individual offensive schemes: 2-, 3-, 4-, and 5-man situations on both half- and full court. [S1, pp.52-53]
7. **Use shot and game clocks in drills.** This creates a more realistic environment, makes players aware of time-score-situation, and teaches spontaneous reaction under pressure. [S1, p.53]
8. **Extend the defense and pick up opponents early.** "We love to extend our defense and pick up opponents early." [S1, p.53]
9. **Practice on both full-court and half-court.** Even if personnel doesn't warrant full-court pressing, practicing full-court improves players and helps conditioning. [S1, p.53]

## Practical Implementation
- Begin with individual fundamentals: defensive stance, slides, pivots
- Progress through 1v1, 2v2, 3v3, 4v4, 5v5 on half- and full court
- Add specific offensive situations to defend (screens, cuts, post feeds, DHOs, pick-and-roll)
- Build toward the shell drill which ties all individual responsibilities together
- Repeat with constant review throughout the season — not just preseason

## Common Mistakes
1. Assuming a concept has been learned after one practice → review it repeatedly throughout the season
2. Allowing players to switch immediately instead of fighting through screens → build communication habits first
3. Running drills without competitive stakes → players don't prepare for game pressure
4. Only practicing half-court defense → full-court work improves conditioning and teaches early ball pressure
