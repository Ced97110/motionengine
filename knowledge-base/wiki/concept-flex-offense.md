# Flex Offense

## Summary
The flex offense is a continuous half-court motion system built around back screens, vertical screens, and screen-the-screener actions. Developed and popularized at the international level by Argentine national team coach the head coach, the flex keeps all five players moving and interchangeable — no player is permanently stationed at one spot. Every player, regardless of position, plays both inside and outside the paint, facing the basket and with their back to it.

Magnano adopted the flex because his Argentina teams lacked a dominant post player but possessed highly versatile, skilled off-ball movers. The offense exploits larger, slower defenders by requiring them to navigate a constant stream of screens, and it wears down opponents throughout the game through continuous movement. Every player becomes a scoring threat, making it nearly impossible for defenses to key on one or two players. [S7, pp.137-146]

## When to Use
- Primary half-court set when transition advantage does not materialize
- Teams with multiple perimeter scorers and versatile forwards/bigs who can shoot from outside
- When opponents have size advantages in the post — spread them out and attack off screens
- Ideal when opponent is slower and susceptible to being worn down by continuous movement
- High school through pro/international levels

## Key Principles
1. **All five players move** — no permanent positions; center, power forward, and perimeter players all rotate through inside and outside spots
2. **Four passing options** — the ball handler must always have four available passes; if he doesn't, the spacing is wrong
3. **Screen the screener** — immediately after a player sets a back screen for a teammate, a third player sets a screen for that screener; the screener is often the most open player on the court
4. **Read the defense** — cutters choose over or under each screen based on how the defender plays; ball handler reads the cutter's move and reacts
5. **Proper spacing and timing** — players must not arrive at the screen too early or too late; flow is as important as execution of individual actions
6. **Vertical and back screens for perimeter players** — emphasis on these two screen types; screeners always pop out or roll after setting
7. **Overload one side** — use ball movement to concentrate defenders, then attack the overloaded side or kick to the weak side
8. **Teach the whole offense first** — show the complete flow before breaking into parts; players need a global picture to grasp the nuances [S7, p.139]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (1)**: Initiates the offense with the first pass; sets screens and uses screens; pops out after screening; can occupy any perimeter or low-post position depending on the flow
- **SG (2)**: Primary back-screen cutter; reads the screen (over or under); posts up if the pass is not available; can also receive screen-the-screener action and pop high
- **SF (3)**: Occupies the weak-side corner/wing; cuts to and from the low post; sets back screens; exploits vertical screens to receive passes at the high post or wing
- **PF (4)**: Passes and screens in the high-post/elbow area; rolls or pops after screens; can play both high post and low post positions in the flow
- **C (5)**: Often receives the first pass from 1; sets vertical screens; rolls to the basket after screening; occupies the low post and can be the primary post target in Option 3

## Variations

### Basic Flex (Initial Action)
1 passes to 5. Simultaneously, 2 uses 4's back screen to cut to the low-post area inside the three-second lane. 4 immediately receives a screen from 1 (screen-the-screener). 4 comes high; 1 pops out. The same pattern repeats on the other side: 5 passes to 4, 2 back-screens for 3, and 5 screens the screener (2). [S7, p.139]

### Option 1 — Vertical Screen Counter
1 passes to 5, then sets a vertical screen for 4. 4 uses 1's screen to come up and receive from 5. 1 rolls to the opposite low post. When 4 has the ball, 1 back-screens for 3, then receives a vertical screen from 5 (screen-the-screener). 4 passes to 1. From here, 1 has four pass options: to 2 off a screen of 3 or 4; to 3 off 5's screen; to 5 in the low post on the ball side; or to 4 at the opposite low post. [S7, pp.140-141]

### Option 2 — Defensive Switch Counter
When defenders switch on the 3-screens-for-2 action, 2 curls high around 3 and makes a back screen for 4. 3 exploits 5's screen on the other side and goes to the opposite corner. Now 1 has: pass to 4 after 2's back screen; pass to 2 who pops; pass to 3 off 5's screen; or pass to 5 who rolls after screening 3. [S7, pp.141-142]

### Option 3 — Post Feed
Used specifically when the team needs to enter the ball to the low post. 1 passes to 5; simultaneously 2 exploits 4's back screen and goes to the opposite low post. 5 makes a return pass to 1, who has gotten open. 4 ducks into the lane to receive from 1 and plays his defender 1-on-1. [S7, pp.142-143]

### Corner Overload
When the ball reaches the corner (e.g., 2 receives in the corner), the low-post player on the ball side (1) sets a vertical back screen for 5, who goes to the low post. 2 can pass to 5 in the low post or to 1, who pops out after screening. [S7, p.140]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Cutting too close to the screener** → Cutter must read the defender and go over or under with deliberate footwork; shoulder-to-hip contact
2. **Screener leaving too early** → Set and hold the screen until the cutter has gone by; then pop or roll
3. **Ball handler waiting instead of reacting** → The pass must be made immediately when a cutter gets open; delay kills the advantage
4. **Poor spacing** → If cutters end up stacked on the same side, passing lanes close and the defense recovers easily
5. **Teaching parts before the whole** → Show the entire offense in flow first so players understand what they are working toward [S7, p.139]
6. **Big men unwilling to play outside** → In the flex, everyone plays all positions; bigs must be comfortable setting perimeter screens and popping to the top
