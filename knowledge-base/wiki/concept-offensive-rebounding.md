# Offensive Rebounding

## Summary
Offensive rebounding begins with reading the shot and anticipating where the ball will land after hitting the rim or backboard. Once the shot is identified, the offensive rebounder must then defeat the defensive box-out using one of three contact-escape techniques and reach the ball before the defense can secure it. 

The shot location determines the likely rebound angle: misses tend to bounce to the opposite side from which the shot was taken. Offensive rebounders who are the shooter, one pass away, or more than one pass away all face different distances and angles to the ball — each requiring a different sense of urgency and approach path.

## When to Use
- On every shot attempt — all five offensive players should have a plan.
- Especially when the defense is not communicating or is slow to box out.
- In late-game situations where a second-chance opportunity is critical.

## Key Principles
1. **Locate the ball first:** Read the shot trajectory as it leaves the shooter's hand.
2. **Anticipate landing zone:** Ball tends to bounce to the weak side or opposite the shot origin.
3. **Attack early:** Do not wait for the defender to make contact — move before the box-out is set.
4. **Defeat the box-out with a technique:** One of three contact-escape methods must be used.
5. **Attack the defender's blind side:** When contact is broken and the defender turns their head to find you, attack the side opposite where they look.

## Three Escape Techniques
### 1. Butt-to-Butt Spin
As the defender makes contact trying to box out, go butt-to-butt and spin off the defender, continuing toward the ball. Works best when the defender has not yet established solid hip-to-hip contact.

### 2. Swim Technique
Use an arm-over (swimming) motion to escape the defender's body contact before the box-out is fully established, then hustle directly to the ball.

### 3. Step-Back and Slash
Step backward away from initial contact, scraping off the defender. Once free, slash to the ball. Read which shoulder the defender turns — if they look over their right shoulder, attack their left side.

## Three Distance Scenarios
- **Shooter:** Must react immediately; the defender is already in close proximity.
- **One Pass Away:** Has a moment to read the shot but must fight through contact from close range.
- **More Than One Pass Away:** Greatest distance to travel; must read shot early and sprint to anticipate the landing zone.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Watching the shot instead of the defender** → Correct: Find the defender first, then go get the ball.
2. **Trying to go through the defender** → Correct: Use a technique to go around, over, or away from them.
3. **Attacking the side where the defender is looking** → Correct: Attack the opposite shoulder — blindside the defender.
4. **Not anticipating the rebound angle** → Correct: Study shot trajectory and typical miss patterns for each shooter.
