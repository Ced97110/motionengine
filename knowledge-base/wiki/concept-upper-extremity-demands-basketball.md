# Upper Extremity Movement Demands in Basketball

## Summary
While the lower extremities provide explosive locomotion, the upper extremities serve as the primary contact point with the ball and are critical for shooting, dribbling, passing, defending, rebounding, and absorbing contact. Basketball players perform more than 200 overhead upper extremity movements per game (defined as any movement with >90° of shoulder elevation), with backcourt players performing significantly more than frontcourt players. Unlike lower extremity demands, upper extremity demands have been less systematically studied, partly because upper extremity injuries are less common and motion-capture technology has historically been lower-extremity focused.

## When to Use
- When designing position-specific upper extremity strength and conditioning programs
- When estimating upper extremity training loads and recovery requirements
- When evaluating the sport-specificity of shoulder, elbow, wrist, and hand rehabilitation exercises

## Key Principles
1. **>200 overhead movements per game**: All basketball players exceed 200 overhead upper extremity actions (>90° shoulder elevation) per game; guards perform more than frontcourt players [S10, pp.53-54]
2. **Dribbling is position-dependent**: Backcourt players dribble ~60 times per game; frontcourt players ~20 times. Each backcourt dribbling episode lasts ~4 seconds over ~12-13 m. Backcourt players travel >700 m while dribbling — 10-15% of total game distance [S10, p.54]
3. **Jumping as a proxy for upper extremity demand**: Basketball players rarely jump without upper extremity elevation; ~40 jumps per game provide the baseline [S10, p.54]
4. **Jump type breakdown**: ~20 jumps for shot blocking (defense), ~12 for jump shots, ~13 for rebounds, ~10 for layups per game [S10, p.54]
5. **Physical contact through the upper extremities**: Screen setting (~49 instances/game for frontcourt) and boxing out (~72 instances/game for frontcourt) require significant upper extremity contact and force absorption [S10, p.54]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG (Backcourt)**: ~60 dribbles/game, >700 m of dribble distance, more overhead movements than frontcourt — highest upper extremity skill demand; dribbling with non-dominant hand significantly decreases speed and must be trained bilaterally
- **SF**: Mixed demand — some perimeter dribbling and some post contact; bridging between guard and big demands
- **PF/C (Frontcourt)**: ~20 dribbles/game but highest physical contact load — 49 screens set and 72 boxing-out contacts per game require upper extremity strength and stability

## Variations
### Overhead Movement Demands by Skill
- Shooting: shoulder flexion 90–135° during ball elevation phase, coupled with elbow extension, wrist flexion at release
- Blocking: maximal overhead shoulder elevation and extension against offensive force
- Rebounding: bilateral overhead reach with high contact forces from opposing players

## Common Mistakes
1. **Training only the dominant hand** → Dribbling speed loss is more marked with the non-dominant hand and more during direction changes; bilateral training is essential for all backcourt players [S10, p.55]
2. **Neglecting upper extremity strength for frontcourt players** → Screen setting and boxing out generate significant forces — frontcourt players need upper extremity contact resistance strength, not just skill
3. **Underestimating overhead injury risk** → 200+ overhead movements per game creates cumulative shoulder stress that should be monitored and addressed with strength/mobility work
