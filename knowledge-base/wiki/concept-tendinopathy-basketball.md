# Tendinopathy Rehabilitation in Basketball

## Summary
Tendinopathy is one of the most common overuse injuries in basketball players, with **patellar tendinopathy** (jumper's knee) and **Achilles tendinopathy** occurring most frequently. These injuries typically result from excessive load — acute or chronic — placed on the tendon with inadequate recovery time. The primary symptoms are localized tendon pain and stiffness, accompanied by alterations in tendon structure, mechanical properties, and muscle-tendon function. A critical challenge is that athletes can often initially continue playing through symptoms, delaying treatment until the condition becomes debilitating [S10, pp.749–755].

Controlled tendon loading is supported by the **highest level of evidence** for tendinopathy treatment — not rest alone, and not passive modalities alone. Recovery time is notoriously long compared to other overuse injuries.

## When to Use
- Player presents with localized tendon pain and stiffness provoked by activity
- Pain initially after activity → progresses to pain at onset of activity → finally pain throughout activity and at rest
- Patellar: inferior pole of patella pain with jumping/squatting
- Achilles: mid-substance tendon pain aggravated by running/jumping; morning stiffness
- Stiffness most pronounced in the morning and after prolonged rest

## Key Principles

### Diagnosis

**Patellar Tendinopathy:**
- Pain localized to **inferior pole of patella**
- Provoked by jumping and squatting
- **Single-leg decline squat test** (25° decline, single-leg squat to 50° knee flexion) — pain rating on NPRS; positive for patellar tendinopathy; test-retest reliability: Kappa = 1.0; +LR: 4.2

**Achilles Tendinopathy:**
- Pain at **mid-substance of tendon** (or at insertion sites)
- Aggravated by running and jumping
- **Royal London Hospital Test**: palpate most symptomatic region; positive if symptoms decrease when foot is dorsiflexed or plantarflexed
- **Painful Arc Sign**: palpate focal thickening nodule; positive if nodule moves beneath fingers with plantarflexion/dorsiflexion

**Ultrasound Findings:**
- Hypoechoic regions
- Tendon thickening
- Loss of collagen fiber organization
- Potentially neovascularization

### Mechanotherapy — The Mechanism of Controlled Loading
1. Loads on the tendon create **strain** (relative tendon lengthening)
2. Tendons respond through **mechanotransduction** — cells convert mechanical stimuli into biochemical responses → collagen synthesis and tendon remodeling
3. **Dose-response relationship**: up to a threshold, greater load = greater collagen synthesis
4. **Duration matters**: longer duration contractions → greater tendon structure improvements vs. short duration contractions
5. There is likely an **upper limit** beyond which load is detrimental — optimal parameters not yet established
6. Practical implication: higher loads at longer durations are preferred over shorter duration/lower loads — within tolerable pain limits

[S10, pp.754–755]

### Three-Phase Rehabilitation Framework

**Phase 1: Symptom Management and Load Reduction**
- Goal: reduce symptom severity, initiate therapeutic loading, curtail abusive training
- Educate player on treatment plan, recovery timeline, and load management
- Load management discussion involves player, coaches, managers, team doctors, training staff
- Therapeutic loading: begin with **bilateral bodyweight exercises** (heel raises, squats)
- Highly symptomatic: isometric exercises, constrained ROM, partial bodyweight
- Curtail sport-specific loads: reduce minutes played, substitute form shooting for jump shots, add recovery days
- **Complete cessation of activity is NOT necessary** — use pain-monitoring model to guide continued activity

**Phase 2: Controlled Tendon Loading**
- Advance to **unilateral activities** with external resistance
- Progress to full ROM and greater external loads as symptoms improve
- Contraction type (eccentric, concentric, combined) produces similar collagen synthesis when resistance is similar — use what the athlete tolerates
- Therapeutic exercises can be done on the court integrated with basketball activities

**Phase 3: Return to Sport**
- Sport-specific loading to increase tissue tolerance to running and jumping
- Requires careful prescription of **recovery days** between sport loading sessions
- Progress from sport-modified activities → full game demands

### The Pain-Monitoring Model (Key Tool for Phase 1 and 2)
Allows continued activity using NPRS (0–10 pain scale) as a guide:

| Zone | NPRS Range | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Safe zone | 0–2 | No or minimal pain — ideal |
| Acceptable zone | 2–5 | Acceptable — can continue activity |
| High risk zone | 5–10 | Stop or reduce activity |

**Four Rules:**
1. Pain is allowed to reach **5/10** during activity
2. Pain after completion of activity is allowed to reach **5/10**
3. Pain the **morning after** activity should not exceed **5/10**
4. Pain and stiffness must **not increase from week to week**

[S10, p.755]

### Two Types of Tendon Loading in Rehabilitation
1. **Therapeutic loading** — strengthening exercises promoting tendon remodeling → can be performed daily, initiated immediately
2. **Sport-specific loading** — running/jumping to increase tissue tolerance → requires recovery days, initiated in later phases

## Pain Classification by Effect on Athletic Performance

| Level | Pain Description | Sports Performance |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | No pain | Normal |
| 2 | Pain only with extreme exertion | Normal |
| 3 | Pain with extreme exertion and 1–2 hr afterward | Normal or slightly decreased |
| 4 | Pain during and after vigorous activities | Somewhat decreased |
| 5 | Pain during activity, forcing termination | Markedly decreased |
| 6 | Pain during daily activities | Unable to perform |

[S10, p.749]

## Outcome Measures

### For Patellar Tendinopathy
- **VISA-P Questionnaire** — 8-item, 0–100 (higher = better); ICC 0.91–0.97; minimal detectable change: 12.2 points
- **OSTRC Overuse Injury Questionnaire** — sensitivity 79%, specificity 98% for patellar tendinopathy
- **Five single-legged squat test** — pain on VAS during 5 single-leg squats
- **Single-leg decline squat test** — primary provocation test (detailed above)
- **Ultrasound thickness** (1 cm distal to inferior patella pole, longitudinal): ICC 0.82–0.91 intra-rater
- **Ultrasound cross-sectional area**: ICC 0.87–0.96 intra-rater

### For Achilles Tendinopathy
- **VISA-A Questionnaire** — 8-item, 0–100 (100 = no symptoms, full activity); ICC = 0.93; minimally clinically important difference: 16 points
- **Heel-Rise Test** — single-leg on 10° inclined surface, 2 fingertips on wall for support; max reps at 30/min; ICC 0.78–0.84
- **Hopping** — continuous single-leg rhythmical jumping (like jump rope); ICC 0.83–0.94
- **Drop Countermovement Jump** — single-leg on box, drop and maximal jump; ICC 0.88–0.92
- **Ultrasound thickness**: ICC 0.98–0.99 intra-rater
- **Ultrasound cross-sectional area**: ICC 0.89–0.97 intra-rater

[S10, pp.751–753]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Communicate pain levels honestly using the NPRS scale; comply with load reduction recommendations; complete therapeutic loading exercises daily as prescribed
- **Coaches/Managers**: Collaborate with medical staff on training modifications; reduce provocative training demands during rehab phases

## Training and Competition Review Checklist (for Load Management)
- Frequency and duration of training and competition
- Type and intensity of training (strength, jump training)
- Recent changes in training/competition load
- Current phase of the competitive season
- Recovery time between bouts of activity
- Provocative activities identified
- Pain levels: morning, before/during/after activity

## Common Mistakes
1. **Complete rest** → deconditioning and no stimulus for tendon remodeling; controlled loading is superior
2. **Returning to sport when pain-free** → pain absence ≠ full tendon recovery; impairments persist after symptoms resolve
3. **Eccentric-only protocols** → combined concentric/eccentric/isometric protocols are equally effective when resistance is matched
4. **Using the injured side against the uninjured side for all criteria** → for Achilles, compare ruptured side to its own maximum available height, not the healthy side
5. **No communication between medical staff and coaches** → load management decisions require everyone to be informed

## Variations
### Patellar Tendinopathy (Jumper's Knee)
- Pain at inferior pole of patella with jumping and squatting
- Single-leg decline squat test is the primary provocation test
- Begin therapeutic loading with bilateral squats and progressing to single-leg eccentric squats

### Achilles Tendinopathy
- Pain at mid-substance of tendon with running and jumping
- Royal London Hospital Test and Painful Arc Sign are primary diagnostic tools
- Heel-rise test is the primary functional performance measure
- Greater emphasis on calf/plantar flexor progressive loading
