# Olecranon Bursitis and Elbow Neuropathies in Basketball

## Summary
Olecranon bursitis is reportedly common in basketball players despite a low general population incidence (0.01–0.1% of hospital admissions). It results from repetitive posterior elbow trauma or pressure, occasionally from a single fall. Distinguishing non-infectious from infectious (septic) bursitis is critical. Compressive neuropathies of the ulnar, median, and radial nerves at the elbow can cause pain and weakness affecting basketball performance. Most resolve with nonoperative management; surgical decompression is available when conservative care fails. [S10, pp.288-290]

## Olecranon Bursitis

### Anatomy
- Olecranon bursa: superficial bursa between olecranon and skin; forms ages 7–10 years [S10, p.288]
- Function: lubricating structure protecting the posterior elbow
- Bursitis = abnormal increase in fluid volume [S10, p.288]

### Etiology in Basketball
- Repetitive trauma or pressure to the posterior elbow
- Single fall onto elbow
- Reportedly common in basketball players (incidence in basketball unknown) [S10, p.288]

### Diagnosis
- **History**: falls onto elbow or repetitive posterior pressure; feeling of "fullness" over back of elbow
- **Exam**: palpable fullness over olecranon; preserved elbow ROM (pain localized to olecranon, NOT with pronation/supination — this rules out infectious joint)
- **Distinguishing septic from non-septic**:
  - Both present with posterior erythema
  - Systemic infection signs help diagnosis
  - **Gold standard**: positive culture from aspiration [S10, p.288]
  - Radiographs: rule out fracture
  - Transillumination or ultrasound: identify loculations in chronic cases [S10, p.288]

### Treatment
- **Nonoperative (first-line)**:
  - Avoid inciting activities (resting elbow on table/armrest)
  - Anti-inflammatory medications + compression
  - Aspiration: 16-gauge angiocatheter with surrounding dressing for 3 days = fewer recurrences vs. single aspiration [S10, p.288]
- **Surgical**: excision of bursa when nonoperative fails

### Rehabilitation After Surgery
- Splint worn 2 weeks
- Compressive wrap after splint
- Return to basketball: **6 weeks after surgery** [S10, p.289]
- Note: resolution of bursitis can take up to **3 months**; compliance with compression and anti-inflammatories is mandatory [S10, p.289]

## Elbow Neuropathies

### General
- Compressive neuropathies can cause elbow pain, paresthesia, and weakness in basketball players [S10, p.289]
- Diagnosis: electrophysiologic testing (confirm diagnosis, extent of damage) + MRI (soft tissue anatomy around nerve) [S10, p.289]

### Ulnar Nerve
- Travels through arcade of Struthers at intermuscular septum (~70% of individuals) [S10, p.289]
- Descends through cubital tunnel deep to Osborne ligament
- Enters forearm between two heads of FCU
- **Symptoms**: paresthesia of ring and small fingers; provoked by prolonged elbow flexion (talking on phone, sleeping)
- **Chronic compression**: intrinsic hand muscle atrophy
- **Tests**: positive Tinel at cubital tunnel; prolonged elbow flexion test
- Ulnar nerve may subluxate over medial epicondyle → friction injury and predisposition to neuropathy [S10, p.289]

### Median Nerve
- Crosses antecubital fossa medial to brachial artery; passes under ligament of Struthers (found in ~1% of population) and between two heads of pronator teres [S10, p.289]
- **Symptoms**: pain in proximal anterior forearm; paresthesia in median nerve distribution
- **Weakness**: pronation, flexion, and thenar atrophy [S10, p.289]

### Radial Nerve
- Enters radial tunnel at the radiocapitellar joint
- Posterior interosseous nerve (PIN) branches and enters supinator under arcade of Frohse [S10, p.289]
- **Radial tunnel syndrome**: lateral elbow pain (mimics lateral epicondylitis); NO motor weakness
- **PIN syndrome**: motor neuropathy → muscle weakness [S10, p.289]

### Treatment (All Neuropathies)
- **Nonoperative (3–6 months)**:
  - Activity modification
  - Anti-inflammatory medications
  - Splinting
  - Injections [S10, p.289]
- **Surgical decompression**: if nonoperative fails OR motor symptoms develop

### Post-Decompression Rehabilitation
- Mobilization should begin within 1 week to avoid elbow stiffness [S10, p.290]

## Key Takeaways for Coaches and Medical Staff
- Olecranon bursitis is common in basketball; distinguish from septic bursitis using culture
- Non-infectious bursitis: compression + anti-inflammatories + aspiration; return in 6 weeks after surgery
- Ulnar nerve neuropathy: suspect with ring/small finger paresthesia and prolonged flexion symptoms
- Radial tunnel vs. PIN: key distinction is presence or absence of motor weakness
- 3–6 months of nonoperative care before surgical decompression for neuropathies [S10, pp.289-290]
