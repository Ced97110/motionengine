# Defensive Coaching Philosophy (Herb Brown)

## Summary
Herb Brown's defensive philosophy treats defense as a complete team identity — not a collection of individual assignments. The system demands 94-foot pressure after every made shot, full five-man weak-side accountability, aggressive post fronting, and total commitment to defensive rebounding. Brown argues that great defense directly creates easy transition scoring opportunities, making the game fun and building team culture from the inside out.

The philosophy is built on trust: every player covers each other's back, communicates constantly, and never takes the easy way out. "Defense equals five men working together to stop an opponent." [S1, p.8]

## When to Use
- As the foundational system for any team committed to defense-first basketball
- After made baskets, free throws, dead balls, and to begin quarters/halves: apply full-court pressure
- Adjust pickup point to three-quarter or half court as a tempo change when full-court is not viable
- Against elite offensive players: deny the ball early and limit touches

## Key Principles
1. **94-foot defense** — Press full court after all made shots when possible. Vary to three-quarter or half court to disrupt opponent rhythm and eliminate their indecision. [S1, p.9]
2. **Transition defense first** — After misses, steals, defensive rebounds, and turnovers: protect the goal, get level with the ball, locate and stop the ball. Do NOT reach or flail in transition. [S1, p.9]
3. **Maximum ball pressure** — Every defender on the ball must apply maximum pressure; make passers become drivers if the dribble is unused. [S1, p.9]
4. **Passing lane bodies** — Get bodies up and into passing lanes; front or three-quarter all post players based on ball position. [S1, p.10]
5. **Contest all shots** — Always have a hand up to distract the shooter; prefer to give up a contested jump shot rather than a drive. Close out long or short depending on the shooter's ability. [S1, p.10]
6. **Block out on every possession** — All five defenders box out. Find the ball before leaving your feet. Long shots equal long rebounds. [S1, p.10]
7. **Help and support defense** — Shadow, dig, and give full-bodied help; stop all penetration; shrink the court and pack the paint. [S1, p.11]
8. **Weak-side rotation** — Develop pro Shadow or Tilt principles; limit ball reversal; channel and influence ball direction; do not permit help defenders to get beaten. [S1, p.11]
9. **Post defense half-a-man** — When offense overloads the strong side near the low post, do NOT front (high-low lob risk). Body up, stay connected, play 'half a man.' Only allow lob if certain of weak-side help. [S1, p.13]
10. **Move on pass or dribble, not the catch** — Weak-side defenders must react and move while the ball is in the air, not after it is caught. [S1, pp.12-13]
11. **Disguise your defenses** — Show zone, play man. Change on dead balls. Trap the first wing pass over half court or double the first guard-to-guard pass. Keep offense off balance. [S1, pp.11-12]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Pressure the ball full court; call defensive signals; apply ball pressure to force passers to become drivers; lead transition defense by protecting the goal first.
- **SG**: Body up on cutters (straight-basket and weak-side); contest all perimeter shots; box out the shooter on free throws.
- **SF**: Guard weak-side flares with physical body-up coverage; shrink to help on penetration; tip uncontrollable rebounds to designated corners.
- **PF**: Front or three-quarter the post based on ball position; play 'half a man' on strong-side overloads; pinch key rebounders on free throws.
- **C**: Provide weak-side help and shot-blocking presence; determine ball-channeling direction (sideline vs. middle) based on shot-blocking ability; box out and limit second shots.

## Variations
### Full-Court Press (After Made Baskets)
Apply maximum pressure the full 94 feet after all made field goals and free throws, time-outs, dead balls, and to begin quarters or halves. [S1, pp.8-9]

### Three-Quarter or Half-Court Pickup
Alternate press pickup depth to keep offense off balance. Substitute aggressive on-ball defenders to change game tempo. [S1, p.9]

### Disguised Defense
Show one defense (e.g., zone), execute another (e.g., man-to-man). Use to trap the first wing pass over mid-court or double the first guard-to-guard pass in the offensive end. [S1, pp.11-12]

### Shrink-and-Pack Defense
Contrasting philosophy: shrink the floor, pack the paint, force opponents to shoot over. Brown's preferred system is pressure-extension, but acknowledges both approaches work. [S1, p.14]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Reaching or flailing in transition** → Stay level with the ball, protect the goal, get bodies back before contesting. [S1, p.9]
2. **Fronting the post on strong-side overloads** → Play 'half a man'; body up and stay connected to prevent the high-low lob. [S1, p.13]
3. **Moving on the catch instead of the pass/dribble** → Defenders must react and help while the ball is in the air, not once it lands. [S1, pp.12-13]
4. **Tipping rebounds long** → Designate tip zones (e.g., corners); tipping long starts the opponent's fast break. [S1, p.11]
5. **Running back to your own man after a switch** → After a switch, find the free man or the nearest threat — do not abandon coverage. [S1, p.25]
6. **Passive defense at game start** → Set aggressive tone from tip-off; can't turn up intensity late if passive early. [S1, p.13]
