# Explosive Calisthenics Programming Philosophy

## Summary
In Chapter 14 of *Explosive Calisthenics*, Paul Wade articulates a programming philosophy that prioritizes self-directed progressive improvement over fixed workout templates. His central argument: **the progressions are the program**. Templates are secondary tools; what matters is an athlete's understanding of training principles, concentration, effort, consistency, and body wisdom [S9, pp.19-20].

This philosophy has direct implications for basketball coaches: it argues against giving athletes rigid workout scripts and instead advocates building the capacity for self-regulation and intuitive training management.

## When to Use
- When designing off-season individual player development programs
- When teaching players to own their personal athletic development
- When transitioning from coach-directed to player-directed training models
- When evaluating whether a template-heavy program is actually serving athlete development

## Key Principles
1. **Progressions > Templates**: "The coaches, experts and athletes who really 'got' [the system] were the folks who understood that the progressions were the key to the whole thing — not the workouts." [S9, p.19] A template without understood principles is just noise.
2. **Self-coaching is the ultimate skill**: "The most important thing an athlete can learn is the art of self-coaching. That begins (and pretty much ends) with your own sweat. You work out, and learn as you go." [S9, p.19]
3. **Consolidation training defies fixed templates**: The majority of skill-based exercises in explosive training require a flexible, intuitive, ad hoc approach — not a rigid session structure [S9, p.20]
4. **Work sets only**: All program templates describe "work sets" — the sets done after warming up. Warming up is especially critical before explosive training and must be planned separately [S9, p.20]
5. **Warm-up is non-negotiable**: Before any explosive work, a proper warm-up protocol must be completed. This is treated as a prerequisite, not optional [S9, p.20]
6. **Focus on getting better, not following the program**: "Only the athlete can make the magic, with their concentration, effort, consistency and body wisdom. Focus on getting better, and the program will happen by itself." [S9, p.19]

## The Anti-Template Argument
Wade notes that the internet era has produced athletes who spend enormous energy analyzing, discussing, and searching for the "magical" workout — but never actually train. His prescription: "Put all that energy and curiosity into your NEXT workout." [S9, p.20]

This is particularly relevant for basketball skill development: players who obsess over drill selection while getting insufficient reps are less developed than players who just *practice more*.

## Application for Basketball Coaches
- Design programs around **movement progressions**, not arbitrary exercise lists
- Build players' capacity to **recognize their own adaptation signals**: when to push, when to back off, when to switch methods
- Use templates as starting scaffolding, but expect and encourage players to adapt them as they develop body wisdom
- Separate warm-up protocols from working-set programming; never conflate the two
- Teach the difference between power-training sessions (brief, hard, infrequent) and skill-practice sessions (frequent, fresh, low-intensity) [S9, pp.1-2]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Template-worship** → Spending more time researching programs than actually training. "There is nothing magical about a workout template, no matter what genius invented it." [S9, p.19]
2. **Ignoring the warm-up** → Performing work sets cold increases injury risk significantly, especially for explosive movements
3. **Conflating skill practice with power workouts** → Skill movements (kip-ups, flips, complex footwork) require consolidation training or time surfing — not a sets × reps schedule [S9, p.20]
4. **Giving athletes rigid programs without teaching principles** → Players cannot self-regulate without understanding *why* the program is structured the way it is
