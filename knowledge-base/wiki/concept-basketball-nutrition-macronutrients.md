# Basketball Macronutrient Requirements

## Summary
The macronutrient distribution in a basketball player's diet — carbohydrates (CHO), protein, and fat — must support the physiological demands of intermittent high-intensity play, training adaptations, and recovery. Scientific organizations including ACSM and ISSN have established evidence-based recommendations, but basketball players consistently under-consume CHO and over-consume protein and fat relative to these guidelines. Optimal performance requires a well-balanced, periodized macronutrient plan that varies by phase of the season and player position. [S10, pp.67-71]

## When to Use
- When designing weekly/daily nutrition plans for basketball players
- When counseling players about pre-game, during-game, and post-game eating
- When adjusting diet across preseason vs. in-season vs. playoff phases
- When identifying players at risk for poor recovery or injury from nutritional deficits

## Key Principles

### Carbohydrates
1. **Primary fuel**: CHO is the main substrate for both aerobic and anaerobic pathways; provides key fuel for the brain and CNS [S10, p.68]
2. **Daily target**: ≥6 g/kg/day; ideally 6–10 g/kg/day — expressed relative to body weight, not as % of energy intake [S10, pp.68]
3. **Competition target**: 55–65% of total energy intake during heavy training and competition [S10, p.68]
4. **Pre-game meal**: Easily digestible, low-fat/fiber CHO sources 2–4 h before a game to maximize glycogen stores and maintain blood glucose [S10, p.69]
   - High-glycemic index (HGI): instant mashed potato, white rice, sports drink, watermelon
   - Low-glycemic index (LGI): pasta, orange, banana, porridge, milk
5. **During exercise**: 6% CHO-electrolyte solution enhances physical performance in the last quarter [S10, p.69]
6. **Recovery**: Rapidly absorbed CHO (1 g/kg body weight) + hydrolyzed whey protein in a **3–4:1 CHO:protein ratio** within 0–2 h post-game [S10, p.69]
7. **Reality**: Most basketball players consume below recommendations — 3.3–4.0 g/kg/day observed vs. 6–10 g/kg/day recommended [S10, p.68]

### Protein
1. **Daily target**: 1.2–2.0 g/kg/day (10–15% of total energy) for metabolic adaptation, repair, and remodeling [S10, p.70]
   - Endurance emphasis: 1.2–1.4 g/kg/day
   - Strength/power emphasis: 1.4–2.0 g/kg/day
2. **Observed intakes**: 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day in male and female basketball; typically lower in competitive vs. preparatory phase [S10, p.70]
3. **Protein timing**: 10 g (0.25–0.3 g/kg body weight) of essential amino acids within 0–2 h after exercise optimizes muscle protein synthesis [S10, p.71]
4. **High-quality sources**: Whey, casein, soy, egg, beef, pork — stimulate mTOR and muscle protein synthesis [S10, p.70]
5. **Distribution**: Regular, moderate amounts spread across daily meals and match schedule — not front-loaded

### Fat
1. **Daily target**: 20–35% of total energy intake; approximately 0.9–1.1 g/kg/day [S10, p.71]
2. **Role**: Energy provision, cell membrane integrity, fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), aerobic fuel
3. **Reality**: Basketball athletes over-consume fat (~37% of energy vs. 20–35% recommended) [S10, p.71]
4. **Minimum threshold**: <20% fat provides no performance benefit and compromises fat-soluble vitamins and essential fatty acids [S10, p.71]
5. **High-fat diet warning**: Can increase fat oxidation during exercise but may reduce muscular training adaptations [S10, p.71]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Monitor daily CHO intake to ensure ≥6 g/kg/day — the most common deficit in basketball nutrition
- **Guards/Wings**: Higher sprint/shuffle demands → lean toward upper end of CHO recommendations
- **Bigs (PF/C)**: Higher strength/collision demands → lean toward upper end of protein range (1.6–2.0 g/kg/day) during preseason

## Seasonal Variation
| Phase | CHO | Protein | Fat |
|---|---|---|---|
| Preseason | High (>6 g/kg) | Higher (1.6–2.0 g/kg) | 20–25% |
| In-Season | High (6–10 g/kg) | Moderate (1.2–1.7 g/kg) | 20–25% |
| Playoffs | Very High (>8 g/kg) | Moderate | Minimal junk fat |

## Common Mistakes
1. **Low CHO intake** (3–4 g/kg instead of 6–10 g/kg) → glycogen depletion, poor fourth-quarter performance, impaired recovery
2. **High fat intake** (37% of energy) → reduces training adaptations, displaces needed CHO
3. **Eating LGI-only foods** → may not provide enough CHO volume to restock glycogen stores [S10, p.69]
4. **Skipping post-game nutrition** → window of 0–2 h post-exercise is critical for protein synthesis and glycogen replenishment
5. **Ignoring phase differences** → preseason requires more protein; competitive period requires more CHO precision
