# Multidirectional Skipping (Assorted Biomotor Skills)

## Objective
Improve quickness and coordination in locomotive mechanics — building the multi-directional movement patterns that underlie basketball cuts, defensive slides, and transition footwork. [S8, p.145]

## Setup
- 1 player
- Open court or field
- Optional: coach or partner to give directional commands

## Execution
1. Begin skipping in a forward direction.
2. On a **command or cue** from the coach or partner, change direction — using forward, backward, or side-skipping as directed.
3. **Stay facing a fixed target** in front of you throughout all direction changes — do not rotate your entire body; use crossover/side steps to maintain forward-facing orientation.
4. Continue until the next command triggers another direction change.

## Coaching Points
- "React to the command — don't anticipate it"
- Keep arms active and at a 90-degree bend — active arm drive increases skip amplitude and rhythm
- Focus on the **first skip after the command** — that's where quickness is built [S8, p.145]
- Maintain an athletic base; don't let the feet come together between direction changes

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Forward and lateral skipping only; predictable command pattern (every 5 skips)
2. **Intermediate**: Add backward skipping; increase amplitude (bigger skips); commands become random
3. **Advanced**: Add a **sprint or specific skill** (e.g., defensive slide, shot fake) immediately on command — converts the locomotive pattern into a game-speed reactive drill [S8, p.145]

## Concepts Taught
- [[quickness-and-reaction-time-training]] — reacting to directional commands trains choice reaction in a movement context
- [[basketball-acceleration-and-deceleration]] — rapid direction change is the core of on-court acceleration
- [[defensive-transition-principles]] — multidirectional locomotion patterns are the foundation of transition defense footwork
