# Achilles Tendon Injuries in Basketball: Orthobiologic Management

## Summary
Achilles tendon injuries represent a spectrum from chronic tendinopathy to complete rupture, both devastating for basketball players. Complete Achilles tendon rupture is one of the most serious injuries in the sport, with only 61.1–68% of professional NBA players returning to play at the professional level, and most return-to-play athletes experiencing decline in performance, games played, and minutes compared to pre-injury levels [S10, p.570]. PRP has been extensively studied for both conditions, but current best evidence does not support its use for either chronic Achilles tendinopathy or as augmentation during primary Achilles repair [S10, pp.569-570].

## When to Use Injections
- **Tendinopathy**: After failure of eccentric loading programs, physical therapy, ESWT, and other conservative measures; corticosteroid injection is contraindicated near Achilles due to rupture risk
- **Rupture repair augmentation**: Consider only within clinical trial context given conflicting evidence

## Key Principles

### Achilles Tendinopathy — PRP Evidence
1. Multiple level-I RCTs have failed to demonstrate superiority of PRP over placebo or saline for chronic Achilles tendinopathy [S10, p.569]
2. Krogh et al. (2016): LR-PRP vs. saline — no VISA-A difference; PRP group showed *significantly increased tendon thickness* (potential concern) [S10, p.569]
3. Boensen et al. (2017): Both HVI (high-volume injection) and PRP more effective than placebo for pain and VISA-A; HVI may be superior to PRP short-term [S10, p.569]
4. Usuelli et al. (2017): Adipose-derived stromal vascular fraction (SVF) showed significantly better early improvement (15 and 30 days) vs. LR-PRP; no difference at 6 months [S10, p.569]
5. **Current recommendation: Evidence does NOT support PRP for chronic Achilles tendinopathy** [S10, p.570]

### Achilles Tendon Rupture — PRP Augmentation Evidence
1. Schepull et al. (RCT, n=30): PRP vs. no PRP at repair — PRP group had *lower* Achilles Tendon Total Rupture Score (p=0.014), suggesting potential detrimental effect [S10, p.570]
2. De Carli et al. (n=30): Mini-open repair ± 2 PRP injections — no difference in structural or functional outcomes [S10, p.570]
3. Sanchez et al. (case-control, n=12): PRGF augmentation → earlier ROM recovery and return to training [S10, p.570]
4. Zou et al. (prospective, n=36): LR-PRP augmentation → improved ankle ROM, Leppilahti score, SF-36 at 2-year follow-up [S10, p.570]
5. **Current recommendation: Evidence does NOT support PRP or cell-based therapies for Achilles tendon repair augmentation** [S10, p.570]

### BMAC Augmentation of Achilles Repair
1. Stein et al. (retrospective, n=27, 28 tendons): Open Achilles repair + BMAC augmentation — 92% returned to sport at mean 5.9 months postoperatively; mean Achilles Tendon Rupture Score = 91 [S10, p.570]
2. 10/27 patients were basketball players
3. Promising but retrospective and uncontrolled; further study required

## NBA-Specific Return-to-Play Data
- Only 61.1–68% of NBA players return to professional play after Achilles rupture [S10, p.570]
- Return-to-play athletes typically show decline in performance metrics (points, minutes, games) vs. pre-injury baseline
- Return-to-play timeline and functional outcomes remain the primary goals of rehabilitation regardless of biologic augmentation

## Common Mistakes
1. Using PRP for Achilles tendinopathy based on patellar tendinopathy evidence — these are biologically distinct tissues with different evidence bases
2. Using corticosteroid injections near the Achilles tendon — high rupture risk; generally contraindicated
3. Overpromising return-to-play outcomes for NBA players after Achilles repair — realistic expectation setting is essential
4. Translating animal model PRP results directly to humans — Achilles tendon size and loading differences between species dramatically affect outcomes [S10, p.570]
