# Lower Crossed Syndrome in Basketball Athletes

## Summary
Lower crossed syndrome (also called pelvic crossed syndrome) is a common muscle imbalance pattern in basketball athletes characterized by overactivity of the hip flexors and lumbar extensors paired with weakness of the lower abdomen, gluteus maximus, and gluteus medius. The demanding lateral movements, rapid direction changes, and eccentric loading of basketball specifically stress the neuromuscular control of the lower extremities and the hip-pelvis complex — creating conditions where this syndrome can develop and contribute to injury risk [S10, pp.96-97].

## When to Use
- Assessed during the PPE musculoskeletal exam
- Used by S&C coaches during preseason to identify sport-specific weaknesses
- Recognized as a contributing factor in lower extremity injuries including ankle sprains, knee pain, and hip pathology
- Applied during rehabilitation to guide corrective exercise selection

## Key Principles
1. **The hip and pelvis are the force transfer link** between the lower extremity and torso — imbalances here affect the entire kinetic chain [S10, p.96]
2. **Two distinct subtypes** (Type A and Type B) present with different postural patterns — examination must distinguish them
3. **Basketball's dynamic demands drive this pattern**: lateral movements, quick direction changes, and eccentric loading create overuse of hip flexors and lumbar extensors while underusing gluteals [S10, p.96]
4. **FMS is not a reliable predictor** for this injury pattern alone — use individual muscle testing for accuracy [S10, p.96]
5. **Management targets the compensatory pattern reversal**: strengthen weak muscles before releasing/inhibiting tight muscles

## Two Subtypes

### Type A (Most Common)
- Anterior pelvic tilt and hip flexion
- Hyperlordosis of the lumbar spine
- Hyperkyphosis of the thoracic spine
- Visible postural deformity: exaggerated lower back curve

### Type B
- Minimal lumbar lordosis
- Minimal thoracic kyphosis
- Anterior head carriage
- Knee recurvatum (hyperextension)
- Flat back appearance with head forward

## Tight / Hypertonic Muscles
- Rectus femoris
- Psoas / iliopsoas
- Piriformis
- Quadratus lumborum
- Erector spinae mass
- Gastrocnemius and soleus
- Tibialis anterior
- Peroneus longus and brevis

## Weak / Pseudoparetic Muscles
- Internal and external obliques
- Gluteus medius
- Peroneus longus and brevis (in compensatory patterns)
- Gastrocnemius and soleus (in some patterns)
- Iliotibial tract and fascia lata
- Thigh adductors
- Sacrotuberous ligaments
- Posterior sacroiliac ligaments
- Iliolumbar ligaments
- Quadratus lumborum (in some patterns)
- Rectus abdominis
- Glutei
- Quadriceps femoris (except rectus femoris)
- Hamstrings

## Examination Protocol
1. **Inspection** of compensatory postural patterns (standing, from front, side, and back)
2. **Gait analysis** — look for hip abduction and lateral trunk flexion during hip extension phase
3. **Passive muscle testing**:
   - Thomas test (hip flexor length)
   - Straight leg raise (hamstring length)
   - Quadratus lumborum testing
   - Erector spinae flexion and extension
4. **Active muscle testing**:
   - Hip extension (observe for hyperextension phase gait pattern)
   - Hip abduction (will show hip abduction + lateral flexion compensation)
   - Trunk curl (compare abdominal strength vs. iliopsoas dominance)
   - Gluteus muscle firing pattern assessment

## Management
1. **Rehabilitative exercises** targeting posture, core strengthening, and gait training
2. **Reverse the compensatory pattern**: activate and strengthen weak muscles (glutes, abdomen, obliques)
3. **Release hypertonic muscles**: stretching, manual medicine, trigger point injections
4. **Adjunct therapies**: ultrasound, laser therapy, dry needling, and/or acupuncture as indicated
5. **Gait retraining**: address movement patterns in running, cutting, and jumping mechanics

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating tightness without addressing weakness** → Releasing hip flexors without strengthening glutes creates temporary relief but no long-term correction
2. **Missing Type B** → Type B is less obvious visually (no hyperlordosis); look for knee recurvatum and anterior head carriage
3. **Using FMS as the sole detection tool** → Individual muscle testing provides more accurate identification of this pattern than composite FMS scores [S10, p.96]
4. **Ignoring this pattern in asymptomatic athletes** → Lower crossed syndrome is common in basketball athletes without current symptoms but represents elevated injury risk
