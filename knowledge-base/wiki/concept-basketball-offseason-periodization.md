# Basketball Off-Season Periodization

## Summary
The off-season phase for basketball athletes should be dedicated to physical development — inducing positive, holistic changes in strength and power. Regardless of age or level of play, the off-season program must meet the specific needs of the individual athlete. An effective 12-week off-season resistance training program progresses through three microcycles: General Physical Preparedness (GPP), Strength, and Power — so that basketball-specific movements can be executed more efficiently and explosively during the preseason and in-season phases [S11, pp.301-307].

Periodization is not one-size-fits-all. A high school freshman and a 10-year NBA veteran require radically different approaches even if both compete at elite levels. The critical factors — training age, movement competency, injury history, and current strength level — must all be assessed before designing a program.

## When to Use
- Off-season (no games or practices): 12-week block
- Post-season recovery transitions into GPP within 1-2 weeks
- High school athletes: entire off-season may be needed for GPP and base strength
- College athletes (3rd/4th year): GPP transitions quickly; more time for maximal strength and power
- Professional athletes: 1-2 weeks GPP then specific training targeting weak qualities

## Key Principles
1. **Match program to training age** — a young athlete who excels on the court will not necessarily demonstrate excellent weight room skill; do not assume court performance equals resistance training readiness [S11, p.302]
2. **Technique before load** — refrain from introducing external loads when technique is lacking; even bodyweight exercises can and should be modified (e.g., incline push-up before floor push-up) [S11, p.302]
3. **Progressive overload by microcycle** — weeks 1-4: GPP/bodyweight emphasis; weeks 5-10: strength (external load added, reps decrease, sets increase, working sets increase); weeks 11-14: power (velocity emphasis, maximal strength + RFD) [S11, p.303]
4. **Distinguish warm-up sets from working sets** — a "4×5" prescription may include 3 warm-up sets + 1 working set; three working sets at 85% 1RM produce a very different training effect than one working set at 85% 1RM [S11, p.303]
5. **Velocity matters in power phase** — under heavier loads, movements must emphasize movement velocity; exercises shift to X tempo (explode) concentric phase [S11, p.303]
6. **Long-term exercise progressions** — example: goblet squat (week 1) → front squat (week 5) → back squat (week 9), creating a neuromuscular learning curve over multiple macrocycles [S11, p.303]
7. **Off-season complements preseason plyometrics** — in the preseason, external loads decrease and velocity focus increases; the off-season builds the strength foundation that maximizes stretch-shortening cycle gains in the preseason [S11, p.304]
8. **Individual variation at the professional level** — a rookie cannot train the same as a 10-year veteran; professional status does not guarantee higher training age [S11, pp.304-305]

## The Three Microcycles

### Microcycle 1: General Physical Preparedness (GPP) — Weeks 1-2
- Establish foundation, familiarity with movement patterns
- High school: bodyweight to low-load emphasis; no external load until technique is mastered
- College/Pro: GPP is condensed into warm-ups; can begin more complex protocols sooner
- Tempo: controlled eccentric (3:1:1 or 1:3:1); moderate loads 53%-76% 1RM
- Volume: 3-4 sets × 8-12 reps for most exercises
- Focus: core stability, unilateral movements, injury prevention exercises

### Microcycle 2: Strength — Weeks 3-10
- External load added progressively; reps decrease (5 reps); sets increase
- Load range: 53% → >92% across 5 sets
- Eccentric focus on primary lifts (3:3:1 or 3:1:1 tempo)
- Olympic lifts introduced (power clean, split snatch) at X:X:X tempo
- Advanced complex added for college/pro athletes
- Rest increases to 1-3 minutes between sets on primary lifts

### Microcycle 3: Power — Weeks 11-14
- Focus on RFD and movement velocity
- Lower loads (40-50% 1RM) at high velocities on primary lifts
- PAP (Post-Activation Potentiation) pairings: heavy back squat → bodyweight jump squat
- Accentuated eccentric loading (AEL) on jump squats (20-40%)
- X:X:X tempo dominates; "explode during that phase"
- Plyometric volume peaks in preseason following this phase

## Position-Based Differences (Guards vs. Bigs)
- **Guards**: front squat with eccentric focus in GPP; bench press as upper body primary
- **Bigs**: front squat sit-to-box (eccentric focus) in GPP; Bulgarian split squat in strength phase; deadlift off blocks in bigs' strength program
- Both positions use identical three-day split structure (Lower/Upper/Velocity)
- Bigs program includes more hip hinge variation (deadlift off blocks) and lateral power (lateral DB clean, side lunge)

## Tempo Notation System
- Written as "eccentric : pause : concentric" in seconds [S11, p.306]
- Example: "3:1:1" for back squat = 3 sec lower, 1 sec pause, 1 sec stand
- "X" = athlete should explode during that phase
- "X:X:X" = power exercises with no prescriptive time (e.g., Olympic lifts, jump squats)

## Common Mistakes
1. **Mimicking adult programs for youth athletes** → use general progressions under higher volume and lower intensity; court ability does not predict weight room readiness [S11, p.302]
2. **Ignoring warm-up vs. working set distinction** → failing to account for this causes stronger athletes to be under-loaded and weaker athletes to be over-reached [S11, p.303]
3. **Skipping GPP for professional athletes** → even pros need 1-2 weeks of GPP unless they have an extensive resistance training background; movement competency and joint stability remain critical regardless of age [S11, p.305]
4. **Over-complexity in veteran programming** → movements should not become so complex that advancements in strength and power are sacrificed [S11, p.305]
5. **Missing the velocity component in the power phase** → heavier loads without velocity intent produce strength, not power; the power phase requires intent to move as fast as possible [S11, p.303]
