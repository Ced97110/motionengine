# Basketball Teamwork Economics: The Ultimatum Game

## Summary
Economic game theory provides a powerful lens for understanding basketball team dynamics. The **Ultimatum Game** — where one player proposes a split of money and the other accepts or rejects — mirrors how basketball teammates negotiate credit for team success. Just as Ultimatum Game partners must cooperate enough to win the money before splitting it, basketball teammates must cooperate enough to win games before claiming individual credit.

The **common-goods game** — where players contribute to a shared pot that benefits all — mirrors team effort dynamics: when some players perceive that others are being unfairly rewarded, they contribute less effort, degrading team performance. Phil Jackson's success with the Bulls and Lakers represents a real-world basketball solution to this economic problem.

## When to Use
- When managing a team with a dominant star and role players
- When addressing locker room tension about roles, shots, or credit
- When designing playing time and offensive system decisions
- When evaluating whether a star-heavy system is actually optimal for winning

## Key Principles
1. **Fair split instinct**: In the Ultimatum Game, average offers are ~45% (near 50%) — humans demand approximate fairness even when any amount is "free money" [S16, p.114]
2. **Cooperative prerequisite**: Teammates must cooperate enough to win the game *before* they can claim individual credit — internal competition that prevents winning is self-defeating [S16, p.115]
3. **Common-goods breakdown**: When a few players are unfairly rewarded (e.g., one player gets 70% of the shots), contributing teammates reduce effort over time — the team degrades [S16, p.115-116]
4. **Reward supporting players**: Coaches who find ways to recognize and reward role players maintain cooperation and sustained effort — this is Phil Jackson's documented strength [S16, p.116]
5. **Greedy players get cut off**: In economic common-goods experiments, teammates naturally withhold contributions from free riders; in basketball, this manifests as withheld passes, screens, or defensive effort for ball-dominant teammates [S16, p.116]
6. **Credit can be quantified**: The economic games illustrate that teammates understand at some level how much each person contributes — this supports the case for objective individual performance metrics [S16, p.116]

## Player Responsibilities
- **Star players**: Recognize that dominating shot distribution beyond what optimizes winning creates resentment and reduced effort from teammates — "is that 70 percent actually better for the team?" [S16, p.115]
- **Role players**: Effort and cooperation depend on perceiving fair treatment; communicate needs to coaching staff
- **PG**: As primary ball-distributor, sets the tone for whether distribution feels fair to all five players
- **Coaches**: Create feedback systems where effort is recognized and rewarded; find non-scoring ways to validate role players' contributions

## Variations
### The Phil Jackson Model
Jackson succeeded with dominant stars (Jordan, Pippen, Kobe, Shaq) by engineering systems (Triangle Offense) that required ball movement and created multiple scoring opportunities for all five players, making every player feel involved and fairly credited. This mirrors the economics finding that cooperative systems maintain effort better than pure-star systems [S16, p.116].

### The Isaiah Rider Problem
A player who demands credit disproportionate to their cooperative contribution — the economic equivalent of an Ultimatum Game proposer offering only 5% — triggers the rejection response: teammates reduce cooperative effort, the team wins less [S16, p.115].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Assuming the best player should always take the most shots** → May be mathematically suboptimal if it reduces cooperative effort from teammates; the question is whether 70% shot share produces more wins than a balanced approach
2. **Ignoring role players' perceived fairness** → Small acts of recognition (public acknowledgment, meaningful minutes, set plays for role players) are high-ROI coaching investments
3. **Treating teamwork as purely cultural/emotional** → Economic games show teamwork dynamics are *predictable* and *rational*; they respond to incentive structures that coaches can design
