# Pilates Abdominal Circuit for Basketball

## Summary
Pilates is a conditioning approach developed by Joseph Pilates that emphasizes whole-body integration rather than isolated muscle work. Rich Dalatri incorporates a five-exercise Pilates abdominal circuit into pro player conditioning because "each movement incorporates the player's whole body in synchronicity instead of concentrating on one precise muscle group." The core is the central driver of each movement, but it works in conjunction with the full body — exactly as it must on the court. [S7, pp.335-336]

These exercises can be performed with or without a reformer machine. A stick (dowel rod) held overhead is used in most exercises to provide a lever and engage the upper body simultaneously. Perform 6-10 repetitions of each.

## The Five Pilates Exercises

### Pilates Exercise 1 — Leg-Scissors with Arm Reach
- Lie on back: one leg straight up, one leg down.
- Hold stick overhead, arms straight.
- Raise upper body + arms; **switch legs** as arms come up.
- Lower arms and upper body; **switch legs again**.
- Continuous, controlled alternating leg switch. [S7, p.335]

### Pilates Exercise 2 — Bicycle Crunch with Stick
- Lie on back: one knee bent to chest, other leg straight.
- Hold stick overhead, arms straight.
- Raise upper body + arms; **pull the other knee to chest** while extending the bent leg.
- Legs alternate in a **bicycle pumping motion** continuously.
- Arms stay overhead and move up/down as a coordination counter. [S7, p.335]

### Pilates Exercise 3 — Open/Close Legs with Arm Reach
- Lie on back: **both legs at 45° from the floor, open (wide)**.
- Hold stick overhead, arms straight.
- As arms come up: **close legs**.
- As arms go down: **open legs**.
- Continuous, rhythmic open-close synchronized with arm movement. [S7, p.335]

### Pilates Exercise 4 — Legs with Basketball Between Ankles
- Same starting position as Exercise 3 (legs at 45°).
- **Hold a basketball between the ankles** for resistance.
- Legs remain in position; arms (stick overhead) come up and down continuously.
- The held position of the legs plus the ball creates sustained abdominal tension. [S7, p.336]

### Pilates Exercise 5 — Leg Raise with Arm Reach
- Same starting position as Exercise 4.
- As arms come up: **legs also raise approximately 6 inches**.
- As arms come down: **legs lower back to starting position**.
- Continuous, rhythmic; the simultaneous arm-leg movement maximizes core engagement. [S7, p.336]

## Coaching Points
- Keep **arms straight** throughout all exercises — bending elbows reduces the shoulder girdle's engagement and makes the core work easier (which is the opposite of the goal).
- Movements should be **slow and controlled** — this is core endurance work, not explosive training.
- Don't let the legs drop to the floor between reps; maintain tension throughout the set.
- Perform in a circuit: complete one set of each exercise in sequence, then rest.
- "The core is working in conjunction with the rest of the body, as he would on the court."

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: 6 reps of each exercise; slow tempo; no basketball in Exercise 4.
2. **Intermediate**: 8-10 reps; add basketball in Exercise 4; increase tempo slightly.
3. **Advanced**: 10 reps; add resistance (hold a weight plate instead of a stick in some exercises); combine the Pilates circuit with the Core Stability Circuit as a back-to-back superset.
