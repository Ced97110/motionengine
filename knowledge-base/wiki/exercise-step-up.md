# Step-Up

## Objective
Build unilateral lower-body strength (glutes, hamstrings, quadriceps) through a step-up movement that closely mimics the single-leg drive pattern used in basketball acceleration, jumping, and stair-climbing cuts.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Weight room with a bench or box approximately knee height (height can be increased as athlete progresses)
- Equipment: barbell (high bar position), two dumbbells/kettlebells, or one dumbbell/kettlebell

## Execution
1. Position a barbell across the shoulders (high bar back squat position) or hold dumbbells at the sides. Torso fully erect.
2. Stand in front of a bench or box (approximately knee height).
3. Flex one hip and knee to place the **lead foot** up on top of the bench.
4. Press through the lead foot and extend the hip and knee of the lead leg to step up onto the bench.
5. Carry the trailing leg to place that foot on top of the bench next to the lead foot — **pause** in the standing position.
6. Reverse the movement: step down with the same trailing leg.
7. Return to the floor by carrying the lead foot next to the trailing foot — **pause**.
8. Switch legs (new lead leg for each rep, or complete all reps on one side first).

**Breathing:** Exhale during the upward phase; inhale during the lowering phase.

## Coaching Points
- "Press through the heel" — keep most weight over the midfoot-to-heel of the trailing leg, not the ball of the foot
- **Do not hop or jump up** with the trailing leg or foot — the lead leg must do all the work
- As the athlete progresses, increase box height to demand greater hip and knee flexion/extension range
- Keep the lead knee tracking over the toes; do not let it collapse inward

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Bodyweight step-up, low bench (6–8 inches); focus on single-leg control
2. **Intermediate**: Add barbell or two dumbbells; standard knee-height box
3. **Advanced**: Increase box height progressively; single dumbbell held ipsilateral or at the chest
