# Play Selection Principles

## Summary
Choosing the right plays — and the right number of plays — is as important as the plays themselves. Coach Mac outlines three foundational principles for play selection: start plays from the same formation to prevent the defense from predicting actions, have players avoid telegraphing movements with their body language, and select plays that leverage your specific personnel's strengths. For youth coaches in particular, keeping the playbook minimal (1-2 plays) is strongly recommended so practice time is spent on skill development rather than play memorization. [S4, pp.2-3]

## When to Use
- When building or trimming your team's playbook at the start of a season
- When evaluating why a play keeps breaking down in games
- When scouting your own team to understand what the opponent can anticipate
- When coaching youth basketball where cognitive load must be managed

## Key Principles
1. **Start all plays from the same formation.** If every play begins from the same look, the defense cannot predict which action is coming before the ball is in play. Teams that set up in predictable formation-specific positions give opponents a scouting edge. [S4, p.2]
2. **Have players face different or neutral directions.** Young players especially tend to face the direction they're about to cut or screen — this telegraphs the action. Players should mask their intentions with neutral body positioning before moving. [S4, pp.2-3]
3. **Select plays that match your personnel.** If your team has strong wing shooters, run plays that get them open off screens. If you have a dominant post player, run plays that isolate them in the post. Don't choose plays because they "look cool." [S4, p.3]
4. **Keep the playbook small.** Fewer plays mean players spend more mental energy executing correctly and less time remembering the next step. For youth teams: 1-2 regular plays and 1 BLOB. [S4, p.2]
5. **Be sparing with SLOB plays.** Sideline out-of-bounds plays are often unnecessary — players can simply pass in and run a regular set. Reserve the complexity for situations that genuinely need it. [S4, p.2]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: As the primary decision-maker and often the in-bounder or ball-handler, the PG must disguise reads and not betray the play's direction with eye contact or body language.
- **SG/SF**: Perimeter players are often the action targets (shooters coming off screens); they must set up cuts going the wrong direction first.
- **PF/C**: Post players frequently set screens in drawn plays; they must not face the direction of the screen before the play begins.

## Variations
### Youth Basketball Version
Limit to 1-2 half-court sets and 1 BLOB. Spend the majority of practice time on individual skill development. Introduce plays only after players have mastered fundamental execution. [S4, p.2]

### Advanced / Varsity Version
Multiple plays (BLOB zone, BLOB man, set zone, set man, SLOB man) can be carried, provided all share starting formations by category and players have the cognitive capacity to execute reads correctly under pressure.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Choosing plays that start in different formations** → defense keys on the formation and predicts the play before it starts. Keep starting positions consistent across a play family.
2. **Players facing the direction of their cut/screen** → gives the action away immediately. Teach neutral body positioning and misdirection steps.
3. **Running a play too frequently** → quick-hitter element-of-surprise plays stop working when overused. Save certain plays for specific game situations. [S4, pp.6, 14]
4. **Choosing plays for aesthetics rather than personnel fit** → mismatches the play to your team's strengths and leads to poor execution.
