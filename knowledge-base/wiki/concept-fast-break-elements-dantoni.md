# Elements of the Fast Break — D'Antoni System

## Summary
Mike D'Antoni, Alvin Gentry, and Marc Iavaroni (pro team) built a system where more than half their points came off the fast break. Before running specific sets, they identify six prerequisite elements that make up-tempo basketball sustainable. Without all six, the running game breaks down. [S7, pp.163–166]

## When to Use
- Building a fast-break program from scratch
- Diagnosing why a running offense is underperforming
- Evaluating personnel decisions and practice design for a transition-heavy team

## Key Principles

### 1. Point Guard Quality
- The PG must see the whole floor and pass without wasted dribbling
- Reads situations instantly and delivers to open teammates
- If the PG fails to deliver to hard-cutting teammates, those players stop moving hard — the entire system collapses [S7, p.164]
- "A 'move it, *now*' mindset is essential" [S7, p.163]

### 2. Conditioning
- All players — including reserves — must sprint from tip to buzzer
- Run all drills at maximum speed, full-court and half-court [S7, p.164]
- Conditioning is built by running in practice, not talked into existence

### 3. Defense
- Defense must fit the running offense — aggressive disruption, not deliberate slug-it-out
- Fast break is most productive when defense creates steals, stepped-into lanes, contested shots, and blocked-out boards [S7, p.165]
- Goal: force bad shots → misses → fast break starts
- The defense wears opponents down by dictating transition pace [S7, p.165]

### 4. Rebounding
- More possessions = more fast-break opportunities
- All 5 players block out on every shot; requires guards and small forwards to commit, not just big men [S7, p.165]
- Example: Shawn Marion was among the league's best rebounders as a small forward

### 5. Ball Handling
- Every player — starters through reserves — must handle the ball at full speed
- Forwards and centers will be required to drive at times on the break [S7, p.165]
- Ball handling includes both dribbling at full speed and proper passing decisions

### 6. Shooting
- Players have the green light to shoot when open on the primary break
- Players must not hesitate to shoot open shots; coaches get "much more upset when players fail to shoot open shots than when they miss" [S7, p.165]
- Key tactic: spot up on the 3-point arc — forces defenders into no-man's land between protecting the rim and closing out on the arc [S7, p.165]
- Shot selection = knowing which shots are good shots *for you*

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Read/react, advance ball, deliver to cutters, no wasted dribbles
- **SG/SF**: Sprint wide lanes to deep corners, spot up at the arc, be ready to shoot without hesitation
- **PF**: Versatile — first or second trailer, post-up briefly, face-up and shoot from outside, drive and kick
- **C**: First trailer, post down, or pop to 3-point line; must handle ball under pressure

## Variations
### 3-Out System
Always keep at least 3 (ideally 4) players on the perimeter. Post big men for only a few seconds — exploit their quickness and outside shooting skills rather than traditional post-up sequences. [S7, p.169]

## Common Mistakes
1. **PG wastes dribbles advancing the ball** → Drill outlet-to-push reads relentlessly
2. **Players pass up open shots on the break** → Install a culture where missed open shots are criticized, not missed shots themselves [S7, p.165]
3. **Big men post and hold** → Keep the middle of the court open; post briefly then transition to a face-up or perimeter role [S7, p.171]
4. **Neglecting reserves' conditioning** → Sub-par conditioning from bench players undermines the running system's depth advantage
