# Basketball Athlete Energy Requirements

## Summary
Optimum energy intake is the foundation of an athlete's diet. Basketball is an intermittent, court-based team sport requiring alternating aerobic and anaerobic metabolism, covering 5–6 km per 40-minute game at intensities above 85% HRmax. Total energy expenditure (TEE) increases across the season, from the preparatory period through the competitive period, and varies significantly by player position. An individualized approach to energy assessment is required to meet each athlete's true needs.

Energy intake (EI) must balance TEE — the sum of resting energy expenditure (REE), thermal effect of food, thermal effect of daily activity, and thermogenesis of resting activity. Deficient EI negatively affects lean tissue, strength, endurance, immune function, and recovery. [S10, pp.65-67]

## When to Use
- When designing seasonal nutrition plans for basketball players at any level
- When monitoring body composition changes across the preseason, in-season, and playoff phases
- When counseling players experiencing fatigue, injury, or poor recovery
- When comparing energy needs of guards vs. forwards/centers

## Key Principles
1. **TEE increases across the season**: TEE and REE significantly increase by 15.7±11.2% and 7.0±22.2% from the beginning to the competitive period [S10, p.67]
2. **Average caloric intake (DLW-measured)**: ~4,764±622 kcal/day for males; ~3,677±330 kcal/day for females [S10, p.67]
3. **Indirect TEE estimation**: Cunningham and Harris–Benedict equations (REE × activity factor) are practical field tools; METs offer another method; doubly labeled water (DLW) is the gold standard
4. **Position differences matter**: Guards perform more high-intensity activity than forwards and centers (5.9% vs. 5.4% and 4.5% sprinting; 9.3% vs. 9.2% and 7.9% high-intensity shuffling) — energy expenditure must be classified by position [S10, p.66]
5. **Skill level increases demands**: International-level players experience greater physiological demands than national-level players (94.6% vs. 90.8% HRmax) [S10, p.66]
6. **Deficient EI consequences**: Lean tissue catabolism, impaired immune/endocrine/musculoskeletal function, increased fatigue, injury, and illness risk [S10, p.67]
7. **Preseason peaks**: Preseason requires greater energy and protein for muscle mass building; competitive period demands careful CHO-protein management [S10, p.71]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG**: Highest energy expenditure position — greater sprint and shuffle demands; most likely to under-eat on match days due to game stress
- **SF**: Intermediate energy demands
- **PF/C**: Lower intensity demands but high strength/collision-based energy needs

## Variations
### Preseason Phase
Higher total energy intake required; greater protein contribution to support muscle mass gain alongside double daily sessions.

### In-Season / Competitive Phase
Moderate energy needs; precise CHO-protein timing before, during, and after games is the primary focus. TEE and REE are at their highest.

### Playoff Phase
Back-to-back game scheduling increases acute recovery demands; rapid CHO replenishment is critical.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Fixed calorie prescription** regardless of phase → should adjust upward from preparatory to competitive period
2. **Ignoring position differences** → guards need more carbohydrate-rich fuel for sprint/shuffle demands; bigs may need more protein for contact and strength maintenance
3. **Eating less on game days** → team sport athletes tend to eat less on match days due to stress, travel, or schedule disruption; this must be actively countered [S10, p.68]
4. **Overestimating activity factor** in TEE equations → can over- or underestimate needs; DLW or lab-based assessment preferred when possible
