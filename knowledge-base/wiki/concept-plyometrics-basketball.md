# Plyometrics for Basketball

## Summary
Plyometric training enhances the ability to produce maximal force in minimal time — the defining physical quality in basketball. Plyometrics work through the **stretch-shortening cycle (SSC)**: a rapid eccentric (lengthening) prestretch of the muscle-tendon unit is immediately followed by a concentric (shortening) contraction, releasing stored elastic energy as explosive force.

This is not a theoretical exercise — it occurs in every basketball game. When a player misses a jump shot and immediately jumps again for the rebound, the landing stretches the quadriceps and gastrocnemius (eccentric phase), and the immediate next jump contracts those same muscles (concentric phase). The **amortization phase** is the brief transition between the two and is the critical variable in plyometric effectiveness. [S2, pp.105–106]

## When to Use
- When an athlete has a solid strength base and no current injuries
- Pre-season and in-season power development phases
- For developing first-step quickness, vertical jump, and reactive ability
- In combination with strength training — power training alone outperforms strength training alone for athletic development

## Key Principles
1. **Stretch-Shortening Cycle (SSC)**: Eccentric prestretch → amortization transition → concentric contraction. All three phases matter.
2. **Amortization phase is critical**: The shorter the amortization phase (time spent on the ground), the less elastic energy is lost as heat. "Get off the ground fast."
3. **Rate of stretch, not length of stretch**: How quickly the muscle-tendon unit is stretched determines the amount of energy available for the explosive contraction — not how far the stretch goes. [S2, p.106]
4. **Strength base first**: Do not begin plyometric training without adequate base strength. The stress on tendons and joints is significant.
5. **Power + strength = optimal performance**: Plyometrics bridge the gap between maximal strength and sport-specific speed/power. Use both in training programs.
6. **Land softly, jump quickly**: "Soft landing" absorbs impact through the joints and repositions for the next jump — athletes should land with knees bent, not stiff-legged.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Relevant to every position. PF and C benefit especially for rebounding; PG and SG for first-step and agility.

## Variations
### Lower-Body Plyometrics
Focused on vertical jump and reactive ground contacts: tuck jump, single-leg hurdle jump, depth jump into jump shot, multiple-box jump, skater hop, split jump into run.

### Upper-Body Plyometrics
Focused on reactive arm/chest power: pivot step to medicine ball chest pass, medicine ball slam into vertical jump, medicine ball push-up. [S2, p.107]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Starting plyometrics without a strength base** → Establish squat, lunge, and hip hinge proficiency first
2. **Long ground contact times** → Cue: "Hot coals — get off the ground fast"
3. **Landing stiff-legged** → Teach soft landing with joint flexion to absorb forces and protect knees
4. **Skipping single-leg progressions** → Double-leg competency must precede single-leg work
5. **High volume too soon** → Plyometric volume is measured in ground contacts; start low (60–80/session) and progress
