# Run-and-Jump Defense

## Summary
The run-and-jump is a full-court pressure defensive tactic in which the nearest defender to the dribbler — not the dribbler's own defender — rotates and attacks the ball to force a turnover, cause a hurried pass, or make the ball handler pick up their dribble. The original defender then rotates to cover the run-and-jump helper's assignment. Done correctly, it creates confusion and spontaneous decision-making for the offense without requiring every player to commit to a full trap. [S1, pp.177-182]

Krinsky's "that press-based defense" — derived from the originating head coach's system at the college program — used run-and-jump tactics as the foundation of a complete pressure system. [S1, p.182]

## When to Use
- When the nearest helper defender is **close enough** to the dribbler to get there without abandoning their assignment for too long
- When you want the **ball out of the opposing point guard's hands** quickly
- When you want to force **hurried, spontaneous decisions** without giving the offense time to read and react
- In the perimeter (sideline, wing areas); prefer run-and-trap on the baseline
- As a surprise change-of-tempo tactic during the game's critical moments

## Key Principles
1. **Level with the ball, not behind it** — a defender whose opponent is trailing behind the ball should level to the line of the ball; from there they are positioned to run-and-jump the dribbler from behind and also double-team from the blind side.
2. **The jumping defender attacks the dribbler's blind side** — approach from behind or the side where the dribbler cannot protect the ball easily.
3. **The original defender rotates to the jumper's man** — immediately after the run-and-jump, the original on-ball defender sprints to pick up the now-uncovered player.
4. **Fake the trap when you can't get there** — if the distance is too great to run-and-jump effectively, fake the jump to keep the dribbler uncertain and off-balance.
5. **Sprint out of the trap when the ball is passed** — if the double-team is beaten by a pass, the trapping defender does NOT chase; they rotate to the position the help came from.
6. **Always take the free man in the direction of the help** — if your original assignment is now covered, pick up the next free man closest to the basket in the direction the help came from.
7. **Communication is mandatory** — "Ball!" is called when a pass is made; all retreating defenders raise arms in passing lanes and look over their shoulders to locate the ball. [S1, pp.177-182]

## Player Responsibilities
- **On-ball defender**: Pressure the dribbler, force toward sideline or baseline; be alert for the run-and-jump arrival and immediately rotate to the helper's assignment upon contact.
- **Nearest off-ball defender**: Read the dribbler's path; if close enough, attack the blind side and force a stopped dribble or hurried pass; rotate back to help position if ball is passed.
- **Other three defenders**: Deny nearest outlets; anticipate the forced pass; sprint into passing lanes with arms up; look over shoulder to track the ball when retreating.
- **All five players**: Communicate every switch and rotation aloud.

## Variations
### Run-and-Jump on Perimeter + Trap on Baseline
Run-and-jump when the dribbler is in the open perimeter (sideline, wing); switch to a full run-and-trap or double-team when the dribbler is on the baseline where the sideline acts as a third defender. [S1, p.177]

### college-era full-court press (Krinsky Variation)
Man-to-man full-court with constant run-and-jump opportunities. When a trap is sprung and the ball is passed: the player who was guarding the dribbler rotates in the direction where the trapper came from and picks up the free man in that direction. Used whenever forwards scored. [S1, p.183]

### Carolina Defense Shell Sequence
Built 1v1 → 2v2 → 3v3 → 4v4 → 5v5 on full court. Players learn to run-and-jump at every opportunity and fake the trap when they can't. Teaches the decision-making process, not just the execution. [S1, pp.180-182]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Running and jumping when too far away** → Correct: if you can't get there, fake the trap; a failed jump leaves your assignment open.
2. **Original defender chasing the jumped player instead of rotating** → Correct: as soon as the jump defender attacks, the original defender immediately picks up the helper's man.
3. **Trapping without rotating out of the trap** → Correct: when the ball is passed from the trap, the trapper sprints to the position the help came from — never chases the pass.
4. **Not communicating** → Correct: every run-and-jump must be verbally signaled; "Ball!" on every pass.
