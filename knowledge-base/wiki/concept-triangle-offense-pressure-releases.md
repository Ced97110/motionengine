# Triangle Offense — Pressure Releases

## Summary
When the defense denies the entry pass to the wing, the triangle offense has a layered system of pressure releases to get the ball into the offense. Phil Jackson and Tex Winter defined precise terminology — "moment of truth," "line of truth," "lag principle," "blind pig" — to give players a shared language for navigating front-line defensive pressure and still initiating the offense. [S7, pp.105-109]

## When to Use
- When the defense denies the entry pass to the wing
- When aggressive man-to-man defense over-plays all wing/guard receivers
- As the counter any time a primary entry is shut down

## Key Principles
1. **Moment of truth:** The critical moment when 1 (the ball handler) picks up the dribble approximately 3 feet in front of his defender. 3 (the wing) must time his pop-out to receive the ball exactly at this moment — it is a coordinated action requiring practiced timing. [S7, p.105]
2. **Line of truth:** The imaginary line across the floor 3 feet in front of the defensive player guarding the ball handler. Passes that penetrate this line initiate the offense (numbered passes); passes that do not penetrate it are not numbered and serve only as pressure releases. [S7, p.105]
3. **Lag principle:** If 3 is not open at the moment of truth, 2 lags 3+ feet behind the line of truth as a safety-valve receiver. 1 passes to 2 (lag pass); 2 quickly passes to 4 as he pops out for the wing entry. All three players must coordinate timing; significant dribbling is required. [S7, p.105]
4. **Complete cuts:** Even in pressure situations, cutters must complete their movements so passers can commit confidently.
5. **Continuity survives pressure:** Every pressure release feeds back into the triangle formation — pressure cannot eliminate the offense, only delay it.

## The Pressure Release System

### Moment of Truth + Lag Principle
- 1 dribbles to create the moment of truth; 3 attempts to pop out and receive.
- If 3 is denied: 2 steps behind the line of truth as the lag receiver.
- 1 lag-passes to 2; 2 passes to 4 as 4 pops to the wing entry spot.
- All three must move in coordination. [S7, p.105]

### Blind Pig Action
Triggered when 2, the lag receiver, is also overplayed:
1. 4 flashes to the top of the lane.
2. 1 passes quickly to 4.
3. 2 makes a backdoor speed cut; if open (head and shoulders past defender), 4 drops a pass to 2 for a basket cut. [S7, p.106]

**Continuations if 2 is not open:**
- 2 continues to the weak-side freeze spot.
- 1 cuts over the top of 4 and receives from 4; 1 drives to basket or dribbles to operating spot (wing or corner). [S7, p.106]
- If 1 is not open: 1 continues to basket area, holds for a late pass from 4; 4 dribble-weaves to meet 2 coming off a down screen by 3. 4 can also pass to 2 directly in the corner to form the triangle. [S7, p.106]

**Options for 4 with the ball:**
- Pass to 3, screen down for 1 (1 moves to top); 2 pops to corner — new sideline triangle forms. [S7, p.106]
- Pass to 1 and set a screen for 1.
- If no one is open, 4 initiates the sideline triangle with 3, 5, and 2; 1 passes to 3 and gets to the top of the circle. [S7, p.107]

**After blind pig, 1 has the ball at the top:**
- Drop pass to 4 on screen-and-roll
- Drive right to basket
- Penetrate and clear for jump shot
- Penetrate and pass to 5 on the block, 3 on the weak side, or 2 in the corner
- Staggered screen: 4 sets second screen for 2 (first screen from 3); 1 is isolated one-on-one on the strong side. [S7, p.107]

### Wing Entry on the Blind Pig
When 1 cannot pass to 4 after the blind pig setup:
1. 1 passes to 3 (wing entry preserved).
2. 4 sets a back screen for 1; 1 speed cuts.
3. 3 looks for a high over-the-top pass to 1 into the cleared right side.
4. After the screen, 4 sets a side screen ("wing screen and roll") for 3, who drives to the lane.
5. 1 spots up in corner or returns for dribble weave with 3.
6. 4 screens a third time in the corner for 2 (defensive balance coverage). [S7, p.108]

### Blur Screen
When 2 is overplayed AND violates the lag principle:
1. 2 cuts first; 4 cuts immediately after ("right off 2's tail") — this is called a **blur screen**.
2. 4 receives from 1; 1 speed cuts off the back side of 4.
3. 3 screens for 2, who comes up for defensive balance.
4. 1 can also make a high pass to 2 if open. [S7, p.109]

### Wing Reverse
If 1 cannot pass to 2 or 3:
1. 1 passes to 5, who has flashed to the high-post elbow ("wing reverse").
2. 3 cuts backdoor; 5 drop-passes to 3 if 3 is head and shoulders past defender.
3. If 3 can't receive: 1 screens on 2 ("guard squeeze action"); 2 cuts off 1's screen to 3 feet in front of 5; 5 passes to 2 if open.
4. After the screen, 1 rolls to the free-throw area and can receive from 5. [S7, p.109]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (1):** Primary ball handler managing the moment of truth; lag passer; over-the-top cutter off back screen; wing reverse initiator.
- **SG (2):** Lag safety valve; blind pig backdoor cutter; blur screen first cutter; defensive balance position.
- **SF (3):** Primary wing entry receiver; down screener in lag sequence; wing entry on blind pig passer.
- **PF (4):** Flash to top of lane on blind pig; screen chain initiator (wing screen-and-roll, corner screen); blur screen second cutter.
- **C (5):** High-post flash receiver on wing reverse; drop passer to backdoor; guard squeeze action passer.

## Common Mistakes
1. **2 not lagging far enough behind the line of truth** → triggers defensive interception of the lag pass. Stay 3+ feet behind the line.
2. **4 flashing too early on the blind pig** → defender adjusts; time the flash to when 2 is denied.
3. **Cutters stopping when the pass doesn't come** → always complete the cut; the passer may commit to the delivery.
4. **Not re-forming the triangle after a pressure release** → every release feeds back into a new triangle somewhere on the floor.
