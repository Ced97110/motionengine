# Defensive Strategies — Del Harris

## Summary
Del Harris (pro team, team) identifies three phases of defensive strategy: pregame plan, in-game adjustments, and late-game adjustments. His framework covers choosing a primary vs. secondary defense, matchup principles, pick-and-roll coverage options, and press deployment strategy. The overarching principle: master one defense deeply rather than spreading effort across many. [S7, pp.253-256]

## When to Use
- **Pregame**: Study film and build a specific plan for the opponent's key plays and personnel
- **In-game**: Adjust matchups when a player gets hot, swap to secondary defense when primary is failing
- **Late-game**: Deploy presses or zone changes to slow tempo, create turnovers, or protect leads

## Key Principles
1. **One bread-and-butter defense**: master man-to-man (or your primary) first; use secondary (zone, press) only as a change-of-pace
2. **Teach your secondary to practice against it**: you can't run effective offensive practices against a zone if no one on your team knows how to run it
3. **Cross-matching is underutilized**: matching your best perimeter stopper against the opponent's best scorer — regardless of position — can swing momentum
4. **Pick-and-roll strategy must be location-specific**: top of key, wing, high wing, and elbows require different techniques
5. **Have a Plan A and a Plan B for PnR coverage**: don't try to stop too many things at once
6. **Press can speed up OR slow down tempo** — choose a style that matches your goal

## Player Responsibilities

### Cross-Matching Principles
- **Best perimeter stopper**: can guard 2-3 positions; deploy immediately on opponent's best perimeter scorer, or wait to save him from foul trouble
- **Guarding quick small PGs**: if opponent is still quicker, put a *taller* defender on them — use length to challenge shots while keeping enough space to contain
- **Small on a big**: can work if the small player is quicker — fronts, denies the entry, outrusts the big to his spots; your own big can provide weakside lob help

## Variations

### Pick-and-Roll Coverage Options

#### Hard Help (Hedging / Showing)
- The screener's defender jumps out at a **90-degree hard angle** (facing the sideline) into the ball handler's path before he can "turn the corner"
- Ball handler's defender goes under the helper and over the screener to re-engage the ball handler
- Helper recovers back to the screener
- If the helper gets too extended, he rotates to the paint and picks up an open offensive player
- Variations: helper stays with ball handler for 0, 1, 2, or 3 steps before returning
- Best against: teams that penetrate and kick, or drive to roll

#### Soft Help (Cover the Turn)
- Helper drops back to roughly the box on the strong-side foul lane at a **45-degree or softer angle**
- Gives zone-like coverage to the ball handler AND the foul lane
- Ball handler's defender continues hard pursuit over the screen
- Less aggressive; prevents splits and rolls; concedes the catch-and-shoot more
- Best against: teams that are poor at the roll but strong at the pull-up jumper

#### Over or Under the Screen
- **Go under** the screen: used on picks set far out on the floor and against poor shooters
- **Go over** the screen: used on closer setups and against good shooters
- Decision driven by two factors: (1) distance of the pick from the basket, (2) ball handler's shooting ability vs. penetration ability

### Press Deployment Strategy
- **Full-court man-to-man press** → disrupt timing, wear thin bench down, speed up game
- **Softer position press (2-2-1)** → slow opponent down; can trap selectively (one trap, then retreat to zone)
- **To increase tempo**: trap early and continue trapping as ball advances downcourt
- **To decrease tempo**: 2-2-1 at ¾ court, selective trapping, retreat to half-court zone after one trap
- **Deny the ball handler from re-receiving after a trap**: his original defender denies him the re-catch after the press

## Common Mistakes
1. Automatic position-for-position matchups → missing the opportunity to cross-match your best stopper onto the opponent's best scorer
2. Trying to run equal commitment to 4-5 different PnR coverages → pick one primary and one counter
3. Running the press and abandoning it after 1-2 easy baskets → commit to your system
4. Using zone only when talent is lacking → also use it as a deliberate pace-change tool
