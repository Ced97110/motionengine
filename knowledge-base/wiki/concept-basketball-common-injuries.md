# Common Basketball Injuries — Diagnosis and Management

## Summary
The inherent musculoskeletal demands of basketball — jumping, landing, cutting, contact — create a predictable pattern of orthopedic injuries. The most prevalent are lateral ankle sprains, ACL tears, Achilles/patellar tendinitis, muscle strains, finger/wrist injuries, and facial trauma. Understanding the mechanism, diagnosis, and management of each is essential for any basketball medical professional or coach making return-to-play decisions [S10, pp.136-138].

## When to Use
- Sideline assessment of an injured player
- Planning injury prevention programs (preseason and in-season)
- Educating players on injury recognition
- Determining imaging needs and specialist referral

## Key Principles
1. **Ankle sprains are the most prevalent orthopedic injury** in college basketball — prevention should focus on strength, flexibility, and proprioceptive training
2. **ACL tears are season-ending** — women suffer ACL tears ~3x more often than men, predominantly by non-contact valgus/rotational mechanism
3. **Tendinopathies are overuse injuries** — acute increase in training load is the most common trigger; respond well to RICE and load modification
4. **Muscle strains resolve with time** — hamstrings can take 6+ weeks; the progressive stretching/strengthening pattern is critical
5. **Never miss a scaphoid fracture** — clinical suspicion > imaging; immobilize early even without X-ray evidence of fracture
6. **Facial trauma** requires systematic exam of surrounding structures — a septal hematoma must be evacuated immediately [S10, pp.136-138]

## Injuries by Type

### 13.2.1 Lateral Ankle Sprain (Most Prevalent)
**Mechanism**: Excessive ankle inversion injures ATFL first, then CFL, then PTFL (sequential)
**Grading**: Grade I–III based on number of ligaments injured and joint laxity
**Diagnosis**:
- Palpable tenderness over affected ligaments
- Anterior drawer and talar tilt tests for joint laxity
- Ottawa Ankle Rules to determine need for X-ray:
  - X-ray needed if: bone tenderness at posterior edge/tip of lateral OR medial malleolus, OR inability to bear weight for 4 steps
  - Foot X-ray needed if: bone tenderness at base of 5th metatarsal, navicular, OR inability to bear weight

**Management**: RICE, activity modification, taping/ankle brace/high-top shoes
**Return**: When ROM, normal strength, and ankle stability are restored
**Prevention**: Strength, flexibility, and proprioceptive training [S10, p.136]

**High Ankle Sprains** (syndesmotic): Result from higher force + rotation; anterior and/or posterior tibio-fibular ligament injury; pain above the ankle extending into the lower leg; significantly longer recovery than lateral sprains [S10, p.137]

### 13.2.2 Achilles and Patellar Tendinitis (Jumper's Knee)
**Mechanism**: Repetitive cutting, jumping, and landing → chronic tendon tensioning
**Diagnosis**: Focal tenderness and swelling at midsubstance or bony attachment; often triggered by acute training load increase
**Management**: RICE, flexibility/strength rehabilitation, activity modification; counterforce patellar strap helpful for some athletes
**Achilles tear**: Positive Thompson/Squeeze test with loss of passive plantarflexion; acute complete tears in basketball players generally require surgical repair for return to play [S10, p.137]

### 13.2.3 Muscle Strains
**Most involved muscles**: Gastrocnemius/soleus, hamstrings, quadriceps, lumbar paraspinals
**Mechanism**: Transient overload or over-stretching → muscle fiber disruption
**Diagnosis**: Loss of ROM, focal tenderness, spasm, hematoma; frank defect in higher-grade injuries
**Management**: Time + progressive strengthening + ROM restoration
**Timeline**: Most resolve in several weeks; hamstrings may require 6+ weeks [S10, p.137]

### 13.2.4 ACL Tear
**Mechanism**: Non-contact valgus + rotational load (women 60% non-contact; men 59% contact)
**Epidemiology**: Women suffer ACL tears ~3x more often — 0.22/1000 athlete exposures [S10, p.138]
**Diagnosis**: Player feels/hears a "pop"; rapid hemarthrosis; instability; positive Lachman and anterior drawer tests (increased laxity, no firm endpoint)
**Management**: Surgical ACL reconstruction required to restore sufficient stability for basketball demands
**Timeline**: 9–12 months post-op rehabilitation; recurrent ACL injury risk is high, especially in female collegiate athletes [S10, p.138]

### 13.2.5 Finger and Wrist Injuries
**Ball-induced trauma**:
- Mallet finger: extensor mechanism injury at DIP joint
- Boutonnière deformity: PIP joint extensor injury
- IP joint dislocations and fractures

**UCL (thumb MCPj) injury** — "gamekeeper's thumb" / "skier's thumb":
- Grade III (complete disruption): surgical correction
- Grade I–II: splint 4–6 weeks

**Scaphoid fracture**:
- Pain in anatomic snuffbox; positive scaphoid shift test
- X-rays often negative initially — manage by clinical suspicion, not imaging
- Immobilize with thumb spica splint/cast until definitive diagnosis
- Generally managed operatively in basketball players due to high nonunion risk [S10, p.138]

### 13.2.6 Facial Trauma — Nasal Fractures
**Mechanism**: Elbow contact during rebounding or defense
**Diagnosis**: Nasal fracture at bony bridge or cartilage; evaluate for septal hematoma (bluish asymmetric swelling visible through nares)
**Critical action**: Septal hematoma must be immediately evacuated to prevent septal breakdown
**Associated structures**: Orbits, maxillae, zygomatic arches must be systematically assessed
**Imaging**: CT scan warranted for facial pain, asymmetry, loss of extraocular muscle function, or diplopia [S10, p.138]

## Variations
### Injury Prevention Implications
- Ankle proprioception training reduces sprain recurrence
- ACL neuromuscular training programs (e.g., Prevent Injury and Enhance Performance — PEP) reduce non-contact ACL risk, especially in women
- Progressive tendon loading programs prevent chronic tendinopathy progression

## Common Mistakes
1. **Missing high ankle sprains** → Pain above the lateral ankle = syndesmotic injury; treat more conservatively with longer timeline
2. **Returning too early from hamstring strain** → Reinjury risk is high; 6-week minimum for high-grade injuries
3. **X-ray over-reliance for scaphoid** → Clinical suspicion mandates splinting even with negative X-rays
4. **Missing septal hematoma** → Must look inside the nares — this is easy to miss and catastrophic if untreated
