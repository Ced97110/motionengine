# Defensive Box-Out Footwork

## Summary
Proper pivoting is the most important component of the defensive box-out. Before a defender can establish body contact with an offensive rebounder, he must execute the correct pivot — either a front (crossover) pivot or a reverse pivot — depending on his defensive position relative to the ball when the shot is taken. Without the correct initial pivot, the box-out can never succeed, regardless of how good the remaining fundamentals are. [S6, pp.201-205]

Two rules govern which pivot to use: the on-ball defender (defending the shooter) always uses the **front (crossover) pivot**; all off-ball defenders — whether one pass away or helpside — always use the **reverse pivot** to initiate contact with their assigned offensive rebounder.

## When to Use
- On every shot by the opponent — all five defenders must box out simultaneously
- Anytime a defender reads a shot going up, he must immediately pivot and initiate contact
- Critical in late-game situations where one offensive rebound can lead to a game-winning stickback
- Applies equally against zone and man-to-man defensive schemes

## Key Principles
1. **The pivot is the first and most critical phase** of the box-out — get it right before worrying about anything else [S6, p.204]
2. **On-ball defender uses front (crossover) pivot**: facing the shooter, read the direction the shooter moves toward the missed shot, then step across with the opposite foot directly into the shooter's path [S6, pp.204-206]
3. **All off-ball defenders use the reverse pivot**: swing the free (top) foot 180° around the pivot (lower/baseline) foot directly into the offensive opponent's predicted path [S6, pp.207-208]
4. **After the pivot, contact is made butt-to-gut** with the offensive rebounder — the defender's back presses into the opponent's midsection [S6, p.206]
5. **Wide base with elbows at shoulder height, bent 90°, pulled back to form a V** — this V controls the offensive rebounder [S6, p.203]
6. **Hands up, head up** — see the ball come off the backboard and be ready to grab the rebound [S6, p.203]
7. **Short, choppy steps** maintain balance and allow the defender to cut off the offensive rebounder's paths to the ball [S6, p.203]
8. **Never reach down or grab the opponent** — it causes fouls and prevents the defender from catching the rebound [S6, p.203]
9. **Stagger the feet** if the offensive rebounder is much stronger physically, to resist being pushed under the basket [S6, p.204]
10. **Don't get shoved under the basket** — there are no defensive rebounds under the backboard [S6, p.204]

## Player Responsibilities

### On-Ball Defender (defending the shooter)
- Pressure the shooter with hand high near the shooting hand — attempt to alter (not block) every shot: *"Don't leave the ground until the shooter leaves the ground"* [S6, p.204]
- Read the direction the shooter moves toward the missed shot
- Immediately make a **front crossover pivot**: step across with the foot matching the direction the shooter moves (shooter goes left → step with left foot; shooter goes right → step with right foot)
- Drive butt-to-gut into the shooter's path; elbows high and locked
- Use short, choppy steps to maintain contact

### One-Pass-Away Defender (denial stance)
- Is already in a denial stance with the lead (top) foot up and trail (lower) foot back
- On the shot, **reverse pivot off the trail (lower) foot**, swinging the lead (top) foot 180° into the path the offensive opponent must take
- The top foot is the free foot; the bottom foot is always the pivot foot [S6, p.208]
- Makes butt-to-gut contact and maintains it with short, choppy steps

### Helpside Defender (2+ passes away)
- Is in a pistols stance (ball-you-man flat triangle) sagging off their man, 1–1.5 steps off the passing line
- On the shot, **sprint under control toward the opponent**, running at the opponent's high (inside) shoulder — called "fanning" [S6, p.209]
- Fanning forces the offensive player toward the baseline (predictable direction)
- Initiate contact with a **reverse pivot outside the free throw lane** — the lower (baseline) foot is the pivot foot; swing the top (inside) foot into the opponent's predicted path
- **Critical: make contact outside the lane** — if contact is made inside the lane, the defender gets boxed in and the offensive player can retrieve the rebound over the defender's head [S6, p.210]

## Variations
### Front Crossover Pivot (On-Ball Defender)
Defender is facing the shooter. As the shot goes up, defender's momentum is moving slightly forward. He reads which direction the shooter cuts toward the rebound, then steps across with the matching foot directly into the shooter's path. He can also slide one step with the shooter before pivoting. The front pivot allows immediate ball-vision, letting the defender read the shot's ricochet direction. [S6, pp.204-206]

### Reverse Pivot (Off-Ball Defender — One Pass Away)
Defender is in a denial stance. The lead foot (top foot closest to the offensive player's path to the basket) becomes the free foot. The trail (lower) foot is the pivot foot. The defender swings the free foot 180° into the only path the offensive opponent can take toward the basket. [S6, pp.207-208]

### Reverse Pivot with Fanning (Helpside Defender)
Defender sprints at the high shoulder of the offensive rebounder — the "fan" — to channel the offensive player toward the baseline. Once close enough, the defender reverse pivots off the lower (baseline) foot, swinging the inside foot into the opponent's path. Contact must be initiated outside the free throw lane. [S6, pp.209-212]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using the wrong pivot type** → On-ball defenders sometimes try to reverse pivot, turning their back to the ball too early and losing ball-vision; off-ball defenders sometimes use a front pivot, which doesn't allow them to pick up the ball's flight path quickly enough [S6, p.204]
2. **Making contact inside the free throw lane** (helpside defenders) → The defender gets boxed in and the offensive rebounder has an over-the-head path to the ball; always initiate contact outside the lane [S6, p.210]
3. **Reaching down with hands to grab or hold the opponent** → Causes holding fouls AND prevents the defender from catching the rebound; keep hands up [S6, p.203]
4. **Not using short, choppy steps after initial contact** → Defender gets pushed aside; short, choppy steps maintain balance and block all paths [S6, p.203]
5. **Getting shoved under the basket** → No defensive rebounds exist under the basket; if an opponent is stronger, stagger the feet for more resistance [S6, p.204]
6. **One weak link in the box-out scheme** → Any defender who does not box out properly gives the opposition an offensive rebound opportunity that demoralizes the entire defensive unit [S6, p.212]
