# The Ten Steps Progression System

## Summary
Each of the six Explosive Six movement families is divided into ten progressive exercises called the **Ten Steps**. Step 1 is the easiest and should be accessible to any reasonably fit athlete. Each subsequent step increases in difficulty. The complete series from Step 1 to Step 10 is called a **chain**. Step 10 — the hardest exercise in each chain — is called the **Master Step** [S9, pp.16-18].

The key insight of this system is that speed, power, and agility are built by *mastering current steps*, not by racing to harder ones. Moving to a harder step only demonstrates previously built qualities — it does not build them.

## When to Use
- When introducing a player to explosive bodyweight training for the first time
- When designing multi-week athletic development progressions
- When a player plateaus in their explosive development — return to earlier steps and master them more deeply
- As a framework for coaching patience and long-term athletic development mindset

## Key Principles
1. **Begin at Step 1** — Even experienced athletes benefit from exploring earlier steps. Joint protection and body wisdom are built at the lower steps. "Relearning the basics is never a mistake" [S9, p.17].
2. **Progressions vs. Regressions**: Harder variations of a movement are called *progressions*; easier versions are called *regressions*. Both are valuable tools.
3. **Build speed and power by working steps you can do well** — The athlete who spends extra time mastering Step 3 will arrive at Step 5 more powerful than the athlete who rushed through.
4. **Moving up a step demonstrates, not builds**: "Moving up a step does NOT build speed, power or agility — it simply demonstrates how many of these qualities you have already built with patient work on the earlier steps" [S9, p.18].
5. **Milk each step for all it is worth** — Most athletes who struggle to advance have simply not invested enough time at the current step.
6. **Not all athletes need all ten steps** — But most who fail to advance have under-invested in prior steps, not over-invested [S9, p.18].

## Step Structure (Back Rotation Chain — Example)
The backward rotation chain illustrates how a Master Step (back flip) is made achievable through progressive steps:
1. Basic backward roll
2. Backward roll using hands
3. Bridge kick-overs (rotate backward from floor)
4. Monkey flip progressions (approach backwards rotation side-on)
5-8. Progressive monkey flip variations
9. Back handspring
10. **Back Flip** (Master Step) — stand, jump, rotate 360° backward, land on feet [S9, pp.11-12]

## Coaching Applications for Basketball
- **Vertical jump progressions**: Begin with stationary jumps → depth jumps → reactive jumps → sport-specific explosive jumps
- **Upper-body power progressions**: Standard push-ups → explosive push-ups → clap push-ups → plyometric progressions
- **Agility progressions**: Cone drills → reactive agility → skill-based explosive rotational movements
- The patience philosophy directly applies to skill teaching: players learn shooting, dribbling, and defense in exactly the same progressive fashion

## Common Mistakes
1. **Racing through early steps** → Deprives the joints of conditioning time and the nervous system of adaptation time. Return to earlier steps and master them.
2. **Skipping steps entirely** → Creates gaps in the chain; later steps become unstable because the foundation was never built properly.
3. **Measuring progress only by which step you've reached** → Progress is better measured by how well you execute the steps you have — quality of movement, not step number.
4. **Using this system for only one movement family** → The Ten Steps system must be applied to all six movement families for complete explosive development.
