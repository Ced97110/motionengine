# Halftime Adjustments

## Summary
The 14-minute halftime break is brief, so the process of delivering adjustments must be efficient and focused. pro coaching staffs use the break in two stages: a quick coaches-only meeting to identify key changes, followed by a structured team address. The goal is to present the two or three most impactful adjustments clearly — not overwhelm players with information. [S7, pp.297-298]

All major defensive schemes, anticipated matchups, and backup options should have already been rehearsed in pre-game preparation. Halftime adjustments are refinements and corrections, not wholesale reinventions.

## When to Use
- At the end of every first half, regardless of score
- When a specific offensive or defensive pattern is being exploited
- When a key matchup has gone wrong and requires a physical change
- When the opponent has made an unexpected tactical decision

## Key Principles
1. **Brevity is critical**: The 14-minute break is short. Coaches' meeting and team address combined must be concise. Prioritize the two or three most important changes.
2. **Observe systematically during the game**: Take written notes throughout the first half using a structured form (offensive notes, defensive notes, key plays, score). Do not rely on memory alone.
3. **Staff first, then team**: Coaches confer briefly while players handle personal needs and speak to the trainer. Use this time to align on message before addressing the team.
4. **Individual conversations follow**: After the group address, confer individually with players who need specific technical or matchup guidance.
5. **Postgame analysis depends on schedule**: Immediately after the game, quick postgame comments. The following day, depth of review is calibrated to upcoming game proximity.

## Halftime Adjustment Form (Figure 18.3)
A sample form from an actual pro game (vs. Charlotte, 3-16-07, final LA 102 – Opp 93) shows the following structure:

**Offense section:**
- Key observations (e.g., "P/Rolls were effective — keep good spacing")
- Tactical adjustments (e.g., "Hurt them inside — ready for quicker 2×s — read & respond")

**Defense section:**
- Priority assignments (e.g., "P/Rolls w/ Felton — contain dribble, no splits!")
- Matchup concerns (e.g., "Isolations w/ Wallace — weakside help was slow — anticipate!")
- General notes (e.g., "Single-Double w/ Carroll — better closeouts")

**Diagrams section:** Court diagrams of 4–6 key plays to review or adjust

**Post-game notes:** Board focus areas (rebounding, transition, look at specific rotations)

## Common Mistakes
1. Over-adjusting at halftime → pick the 2–3 highest-impact changes, not everything you noticed
2. No pre-game notes → without a written first-half observation record, adjustments are reactive and incomplete
3. Speaking directly to the team without a coaches' meeting first → misaligned staff undermines message clarity
4. Not following up individually with specific players → general team instructions miss personal technical corrections
