# Shooting Off the Move: Cutting or Dribbling

## Summary
Shooting off a dribble and shooting off a cut are both forms of shooting "off the move." The footwork and handwork for both situations are nearly identical: the shooter pivots off the **inside heel**, stops lateral momentum, pulls the ball into the shooting pocket, swings the free foot to square up, and rises straight into the shot. As Coach John Wooden always said: *"Be quick, but not in a hurry."* [S6, pp.161-163]

Mastering this technique in both scenarios (pass reception and dribble) gives players a consistent foundation. The closer the two motions are in rhythm and feel, the better the player becomes at both.

## When to Use
- Any time a player receives a pass while cutting laterally across the court
- Any time a player pulls up off the dribble after a drive or speed dribble
- Applies to both half-court catch-and-shoot and on-the-move pull-up situations
- Applies at all distances: mid-range, three-point line, and in transition

## Key Principles
1. **Outside hand dribbles, inside heel plants.** When dribbling on the left side of the court, dribble with the left (outside) hand; the right (inside) heel stops the lateral momentum. Vice versa on the right side. [S6, p.161]
2. **Last dribble is lowest and hardest.** The final dribble before the shot should be the lowest and hardest of all dribbles — this generates upward momentum for the takeoff. [S6, p.162]
3. **Land where you launch.** Good shooters rise straight up and come back down in nearly the same spot. Falling away or floating left/right dramatically reduces accuracy. [S6, p.162]
4. **Hands match the catch position.** When killing the dribble, both hands should arrive in a position similar to receiving a pass — identical shooting pocket entry for both scenarios. [S6, p.162]
5. **Free foot swings to square up.** After planting the inside heel, the free (outside) foot swings around to face the basket, squaring the shoulders before the jump begins. [S6, p.163]
6. **Same rhythm as the pass.** The timing and rhythm of getting hands into the shooting pocket should be nearly identical whether receiving a pass or picking up the dribble. [S6, p.162]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions:** Execute the inside-heel stop and free-foot square-up any time shooting off lateral movement. The technique does not change by position.

## Footwork Detail by Side

### Dribbling Left (Diagram 3-15)
- Dribble hand: **left** (outside, away from defender)
- Pivot/inside foot: **right**
- Execution: Drive right heel straight down into the floor as the last dribble contacts the floor; pull ball into shooting pocket; swing left (free) foot around to face the basket; jump and shoot.

### Dribbling Right (Diagram 3-16)
- Dribble hand: **right** (outside, away from defender)
- Pivot/inside foot: **left**
- Execution: Drive left heel straight down as the last dribble hits the floor; pull ball into pocket; swing right (free) foot around to square up; jump and shoot.

## Variations
### Shooting Off a Cut (Pass Reception)
Footwork is identical to the dribble pull-up. The inside heel drives down the moment the ball contacts the shooting (target) hand. The free foot swings around to complete the square-up. [S6, p.161]

### Exception: Outside-Foot Pivot
In stationary half-court offense (zone or man), a shooter with a very strong preference may be allowed to pivot off the outside foot rather than the inside foot — but only as an exception and only in stationary situations. [S6, p.161]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Drifting on the shot** → Drive the inside heel straight down to kill lateral momentum; do not let it fade into the jump.
2. **Soft last dribble** → Make the final dribble the lowest and hardest to generate takeoff energy.
3. **Hands not ready** → Hands should be arriving in shooting pocket simultaneously with the inside heel plant; late hands disrupt rhythm.
4. **Free foot doesn't swing all the way around** → Full square-up requires the free foot to complete its arc before the jump — do not shortcut this.
5. **Different mechanics for pass vs. dribble** → Train both scenarios so the rhythm is identical; inconsistency between the two reduces overall shooting effectiveness.
