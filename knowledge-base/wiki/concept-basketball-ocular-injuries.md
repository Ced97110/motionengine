# Basketball Ocular Injuries

## Summary
Basketball is the most common cause of sports-related ocular injuries, accounting for 22.6% of total sports-related ocular trauma in the United States — ahead of baseball and softball. The estimated incidence is 1 injury per 10 athlete years in NCAA men's college basketball. Most injuries result from direct trauma from opponents' hands or elbows. Common injuries include eyelid lacerations, periorbital contusions, and corneal abrasions. Less common but serious injuries include traumatic hyphema, globe rupture, and orbital fractures [S10, pp.217-219].

## When to Use (Relevance)
- Sideline evaluation after finger-to-eye contact, elbow strike, or facial trauma
- Determining which injuries can be managed courtside vs. require ophthalmology referral
- Post-game/practice review of contact eye injuries

## Key Injury Types and Management

### 1. Eyelid Lacerations
- Common in basketball
- Simple lacerations: standard repair by medical personnel
- **Refer to ophthalmology if** laceration involves: eyelid margins, tarsal plate, levator palpebrae muscle, orbicularis oculi, orbital fat, lacrimal sack, or lacrimal duct (many require microsurgery)
- Perioperative antibiotics typically indicated [S10, p.218]

### 2. Periorbital Contusion
- Direct trauma → periorbital contusion (benign, but must rule out complications)
- Examination: test ocular motion, visual acuity; palpate orbital rim and midface for fractures
- If no complications: athlete may return to play; manage with ice and anti-inflammatory medication [S10, p.218]

### 3. Corneal Abrasions
- Most common cause: trauma from another player's finger
- More common in women's basketball (possible relation to fingernail length)
- Diagnosis: fluorescein dye staining + Wood's lamp
- Most heal within 72 h
- Small abrasions: ophthalmic ointment for comfort
- Large abrasions / contact lens wearers: antibiotic ointment; no contact lenses until healed
- Decreased visual acuity → ophthalmology referral [S10, p.218]

### 4. Orbital Fracture ("Blowout" Fracture)
- Uncommon but significant — orbital fractures account for 17% of all sports maxillofacial injuries
- Most commonly involves orbital floor; medial floor also common
- Signs: periorbital ecchymosis, orbital rim tenderness/step-offs, pain with extraocular movements
- **Limited upward gaze** → inferior rectus entrapment
- **Proptosis + visual changes** → retrobulbar hematoma
- **All suspected orbital fractures**: urgent referral for ophthalmologic and maxillofacial evaluation; surgical consideration
- Return to sport: wear rigid transparent face shield until completely healed [S10, p.218]

### 5. Traumatic Hyphema
- Blood in the anterior chamber between cornea and iris from shearing of microvasculature
- Traumatic iritis is the most common cause
- Examination: visible layering of blood in anterior chamber
- Management: analgesics, antiemetics; eye patch, cycloplegics, and avoiding supine position suggested but lack evidence
- Most resolve without intervention
- **All hyphemas**: refer for ophthalmology evaluation (minority complicated by re-bleeding, corneal staining, optic nerve atrophy) [S10, pp.218-219]

### 6. Globe Rupture
- High-force blunt trauma; challenging to diagnose (ruptured portion not always visible)
- **Warning signs**: pain, visual loss, hyphema, pupil irregularity, 360° subconjunctival hemorrhage
- Seidel test: fluorescein dye with subsequent leakage from globe = positive
- CT imaging confirms diagnosis
- **Immediate management**: rigid eye shield; anti-emetics + analgesics (Valsalva/vomiting can worsen injury); minimize eye manipulation
- **Emergent ophthalmologic consultation** — operative repair required; delay risks endophthalmitis and blindness [S10, p.219]

## Key Principles
1. Basketball is the #1 sport for ocular trauma — take all eye injuries seriously
2. Any visual change, pupil irregularity, or extraocular movement limitation = immediate referral
3. Globe rupture is a true ocular emergency — rigid eye shield and anti-emetics before anything else
4. Corneal abrasions are common and self-limiting; antibiotic ointment for large/contact-lens cases
5. Do not return player with hyphema or suspected globe rupture under any circumstances

## Common Mistakes
1. **Missing complex eyelid laceration anatomy** → performing simple repair when microsurgery is needed
2. **Allowing contact lenses after corneal abrasion** → risk of Pseudomonas/Acanthamoeba infection
3. **Minimizing a hyphema** → re-bleeding risk; always refer ophthalmology
4. **Missing globe rupture due to lack of obvious exterior wound** → use Seidel test and CT; look for all warning signs
