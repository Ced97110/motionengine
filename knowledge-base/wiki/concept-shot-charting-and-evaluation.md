# Shot Charting and Shooting Evaluation

## Summary
Becoming a great shooter requires more than technique — it requires documentation and honest self-evaluation. the shooting specialist argues that keeping a shooting journal (shot chart) and regularly evaluating shooting mechanics is "in many ways, the most crucial step toward evolving into a truly great shooter." Without charting, players cannot track improvement, identify weaknesses, or hold themselves accountable to a standard.

The system has two components: (1) a **shot chart** that logs every practice workout with date, shot types, locations, and percentages; and (2) a **shooting evaluation form** that systematically checks every mechanical element of the shot from grip to follow-through.

## When to Use
- Every individual shooting workout (shot chart)
- At the beginning and end of the off-season (evaluation form)
- After any significant technique change to track improvement
- When a player is in a slump or struggling with consistency
- As a coaching tool for player development feedback

## Key Principles

### Shot Charting
1. **Chart every workout**: Record the date, time, duration, gym location, shot types worked, and coaching observations.
2. **Track both percentage AND shot types**: Total makes/attempts (shooting percentage) tells you efficiency; shot types tell you what you've worked on.
   - Example shot types to track: midrange catch-and-shoot, threes on the move (left-to-right, right-to-left), show-and-go right, show-and-go left, free throws
3. **Note coaching feedback**: If a coach or partner observes a flaw (e.g., "dropping the balance hand too soon," "misses consistently short"), log it and plan to address it next session.
4. **Mark shot locations on the chart diagram**: Use dots to denote shooting spots; write your percentage below (e.g., 40/50 = 80%).
5. **Charting builds discipline and confidence**: Documentation shows a desire to improve and provides concrete evidence of progress — or areas that need work. "All players lose confidence in their game when they become unsure of themselves. Charting your shots provides you with documentation."
6. **Work on technique in the off-season**: Technique changes take extensive practice hours — making them mid-season is difficult. Use the off-season to evaluate, adjust, and chart results.

### Shooting Evaluation Form (Figure 10.2)
A comprehensive checklist covering every mechanical element. Key categories:

**Grip**
- Do thumbs form a T?
- Is there a "peek-through" (ball is off the palms)?

**Balance Hand**
- Do fingers point toward ceiling?
- Is hand on the side of the ball?
- Does thumb go back toward ear?
- Is ball off the palm?

**Shooting Hand**
- Is ball off the palm?
- Does index finger point to eyebrow?
- Are there wrinkles in the wrist (fully cocked)?

**Shooting Arm**
- Is arm in an L-shape (not S or V)?
- Is elbow under the ball ("lifter")?
- Is elbow ahead of the ball ("slinger")?
- Is elbow behind the ball ("pusher")?

**Body Position and Setup**
- Feet shoulder-width apart? Not too close, not too wide?
- Shoulders square?
- Both heels leave the floor at the same time?
- Both knees bend the same amount at the same time?
- Body goes straight up?
- Dominant foot forward? Dominant toe pointing to target?
- Head and hips in line? Head stays down and level on the shot?
- Eyes watching the rim, not the ball?
- Elbow directly under the ball?

**Finish**
- Elbow above the eyebrow?
- Shooting hand in the hoop?
- Balance hand up and still?
- Proper arc on the shot?

**Catching and Shooting: Right**
- Inside-foot plant? Toe open toward basket before planting? Feet squared? Feet land in same spot?

**Catching and Shooting: Left**
- Inside-foot plant? Outside-foot reverse plant? Toe open? Feet squared? Feet land in same spot?

**Shooting Off the Dribble: Right**
- Dribbling with right hand? Inside-foot (left) plant? Toe open? Picks up ball with knees flexed and two hands? Feet squared? Land in same spot?

**Shooting Off the Dribble: Left**
- Dribbling with left hand? Inside-foot (right) plant? Toe open? Picks up ball with knees flexed and two hands? Feet squared? Land in same spot?

### Workout Structure
Hopla recommends the following session structure:
1. **Start with one-hand form shooting** — isolate shooting arm mechanics
2. **Add the balance hand** — integrate the non-dominant hand
3. **Progress to game shots at game speed** — only after the body and mind are warm and technically prepared

> "Too many players practice slower than game speed and then have trouble making shots during an actual game." [S5, p.130]

> "Perfect practice makes perfect." [S5, p.130]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not charting at all** → Players have no baseline, can't track progress, and repeat the same errors workout after workout
2. **Only tracking makes, not shot types** → Knowing you shot 70% is meaningless if you don't know which shots and from where
3. **Making technique changes in-season** → Mechanical changes require extensive off-season reps; in-season changes disrupt game performance
4. **Skipping form shooting at the start of a session** → Starting with game-speed shooting before the mechanics are dialed in builds bad habits
5. **Practicing at half-speed and expecting game results** → Must train at game speed once technique is established
