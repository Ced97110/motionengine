# Getting Open on the Perimeter

## Summary
Before a perimeter player can attack with the ball, they must first get open to receive it. Stan Van Gundy's system identifies five foundational ways to create separation from a defender, plus one advanced "circle around" move. The core principle across all methods: read the defender's position, use body contact to freeze them momentarily, then change direction decisively to create separation at the right spot on the floor.

This skill is foundational — Dwyane Wade, Rashard Lewis, and Hedo Turkoglu all excelled at freeing themselves perimeter-side before attacking with the ball [S7, pp.19-20].

## When to Use
- Any time a perimeter player needs to receive a pass from a teammate
- When the defender is playing at a three-quarter contesting position
- When spacing must be preserved — players must get open in a spot from which they can pass, drive, or shoot
- Should be initiated only when the teammate with the ball is ready to pass it
- Take no longer than 2–3 seconds to get open; longer allows defense to recover

## Key Principles
1. **Move with normal steps** toward the defender — short, choppy steps or watching the passer telegraphs your cut to the defense
2. **Make contact (bump)** with the defender using the inside shoulder and forearm (no push); this freezes the defender momentarily
3. **Pop out at a different angle**, not back and forth on the same line — forces the defender to cover greater distance
4. **Stay low** (knees bent) so you're ready to attack immediately after receiving the ball
5. **Use the hand farthest from the defender** as a target for the passer; catch with two hands
6. **Land facing the basket** — turn your feet toward the basket as you land so you can see the whole floor and play one on one immediately
7. **Know your shooting range** — receive the ball only in spots from which you can effectively pass, dribble, or shoot

## The Five Cutting Methods

### V Cut
The most common way to get open at any spot on the floor. To receive high: bring the defender low, then pop out high. To receive low: bring the defender high, then pop out low.

**First count:** Face the basket; take 1–2 steps toward the defender (shoulders squared to basket). Plant the inside foot (nearest baseline) between the defender's legs; lean on the defender's chest with right forearm and shoulder (no pushing). Pivot on the outside foot, make a crossover step with the right leg over the defender's forward foot to freeze them.

**Second count:** Push off on the ball of the inside foot, make a stride with the outside foot. Land in a two-count stop (left foot first → pivot foot, then right foot). Ask for the ball with the outside hand (farthest from defender). On catch: make a front turn to face the basket in triple-threat position.

### Reverse Cut
Used at any phase of the game — with or without the ball.

**First count:** Take 1–2 steps toward defender; plant the outside foot between their legs; lean with left forearm and shoulder on defender's chest (no push). Pivot on outside foot and make a reverse, "sitting" momentarily on the defender's forward leg to freeze them.

**Second count:** Step forward to create space, receive the ball, make a front turn to face the basket in triple-threat position.

### V Cut and Reverse (Combination)
If the defender maintains tight coverage after a V cut, immediately chain into a reverse to create the needed separation. Start the V cut; if coverage remains tight, execute the reverse as described.

### L Cut
Used when starting with your back to the baseline (from mid-to-low post area).

**First count:** Start near the lane (mid-to-low post), facing midcourt; ball is in the middle of the court, defender is face-to-face. Take 1–2 steps up toward midcourt, then plant the inside foot (nearest the lane) near or over the defender's outside foot; lean inside shoulder and forearm on defender's chest (no push).

**Second count:** Shift weight onto the ball of the inside foot; make a lateral stride and hop; land in a two-count stop; receive the ball; make a front turn to triple-threat position.

### L Cut and Reverse (Combination)
If the defender maintains coverage after the L cut, chain into a reverse move immediately.

### Special Move: Circle Around
Used when the defender is covering nearly face-to-face.
1. Bring the defender low with both hands extended overhead
2. When near the baseline, bring hands down (arms extended as if swimming) around the inside hip of the defender to momentarily freeze them
3. Curl around the defender and pop out to the **same side** you started the move from
4. Key: do not push the defender; go around them

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Master all five cutting methods; most frequently uses V cut at top of key and wing
- **SG/SF**: V cut and reverse off the wing; L cut from mid-post; circle around vs. tight face-guarding
- **PF**: L cut from low/mid post to receive at the elbow

## Common Mistakes
1. **Short, choppy steps and watching the passer** → defender reads the cut; use normal steps and don't watch the ball
2. **Popping out on the same line back and forth** → use different angles to force the defender to cover more distance
3. **Receiving in the wrong spot** → choose a reception point where you can shoot, drive, or pass — know your range
4. **Not facing the basket on catch** → turn feet toward the basket as you land; failure to do so wastes time before attacking
5. **Overextending the bump** → lean with shoulder and forearm only; a push is a foul and loses credibility of the move
6. **Getting open too slowly** → exceeding 2–3 seconds allows the defense to recover; be decisive and quick
