# Kip-Up Progression Chain

## Objective
Develop total-body explosive power — specifically waist (hips/lower back), leg speed, and dynamic agility — through a progressive floor-to-standing movement chain culminating in the no-hands kip-up.

## Setup
- **Players**: 1 (solo drill)
- **Space**: Any flat surface with enough room to lie down; a soft or cushioned mat recommended for beginners
- **Equipment**: None (bodyweight only)
- **Note**: Avoid if you have neck problems

## Execution

All steps in this chain share a common foundation:
1. **Start position**: Sit on the floor with knees bent and heels on the ground (or lying flat for harder progressions).
2. **Roll back**: Swing legs up and place hands in bridge position (by ears, fingers pointing toward feet) — or in the arm position specific to each step.
3. **Tight ball**: Roll onto upper-back and shoulders, pulling bent knees close to head to maximize spring position.
4. **Unravel**: Explode legs in an arc — *up*, then *down* toward the ground — as the arms push simultaneously.
5. **Land and stand**: Feet touch down on balls of feet into a squat; head and hands forward for balance; stand upright.
6. **Reset**: Squat down, roll back, and repeat.

**Universal Progression Note**: Beginning from a lying-flat position (instead of sitting) adds momentum difficulty to ANY step in this chain.

---

## Progressions

### Step 4 (Beginner): Bridge Kip
**Goal**: Generate enough lower-body power to lift the head and upper-back off the floor — *not* to get to standing yet.

1. Sit, roll back, squeeze into tight ball with knees near head.
2. Explode legs in arc up-then-down; simultaneously push palms into the floor.
3. Use momentum to lift head and upper-back off the floor.
4. As feet strike the floor, hold the bridge position (supported only by palms and both feet).
5. Lower and repeat.

**Regression**: Practice kicking in an arc and striking feet to ground while keeping shoulders/head on floor. Head/shoulders will lift with repetition.
**Progression**: Improve bridge quality — extend arms and legs more fully each session. [S9, p.2]

---

### Step 5 (Beginner): Butt Kip
**Goal**: Lift hands off the floor and throw torso forward to land on butt with torso upright.

1. Same setup as Bridge Kip.
2. Explode legs in arc; push palms *hard enough to lift them off the ground*.
3. As you kick off, throw torso and hands forward — like an explosive sit-up.
4. Aim to land on butt AND feet simultaneously with torso upright (not on feet alone yet).
5. Lower and repeat.

**Regression**: Begin with minimal momentum — kick in arc, land on butt with head/back just slightly off floor. Build to full upright finish.
**Progression**: Generate more momentum until you land on feet a fraction of a second before falling back to butt — this is the threshold for Step 6. [S9, p.4]

---

### Step 6 (Intermediate): Half Kip
**Goal**: Fuse leg-arc and torso-throw into a single explosive pattern — landing on both feet, even briefly.

1. Same setup. Roll back, tight ball.
2. Explode legs in arc; push palms off floor.
3. As feet touch down, *continue* throwing torso and hands forward.
4. Land on both feet — even if only for a fraction of a second — before falling back to butt.
5. Roll back and repeat.

**Regression**: If tipping backward, you are close — the body is nearly achieving full kip. Continue drilling.
**Progression**: Use a stable object or partner's hands to briefly support the upright position. Then drill without assistance — power and repetition are the only path forward. [S9, p.6]

---

### Step 7 (Intermediate): Kip-Up
**Goal**: Land stably on both feet in a squat and stand fully upright.

1. Same setup. Roll back, tight ball.
2. Explode legs in arc; push palms off floor.
3. As feet touch down, keep torso and hands forward.
4. Land on *balls* of both feet in a squatting position; keep head and hands forward until stable.
5. Stand upright. Squat back down, roll back, and repeat.

**Regression**: Try landing in a wider stance to get feet under hips faster.
**Progression**: Land with feet closer together (requires more forward momentum). Works for all kip-up styles. [S9, p.8]

**Coaching Note**: *"Achieving this infamous move is impossible without an explosive waist (hips and lower back), super-fast legs and the total-body agility of a panther."* [S9, p.8]

---

### Step 8 (Advanced): Straight-Leg Kip-Up
**Goal**: Perform the kip-up with straight legs, forcing hips and waist to generate all momentum.

1. Sit with legs *straight* (not bent).
2. Roll back, swing straight legs up over head; knees should come close to head.
3. Explode by straightening at hips; simultaneously push palms off the floor.
4. As you take off, *bend* legs quickly and whip them underneath you to catch the forward motion.
5. Land on balls of both feet in a squat; stand upright.

**Regression**: Once you can do a regular kip-up (Step 7), reduce leg bend gradually. More knee bend = easier.
**Progression**: Begin lying flat on the floor instead of rolling from sitting — eliminates the momentum advantage of the sitting roll. [S9, p.10]

---

### Step 9 (Advanced): Wushu Kip-Up
**Goal**: Kip-up with arms extended out to the sides (not in bridge position) — intermediate step to no-hands kip-up.

1. Sit, roll back; place arms *straight out at right-angles* from the body on the ground.
2. Tight ball, knees close to head.
3. Explode legs in arc; push down through backs of arms (not palms).
4. As feet touch down, keep torso and hands forward.
5. Land on balls of both feet in squat; push hips and chest forward until stable; stand upright.

**Why outstretched arms**: Bridge-style arm position is very powerful. Outstretched arms force you to use *much less* arm power — training the waist/hips to compensate. This is the correct bridge to no-hands kip-ups; reducing to one arm fails because the body just compensates by pushing harder through the remaining arm. [S9, p.12]

**Regression**: Begin with bridge arm position but push through knuckles/backs of hands. Straighten arms progressively over time. Fully outstretched = hardest arm variation.
**Progression**: Combine with straight-leg technique from Step 8.

---

### Master Step (Elite): No-Hands Kip-Up
**Goal**: Kip-up with arms never touching the floor — pure waist and leg power.

1. Sit, roll back; keep arms to the *sides* of the body at all times — they must never contact the floor.
2. Tight ball.
3. Explode legs in arc; keep arms off floor and to the sides.
4. As feet touch down, keep torso and hands forward.
5. Land on balls of both feet in squat; arms to sides; stand upright.

**Elite progressions**: Begin from lying flat; attempt with straight legs; cross arms over chest.

**Coaching Note**: *"Master this advanced drill, kid, and your total-body speed and agility will start bursting off the charts. The power here is really generated by the waist and legs."* [S9, p.14]

**Safety**: Avoid if you have neck problems. [S9, p.14]

## Coaching Points
- The kip-up family is NOT a binary all-or-nothing skill — it is a *family of techniques* with a true beginner-to-elite progression
- The tight-ball position (knees close to head) is the key loading position — the tighter, the better spring
- The "unravel" cue: legs explode up THEN arc DOWN toward the ground; it is not a straight kick
- Keep head and hands forward on landing — this is the balance anchor
- Beginning from lying flat (vs. sitting) increases difficulty at ANY step in the chain
- Feet-together landing is harder than wide-stance landing for all kip-up styles
- The wushu arm position is a *teaching tool* — it forces hip/waist dominance and is the correct bridge to hands-free performance [S9, pp.2-20]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Kicking straight instead of in an arc** → Cue: "up then down" — the arc generates the rotational momentum needed to project the center of gravity forward
2. **Not squeezing into a tight ball** → Correction: Pull knees as close to the head as possible before unraveling
3. **Passive arm push** → Correction: Push down through palms explosively enough to lift them off the floor (Steps 5+)
4. **Not throwing torso forward** → Correction: The torso throw is a simultaneous action with the leg arc, not sequential
5. **Treating kip-ups as all-or-nothing** → Correction: Start at Step 4 regardless of skill level; the chain builds all required qualities progressively
