# Anatomical Core Training for Basketball

## Summary
The **anatomical core** refers to the musculature with attachments originating on the spine, rib cage, and skull — including spinal erectors, glutes, abdominals, and obliques. Together these muscles function as support structures of the spine, providing stability as the athlete moves in all dimensions. Without a strong core, performance is compromised and injury risk increases significantly [S11, Ch.8].

The core acts as a bridge that transfers forces generated by the legs and hips to the upper body. A basketball athlete's ability to accept body contact and maintain a stable torso on both offense and defense is a critical aspect of the game. Training must address both **deep stabilizers** (transverse abdominis, erector spinae, multifidus, quadratus lumborum) and **superficial muscles** (rectus abdominis, internal/external obliques, erector spinae) working synergistically in all three planes of movement [S11, Ch.8].

## When to Use
- Year-round as part of every strength and conditioning session
- Post-play position battles — center holding low-post position requires sustained isometric core strength
- Transition defense — maintaining torso stability during rapid direction changes
- Contact absorption — screening, boxing out, fighting for rebounds
- Shooting and passing — dynamic core transfers leg/hip force through the trunk to the shooting arm

## Key Principles
1. **Isometric core strength** — generating tension without joint movement; essential for holding low-post position while absorbing contact [S11, Ch.8]
2. **Dynamic core strength** — transferring forces between the lower and upper body during athletic movements such as jumping, driving, and shooting [S11, Ch.8]
3. **Train all dimensions** — sagittal (flexion/extension), frontal (lateral flexion), and transverse (rotation) planes must all be trained
4. **Deep before superficial** — stabilizers (transverse abdominis, multifidus) must be activated first; they provide the foundation for the more visible outer muscles
5. **Lumbar spine protection** — all exercises should protect against excessive lumbar extension and rotation

## Player Responsibilities
- **C/PF**: Isometric core critical for post positioning — "stay low and wide, torso upright" while absorbing contact from defenders
- **PG/SG/SF**: Dynamic core essential for drive-and-kick, change of direction, and contact finishing
- **All positions**: Rotational core for pivoting, screening, rebounding

## Variations
### Isometric Holds
Front plank, side plank, deadbug hold — sustained tension without movement. Foundational for beginners.

### Dynamic Core
V-up, side rotational V-up, birddog plank — introduce movement through full range while maintaining stability.

### Anti-Rotation
Pallof press, standing Pallof press, standing Pallof overhead press — resist rotational force from a cable; most basketball-specific as they mirror defensive positioning and contact scenarios.

### Progressive Complexity
Suspension side plank, standing Pallof overhead press with lateral step — combine instability with movement, mirroring the multi-directional demands of basketball.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Lumbar extension during leg lift** (birddog plank) → Cue: "Keep hips level and spine neutral; if back arches, reduce range of motion"
2. **Gap between lumbar spine and floor** (deadbug) → Cue: "Tuck buttocks under to flatten low back before starting movement"
3. **Hips sagging or piking in front plank** → Cue: "Ankle, knees, hips, torso, and head all in line; squeeze glutes"
4. **Rotating the torso in Pallof press** → Cue: "Resist the cable; squeeze glutes to keep pelvis neutral"
5. **Using momentum instead of core** (V-up) → Cue: "Slow and controlled; knees and elbows stay fully extended throughout"
