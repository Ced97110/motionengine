# Pick-and-Roll Defense — Complete Framework

## Summary
The pick-and-roll (PnR) screen on the ball is one of the most frequently used and effective offensive sets in basketball. It attacks guards who cannot get over screens, post players who won't step out with the screener, and weak-side defenders who sleep on jump shooters. Elite PnR guards (Nash, Chris Paul, John Wall) make their living running PnR almost exclusively. Coaches must have a well-developed defensive plan that addresses multiple PnR locations, entry patterns, and personnel situations [S13, p.210].

## When to Use
- Every half-court possession against teams that run PnR-based offenses
- In transition when drag screens are set while the defense is retreating
- In any game situation as the primary or secondary defensive scheme

## Key Principles — Three Universal PnR Rules
1. **The defender on the screen setter must communicate** — talk and let the ball defender know the pick is coming before it arrives.
2. **The ball defender must not let the guard split the two defenders** — no splits, no middle, keep the dribbler to the side.
3. **If the decision is to trap, stay in it until the guard passes** — abandoning a trap halfway is worse than not trapping at all [S13, p.210].

## Five Defensive Technique Options
The choice depends on talent, team speed/quickness, the ability to anticipate, and the opponent's PnR effectiveness:

1. **Trap** — X1 and X5 apply a full double-team; all other defenders rotate to cover roll and kick-outs.
2. **Hedge and Get Back (Soft Show)** — X5 takes a quick step out showing as if to trap, buying time for X1 to recover, then gets back to O5.
3. **Hard Hedge** — X5 steps out aggressively and does NOT let the dribbler split or turn the corner; X1 fights over.
4. **Show or Get Through** — X5 shows but backs off to help X1 maintain a good defensive position; primarily used to keep the dribbler in front.
5. **Switch** — X1 and X5 switch assignments; best against size-neutral matchups; worst when it creates a mismatch [S13, p.210].

## Sideline PnR
- Most important decision: how to play the guard with the ball, based on tendencies.
- **Go over the screen** → against good jump shooters.
- **Go under** → against players who prefer to attack the basket.
- Rotation on trap: ball goes to O3 → X4 covers rolling O5; ball goes to O4 → X3 covers O5; X5 rotates to basket and picks up the open man; X2 sinks to find O4 if X4 rotates [S13, pp.211-212].

## Middle PnR
- Difficult to defend because the screener (O5) rolls to the basket OR pops out for a medium-range jumper; creates a high-low threat.
- All-important decision comes **at the point of the screen** — not letting O1 split X1 and X5.
- **Soft coverage option**: X1 drops back even with O5, avoiding the screen and playing soft to keep O1 in front — allows X2/X3 to stay home and reduces three-point and draw-and-kick opportunities.
- When X5 stays with the dribbler, O5 rolls immediately — forcing rotation. O4 then pops up the lane, creating a dangerous high-low.
- **Zone, switch, or attacking the ball handler** can eliminate rotations [S13, pp.212-213].

## Drag PnR (Transition)
- **Most difficult** — screen is set while the defense is retreating; spontaneous and random.
- Normally set just after the dribbler enters the frontcourt to either sideline, allowing penetration to the middle.
- Elite guards (NBA level) prefer drag screens at the top of the key for a middle drive or draw-and-kick.
- **Best coverage**: Call out the screen early; if X1 gets screened, X5 must switch, slow down (string out), and delay O1 from the middle — giving X1 time to recover. X5 must not let O1 penetrate the middle off the dribble.
- If O1 beats X1, X5 must be prepared to help [S13, pp.214-215].

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (X1)**: Must not be split; fight over or under the screen based on predetermined technique; recover immediately if screened in a drag.
- **C/PF (X5)**: Communicate the screen; execute the chosen technique (show, hard hedge, switch, or trap); rotate to basket on the pass out of a trap.
- **X2, X3, X4**: Rotation responsibilities based on which direction the kick-out pass goes; must be mentally prepared before the trap is applied.

## Common Mistakes
1. **No communication before the screen** → X5 must yell before contact, not during.
2. **Allowing the dribbler to split X1 and X5** → This is the cardinal PnR defensive sin — opens the lane completely.
3. **Abandoning a trap before the pass** → Half-hearted traps give the ball handler the worst of both worlds: a defender in his face AND an open roll man.
4. **X5 not rotating back on the kick-out** → After trapping, X5 must sprint back to the basket to pick up the open man.
5. **Playing straight up on elite PnR guards** → Dallas showed this in the 2011 NBA Finals — zone adjustments can neutralize the Miami Heat's PnR-based attack [S13, p.212].
