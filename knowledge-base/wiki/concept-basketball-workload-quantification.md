# Basketball Workload Quantification

## Summary
Before fatigue can be managed, the training and competition workload that produced it must be accurately quantified. In basketball, two primary methods are used: **microtechnology** (inertial measurement units/accelerometers measuring external load) and **session rating of perceived exertion (s-RPE)** measuring internal load. These tools measure different constructs and must be used together for a complete picture of athlete workload. [S12, pp.6-8]

The individual dose-response relationship to workload varies based on modifiable factors (strength, aerobic/anaerobic capacity) and non-modifiable factors (age, sex, anatomy, genetics). A strong correlation (r = 0.68) between the Yo-Yo intermittent recovery test and s-RPE in elite European basketball athletes demonstrates that aerobic fitness mediates how hard a given session feels — fitter athletes perceive the same work as easier.

## When to Use
- Every training session and competition throughout the full season
- To compare demands of different drill types (3v3 vs 4v4 vs 5v5)
- To verify pre-season training is adequately preparing players for competition demands
- To adjust training load based on game schedule (1-game weeks vs 2-game weeks)
- To develop position-specific conditioning programs

## Key Principles
1. **Measure both external and internal load** — External load (what the body does) and internal load (how the body responds) measure different constructs; lateral basketball movements increase s-RPE by 13–25% even when external load is controlled [S12, p.8]
2. **s-RPE is the universal cross-modality tool** — Because resistance training (sets × reps × weight) and court training (accelerations, velocity) use incompatible units, s-RPE is the only metric that can be directly compared across all training types [S12, p.7]
3. **IMUs provide position-specific insight** — Accelerometer Load (AL) data reveals that point guards accumulate higher AL than other positions regardless of drill type, likely due to greater movement demands and lower body mass enabling faster acceleration [S12, p.7]
4. **Game-based drills > competition in pre-season** — Semi-professional basketball pre-season training produces significantly higher AL than competition (624 ± 113 AL vs. 449 ± 118 AL; ES = 1.54), supporting the adequacy of pre-season preparation [S12, p.7]
5. **Larger-sided games produce less AL** — Full court 3v3 and 5v5 produce higher AL/min than 2v2 and 4v4 (18.7 vs 14.6 and 17.9 vs 13.8 AL/min respectively), suggesting the optimal drill size for conditioning load may be 3v3 or 5v5 [S12, p.7]

## Microtechnology (IMU/Accelerometer)

### What It Measures
- Position, direction, velocity, accelerations, decelerations
- Integrates triaxial accelerometers, gyroscopes, and magnetometers into a single Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU)
- Accelerometer Load (AL) calculated by summating instantaneous changes in resultant acceleration over time using the Cartesian formula: √[(xn−xn−1)² + (yn−yn−1)² + (zn−zn−1)²]

### Strengths
- Objective, continuous external load measurement
- Detects basketball-specific movements (shuffling, cutting, jumping)
- Can inform position-specific conditioning prescription
- Enables drill-by-drill comparison

### Limitations
- Played in indoor stadiums (GPS not available; IMU required)
- Equipment acquisition cost and feasibility
- Reliability/validity for basketball-specific lateral movements still being established
- Does not capture internal physiological response

### Key Findings (Basketball-Specific)
- Full court 3v3: 18.7 ± 4.1 AL/min
- Full court 5v5: 17.9 ± 4.6 AL/min
- Full court 4v4: 13.8 ± 2.5 AL/min
- Full court 2v2: 14.6 ± 2.8 AL/min
- Point guards consistently highest AL regardless of drill
- Pre-season training AL >> competition AL in semi-professional basketball [S12, p.7]

## Session Rating of Perceived Exertion (s-RPE)

### How It Works
- Modified Borg RPE scale (0–10; 0 = rest, 10 = maximal exertion)
- Athlete rates session intensity; multiply rating × duration (minutes) = arbitrary units (AU) of internal training load
- Administered 30+ minutes post-session to avoid acute exercise bias

### Strengths
- Non-invasive, fast, free
- Applicable across all training modalities (court, weights, conditioning)
- Widely validated in basketball literature
- Sensitive to weekly and seasonal training load fluctuations

### Key Findings (Basketball-Specific)
- Weekly s-RPE significantly differs between 0-game weeks vs. 1-game vs. 2-game weeks (3334 ± 256 vs. 2928 ± 303 vs. 2791 ± 239 AU) in elite European basketball
- Strong correlation between s-RPE and Edwards' TRIMP heart rate model (r = 0.68) in the same population
- Moderate correlation (r = 0.49) with accelerometer-derived AL in semi-professional Australian basketball — confirming they measure different constructs [S12, p.8]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using only s-RPE or only accelerometer** → Both are needed; they measure different training constructs and neither alone is sufficient
2. **Comparing across athletes without individual normalization** → Aerobic capacity dramatically affects s-RPE; fitter athletes rate the same session easier
3. **Not accounting for game schedule in training load targets** → Weeks with 2 games require substantially lower practice loads than single-game or no-game weeks
4. **Assuming 5v5 produces the highest conditioning load** → Research shows 3v3 and 5v5 produce similar and higher AL/min than 2v2 and 4v4; optimal drill size depends on the goal
