# Straight-Stick Shooting Concept

## Summary
The straight-stick concept is a foundational alignment framework for basketball shooting, particularly valuable for youth players. The principle is simple: a straight line should be drawable from the shooter's wrist, down through the elbow, through the knee, and down to the toe of the front foot. When all four checkpoints are in one vertical plane, the shot is properly aligned and will travel straight toward the target. Any deviation — especially a crooked elbow — causes the ball to veer off target.

The concept was designed so that coaches can quickly scan a player's shooting form for a single alignment indicator rather than checking multiple independent elements. Once a player internalizes the straight-stick image, self-correction becomes immediate and visual. [S15, pp.181-189]

## When to Use
- Teaching shooting for the first time to beginners and youth players
- Correcting a player who is consistently missing left or right
- Diagnosing the root cause of any crooked shot before working on other mechanics
- As a daily alignment check at the start of any shooting practice

## Key Principles
1. **The straight line**: wrist → elbow → knee → toe of the front foot must form one continuous straight line aimed at the basket
2. **Elbow in**: a flared elbow is the single most destructive fault in shooting; it breaks the straight-stick alignment immediately
3. **Wrist cocked**: the wrist must be cocked backward with a visible wrinkle behind it before the shot; an uncocked wrist causes the ball to roll out of the hand
4. **Fingertips only**: the ball rests on the pads/fingertips of the shooting hand — never in the palm; palm contact destroys alignment and spin
5. **Shooting cup**: spread the shooting hand until it hurts, then relax — the naturally relaxed position IS the proper shooting cup
6. **Ball position**: the ball should be in front of the face, or slightly to the shooting-hand side — never behind the head or to the side
7. **Target eye**: sight the target with the shooting-side eye; shoulders squared to the basket; do not watch the ball in flight

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: the straight-stick principle applies equally to all positions; every player must master it before attempting to extend their range or add complexity

## The Lift-Extend-Flip Sequence
Once aligned, the shooter propels the ball using a three-phase catapult sequence:
1. **Lift** — raise the upper arm to no greater than 45° from parallel to the floor
2. **Extend** — extend the lower arm until the elbow is nearly straight; wrist remains cocked
3. **Flip** — wave good-bye, leaving the palm facing down and fingers pointing toward the floor

The cues should be called so close together they sound like one word: "*lift-extend-flip.*" The ball should arc approximately 15 feet (4.5 m) high and reach its apex about two-thirds of the way to the target. [S15, pp.184-185]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Elbow flared outward** → tuck elbow under the ball directly above the shooting knee and toe; "A crooked shooting elbow is the worst fault in all of basketball" [S15, p.187]
2. **Ball in the palm** → check that daylight is visible under the ball; use the spread-and-relax technique to find the natural cup
3. **Wrist not cocked** → look for the wrinkle behind the wrist before any shot attempt; no wrinkle = no cocking
4. **Ball off to the side** → ball must be in front of the face or on the shooting-hand side, never to the non-shooting side
5. **Watching the flight of the ball** → eyes must stay on the target center throughout the shot and follow-through
6. **Back arched** → body stays erect; do not arch the back to generate power; use legs

## Variations
### Lying Down
Perform the flip-ball drill lying on the back, shooting straight up 8-10 feet. Eliminates all lower body interference; the ball cannot stay in the hand unless wrist and elbow are perfectly aligned. [S15, p.186]

### Wall Check
Stand facing a wall with the shooting toe touching the wall, knee against the wall, and entire lower arm against the wall. This physically locks in straight-stick alignment so players can feel the correct position. [S15, pp.187-188]

### Partner Catapult
Two lines of players face each other 10 feet (3 m) apart; one side lifts, extends, and flips the ball to the other side. The ball should apex two-thirds of the way and reach 15 feet of height. [S15, p.184]
