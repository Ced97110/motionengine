# Motion Offense Offensive Philosophy — the source coach

## Summary
the source coach (Motion Offense) describes the philosophy behind the team's sustained success in pro half-court offense. Lacking a dominant one-on-one player, the team relied on precision screen execution, intelligent movement without the ball, and all five players being actively involved on every possession. The system demands that players react to what the defense gives them — not what they've pre-decided to do. [S7, pp.67–68]

## When to Use
- Any half-court offense where the primary ball handler cannot consistently create off isolation
- Teams with skilled post players, shooters, and unselfish guards
- When the shot clock is tight and quick, precise execution is required

## Key Principles
1. **Read and react to the defense** — play against your man; players who decide in advance become predictable
2. **Stay in great physical condition** — the player in better shape will prevail when fatigue hits in the fourth quarter
3. **Play as a team** — make the extra pass, be willing to give the ball to a teammate in better position, set picks to relieve pressure
4. **Push the ball in transition first** — fast break on every possession to buy time and create easier scoring opportunities before the half-court set is needed
5. **Involve all five players** — reputation for full-roster execution on every offensive set
6. **Achieve good shot selection** — work for high-percentage shots, not just any shot
7. **Maintain court balance and spacing** — poor spacing means players are too clustered, too spread, or easy to guard; proper distribution forces the defense to cover the entire court

## Passing Skill Requirements
- **Post entry passing**: know how, when, and where to pass to the center — timing of the post entry is critical to play success
- **Perimeter passing**: move the ball quickly and sure-handedly around the arc — fast ball movement makes the defense work and creates shot, drive, and entry-pass opportunities
- **Post passing / vision**: the center has a unique vantage point to see the whole court; developing the ability to find cutters and spotting teammates is a high-value skill for bigs

## Screen Execution Philosophy
- Setting screens for post-up position can use smaller players screening for bigger teammates (guard-on-big screen for post-up)
- Big players down-screen for smaller players in many sets
- Proper angle, timing, and pass location are the details that separate success from failure

## Common Mistakes
1. **Pre-deciding cuts** → players commit before reading the defender; correction: force players to verbalize what they see in defensive positioning before moving
2. **Skipping the extra pass** → settling for a contested shot instead of swinging to an open teammate; correction: reward the extra pass with positive reinforcement
3. **Poor spacing on perimeter** → clumping on one side collapses help lanes; correction: enforce spacing rules (3PT line, corners, slots)
4. **Not fast-breaking** → walking the ball up allows defenses to set; correction: push the ball up — even if the fast break doesn't score, it speeds up the game

## Practice Methodology
1. Run screens without defenders to establish reads and timing
2. Add defenders at 50% effort
3. Progress to game-speed defenders once all reads are mastered
