# On-the-Ball Defensive Pivots

## Summary
A good on-ball defender must constantly pressure the dribbler and immediately match every direction change with superior footwork. The source identifies four basic foot movements for ball defenders — the advance step, the drop-and-swing step, the push-off step, and the slide-step — which combine into the "drop and swing step / push-push method." When executed with proper technique, this method dramatically increases the defender's quickness in reacting to dribble penetration. Offensive ball handlers are taught to attack the defender's lead (front) foot, so the defender's first reaction must always be a reverse pivot drop-step off that lead foot. [S6, pp.270-275]

## When to Use
- Any time guarding a live ball handler in man-to-man defense
- When the ball handler uses a blast move (straight drive) or front crossover attacking the defender's lead foot
- When recovering after being caught leaning in the wrong direction
- Equally applicable in zone and zone press defense [S6, p.273]

## Key Principles
1. **Attack the lead foot**: Offensive players are taught to attack the defender's front (lead) foot — the defender must anticipate this and be ready to drop-step [S6, p.270]
2. **Drop-and-swing step**: When the dribbler attacks, the ball defender drop-steps with the lead foot (90–135° reverse pivot) using the trail foot as the pivot foot — the lead arm swings back simultaneously to initiate momentum [S6, p.271]
3. **Push-off step**: After the drop-step, the new trail foot (original lead foot) generates the explosive push in the direction needed to cut off the dribbler — this push is the source of the defender's quickness [S6, p.274]
4. **Slide-step**: Following the push-off, the defender slides in the new direction keeping the body between the dribbler and the basket at all times [S6, p.275]
5. **Advance step**: When the dribbler retreats or the defender wants to increase pressure, push off the back foot and stride forward toward the ball handler [S6, p.273]
6. **Mental anticipation**: "Physically you are here, while mentally you are there" — after cutting off the initial drive, mentally prepare for the counter change of direction and pre-set the reverse pivot [S6, p.273]
7. **Never reach — always move feet**: Reaching for the ball destroys balance and footwork; move feet first, always [S6, p.278]

## The Four Foot Movements

| Movement | Trigger | Pivot Foot | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Advance step** | Dribbler retreats or defender adds pressure | Back foot pushes | Stride forward toward ball |
| **Drop-and-swing step** | Dribbler attacks lead foot | Trail foot (original) | Lead foot swings back 90–135° (reverse pivot); lead arm swings back |
| **Push-off step** | After drop-step | New trail foot (original lead) | Explosive push in direction to cut off dribbler |
| **Slide-step** | After push-off | — | Slide to maintain body-between-dribbler-and-basket position |

*[S6, pp.273-275]*

## Common Mistakes
1. **Turning hips to run instead of drop-stepping** → Opens a direct driving lane — correction: always reverse pivot (drop-and-swing) rather than open-step sprinting [S6, p.271]
2. **Weak push-off after the drop-step** → Defender is always one step behind — correction: drive hard off the new trail foot (original lead), this is where quickness comes from [S6, p.274]
3. **Reaching for the ball** → Off balance, fouls, ball handler beats defender — correction: hands behind back drill variation forces footwork discipline [S6, p.278]
4. **Reacting to the fake instead of the actual dribble direction** → Over-committed on the initial drop — correction: use mental anticipation to pre-load the counter pivot [S6, p.273]
5. **Lead foot too far forward (over-extended stance)** → Too easy to attack the lead foot — correction: maintain an active, balanced defensive stance with moderate lead foot position
