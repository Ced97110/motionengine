# Push-Up (Basketball Strength Exercise)

## Objective
Build foundational upper-body pushing strength — targeting the pectoralis major, triceps, and anterior deltoid — that transfers to explosive chest passes, defensive positioning, and setting screens.

## Setup
- 1 player
- No equipment needed; can be done anywhere
- Floor space sufficient to lie prone fully extended

## Execution
1. Lie face down on the floor. Place hands slightly wider than shoulder width, fingers pointing forward, thumbs in line with the top of the chest. Elbows are close to the torso.
2. Engage the abdominal muscles and maintain a flat (neutral) back throughout the movement.
3. Press palms into the floor and extend the arms, lifting the body away from the floor. Hips and shoulders must rise simultaneously — do not let the hips sag or pike.
4. Slowly lower back to the starting position under control, maintaining hip and shoulder alignment throughout the descent.
5. Perform the prescribed number of repetitions.

## Coaching Points
- "Hips and shoulders rise together" — no sagging hips, no pike position
- Elbows stay close to the torso, not flaring out wide
- Fingers point forward; thumbs aligned with top of the chest
- Control the descent — do not drop to the floor
- Breathing: exhale on the push up, inhale on the way down
- Flat back = engaged core; if the lower back drops, stop the set

## Progressions
1. **Beginner (Knee Push-Up)**: Perform the push-up with knees on the floor instead of toes. Maintains the same upper-body mechanics while reducing the load. Use until full push-up can be performed with proper form.
2. **Intermediate (Standard Push-Up)**: Full push-up as described above with feet on the floor.
3. **Advanced (Platform Push-Up)**: Place feet on a 12–24 inch (30–60 cm) platform (bench or box). Increases the load on the upper chest and anterior deltoid. Challenges core stability under elevated angle.

## Muscles Worked
- **Primary**: Pectoralis major, triceps brachii, anterior deltoid
- **Secondary**: Biceps brachii, latissimus dorsi, rectus abdominis

## Basketball Transfer
The push-up's horizontal pressing motion directly mimics the mechanics of a chest pass — the explosive extension through the pectoralis and triceps translates to passing power. A stronger upper body also supports physicality when defending an opponent, positioning under the boards, or setting a hard screen for a teammate. [S2, pp.74-75]
