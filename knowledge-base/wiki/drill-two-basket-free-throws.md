# Two-Basket Free Throws

## Objective
Create competitive pressure that forces players to concentrate on making free throws when only two baskets are available (game-day practice, shoot-arounds, or smaller facilities) [S13, p.121].

## Setup
- **Players**: Full squad
- **Court area**: Full court, both ends
- **Equipment**: One ball per group
- **When to use**: Game-day practice, shoot-arounds, or any situation where only 2 baskets are available

## Execution

### Variation 1 — Shoot-Until-Miss Rotation
1. Split the team into two groups by shooting ability: good shooters at one end, not-so-good shooters at the other end
2. Each player shoots free throws until they miss, then rotates to the back of the line
3. The coach assigns different consecutive-make goals to each group (e.g., 5 makes for the strong group, 3 makes for the weaker group)
4. Separating groups allows the coach to set appropriate targets by ability level

### Variation 2 — Progressive Team Consecutive Makes
1. Line up all players on both sides of the free-throw lane at one basket
2. Each player must make **1 with no misses**, then **2 with no misses** in sequence
3. A miss resets the player's consecutive count; they return to the back of the line
4. Focus: individual accountability with team awareness of teammates' progress

### Variation 3 — Team vs. Team Consecutive Progression
1. Divide the team into two equal groups, one at each basket
2. Players progress through: **1 consecutive → 2 consecutive → 3 consecutive**
3. Each player must achieve each tier before the team can advance
4. **First team to complete all three tiers wins** — bragging rights are the reward/penalty
5. This competitive format simulates late-game pressure without the threat of physical conditioning

## Coaching Points
- The pressure created by teammates watching and competition against another group effectively simulates game stress
- Monitor individual mechanics — pressure should not cause shooters to rush their routine
- Track which players consistently fail under this simulated pressure; they need extra pressure-simulation work before close games
- Variation 1 works best when ability levels differ significantly within the roster
- Variation 3 creates the most team energy and buy-in through direct competition

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Variation 2 (individual progressive makes with no team stakes)
2. **Intermediate**: Variation 1 (shoot-until-miss rotation with group-appropriate goals)
3. **Advanced**: Variation 3 (team-vs-team progressive competition with bragging rights or additional penalty)
