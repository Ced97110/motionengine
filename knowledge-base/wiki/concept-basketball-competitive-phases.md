# Basketball In-Season Competitive Training Phases

## Summary
The in-season mesocycle is broken into two smaller microcycles — **Competitive Phase I** and **Competitive Phase II** — each with a distinct training emphasis. This structure allows coaches to periodize training across the long competitive season (16-30 weeks) so that athletes reach **peak physical performance during playoff competition**, not at the beginning of the season [S11, Ch.12].

Physical performance naturally rises and falls across a season. The two-phase structure provides a framework to manage this wave intentionally: Phase I builds and maintains maximal strength while athletes acclimate to game demands; Phase II shifts toward power and velocity development as the season progresses toward championships.

## When to Use
- **Phase I**: Early-to-mid competitive season (first half of in-season mesocycle)
- **Phase II**: Mid-to-late competitive season, playoff preparation period
- Phase boundaries are flexible and adjusted based on team schedule, player readiness, and season structure

## Key Principles

### Competitive Phase I — Maximal Strength Maintenance
1. **Higher intensity, lower volume**: Primary exercises at 80-90% 1RM for 1-2 repetitions — maintains maximal strength built in preseason without excessive fatigue
2. **Research-supported maintenance volumes**: Maximal strength is maintained with 1-6 sets of 1-6 reps at 85-100%; strength-power maintained at 70-88% for 3-6 sets of 4-10 reps [S11, Ch.12]
3. **Velocity-based method for primary exercises**: Target 0.3-0.6 m/s for strength-speed zone; this maintains absolute strength and strength-speed qualities simultaneously
4. **Assistance exercise volume reduction**: Instead of 4+ sets in off-season, perform 2-3 sets of 8-12 reps — sufficient for muscular development and strength-endurance maintenance
5. **Exercise order flexibility**: When time is limited, heavy structural exercises performed BEFORE explosive movements can improve performance in both vertical and horizontal tasks — pairing heavy lifts with velocity-focused work addresses both ends of the force-velocity curve efficiently
6. **Low volume early**: Athletes are still acclimating to high basketball-specific workloads; start with minimal resistance training volume to avoid excessive fatigue and soreness

### Competitive Phase II — Power and Playoff Preparation
- Greater emphasis on force-velocity curve (speed-strength end)
- Velocity-based training dominates load prescription
- Primary exercises focus on maximal power output at moderate loads
- Volume may be slightly reduced as game schedule intensifies; quality of movement takes precedence over quantity
- The goal is to arrive at peak athletic output for championship play

## Exercise Order for Limited Time Sessions
Traditional order: Explosive → Heavy structural → Assistance

When time is limited, research supports a modified order:
1. **Heavy loaded structural exercises first** → neurological potentiation effect
2. **Explosive/velocity exercises second** → benefits from PAP effect
3. **Assistance exercises last** → higher rep, lower intensity, addresses durability

This sequence is "time efficient and ensures that both ends of the force-velocity curve are being addressed" [S11, Ch.12].

## Readiness-Based Individualization
Within each phase, athletes enter every session in a different state of readiness based on:
- Fatigue from basketball activities (practice, games)
- Travel and sleep disruption
- Academic or other life stressors
- Nutrition and hydration status

**Solution**: Use velocity-based training (VBT) as the primary load-regulation tool. VBT allows daily fluctuation in load while maintaining target velocity zones — "ideal for the explosive basketball athlete" [S11, Ch.12]. When an athlete's bar speed is low, the system automatically reduces load to match actual readiness.

## Variable Resistance Application
Under heavy loads (>80% 1RM), movement velocity is compromised at the sticking point. **Variable resistance** (bands and chains) addresses this:
- Calculate desired load at the top position
- Determine what percentage of load comes from variable resistance (commonly 20%)
- Example: 200 lb back squat → 160 lb barbell plates + 40 lb chains (20 lb per side)
- **Critical**: Some chain must remain on the floor at the starting position; chains that sway during movement increase injury risk significantly
- Variable resistance is most appropriate in Phase I when heavy loads are being maintained [S11, Ch.12]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Trying to maintain off-season volume in-season** → Assistance exercise volume must be reduced (4+ sets → 2-3 sets); total basketball activity volume replaces some of this stress
2. **Using October 1RM percentages in February** → Shift to velocity-based or RPE-based autoregulation by mid-season; 1RM can change up to 18% day-to-day
3. **Same program for all players** → A DNP athlete needs a completely different program from a 38-minute starter, even if they have the same strength levels
4. **Skipping Phase II in favor of rest** → Reducing all training in the playoff push leads to detraining; maintaining Phase II programming preserves peak performance
5. **Chains that sway** → Ensure some chain remains on the floor throughout the entire range of motion when using variable resistance
