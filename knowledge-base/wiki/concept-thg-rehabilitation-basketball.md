# Trunk, Hip, and Groin (THG) Injury Rehabilitation for Basketball

## Summary
Trunk, hip, and groin (THG) injuries are among the most common in basketball, accounting for **14.6% of all NBA injuries** (hip joint specifically; 24-year study) and **11.5% of all injuries in a 17-year NBA surveillance study** (most commonly strains and contusions). THG injuries are challenging to diagnose and rehabilitate due to overlapping anatomy, varied nomenclature, and high prevalence of concurrent pathology. [S10, pp.701-702]

The rehabilitation approach must address biomechanical issues, movement control deficits, and the unique multidirectional, high-intensity demands of basketball. A graduated **8-phase rehabilitation pathway** is recommended: pain/inflammation reduction → ROM recovery → strength recovery → proprioception/neuromotor control → on-court conditioning → return-to-train → return-to-play → return-to-competition/performance. [S10, pp.704-709]

## When to Use
- Any basketball player presenting with adductor-related, iliopsoas-related, inguinal-related, or pubic-related groin pain
- Hip impingement (FAI), labral tear, hip flexor strain, bursitis
- Rectus abdominis/oblique strain, abdominal hernia, or non-specific low back pain
- Adductor longus tear or inguinal hernia
- Chronic overuse injuries from high acute:chronic workload ratio spikes

## Key Principles

### Epidemiology
- 14.6% of all NBA injuries involve the THG/hip joint [S10, p.702]
- Collegiate basketball: 53.06 per 100,000 athlete-exposures for hip/groin injury (non-contact and overuse most prevalent) [S10, p.702]
- Retired NBA athletes have elevated risk of total hip arthroplasty compared to the general population [S10, p.702]
- Injuries occur acutely (cutting, slipping) or via acute:chronic workload ratio spikes — the "danger zone" begins above a ratio of 1.5 [S10, p.702]

### Differential Diagnosis Categories (DOHA Consensus)
1. **Adductor-related groin pain** — most common; adductor squeeze testing at 0° and 90°
2. **Iliopsoas-related groin pain** — hip flexor strain, tendinopathy
3. **Inguinal-related groin pain** — inguinal hernia
4. **Pubic-related groin pain** — osteitis pubis, pubic symphysis stress
5. **Trunk/abdomen** — rectus strain, oblique tear, abdominal hernia, low back pain
6. **Hip** — FAI, labral tear, hip flexor strain, bursitis
7. **Referred pain** — from lumbar spine and sacroiliac joint [S10, pp.702-703]

> A pathoanatomical approach is recommended — treatment should not simply target singular structures but also identify and treat movement impairments. [S10, p.703]

### Outcome Measures
1. **HAGOS (Hip and Groin Outcome Score)** — reliable, valid, responsive self-report for athletic populations [S10, p.703]
2. **HOOS (Hip Dysfunction and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score)** — 5 subscales: pain, symptoms, ADL function, sport/recreation function, QOL [S10, p.703]
3. **Hip and Lumbar ROM** — flexion, adduction, abduction, rotation (hip); flexion, extension, lateral flexion (lumbar); measured with goniometer [S10, p.703]
4. **HLLMS (Hip and Lower Limb Movement Screen)** — 7-movement battery identifying biomechanical and movement control issues; inability to control lower limb movement may contribute to chronic pain or recurrence [S10, pp.703-704]

### 8-Phase Rehabilitation Pathway

#### Phase 1: Reduction of Pain, Inflammation, and Swelling
- Use isolated corrective exercises (e.g., Copenhagen protocol for adductor-related groin pain)
- All exercises must be retrained without pain — do not stress already-overloaded structures
- Modalities to reduce inflammation; resolve swelling as quickly as possible [S10, p.705]

#### Phase 2: Recovery of Range of Motion
- Address ROM deficits found during assessment
- Increase joint and capsular mobility (hip mobility routines, controlled articular rotations)
- Postural cueing for pelvic setting — anterior pelvic tilt reduces hip flexion, adduction, and internal rotation, potentially leading to degenerative hip impingement [S10, pp.705-706]

#### Phase 3: Recovery of All Elements of Strength
- Progress from isolated to **global, basketball-specific exercises**
- Key principle: demand tissues to tolerate **rapid loading at lengthened states**
- Integrate previously dysfunctional muscles into motor patterns while gradually increasing movement complexity
- Target full kinetic chain; utilize eccentric and concentric contractions
- Compound multi-joint movements to challenge lumbopelvic neutral position: deadlifts, Romanian deadlifts, goblet squats, split squats [S10, p.706]
- **Strength testing** (groin strength): adductor squeeze at 0° and 90°, eccentric hip adduction, eccentric hip abduction, isometric hip flexion at 90°
- **Endurance testing**: side plank, long lever posterior tilt plank, Copenhagen adduction hold [S10, pp.706-708]

#### Phase 4: Proprioception and Neuromotor Control
- Poor motor patterning from underlying muscle imbalances is a common cause of THG injury
- Local stability system is often inhibited by pain, causing compensatory patterns
- Goals: identify/correct imbalances, retrain movement competency, develop lumbar-pelvic proprioception, stability, and dynamic control
- **Functional tests before progressing to on-court rehab**: single leg squat, star excursion balance test, single leg hop for distance (anterior/medial/lateral), triple hop, COD tests (T-test, Illinois agility test) [S10, p.706]

#### Phase 5: On-Court Conditioning and Skill Acquisition
- Basketball-specific conditioning with a coach
- Restore linear running, cutting drills, and cognitive training
- Load monitored via GPS accelerometry — gradually increase toward training accumulative load
- Player must complete full training session at maximal intensity before return to unrestricted participation [S10, p.708]

#### Phase 6: Return-to-Train
- Collaborative decision: rehabilitation professional + player + coaching team
- Begin with pre-planned drills with predetermined movement requirements
- Monitor: cardiovascular fitness, muscular strength/endurance, psychological readiness, team play integration
- Progress to unplanned movement patterning, landing mechanics, change of direction [S10, p.708]

#### Phase 7: Return-to-Play
- Repeat all strength/endurance/functional testing from early rehab
- **Key criterion**: strength deficits <10% on injured vs. uninjured side (especially relevant in adductor-related groin pain)
- Player must reach adequate court fitness AND tactical knowledge before RTP
- Medical and coaching staff collaborate for final clearance [S10, p.709]

#### Phase 8: Return-to-Competition/Performance
- Athlete should return not just to pre-injury level but potentially exceed it if all imbalances (e.g., adductor, glute strength, hip mobility, lumbopelvic control) were addressed
- True goal: unrestricted, symptom-free basketball performance [S10, p.709]

### Surgical vs. Conservative Management
- A systematic review found rehabilitation has significantly quicker RTP for pubic-driven injuries, and similar outcomes to surgery for other THG groups [S10, p.704]
- Following FAI hip arthroscopy surgery: no performance deficits vs. matched NBA controls; a study of 24 NBA players showed 100% return to play with no significant performance decline [S10, p.704]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Overly focusing on single anatomical structures** → THG injuries frequently involve concurrent pathology and movement impairments — treat the pattern, not just the tissue [S10, p.703]
2. **Ignoring posture and pelvic alignment** → Anterior pelvic tilt reduces hip ROM and loads the joint asymmetrically — postural cueing must be part of the program [S10, p.705-706]
3. **Premature return to play** → Returning early increases re-injury risk AND threatens optimal performance; patience and criterion-based progression are essential [S10, p.706]
4. **Not addressing the acute:chronic workload ratio** → Spikes above 1.5 dramatically increase injury risk — monitor load during return-to-train phase with GPS accelerometry [S10, p.702]
5. **Skipping functional tests** → Functional milestones (single leg squat, hop tests, COD tests) must be passed before progression to on-court activity [S10, p.706]
