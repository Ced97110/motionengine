# The Anabolic-Catabolic Systemic-Local Training Model

## Summary
Training adaptation in basketball athletes is governed by the interplay between anabolic hormones (GH, IGF-I, testosterone) and catabolic hormones/inflammatory cytokines (cortisol, IL-6). A successful training program creates an initial catabolic response that is followed by an anabolic "rebound" — resulting in greater muscle mass and improved fitness. Failure to achieve this rebound leads to overtraining and injury [S10, pp.25-26].

A key insight from this model is the **systemic-local divergence**: circulating IGF-I may decrease systemically (catabolic signal) while local muscle IGF-I increases (anabolic signal) from early in training. This simultaneous central catabolism and local anabolism conserves systemic energy while still enabling muscle tissue growth in response to training [S10, p.26].

## When to Use
- Explaining the hormonal basis for periodization (heavy → taper → competition)
- Educating athletes on why adequate nutrition is essential during heavy training phases
- Guiding recovery modality choices based on the athlete's position in the adaptation cycle
- Identifying overreaching before it becomes overtraining

## Key Principles
1. **Initial catabolic phase is necessary** — A sudden training program increase causes initial increases in catabolic hormones and pro-inflammatory cytokines, and decreases in circulating IGF-I. This may be required for ultimate anabolic adaptation [S10, p.26].
2. **Anabolic rebound signals successful adaptation** — With proper training progression, pro-inflammatory cytokines drop, IGF-I suppression diminishes, and IGF-I rebounds above pre-training levels [S10, p.26].
3. **Local muscle IGF-I increases from the start** — Even when circulating IGF-I is suppressed, local muscle IGF-I rises with training — this is GH-independent and represents the primary driver of early muscle mass gains [S10, p.26].
4. **Nutrition is critical to the anabolic rebound** — Since IGF-I can be reduced by nutritional imbalance and weight loss, inadequate nutrition can prevent the anabolic rebound even with appropriate tapering [S10, p.29].
5. **Tapering drives the final anabolic peak** — Reducing training intensity before competition triggers a parallel rise in circulating IGF-I and further improvement in performance [S10, p.29].
6. **IGF-I levels are associated with winning** — Pre- and post-competition IGF-I levels are ~11% higher in winning athletes across team sports — baseline IGF-I, not competition-induced changes, predicts outcome [S10, p.32].

## Variations
### Small-Sided Game (SSG) Hormonal Effects
Different SSG structures produce different hormonal responses: testosterone increased following a defensive-focused long SSG (3×4 min) but decreased following an offensive-focused short SSG (6×2 min) — suggesting tactical and structural SSG design affects anabolic adaptation [S10, p.28].

### Interval Training Structure
Decreasing-distance interval protocols (400→300→200→100 m) produce significantly higher lactate and GH area under the curve than increasing-distance protocols despite identical total volume, running speed, and rest — achieving greater anabolic stimulus [S10, p.27].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not tapering before major competitions** → Without tapering, the anabolic rebound cannot occur and performance will not peak optimally.
2. **Allowing weight loss before major tournaments without nutritional support** → Weight reduction suppresses IGF-I, preventing the anabolic rebound needed for peak performance [S10, p.29].
3. **Using continuous, constant-intensity training year-round** → Teams that train at constant relative intensity throughout a long season (like NBA) show no changes in IGF-I levels and its binding protein — periodization is required for hormonal cycling [S10, p.29].
