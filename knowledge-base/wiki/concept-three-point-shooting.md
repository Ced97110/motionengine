# Three-Point Shooting

## Summary
The three-point shot has become a central element of modern basketball offense. Coaches recognize that 33% from three equals 50% from two in terms of points-per-shot, making accurate three-point shooting extremely valuable. However, the shot is the most frequently over-used and developmentally missequenced skill in youth basketball — players attempt threes before establishing a reliable midrange game, grooving poor mechanics through lack of strength. The foundational principle is clear: **master the midrange shot before extending range to the three-point line** [S5, pp.69-70].

Once a player is ready to shoot from behind the arc, three core techniques must be developed in sequence: (1) straight-on catch-and-shoot, (2) pull-up three off the dribble, and (3) the on-the-move three. Each uses the same fundamental mechanics as the midrange shot — only the power demand changes. The three-point shot should be treated as one weapon in a complete scorer's arsenal, not the entire offense [S5, pp.79-80].

## When to Use
- Inside-out passes from the post or drive-and-kick situations (easiest; all momentum is forward)
- Skip passes across the court that catch the defense shifting
- Transition threes off the dribble at the end of a fast break (numbers advantage, no layup available)
- Shot-clock situations requiring a variety of three-point options
- Do **not** shoot if unable to receive in a balanced, shot-ready position

## Key Principles
1. **Midrange first.** Players should not attempt threes if their form changes from inside the arc. Establish the midrange game, then extend range when strength allows [S5, p.70].
2. **Know your range.** Players who believe they are three-point shooters when they can't make an open 15-footer are hurting their team. Shoot your shot [S5, p.69].
3. **Start 3–5 feet behind the line.** Position yourself behind the arc before receiving so you can time your one-two step and generate the leg power necessary [S5, p.72].
4. **Use your legs.** The biggest, strongest muscles are in the legs. The one-two step into the shot transfers leg power into the ball — especially critical at three-point distance [S5, p.72].
5. **Heel-to-toe step.** Whether moving straight on or on the move, the stride must be heel-to-toe to transfer weight forward and allow pivoting into the shot [S5, pp.72, 75-76].
6. **Be a lifter, not a slinger or pusher.** Do not compensate for distance by altering the elbow bend or pushing the ball. Maintain consistent release mechanics regardless of distance [S5, p.73].
7. **Shoot and stay.** The longer the shot, the longer the potential rebound. Stay in position to track the rebound or be an outlet. "Shoot, stay, then react" [S5, p.73].
8. **Freeze the follow-through.** Dropping the follow-through means dancing feet, which means imbalance. Never attempt a three if you cannot receive in balance [S5, p.73].
9. **Hard last dribble on pull-up threes.** When shooting off the dribble on the move, the last dribble should be a hard dribble — it gives more height on the jump and a quicker shot release [S5, p.77].

## Variations

### Straight-On Catch-and-Shoot Three
Uses the standard one-two step (left foot first for right-handed players). Ball arrives in the shot pocket as the left foot hits the floor. Long, aggressive stride when meeting the ball generates power and keeps the shooter low (eliminating dip/negative motion). Body straightens on the jump; release just before the peak [S5, pp.72-73].

### Pull-Up Three Off the Dribble
Same one-two step footwork as the straight-on. When going with the strong hand, pick up the ball with two hands into the shot pocket. When going with the weak hand, "load the gun" — let the ball rise into the weak hand and bring it across to the shooting hand, keeping the elbow tucked on the shot line [S5, p.73]. Best used at the end of fast breaks; not a high-volume shot in half-court offense [S5, p.74].

### On-the-Move Three (Left to Right, Right-Handed)
Moving from left to right: step aggressively with the inside (left) foot using a heel-to-toe open step toward the target. Pivot into the shot, then bring the shooting (right) foot around and point it at the center of the basket. Hip, shoulder, ball, and elbow all align with the target. Explode into the jump [S5, pp.75-76].

### On-the-Move Three (Right to Left, Right-Handed — "Stub Technique")
Moving from right to left: step with the right foot (heel-to-toe, open toward target), which becomes the pivot foot. Then **stub the left foot** — plant it short so the shooting base matches every other shot. Failure to stub causes the left side to lead, forcing a body twist that pulls the shot line off target [S5, p.76].

### On-the-Move Three Off the Dribble
Same footwork as the pass-receive on-the-move three. When moving left to right (right-handed), dribble with the outside (right) hand and plant the inside (left) foot. Open the left foot and hip toward the target (heel-to-toe), then bring the right foot around. Ball rises off the floor into the shot pocket as the shooting foot plants [S5, p.77].

### Shuffle-Step Three
Used when the shooter is already at the three-point line and slides laterally (like a defensive slide). The most difficult three because there is no forward momentum into the shot. Can be combined with shot fakes, jab steps, or shuffle-step dribble moves. Essential to practice for shot-clock situations [S5, p.78].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Shooting threes before mastering the midrange** → Grooves poor mechanics; build from inside out [S5, p.69].
2. **Altering the release mechanics for distance** → Pushing or slinging the ball; maintain consistent elbow position [S5, p.73].
3. **Not spacing behind the line** → No room for the one-two step, losing leg power; start 3–5 ft back [S5, p.72].
4. **Stepping with the shooting foot first** → Loses forward momentum; always lead with the non-shooting foot on straight-on threes [S5, p.72].
5. **Flat-footed step on the move** → Can't pivot into the shot or square to target; heel-to-toe is essential [S5, p.75].
6. **Overstriding on the on-the-move three** → Knocks alignment off; creates left/right misses [S5, p.76].
7. **Failing to stub the left foot (right-to-left, right-handed)** → Left side becomes the lead side; body twists during shot [S5, p.76].
8. **Soft last dribble on pull-up three** → Loses jump height and quickness; last dribble should be hard [S5, p.77].
9. **Chasing every three-point rebound** → Longer shots produce longer rebounds; shoot and stay [S5, p.73].
10. **Becoming one-dimensional** → Over-relying on the three leaves the player without a countering midrange or drive game [S5, p.80].
