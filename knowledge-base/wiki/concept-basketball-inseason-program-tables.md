# In-Season Sample Programs: Guards and Bigs (Tables 12.4–12.7)

## Summary
Four sample program tables from S11 provide complete in-season resistance training sessions for Guards and Bigs across both Competitive Phase I (Microcycle 1) and Competitive Phase II (Microcycle 2). Each table has two training days per microcycle, with detailed exercise order, load, reps, tempo, and rest prescriptions. Advanced complex exercises (labeled 'college or pro athlete' or 'playoff prep') are included as optional add-ons.

The programs are structured using circuit/complex groupings (1a, 1b, 1c) where athletes perform one set of each exercise in a group before returning to the first exercise for the next set [S11, p.4 (program key)].

**Tempo notation**: Eccentric : pause : concentric (e.g., 3:1:1 = 3 sec lower, 1 sec hold, 1 sec lift). 'X' = explode as fast as possible [S11, p.4].

## When to Use
- During the competitive basketball season in 2+ sessions per week
- Scheduled around practices and game days
- Use Microcycle 1 (Phase I) early in the season; Microcycle 2 (Phase II) as season progresses toward playoffs

## Key Principles
1. **Total body sessions** — each day addresses lower body, upper body push, upper body pull, and core in one session [S11, pp.5,7,9,10]
2. **Compound movements first** — back squat or power clean leads each session as the primary strength/power stimulus
3. **Superset/complex grouping** — exercises are grouped (1a, 1b, 1c) with short rest between exercises in the group (<30s) and longer rest at the end of the full group (1-3 min) [S11, p.4]
4. **Phase I: Strength focus** → Phase II: Push/pull and speed-strength focus [S11]
5. **Advanced add-ons clearly labeled** — college/pro athletes may add complex exercises for greater power stimulus

## Coaching Points
- Use the circuit grouping system: complete 1a→1b→1c before returning for set 2 of the group [S11, p.4]
- 'X' in tempo = maximum explosive effort — used for all power exercises [S11, p.4]
- Advanced complexes (college/pro, playoff prep) are optional and should be used only with adequately trained athletes [S11]
- Guards and Bigs programs differ mainly in the explosive push exercise (push press vs. split jerk; split snatch vs. single-leg squat on box; one-arm push press vs. one-arm push press for bigs in Phase II Day 2) [S11, pp.5,7,9,10]
- The bigs' Day 2 landmine squat to rotational press mimics the rotational demands of post play and defensive rebounding [S11, p.10]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping the power component (1b)** → PAP effect is lost; the pairing of strength exercise + explosive exercise is what drives power adaptation
2. **Not adjusting eccentric tempo** → Phase II shifts to faster eccentric (1:X:X) at higher loads; maintaining 3:1:1 throughout limits velocity development
3. **Omitting core work** → Pallof press, birddog plank, and side plank hold are not optional — they maintain the trunk stability needed for contact resistance and injury prevention
4. **Using absolute load instead of velocity targets** → In these programs, velocity targets (0.6-0.8 m/s for Phase I; 0.8-1.0 m/s for Phase II back squat) guide load selection, not fixed percentages alone
