# Six Guiding Principles (Lee Rose)

## Summary
After nearly half a century as a player and coach at the college and NBA levels, Lee Rose identified six principles that characterize consistent winning teams. These principles apply regardless of talent level, system, or era — NBA and NCAA champions have differed markedly in style and personnel, but all share these six characteristics. Talent is ultimately the deciding factor, but players' abilities are compromised when any of these principles are absent.

Rose frames these principles as a commitment required of both coaches and players — not a checklist but a daily operating system for building and sustaining a winning program [S13, pp.1-2].

## When to Use
- Establishing team culture at the start of a program or season
- Evaluating why a team underperforms relative to its talent level
- Recruiting conversations — setting expectations for incoming players
- Post-game and post-season reflection on team deficiencies

## Key Principles
1. **Be in good physical condition** — Physical conditioning is the bedrock. Players must first be capable of sustaining high-intensity effort for an entire game before any other principle matters. Optimal fitness increases both minutes played and quality of those minutes.
2. **Play hard** — Maximum effort is non-negotiable. Talent is compromised by lack of effort. Great teams play hard consistently — not just in big moments.
3. **Play smart** — Basketball IQ, shot selection, decision-making, and situational awareness. Energy unleashed randomly is rarely constructive.
4. **Execute the plan** — Individual and team execution of the coach's system. Having a plan is not enough; carrying it out with discipline is what separates good teams from great ones.
5. **Be unselfish** — Team-first mentality in all areas: shot selection, defensive rotations, screening, passing. Selfishness is the single greatest individual threat to team success.
6. **Maximize strengths and minimize weaknesses** — Coaches design systems and matchups; players develop skills. Both must understand what the team does best and protect what it does worst.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Primary responsibility for playing smart — controlling pace, decision-making, and executing the plan.
- **SG**: Playing hard and being unselfish — accepting a defined role within the team's scoring hierarchy.
- **SF**: Maximizing strengths on both ends; often the most versatile contributor to multiple principles.
- **PF**: Physical conditioning is especially critical; must play hard on the glass every possession.
- **C**: Physical conditioning is the foundation; protect weaknesses (e.g., perimeter defense) while maximizing paint-area strengths.

## Variations
### Team Context Application
The six principles are universally applicable regardless of system. A pressing team and a half-court team both need all six. Coaches should audit their team against all six principles when diagnosing why results are falling short of talent.

### Individual Player Development
Coaches can use the six principles as a framework for individual player conversations — "which of these six is your biggest current gap?" — to create personal accountability without singling out technical weaknesses.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping the conditioning foundation** → Principles 2-6 all erode when players lack fitness. Address conditioning first, every preseason.
2. **Preaching unselfishness without system clarity** → Players become unselfish when the system makes unselfishness the winning strategy. Define roles explicitly.
3. **Conflating playing hard with playing smart** → Some teams play with maximum effort but make poor decisions. Both principles must be taught and evaluated separately.
4. **Over-indexing on strengths without protecting weaknesses** → Teams that maximize only their strengths become predictable and exploitable. Opponents will attack the weaknesses.
