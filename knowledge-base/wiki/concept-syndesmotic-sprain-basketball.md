# Syndesmotic (High Ankle) Sprain Rehabilitation in Basketball

## Summary
Syndesmotic, or "high ankle," sprains are less common than lateral ankle sprains but are associated with **longer recovery time** and require more restrictive management in the early phases. The typical mechanism is internal rotation of the leg with external rotation and hyperdorsiflexion of the ankle and foot. In basketball, this injury often occurs from awkward landings or direct contact. Without proper management, syndesmotic sprains carry risk of chronic instability and prolonged disability [S10, pp.742–743].

## When to Use
- Player presents with pain at the ankle mortise extending **proximally** (not laterally)
- Increased pain at **end range of dorsiflexion**
- Positive external rotation stress test
- Weight-bearing X-ray shows diastasis ≥6 mm between tibia and fibula 1 cm proximal to plafond

## Key Principles

### Diagnosis
- **External rotation stress test** — positive for syndesmotic injury
- **Talar tilt in 0° plantar flexion** — distinguishes from lateral sprain (positive talar tilt in 20° plantar flexion)
- Weight-bearing X-ray: diastasis ≥6 mm between tibia/fibula 1 cm proximal to plafond = syndesmotic injury confirmed
- **Non-weight-bearing and stress X-rays often produce false negatives** — weight-bearing view required

### Surgical Decision-Making
- Acute sprains **with diastasis or concomitant fracture** → may require surgical management
- Syndesmotic injury **without fracture or diastasis** → manageable nonsurgically
- Surgical options: screw fixation vs. dynamic button fixation (systematic review evidence available)

### Rehabilitation Phases

**Early Phase (both surgical and nonsurgical)**
1. Pain relief and edema management modalities
2. Assistive device use for normal gait pattern
3. Possible immobilization for tissue protection
4. Pain-free ROM — **avoid dorsiflexion and eversion ranges** in early stages
5. Open kinetic chain strengthening
6. Weight-bearing proprioception (weight shifting)
7. Stationary cycling for ROM and cardiovascular benefit
8. Immobilization and protected weight-bearing typically last **6–8 weeks** — discontinued when patient can walk and navigate stairs with minimal pain

**Middle Phase**
1. Restore normal ROM
2. Closed-chain strengthening
3. Advanced proprioception: single limb standing, unstable surfaces
4. Functional testing: jumping, hopping → guide decision for sport reintroduction

**Late Phase / Return to Sport**
1. Dribbling drills, shooting, running, cutting introduced progressively
2. RTP when player can perform sport activities at **game speed** with minimal discomfort and adequate movement quality

[S10, pp.742–743]

### Key Difference from Lateral Sprain
- Rehabilitation progresses **more slowly**
- Dorsiflexion and eversion are **avoided in early stages** (opposite of lateral sprain where these are encouraged)
- More restrictive weight-bearing protocol
- Recovery timeline substantially longer

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using non-weight-bearing X-rays** → likely false negatives for syndesmotic injury; always use weight-bearing views
2. **Progressing dorsiflexion too early** → stresses healing syndesmotic ligaments; restrict this range initially
3. **Treating like a lateral sprain** → underestimates severity and recovery time; syndesmotic sprains require slower progression
4. **Premature return to jumping/cutting** → jumping and hopping functional tests should confirm readiness before sport reintroduction

## Return to Play Criteria
- Able to walk and navigate stairs with minimal pain (to discontinue immobilization)
- Functional jumping and hopping tests → guide initiation of gradual RTP
- Perform sport activities at game speed with minimal discomfort and adequate movement quality
