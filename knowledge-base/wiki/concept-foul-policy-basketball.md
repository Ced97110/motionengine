# Foul Policy — Creating Your Own Foul System

## Summary
Fouls are inevitable in basketball, so planning and practicing a foul policy makes sense. A well-designed foul policy prevents key players from picking up pairs of quick fouls, keeps backup players mentally engaged throughout the game, and removes emotion from coaching substitution decisions. The system described here is inspired by Coach Dean Smith's substitution philosophy: substitute on the 1st, 2nd, and 4th fouls of key players with a predetermined backup [S13, pp.225-226].

## When to Use
- Every game, from the opening tip through the final buzzer
- Especially critical in the first half when preserving foul counts before halftime
- When a key player has four fouls and the team has a lead that can be protected

## Key Principles

### First Foul Substitution
- When the first foul on a starter occurs, the **predetermined substitute reports immediately** to the scorer's table and replaces the starter.
- The substitute has the **responsibility to be prepared** to enter without being told.
- The starter may return on the next dead ball OR play for a longer period — the sub must be ready for either scenario.
- **Why this works**: Prevents a player from picking up two quick fouls. Many players commit an offensive foul, get frustrated, and immediately commit a defensive foul. This policy eliminates that cycle.
- **All backups stay mentally in the game** from the opening tip because they know they can enter at any moment [S13, p.226].

### Second Foul Substitution
- Applies in the same manner as the first foul, but with **greater consequence**.
- Key rule: **Starters must begin the second half with no more than two fouls.**
- After the second foul in the first half, the starter **will not play again until the second half**.
- This requires convincing — players believe they can avoid a third foul, but basketball is a game of reactions and emotions [S13, p.226].

### Fourth Foul Substitution
- The backup substitute **inserts immediately** on the fourth foul (unless specifically told to wait).
- The goal: protect the starter and work the clock down to a point where the starter can return with approximately 3 minutes left.
- **Purdue vs. Duke, 1980 Mid-East Regional Championship**: Joe Barry Carroll (7-foot All-American) picked up his 4th foul with 6 minutes left and a 7-point lead. Freshman backup Ted Benson entered; Purdue ran their control offense (LA offense guard-to-forward, reset) for multiple possessions, eating clock. Carroll returned with ~3 minutes left. Purdue won 68-60 [S13, p.226].
- The offense ran two sequences per possession before looking for a shot — the only way Duke could get the ball was to foul.

### Zone Defense to Protect Foul-Limited Players
- Zone defenses can keep foul-limited players in the game by reducing their direct defensive responsibility and minimizing contact situations.
- When a key player has 4 fouls and the team cannot afford to lose him, switching to a zone can protect him while maintaining defensive integrity [S13, p.226].

### Policy Infrastructure
- Each starter has a **designated substitute** — both the starter and the backup know the policy from day one.
- Reserves know from the **opening tip** that they should be ready to enter on any foul — they "put themselves into the game" rather than waiting for the coach.
- This policy **eliminates emotion** from the coaching staff's substitution decisions; it becomes an established, automatic process.
- The **entire team must accept and understand** this procedure before the season begins [S13, p.226].

## Player Responsibilities
- **Starters**: Accept the policy, communicate fouls immediately to the bench, and trust the system.
- **Backup substitutes**: Stay ready at all times; self-substitute to the scorer's table on the first sign of a foul on their designated starter.
- **Point guard**: Lead the control offense in foul-protection situations (clock management + possession preservation).

## Common Mistakes
1. **No designated substitute** → Foul management becomes reactive and emotional rather than systematic.
2. **Starter not pulled after the second foul in the first half** → Risks a third foul that removes them from the game for a longer period.
3. **Backup not mentally engaged** → Backups who don't expect to play are never truly ready; the policy forces constant readiness.
4. **Not using zone to protect foul-limited players** → Man-to-man defense exposes 4-foul players to constant contact risk.
