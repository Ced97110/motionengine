# Power vs. Skill Training: The Fundamental Distinction

## Summary
Not all athletic training is the same. The most important distinction an explosive athlete must understand is the difference between **power training** (training muscles and joints) and **skill training** (training the brain and nervous system). These two categories require fundamentally different programming approaches, and confusing them leads to poor results or injury.

Power training taxes the muscles and joints, which take time to recover and adapt — often a day or more. Skill training — when the body is conditioned for it — works primarily through the nervous system, which adapts almost instantly. This means power training should be hard, brief, and infrequent; while skill training benefits from high frequency, low fatigue, and maximum repetitions [S9, pp.1-2].

## When to Use
- Use **power programming** when the goal is developing raw speed-strength: jumping, explosive pushing, plyometric force output
- Use **skill programming** when the goal is mastering coordination-dependent movements: complex footwork patterns, acrobatic actions, basketball-specific movement sequences
- Most athletic programs need **both** — the power base enables the skill expression [S9, p.2]

## Key Principles
1. **Power training works muscles and joints** — these adapt slowly; they need rest between sessions (1-2 days minimum). "If you train for power, you'll find that after a day or more of rest, you can come back stronger." [S9, p.1]
2. **Skill training works the brain and nervous system** — these adapt rapidly. "Up to a point, these areas adapt almost instantly." [S9, p.1]
3. **Power training: hard and brief** — Do what you need to do, then stop. Quality over quantity — the first rep is the fastest, not the last. [S9, p.2]
4. **Skill training: high frequency, high volume** — "Do as much as you can! The more you practice your back flip, for example, the better it gets." [S9, p.2]
5. **Fatigue degrades skill training** — As soon as form breaks down due to fatigue, stop the skill session. You cannot learn coordination when exhausted. [S9, p.12]
6. **Power is the prerequisite for skill** — You cannot express complex skill movements without the underlying power to execute them. Build the foundation first. [S9, p.3]

## Player Responsibilities
This concept applies to all positions equally. Different basketball skills map to the power/skill spectrum:
- **Power-type training**: jump training (vertical leap development), explosive push-up variations, first-step acceleration work
- **Skill-type training**: shooting footwork, ball-handling patterns, post move sequences, defensive slide patterns, cutting footwork

## Variations
### Power-Skill Spectrum
Most movements exist on a spectrum between pure power and pure skill. Olympic weightlifting is near the power end; playing piano is at the skill end. Basketball actions like the vertical jump lean power; shooting off a screen leans skill. Program each accordingly.

### Basketball Application
- Treat **plyometrics and jumping** as power training: 1-3 reps per set, rest fully, train every 2-3 days
- Treat **shooting footwork and ball-handling** as skill training: maximize reps, train fresh, practice daily

## Common Mistakes
1. **High reps on explosive exercises** → fatigue teaches the body to move slowly; keep reps to 1-3 for power
2. **Treating skill work like power work** → only practicing shooting twice per week when daily repetition would accelerate learning
3. **Doing skill work when fatigued** → degraded form wires bad patterns into the nervous system; always practice skills fresh
4. **Racing through progressions** → completing a progression step once is not mastery; linear improvement on basics is the goal [S9, p.3]
