# C-ABCD Downed Athlete Protocol

## Summary
When a basketball athlete passes out or collapses, the medical team must follow a systematic primary survey before moving the athlete. The "C-ABCD" protocol — adapted from ACLS and trauma protocols — provides a uniform approach to the downed athlete: CPR/Cervical spine (C), Airway (A), Breathing (B), Circulation (C), Disability (D). Cardiovascular causes are the most prevalent cause of nontraumatic sudden death; they are followed by dehydration, temperature-related illness, electrolyte imbalances, and drug use [S10, pp.165-168].

The fundamental rule: **do not move the athlete until each step of the algorithm is completed**, unless there is immediate danger, cardiorespiratory emergency, or airway compromise requiring relocation.

## When to Use
- Any athlete who collapses, becomes unresponsive, or has an altered level of consciousness during basketball
- First responder assessment before EMS arrival
- Training sessions as well as games

## Key Principles

### Step C — CPR and Cervical Spine (SIMULTANEOUS)

#### CPR First
1. If athlete is unresponsive: check for cardiac arrest signs (unresponsiveness, irregular/absent breathing)
2. Begin chest compressions **immediately** without assessing pulse — CPR can rarely cause harm if no cardiac arrest exists [S10, p.165]
3. Apply AED as soon as available — every medical team must have an electronic defibrillator courtside
4. Defibrillator batteries must be checked before every game and training session [S10, p.166]

#### Cervical Spine Protection (Simultaneous with CPR)
1. If neck injury is possible: maintain in-line manual immobilization — one person on each side of the head keeping head and neck still, OR hands on both shoulders stabilizing the head with forearms [S10, p.166]
2. **Avoid traction, extension, or flexion** of the neck
3. Maintain C-spine immobilization until a rigid cervical collar is applied
4. **Log-roll maneuver**: if athlete must be turned from prone position, minimum 4 people required — 3 assistants on one side, leader at the head coordinating on "count of 3"; ensure athlete's hands are crossed before rolling [S10, p.166]

### Step A — Airway
1. Ask the athlete their name — a response confirms airway and ventilation are clear
2. **Signs of partial airway obstruction**: stridor, gurgling, whistling, gasping sounds
3. **Signs of severe obstruction**: intercostal retraction, accessory muscle use, agitation, altered consciousness
4. **Cyanosis**: purplish discoloration of nail beds and lips — harder to detect in athletes with dark skin [S10, p.167]
5. **Chin lift technique**: place index finger under mandible, pull lower lip down with thumb — do NOT hyperextend or flex the neck [S10, p.168]
6. **Oropharyngeal airway placement**: insert concavity upward along roof of hard palate until soft palate, then rotate 180° so concavity faces down over the tongue [S10, p.168]
7. If airway cannot be maintained: definitive airway required (orotracheal tube, nasotracheal tube, or surgical airway — cricothyroidotomy or tracheostomy) [S10, p.168]

### Step B — Breathing
1. Assess ventilation immediately after airway is secured
2. Look and listen simultaneously: watch for symmetric chest movement + listen for breath sounds over mouth [S10, p.168]
3. Asymmetrical chest movement → suspect chest wall injury
4. Auscultate both lung fields with stethoscope to confirm adequate air entry
5. Deviated trachea on palpation → suspect tension pneumothorax
6. **Bag-valve-mask device**: use if athlete cannot breathe spontaneously; provides both ventilation and oxygen supplementation [S10, p.168]

### Step C — Circulation
- Assess pulse and perfusion
- Identify and control hemorrhage
- Note: detailed circulation assessment follows after airway/breathing are secured

### Step D — Disability
- Neurological assessment (level of consciousness, motor/sensory function)
- Assess for focal neurological deficits
- Only performed after cardiorespiratory threats are cleared

### Medical Precautions
- Wear gloves during all interventions to avoid exposure to body fluids [S10, p.165]
- Do not move the athlete before completing each algorithm step, unless immediate danger exists

## Common Mistakes
1. Moving the athlete before completing the primary survey → risk of exacerbating cervical spine injury
2. Delaying CPR to assess the pulse → start chest compressions immediately on any unresponsive athlete
3. Hyperextending the neck during chin lift → maintain neutral alignment; do not flex or extend
4. Using only 2-3 people for log-roll → minimum 4 required; 3 assistants + 1 leader
5. Confusing agonal respirations with normal breathing → gasping after collapse is a cardiac arrest sign, not normal breathing
6. Attributing a post-collapse seizure to epilepsy before excluding cardiac arrest
