# Practice With a Purpose

## Summary
Coach Shawn Nelson's foundational practice philosophy holds that time in the gym only produces results when athletes practice with intentionality and at game speed. Most players who fail to improve are not lacking effort in terms of hours — they are lacking a clear purpose and the habit of game-speed execution. Once "practicing with a purpose" becomes an ingrained habit, skill development accelerates dramatically.

The program pairs this philosophy with a goal-setting and tracking system: every workout has defined shot counts and benchmarks, and players record their results to measure progress over time.

## When to Use
- Applied to every individual offensive workout, not just during the season
- Introduced at the start of a player's development program as a non-negotiable standard
- Revisited whenever a player's improvement plateaus despite volume of practice

## Key Principles
1. **Practice at game speed all the time.** Slow practice trains slow play. If you practice at game speed, you prepare yourself to excel and adapt to any game situation. [S3, p.9]
2. **Identify your motivation or goal before each workout.** A clear goal keeps athletes focused and determined throughout the session. [S3, p.9]
3. **No shortcuts.** Whatever success athletes achieve must be earned through hard work and adherence to sound practice concepts. [S3, p.9]
4. **Make no excuses.** Players who want success must pursue it relentlessly and own their development process. [S3, p.9]
5. **Video your work.** Players can compare their live-ball moves and scoring opportunities to model examples, giving them a visual reference for where they are in the learning process. [S3, p.9]
6. **Set realistic, progressive goals.** Once a goal is reached, re-evaluate and set a higher benchmark to continue improving. [S3, p.10]
7. **Modify workouts to fit individual needs.** Coaches and players should choose workouts specific to each athlete's development gaps. [S3, p.13]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Own your development. Know your goal before the workout, execute at game speed, track your results, and raise your standard once each goal is achieved.

## Variations
### Goal-Setting for Players
Players set personal benchmarks (e.g., earning a starting spot, making the team, earning a scholarship). They use workout tracking charts to monitor progress toward those benchmarks. [S3, p.10]

### Goal-Setting for Coaches
Coaches can use the same tracking charts to monitor the overall skill development of the team and identify which players need more volume or specific move work. [S3, p.10]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Practicing at half speed → produces half-speed skills.** Enforce game-speed execution on every rep.
2. **Spending time without a target → no measurable improvement.** Assign a specific skill and shot count before the player steps on the floor.
3. **Not tracking results → no accountability.** Use the workout tracking tables to record FGA, FGM, 3PTA, 3PTM, FTA, FTM, and percentages for every session.
