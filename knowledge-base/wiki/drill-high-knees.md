# High Knees

## Objective
Develop the "thigh-parallel-to-the-ground" sprint technique — building the knee drive pattern that produces maximum stride length and hip power.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Open flat surface — court, field, or track
- Distance: 20–30 meters per rep, or stationary for timed sets

## Execution
1. Lift knees high into the air in a **high marching stance**, simultaneously coming off the ground with the feet.
2. Drive each knee so the thigh reaches **parallel to the ground** at the top of the lift.
3. Incorporate **butt-kicker** mechanics simultaneously — heel comes toward the gluteus on the recovery phase before the next knee drive (see butt-kickers drill).
4. When both elements are combined correctly, the two parts of the drill begin to resemble actual sprint mechanics.
5. Move forward progressively as technique improves.

## Coaching Points
- "Knee to parallel" — if the thigh doesn't reach horizontal, the drill loses most of its sprint-transfer value.
- Stay on the balls of the feet — never flat-footed contact.
- Arms match the leg rhythm — 90-degree elbow bend, driving front and back.
- Do not allow the torso to lean backward — stay tall and slightly forward.
- This drill is a foundational sprint-technique builder that must be mastered before moving to higher-intensity speed work.

## Variations
### Backward Knees Drill (Complex Variation)
- Perform high knees while moving backward.
- This challenges the central nervous system differently and improves sprint technique through novel motor demands.
- Useful for athletes who have mastered the forward version.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Stationary high march in place, slow rhythm, 20 seconds
2. **Intermediate**: Forward high knees over 20m, moderate speed
3. **Advanced**: Fast-paced high knees at near-sprint speed, 25–30m; then backward variation
