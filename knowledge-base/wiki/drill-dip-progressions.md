# Dip Progressions (Muscle-Up Chain)

## Objective
Build the pressing strength and explosive power required for the dip-out phase of the muscle-up through a systematic 10-step dip progression chain, culminating in horizontal bar dips and explosive dip variations.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Progression 1-3: bench or ledge
- Progression 4-8: parallel bars
- Progression 9: perpendicular (right-angled) bar
- Progression 10: horizontal bar

## Execution

### Strength Progression Chain (Steps 1–10)

1. **Bent Dips** — Feet on the floor, knees well-bent, hands on a surface behind the body. Legs take the bulk of the load. Easiest entry point.

2. **Straight Dips** — Straighten the legs so the upper body takes on more of the load. Same hand position as bent dips.

3. **Feet-Elevated Dips** — Raise the feet onto an elevated object. This shifts the center of gravity backward, toward the hands, increasing upper-body demand.

4. **Feet-Up Parallel Bar Dips** — Feet raised and crossed; hands on parallel bars. The easiest parallel bar introduction.

5. **Self-Assisted Parallel Bar Dips 1** — Full parallel bar dip with one leg assisting during the pressing phase to push through sticking points.

6. **Self-Assisted Parallel Bar Dips 2** — Self-assistance using the instep (a harder assist position than step 5).

7. **Parallel Bar Dips** — Classic full parallel bar dip. Lean back to emphasize triceps; angle forward to engage shoulders and chest.

8. **Legs-Forward Parallel Bar Dips** — Feet held forward in front of the hips, shifting center of gravity forward, making the exercise significantly harder.

9. **Perpendicular Dips** — Hands on a bar at a right angle to the body; intermediate between parallel bars and the horizontal bar.

10. **Horizontal Bar Dips** — Dip on top of the horizontal bar itself. If the athlete can perform this, they have the pressing strength to complete the muscle-up.

### Coaching Notes for the Strength Chain
- No specific rep standards — "milk" each step until becoming expert at it before moving to the next.
- These are strength-based movements; train them as ancillary work to the muscle-up progression chain.
- "If you can comfortably perform a horizontal bar dip, your chances of completing a muscle-up have skyrocketed."

## Explosive Dip Variations

Once basic parallel bar dips are mastered, explosive variations develop speed-strength for basketball-applicable upper-body power:

### Walking Dips
- In the dip support position, "step" hands laterally along the bars.
- Forces joints and muscles to absorb shock on each step — the foundational explosive dip.

### Hopping Dips
- Press explosively enough to lift both hands off the bars simultaneously and land back.
- Progress to **long hop dips** — hop forward (or backward) along the bars.
- Hands and forearms also train reactively by "catching" the bars on re-contact.

### Clap Dips
- At the peak of the explosive dip press, bring the hands together in a clap before re-gripping the bars.
- Arms must move the *entire bodyweight* explosively — significantly harder than clap push-ups.
- "Build up progressively or this is an injury waiting to happen."

### 180 Power Dips
- Press up explosively, rotate 180° in mid-air, and catch the bar again facing the opposite direction.
- Adds agility and rotational power to the explosive pressing pattern.

### Swing Dips
- Use bar momentum to enhance the dip; the swing aids the press and develops dynamic pushing power.

### Russian Dips
- On parallel bars: lower under control all the way to the elbows, then shift weight forward and straighten arms.
- Mimics the pull-over transition mechanics — useful as muscle-up ancillary work.

### L-Hold Dips
- Parallel bar dips with legs locked out horizontally in front of the hips (L-sit position).
- Trains the midsection simultaneously; shifts center of gravity forward, making dips significantly harder.

### Korean Dips
- Bar positioned behind the body; one-arm dip variant for elite-level athletes.
- "Gorilla shoulders only!"

## Coaching Points
- Dips are an ancillary exercise for the muscle-up — the primary goal is horizontal bar dipping ability.
- The dip-press in the muscle-up requires about half the range of a regular parallel bar dip — but must be done with raw strength, no momentum.
- The dip-press is the most common failure point in the muscle-up for athletes who have already mastered the pull-over.
- Progressive loading principle: begin with maximum assistance and systematically remove it across the 10 steps.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner**: Steps 1–4 (floor-based and feet-up parallel bar work)
2. **Intermediate**: Steps 5–7 (self-assisted through full parallel bar dips)
3. **Advanced**: Steps 8–10 + explosive variations
