# The Four Factors

## Summary
Dean Oliver's Four Factors framework identifies the four aspects of basketball that every team must control to win games. On both offense and defense, a team's efficiency is driven by: (1) shooting percentage from the field, (2) offensive rebounding, (3) turnovers, and (4) getting to the foul line. These four categories capture essentially everything that determines how many points a team scores or allows per possession.

The framework is supported by win-correlation analysis: when teams are equalized on some factors, the remaining factors predict wins at rates ranging from 56% to 80%. Field goal percentage is the most powerful single factor, but when FG% is approximately equal, free throws and turnovers become the decisive edge [S16, pp.89-92].

## When to Use
- Evaluating team offensive and defensive performance
- Identifying what went wrong in a loss
- Building a game plan targeting an opponent's weaknesses
- Evaluating player contributions (did the player help or hurt each factor?)

## Key Principles
1. **Shooting percentage is the most important factor** — teams with higher FG% win 80% of games [S16, p.90]
2. **When FG% is equal, free throws dominate** — the team making more FTs wins ~70% of games where FG% is approximately equal [S16, p.91]
3. **Turnovers and assist-to-turnover ratio are second tier** — both rank in the top four when FG% is held constant [S16, pp.90-91]
4. **Offensive rebounding is less predictive than commonly assumed** — teams with more offensive rebounds win only 46% of games overall (because bad-shooting teams also give up more defensive boards); but becomes more important when FG% is equalized [S16, pp.90-91]
5. **Defensive rebounding total is deceptive** — it's so correlated with FG% that it loses most predictive power when FG% is held constant [S16, p.91]
6. **Offensive rebounding is an offensive skill; defensive rebounding is a defensive skill** — they should be evaluated separately, not combined into "rebounding" [S16, p.88]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Control turnovers, assist-to-turnover ratio; draw fouls on drives
- **SG**: Shooting efficiency; free throw creation
- **SF**: Offensive rebounding on missed shots; free throw creation
- **PF**: Offensive and defensive rebounding; free throw creation inside
- **C**: Defensive rebounding (position advantage); offensive rebounding; opponent FG% (shot blocking/contesting)

## Variations
### Offensive Four Factors
For a team's offense: effective FG% (eFG%), offensive rebound rate, turnover rate, free throw rate.

### Defensive Four Factors
For a team's defense: opponent eFG%, defensive rebound rate, opponent turnover rate, opponent free throw rate.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Overvaluing raw rebound totals** → Use rebound *percentage* (% of available rebounds captured), not total count — raw totals are confounded by opponent shooting [S16, p.89]
2. **Treating "wins the rebounding battle" as predictive** → Teams with more offensive rebounds win only 46% of games; the raw count is nearly meaningless [S16, p.90]
3. **Undervaluing free throws** → In close games where FG% is even, free throws are the single most decisive factor [S16, pp.91-92]
4. **Combining offensive and defensive rebounds** → They require different skills, mentalities, and teaching approaches [S16, p.88]
