# Disrupting Full-Court Transitions

## Summary
Limiting transition baskets requires disciplined half-court offensive design and specific defensive transition principles working in unison. Certain defensive attributes make teams hard to beat: teams that challenge every shot, deny penetration, limit offensive rebounds, minimize easy baskets, stay out of foul trouble, and reduce opponents' transition scores. These qualities are not accidents — they are taught, drilled, and built into the offense from the opening possession [S13, p.215].

## When to Use
- As a system embedded in every offensive possession to create built-in transition defense
- When scouting reveals an opponent with dangerous transition guards or a primary break system
- After made baskets, missed shots, and turnovers — all transition triggers

## Key Principles

### 1. Rebounding as Transition Defense
- Designate specific players to crash the offensive boards on every half-court play AND simultaneously position for transition defense.
- If the shot is missed and the designated offensive rebounder cannot retrieve it, they become the **first line of defense** — positioned to take away the opponent's quick outlet pass.
- **Big men**: Extend arms high and smother (clamp down on) the rebounder, restricting the easy outlet. Best case = deflection or interception; worst case = outlet pass is slowed.
- If the rebounder tries to dribble out, **one defender should cover that player immediately**.
- Teach designated rebounders to attack the boards AND execute the defensive outlet-containment maneuver simultaneously [S13, p.215].

### 2. Stopping the Ball
- Offensive play design should send **three inside players to the offensive boards**, one guard to the free-throw line (for the long rebound), and **one guard back** to defend the long pass.
- In this formation:
  - Inside players jam the rebounders
  - The long-rebound guard has first-pass-out responsibility
  - The guard back picks up and contains the first opponent crossing midcourt [S13, p.215]

### 3. Lane Denial
- Passing is far quicker than dribbling in transition — deny the outlet pass first.
- Big men must **jam the rebounders** to negate the quick outlet.
- In a 3-1-1 tandem alignment: the **up guard** denies first, then stays in front of the offensive guard if he receives the outlet pass.
- After the guard receives the outlet: read whether he is looking to dribble up or pass long. If dribbling → contain from the front. If passing long → a teammate is leaking out and must be covered [S13, pp.215-216].

### 4. Helping
- In normal transition, big men sprint back and provide **middle containment support** for the ball defender.
- **Scramble situations** (turnover outfront, long rebound caught in stride) eliminate big-man containment help — all five defenders must sprint back, defend the paint, and protect the basket.
- Quick guards in today's game go coast-to-coast; no player can wait to sprint back [S13, p.216].

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (back defender)**: Defend against the long pass; contain the first opponent crossing midcourt.
- **SG (long rebound guard)**: Position at the free-throw line; take first-pass-out responsibility after opponent rebounds.
- **SF/PF/C (inside players)**: Attack offensive boards; simultaneously jam the rebounder to slow the outlet; if unable to retrieve, become transition defenders and deny the outlet.

## Common Mistakes
1. **All five players crashing the offensive boards** → Always keep at least one guard back to stop the long pass.
2. **Big men not jamming the rebounder** → Passively watching the rebounder gives the outlet receiver a free run up the floor.
3. **Ball defender defending without help** → Middle containment support from sprinting bigs is essential in normal transition; don't leave the ball defender alone.
4. **Transition left to improvisation** → Transition defense must be built into the offensive play design from the start, not added as an afterthought.
