# Offensive System Design: Fitting Talent to Scheme

## Summary
Building a winning offense begins with an honest assessment of available talent. The coach must identify the best ball handlers, shooters, decision makers, post players, and screen setters on the roster, and then design a system that puts those players in their most advantageous positions. Every player contributes through scoring, assists, rebounding, or shot blocking — and mental awareness (understanding winning basketball + attitude) is evaluated equally alongside physical ability [S13, p.107].

Players must also understand their own role. Finding the one or two things they do better than anyone else on the team and committing to those contributions is the foundation of team offense. Three talent-based offensive frameworks are described: guard-oriented, forward-oriented, and low-post-oriented [S13, pp.107-113].

## When to Use
- **Guard-oriented**: Best ball handlers, shooters, and decision makers are guards
- **Forward-oriented**: Most versatile and dominant scorers play the wing/forward positions
- **Low-post-oriented**: Team has a skilled, physical center who can score and make decisions in the post
- Coaches can shift between frameworks at time-outs to show different looks and keep defenses off-balance

## Key Principles
1. **Talent-first design**: "The coach sees a group of athletes — all with various skills — and blends those skills to form the best offense possible" [S13, p.107]
2. **Role acceptance**: Players must understand how, when, and where they fit — mentally and physically
3. **Dual evaluation**: Players are evaluated on physical abilities AND mental awareness (understanding + attitude)
4. **Floor balance**: Regardless of the system, proper spacing, good ball movement, and willing passers are universal requirements
5. **Build in options**: No single-read offense; every set must have secondary and tertiary options to counter double-teams and defensive adjustments
6. **Inside-out philosophy**: Getting the ball inside first creates more foul opportunities and higher-percentage shots
7. **Consistency of role**: Keep the passers, shooters, and low-post scorer in their most effective positions even as the offensive set changes

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Floor general; carries decision making, ball handling, and delivery to shooters/post — the coach on the floor
- **SG**: Primary perimeter scorer in guard-oriented sets; spot-up shooter and cutter in other sets
- **SF**: Primary scorer in forward-oriented sets; versatile mismatch creator; three-point spot-up shooter
- **PF**: Screen setter, rebounder, post option; staggered double screen participant in forward-oriented attack
- **C**: Dominant inside scorer in post-oriented sets; high-post passer and DHO option in guard-oriented sets; screener in all sets

## Variations
### Guard-Oriented Attack
Use when guards are the team's best ball handlers, shooters, and decision makers. Built-in options for catch-and-shoot, dribble-drive, PnR, draw-and-kick, and two-player game with the post-up forward. See [[play-guard-oriented-attack]] [S13, pp.108-111].

### Forward-Oriented Attack
Use when forwards (wings) are the most versatile scorers. Uses hand-offs, basket cuts off the center's screen, post-ups, split action, and staggered double screens to create perimeter, mid-range, and three-point opportunities for forwards. See [[play-forward-oriented-attack]] [S13, pp.111-113].

### Low-Post-Oriented Attack (Inside-Out)
Use when the team has a dominant center. Philosophy: attack inside first, then kick out. Uses the 1-4 box set to flow into the Power Down and Power Cross attacks. See [[play-power-down-attack]] and [[play-power-cross-attack]] [S13, pp.113-118].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Fitting players to a system the coach prefers rather than what the talent supports** → assess honestly; let the talent dictate the system
2. **Over-crowding the post** → teammates cutting through the post area restrict the big player's moves; everyone must read and space correctly
3. **Ignoring the direct pass to the post** → defenses can immediately double-team; use player movement and indirect entry routes first
4. **Lack of role clarity** → players who don't know their contribution become passive or create offensive chaos
5. **Neglecting free-throw shooting** → teams that get to the line most win most; the inside-out strategy maximizes FT opportunities [S13, p.119]
