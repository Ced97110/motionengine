# Well-Being Monitoring in Basketball

## Summary
Monitoring player well-being in conjunction with workload data is essential for minimizing maladaptive responses — including non-functional overreaching and non-contact injuries. Well-being and workload can trend independently across the season, meaning combined monitoring is more informative than either in isolation. Despite large weekly workload spikes (up to 226%), well-being scores in some studies remained relatively stable (highest spike ~7%), highlighting that well-being provides complementary — not redundant — information to workload metrics [S10, pp.828-829].

## When to Use
- Throughout the entire in-season phase, with weekly frequency
- Before games and practice sessions to assess player readiness
- During periods of high workload (compressed schedules, playoffs) when maladaptive risk is elevated
- When making individual training load adjustments for specific players

## Key Principles
1. **15% Spike Rule** — Spikes in weekly workload exceeding 15% may increase injury risk in team sport athletes; injury risk increases 21–49% when weekly workload changes by >15% [S10, p.829].
2. **Basketball Spike Reality** — Observed weekly workload spikes in male collegiate basketball players reached 226% during the in-season; elite female players showed spikes up to 120% across a 24-week season [S10, p.829].
3. **Well-Being vs. Workload Independence** — Despite massive workload fluctuations, well-being scores can remain stable. This means well-being monitoring adds unique information — it detects cases where physiological stress has exceeded the athlete's recovery capacity even when workload appears manageable [S10, p.829].
4. **1-game vs. 2-game Weeks** — 1-game weeks have substantially higher training workloads; 2-game weeks show higher fatigue and lower sleep quality despite lower training loads. Game factors beyond workload (travel, competition stress) affect well-being [S10, p.829].
5. **Playing Time Prescription** — Consider restricting playing time and resting key players toward the end of the in-season to build playoff preparedness, rather than at the beginning of the season [S10, p.829].

## Well-Being Parameters to Monitor
Common questionnaire parameters used in basketball research include:
- **Fatigue** (Likert scale, typically 1–5 or 1–7)
- **Sleep quality** (Likert scale)
- **Mood** (Likert scale)
- **Stress levels** (Likert scale)
- **General muscle soreness** (Likert scale)

Multi-parameter well-being questionnaires are preferred over single items [S10, p.829].

## Practical Application

### Weekly Monitoring Protocol
1. Collect sRPE within 30 minutes of every training session
2. Administer well-being questionnaire at the same time each week (e.g., morning of practice day)
3. Calculate percent week-to-week workload change
4. Flag any player with >15% weekly change for closer monitoring
5. Cross-reference workload spike with well-being score: if both are elevated, reduce upcoming load; if workload is high but well-being is normal, monitor closely but continue

### 1-game vs. 2-game Week Management
- In 1-game weeks: higher training volume is appropriate — use these weeks to build chronic load
- In 2-game weeks: reduce training volume; monitor fatigue and sleep quality closely; game load already provides adequate stimulus

## Common Mistakes
1. **Monitoring workload without well-being** → Misses athletes whose recovery is compromised despite manageable loads
2. **Monitoring well-being without workload** → Cannot identify whether poor well-being is load-related or external-stressor-related
3. **Using single-item well-being measures** → Multi-parameter questionnaires capture more nuance
4. **Homogenous team load prescription** → Starters and bench players have different game load contributions; individualize training accordingly
5. **Treating 2-game weeks as lower-demand weeks** → Higher game frequency = lower training volume but equal or higher fatigue; adjust accordingly
