# Combination Live-Ball Moves

## Summary
Combination live-ball moves represent one of the most creative aspects of offensive skill development. Rather than using a single attack move, players chain two or more live-ball moves together — using each one to counter the defensive adjustment caused by the previous move. The goal is to put the defender on his heels and keep him off balance long enough to create a high-percentage scoring opportunity.

Every player's combination repertoire will differ, but the underlying logic is consistent: if one type of move is working repeatedly, the defender adjusts — so the offensive player counters that adjustment with the opposite move. For example, repeated rip steps drive the defender's weight low, so a shot fake or step fake then brings the defender up and off balance. [S3, p.12]

## When to Use
- Against a locked-in, active defender who takes away a single first move
- When the defense has scouted and taken away a player's primary scoring option
- In late-shot-clock or isolation situations requiring self-creation
- When a player has established a pattern (e.g., always driving left) and needs to counter the defensive overplay
- Any catch situation from triple-threat position where the first move is denied

## Key Principles
1. **Catch on a two-foot jump stop** — establishes a balanced platform and keeps both pivot foot options open.
2. **Establish the dominant pivot foot** — decide immediately which foot is the pivot before executing any move.
3. **Counter one live-ball move with another** — each successive move exploits the defensive reaction to the previous one.
4. **Use leverage leg, shoulder, and ball movement together** — coordinated body mechanics create momentum into the scoring opportunity; the ball, shoulder, and step must all commit to the same direction.
5. **Find a rhythm and tempo** — combinations have a cadence; rushing kills the deception. "Slow is fast when the defender is off balance."
6. **Dictate direction based on the defender's reaction** — read feet, hips, and weight shift; attack where the defender is not.
7. **Determine which scoring opportunity to use** — combinations can finish with a pull-up, stride stop, big hop, hop-back three, layup, or step-through; the player reads the defense to select the right finish. [S3, p.13]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: Primary ball-handler; must master combinations to break down on-ball defenders and create for themselves or teammates.
- **SG**: Wing scorer; combination moves off catches at the elbow or wing create pull-up or drive opportunities.
- **SF**: Uses combination moves from the mid-post or wing to attack closeouts and beat isolated defenders.
- **PF**: Big who can play facing the basket uses combination moves from the high post or elbow to create layups, hop-back threes, or step-through finishes.

## Variations

### Rip Step + Shot Fake
After repeated rip steps lower the defender's base, a shot fake lifts the defender, creating a drive lane. Execute: rip to hip → shot fake (show ball, don't lift feet) → one-dribble drive past the raised defender.

### Shot Fake + Rip Step
For a defender who crashes hard on shot fakes: show the ball → defender lunges → rip step to hip → drive baseline or middle.

### Hop-Back + Drive
Hop-back to create space → defender closes out hard → attack off the dribble into the close-out. Can finish with stride stop, big hop, or crossover layup.

### Stride Stop + Step-Through
Pull-up stride stop tempts a late-closing defender to contest → immediately step through the raised arm for a layup or short finish.

### Hop-Back to Roll-Out or Crossover Dribble
Hop-back creates separation → instead of rising to shoot, roll-out dribble (outside hand) changes angle → attack the now-closing defender from a new direction.

### 1-Dribble Hop-Back Three + Drive Counter
Establish the hop-back three repeatedly → defender extends to contest the three → drive past the extended defender to the rim.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not catching on a two-foot jump stop** → leads to shuffled footwork, travels, and loss of pivot options. Correct: always land simultaneously on both feet.
2. **Using the same combination repeatedly** → defender adjusts; the combination loses its effectiveness. Correct: vary the sequence each possession.
3. **Rushing the tempo** → kills deception; defenders don't have time to react to the first move before the second. Correct: sell the first move fully before triggering the counter.
4. **Ball, shoulder, and leg not committing together** → defender reads the disconnect and doesn't bite. Correct: all three move in the same direction simultaneously.
5. **Not reading the defender** → player runs a pre-planned sequence regardless of defensive reaction. Correct: the second move is always dictated by what the defender does, not the script.
6. **Ignoring the finish read** → player executes the combination but takes a poor shot. Correct: combinations must end with the highest-percentage available finish.
