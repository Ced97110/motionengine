# Scaphoid Fractures in Basketball

## Summary
Scaphoid fractures are the most common carpal fracture (69% of all acute wrist fractures) and occur at the highest rate in basketball athletes compared to other male sports (11%). They most commonly result from a fall on the outstretched hand (FOOSH) or from wrist hyperextension on a blocked shot. Diagnosis can be challenging — over 30% of plain radiographs are inconclusive, requiring MRI for definitive diagnosis. In professional basketball players, surgical fixation has replaced casting to minimize recovery time and enable earlier return to competition. [S10, pp.294-295]

## When to Suspect Scaphoid Fracture
- Fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH)
- Blocked shot causing wrist hyperextension
- Contact with a closed fist ("puncher fractures")
- Swelling or pain in the anatomic snuff box
- Tenderness on palmar scaphoid tubercle
- Pain with axial compression of the thumb

**If all three clinical tests positive → very high probability of scaphoid fracture** [S10, p.294]

## Anatomy
- Located in proximal carpal row
- Five distinct articular surfaces
- Defined regions: proximal pole (dorsal), waist, distal pole (palmar)
- Blood supply enters distally → proximal pole fractures at high risk for avascular necrosis [S10, p.294]

## Mechanism of Injury
- **FOOSH**: longitudinal loading → flexion moment resisted by ligaments at proximal/distal poles + radioscaphocapitate ligament at waist → tension develops on dorsal cortex → fracture at waist
- **Puncher fractures**: contact with a closed fist → fracture not at waist
- **Blocked shot hyperextension**: commonly reported mechanism [S10, p.294]

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Exam
- Anatomic snuff box tenderness (between 1st and 3rd extensor compartments at medial scaphoid level)
- Palmar scaphoid tubercle tenderness
- Axial compression of thumb → pain
- ROM: flexion, extension, and radial deviation compromised after waist fracture; distal pole fractures have less ROM loss [S10, p.294]

### Imaging
- **Standard radiographs**: PA neutral rotation, lateral, and "scaphoid" view (30° wrist extension + 20° ulnar deviation) [S10, p.294]
- **Sensitivity of plain X-ray**: inconclusive in >30% of cases [S10, p.294]
- **MRI**: highest sensitivity, specificity, and accuracy (all >95%); modality of choice [S10, pp.294-295]
- **CT scan**: evaluates displacement, comminution, and humpback deformity; useful for waist and proximal pole fractures [S10, p.295]

## Herbert Classification and Treatment

| Type | Subtype | Location | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Type A (stable) | A1 | Distal pole | Short-arm thumb spica cast (6 wks); surgery for professional athletes |
| Type A (stable) | A2 | Waist | Same as A1 |
| Type B (unstable) | B1 | Distal oblique | Surgery: internal fixation (screw/plate) or fragment excision if too small |
| Type B (unstable) | B2 | Waist (most common) | Surgery; CT scan for angular deformity |
| Type B (unstable) | B3 | Proximal pole | Surgery; CT scan for proximal fragment size |
| Type B (unstable) | B4 | Waist from trans-scaphoid perilunate dislocation | Surgery |
| Type C | — | Delayed union | Surgery |

[S10, p.295]

## Key Principles

### Professional Athletes
- Surgical fixation is preferred — ensures adequate blood supply, minimizes recovery time, enables earlier return to competition [S10, p.294]
- Upfront surgical fixation has replaced casting as standard of care [S10, p.294]

### Amateur/Non-Professional Athletes
- Nondisplaced waist fractures: **cast immobilization** — 88–95% healing success rates [S10, p.295]
- Distal pole fractures: **cast immobilization for 6 weeks** (best option for most) [S10, p.295]
- Conservative approach is a good alternative to operative treatment for amateurs [S10, p.295]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Relying solely on plain X-rays** → >30% of fractures are missed; use MRI when clinical suspicion is high [S10, pp.294-295]
2. **Missing occult fractures** → Treat suspected fractures clinically even with negative X-ray; confirm with MRI [S10, p.294]
3. **Casting a professional athlete** → Surgical fixation preferred to minimize time lost from competition [S10, p.294]
4. **Missing proximal pole fractures** → Avascular necrosis risk is high; CT or MRI essential; surgery indicated [S10, p.295]

## Variations
### "Puncher Fractures"
Fractures from contact with a closed fist — do not follow the typical FOOSH waist-fracture pattern. Require specific imaging to locate the fracture site [S10, p.294].

### Humpback Deformity
Angular deformity at the waist fracture site. Better visualized on CT scan than plain films (Sanders classification) [S10, p.295].
