# Fast-Break Principles

## Summary
the head coach and a co-author present a comprehensive philosophy for implementing and sustaining a full-court, up-tempo fast-break offense. Their system is built on the belief that a relentless fast break creates more possessions, more open shots, fatigues opponents, and is harder to prepare for than any set-play offense. The two coaches emphasize that only the point guard has a defined role; every other player responds instinctively to the situation in front of them.

The system is not merely about running — it is about a mental commitment first, then physical execution: sprint the court, move the ball faster than defenses can react, and take quick, good shots within each player's defined range. [S7, pp.149-156]

## When to Use
- As a primary offensive philosophy when your roster features multiple athletes with speed, conditioning, and off-ball awareness
- When opponents prefer a slower tempo — the fast break disrupts their comfort zone
- In second halves of close games to exploit fatigue advantages earned from extended running
- After any change of possession: made basket, defensive rebound, steal, turnover, or inbounds situation

## Key Principles

### Five Reasons to Run
1. **Hard to guard**: No defined positions or set plays — defense cannot prepare a specific matchup scheme
2. **Rush the opposition**: Forces opponents to think and move faster than they prefer, causing defensive breakdowns, poor shot selection, and turnovers on their end
3. **Fatigue the opponent**: A full-game sprint pace requires conditioning that most opponents are not specifically prepared for; winded players are vulnerable players
4. **More and easier shots**: More possessions + defense out of position = higher shot volume and better shooting percentage
5. **Player engagement**: Players are more enthusiastic, use a greater variety of skills, and get more touches in an open-court system [S7, p.149]

### Four Implementation Pillars
1. **Apply defensive pressure**: Defense fuels the offense; perimeter pressure forces turnovers and disrupts the opponent's ability to slow the game; play hard and smart — assess the risk/reward of every steal attempt
2. **Rebound the ball**: Four players (all except the PG) crash the defensive boards every time the opponent shoots; no cherry-picking; they cannot advance until they or a teammate secure the rebound
3. **Sprint the court**: Full-speed ahead — not jogging; this is the biggest mental hurdle; coaches must enforce it daily in practice
4. **Move the ball**: Quick, accurate passes; hitting a teammate in stride is the goal; sloppy passes allow the defense to recover [S7, pp.150-152]

### Take Quick, Good Shots
- Players must take immediate advantage of shot opportunities on primary and secondary breaks
- If the shot clock becomes a factor, the team is running too slow
- Players must know their own shooting range and comfort zones
- Not "chuck and duck" — but green-light mentality for each player's defined good shots [S7, p.152]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: The only player with a defined role — receives the outlet pass as far from the basket as possible, dribbles up the middle of the court (not the sideline), and initiates the offensive attack; can penetrate, pass, shoot a pull-up, or launch a three
- **SG/SF (Wings)**: Sprint to the wings (roughly free-throw line extended on each side) as wide as possible; stay wide until reaching the offensive end; read and react — options include crossing in the lane, stopping at the FT-line extended, playing give-and-go with the PG, or any other option the defense allows
- **PF/C (Trailers)**: Usually the last two players up the court (they're rebounding); 4 fills the lane between the PG and a wing; 5 trails to the top of the key area; spacing between the two trailers is critical — they must be far enough apart that one defender cannot cover both

## Variations

### Structured Primary Break
Some coaches run the primary break with defined paths — ball up the sideline or the middle, wings cut at the FT-line extended, give-and-go options. Karl/Moe prefer the freedom model but acknowledge structured approaches work for other teams. [S7, p.156]

### Secondary Break
When the primary break does not produce an advantage, trailers fill in and the team looks for opportunities off ball movement before setting a half-court offense.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Cherry-picking** → Demand that four players attack the defensive boards before running; no player advances until the rebound is secured
2. **Hesitation in transition** → The mental switch from defense to offense must happen within a split second; practice the trigger: rebound, steal, turnover, made basket
3. **Slow outlet pass** → The rebounder must pass quickly to the PG; any delay lets the defense recover and neutralizes the break
4. **Wings running too narrow** → Stay wide along the sideline until the free-throw line extended; narrow wings collapse spacing and allow one defender to guard two players
5. **Holding the ball** → Nothing kills a fast break faster than a stationary ball handler; pass before the defense catches up
6. **Too-close two-on-one** → If both offensive players in a 2v1 are too close together, one defender can neutralize both [S7, p.156]
7. **Turnovers due to underdeveloped passing feel** → Karl/Moe note their Denver team committed 30+ turnovers in an early exhibition; the cure is practice repetition, not slowing down
