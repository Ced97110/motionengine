# Jones Fracture (5th Metatarsal) Rehabilitation in Basketball

## Summary
Foot fractures — particularly Jones fractures of the 5th metatarsal — are a distinctive problem in basketball. A remarkable 61% of foot fractures across all major North American professional sports leagues (NBA, NFL, MLB, NHL) occur in the NBA [S10, p.743]. Foot fractures tend to occur in younger players. Jones fractures have an acute-on-chronic nature: typically an acute event superimposed on a pre-existing bone stress reaction, which means radiographic changes may lag behind symptom onset. Refracture rates in the NBA range from 15–30%, making this one of the most challenging injuries to fully resolve.

## When to Use
- Player presents with lateral foot pain, difficulty walking, and pain to palpation of the 5th metatarsal base
- Radiographic evidence of Jones fracture (zones II and III of proximal 5th metatarsal)
- Biomechanical risk factor screening: varus hindfoot, supinated foot type

## Key Principles

### Epidemiology and Risk Factors
1. Most common foot fracture in basketball players — 5th metatarsal (Jones fracture)
2. **61% of pro sport foot fractures occur in the NBA**; NBA players are the only group to sustain refracture requiring second surgery
3. Has both **acute and chronic components** — typically acute event on pre-existing bone stress reaction
4. Radiographic changes may appear **well after symptom onset** — early imaging may be negative
5. Biomechanical risk factors:
   - **Varus foot** and **varus hindfoot** increase lateral loading on the 5th metatarsal
   - Orthotics with medial wedge may increase lateral load → raises refracture risk

### Body Region Distribution of Bony Stress Injuries in NBA (2005–2015)
- Other foot injury: 37%
- Ankle/fibula: 21%
- 5th metatarsal stress fracture: 18%
- Tibia: 17%
- Knee/patella: 7%

[S10, p.743]

### Surgical Management
- The majority of Jones fractures in basketball are managed **operatively** due to concerns about blood supply and non-union
- Return to play rates in NBA: **57–85%** after Jones fracture
- Refracture rates in NBA: **15–30%** — a major concern
- Pre-injury PER (Player Efficiency Rating) is the **strongest predictor of post-injury performance** [S10, p.743]

### Rehabilitation Framework

**Weeks 1–6: Non-Weight-Bearing**
1. Begin with a period of non-weight-bearing
2. Progressively increase weight-bearing through the first 6 weeks post-injury
3. Prevent/address deconditioning from immobilization (upper body conditioning, contralateral limb work)
4. Non-operative cases require careful observation — high rates of failed conservative treatment

**Middle Phase: Progressive Loading**
1. Transition to full weight-bearing as tolerated
2. Strengthening of hip, knee, and trunk
3. Cardiovascular conditioning (stationary bike, pool)
4. Address biomechanical risk factors:
   - Correct supinated foot type
   - Evaluate and modify orthotics (remove medial wedge if present)
   - Assess and improve dorsiflexion ROM

**Late Phase: Return to Sport**
1. Basketball-specific movement: straight runs → cutting → jumping
2. **Radiographic evidence of union** used as RTP criterion — not time alone
3. Monitor for reinjury signs throughout return to practice and games

### RTP Performance Outcomes
- Majority of players returned with equal playing time as uninjured controls
- Slight decreases in **points per game** and **assists per 36 min** noted post-fracture
- Pre-injury PER is the best predictor of post-injury PER

## Common Mistakes
1. **Early return to weight-bearing** without radiographic union → significantly raises refracture risk
2. **Ignoring varus foot type** → one of the strongest biomechanical predictors of Jones fracture and refracture
3. **Using medial wedge orthotics** → increases lateral loading, raising refracture risk
4. **Non-operative management without close monitoring** → high failure rates require careful observation
5. **Time-based RTP without imaging** → radiographic union must be confirmed before return to jumping/cutting

## Return to Play Criteria
- **Radiographic evidence of union** (primary criterion supported by literature)
- Full weight-bearing with no pain
- Symmetrical strength, balance, and hop tests
- Cleared by treating physician/orthopedist
