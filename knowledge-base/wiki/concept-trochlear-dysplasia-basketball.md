# Trochlear Dysplasia in Basketball Players

## Summary
Trochlear dysplasia — an abnormally shallow or flat femoral trochlear groove — is the predominant anatomical factor inducing patellar instability and patellofemoral pain. A flat trochlea is more likely to cause lateral patellar translation than even an injured medial retinaculum or released vastus medialis. The trochlear groove normally deepens from proximal to distal; when the proximal groove is shallow or flat (as in dysplasia), the lateral structures overcome the medial ones during early flexion, causing asymmetric loading and potential dislocation [S10, pp.413-414].

For basketball players, who experience up to 9-10× body weight joint reaction forces during landing, trochlear dysplasia creates a structurally vulnerable patellofemoral joint under the extreme demands of jumping, pivoting, and cutting.

## When to Use
- Player with recurrent patellar dislocation or subluxation episodes
- Anterior knee pain worsened by early-range knee activities
- Evaluating anatomical contributors to PFP before surgery
- Radiographic analysis showing crossing sign, supratrochlear spur, or double contour

## Key Principles
1. **Trochlear shape is paramount**: The medial and lateral facets converge into the trochlear groove; the lateral facet is normally higher, larger, and more prominent — providing restraint against lateral subluxation [S10, p.413]
2. **Resistance increases with flexion**: At 20° flexion, 74 N needed for 10mm lateral displacement; at 90°, 125 N required — dysplasia is most dangerous in early flexion
3. **Static > dynamic restraints**: Static restraints have less variable and more consistent role in patellar stability than dynamic ones
4. **Three radiographic signs** (on perfect lateral X-ray): Crossing sign, supratrochlear spur, double contour of medial facet
5. **Articular cartilage thickness**: 2-3mm layer covering medial and lateral walls; thinner on medial side
6. **Loss of homeostasis mechanism**: Dysplasia → asymmetric stress concentrated on lateral PF surface → tissue irritation → degenerative cartilage changes → PFP [S10, p.414]

## Dejour's Classification of Trochlear Dysplasia

### Type A
- **Lateral radiograph**: Crossing sign only
- **Axial imaging (CT/MRI)**: Trochlear morphology preserved; shallow trochlea

### Type B
- **Lateral radiograph**: Crossing sign + supratrochlear spur
- **Axial imaging**: Flat trochlea

### Type C
- **Lateral radiograph**: Crossing sign + double contour
- **Axial imaging**: Trochlear facet asymmetry — convex lateral facet, hypoplastic medial facet

### Type D (Most Severe)
- **Lateral radiograph**: Crossing sign + supratrochlear spur + double contour
- **Axial imaging**: Trochlear facets asymmetry + vertical transition between lateral and medial facet ("cliff pattern")

## Radiographic Signs (Lateral X-ray)
- **Crossing sign**: The trochlear groove line crosses the anterior femoral cortex line — indicates the groove is no deeper than the anterior cortex at that level
- **Supratrochlear spur**: Bony prominence at the proximal trochlea, visible on lateral X-ray
- **Double contour**: Shadow of the hypoplastic medial facet visible separately from the trochlear floor

## Biomechanical Consequences
- Lack of congruence between trochlear groove and patella throughout range of motion
- Particularly dangerous in early flexion (proximal groove shallower/flat)
- Lateral structures dominate over medial → lateral subluxation tendency
- Overloading of lateral facet → asymmetric stress concentration → cartilage degeneration
- Progressive PFP as tissues lose homeostasis (Dye's Envelope of Function theory)

## Surgical Considerations
- Trochleoplasty (deepening of the trochlear groove) may be indicated for symptomatic Type B and D dysplasia with recurrent dislocation
- Must be combined with assessment of patella alta, TT-TG distance, and MPFL status for comprehensive instability correction

## Common Mistakes
1. **Imperfect lateral X-ray** → Femoral condyles must be perfectly superimposed to identify crossing sign and supratrochlear spur
2. **Diagnosing on CT/MRI alone** → Lateral radiograph with all three signs is the gold standard classification tool
3. **Treating dysplasia in isolation** → Must assess all PFP contributors (patellar height, TT-TG, MPFL status, VMO, hip/core)
