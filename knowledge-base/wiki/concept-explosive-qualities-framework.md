# The Three Explosive Qualities Framework

## Summary
True athletic explosiveness is not a single quality but a combination of three distinct physical qualities that must be trained together. Paul Wade defines these as: **Power** (the ability to move a load with both strength and speed), **Functional Speed** (moving the *entire body* rapidly), and **Agility** (the ability to change the body's direction at high velocity). A fourth quality — **Reflexes** — is the product of developing these three and can be seen as the sum of the athlete's explosive development [S9, pp.1-2].

No single modern training method develops all three simultaneously. Box jumps build power and functional speed but not agility. Cone drills build agility and functional speed but not power. Olympic lifting builds power but neither functional speed nor agility. Only total-body explosive calisthenics (flips, kip-ups, muscle-ups) develop all three qualities together [S9, pp.5-6].

## When to Use
- When designing off-season athletic development programs for basketball players
- When evaluating whether a training modality is truly developing court explosiveness
- When explaining to players why certain gym exercises don't transfer to game quickness
- As a framework for diagnosing which quality a player lacks (power vs. agility vs. speed)

## Key Principles
1. **Power = Strength × Speed**: The athlete must move a meaningful load (their body mass) as quickly as possible — not just move fast with no resistance, and not just be strong slowly.
2. **Functional Speed = Whole-Body Movement**: Speed of an isolated limb has zero carryover to sport. The entire body must be trained to move rapidly as a unit.
3. **Agility = Direction Change at Velocity**: The body must be capable of *changing its direction of travel* at high speed — not just running fast in a straight line.
4. **Reflexes = Automatic Nervous System Adaptation**: Reflexes bypass conscious processing; they are conditioned (trained) automatically when explosive movements are practiced. You do not train reflexes directly — you train the three qualities and reflexes improve as a byproduct [S9, p.2].
5. **Train all three simultaneously**: Any training method that fails to develop all three is incomplete for athletic development purposes.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating explosiveness as a single quality** → Evaluate each training method against all three criteria: power, functional speed, AND agility.
2. **Relying only on plyometrics (box jumps)** → Box jumps build power and functional speed but the body only moves up and down — agility (direction change) is missing.
3. **Relying only on cone drills** → Cone drills develop agility and functional speed but the load demands are too low to build power.
4. **Relying only on Olympic lifting** → Olympic lifts build power but feet stay near the floor (no functional speed) and the body doesn't change direction (no agility).
5. **Training body parts in isolation** → No meaningful athletic speed or agility exists in isolated body parts; the entire body must be trained as a unit.
