# Motion Offense Screening Rules

## Summary
Motion offense is built on four universal rules that govern how players move, space the floor, and communicate. These rules are not complex, but they must be drilled until they become automatic. When every player follows these rules simultaneously, the defense cannot key on any single action — the offense becomes positionless and unpredictable.

The rules apply to all screen types: down screens, back screens, screen-the-screener, and on-ball screens.

## When to Use
- As the foundational principles whenever teaching motion offense to any age group
- When introducing screening concepts beyond basic catch-and-shoot actions
- As a daily reminder at the start of team offensive practice sessions

## Key Principles
1. **Use the cut your defender gives you** — read the defender's position and choose the cut that wins (V-cut, backdoor, curl, fade).
2. **Maintain 15-foot (4.5 m) spacing** — prevents defensive help and keeps the floor open for drives and skip passes.
3. **Every player must move on every pass** — when the ball moves, all five players move. No standing.
4. **When a player dribbles toward you, you must cut or fade** — clear space for the dribbler immediately; no two players collide in the same area.
5. **When setting a screen for a teammate, call out the teammate's name** — verbal communication eliminates confusion and ensures the cutter times the V-cut correctly.
6. Players may use moves, rocker steps, and dribbling to drive to the basket and pass back outside (penetrate-and-kick).

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Follow all four rules simultaneously. No player has a position — only a read.
- **Ball-side players (2)**: May use on-ball screens (screen-and-roll) and dribble-entry actions. Keep the ball side uncongested.
- **Weak-side players (3)**: Continuously cycle through down screens, back screens, and screen-the-screener actions. One player always has the ball going to them on a given pass.

## Variations
### Restricted Screening
For younger teams, designate only 2-3 screen types allowed on each side of the floor. Limit the cut menu to 2-3 options. Reduces decision-making overload while preserving motion principles.

### Stall Game
Add five defenders and disallow all shots except layups. The same motion offense rules create a legal stall game — the offense never stands still and is always in a 2-ball-side / 3-weak-side structure.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Standing after passing** → Correct: immediately screen or cut after every pass.
2. **Cutter doesn't V-cut first** → Correct: always dip toward the basket before using any screen; this sets up the defender for the perfect screening angle.
3. **Screener doesn't call teammate's name** → Correct: verbal call is mandatory; it synchronizes timing.
4. **Ball-side congestion** → Correct: only 2 players on ball side; cut off any back screen to the ball side triggers a weak-side pass.
5. **Spacing collapses** → Correct: maintain 15-foot minimum gap between perimeter players at all times.
