# Shoulder Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Although shoulder injuries are less common than ankle or knee injuries in basketball, they can significantly affect athlete performance and may lead to career-ending consequences. Basketball has evolved into a highly physical sport, increasing susceptibility to both acute and chronic shoulder pathologies. The primary mechanism of shoulder injury is player-to-player contact. Acute injuries include glenohumeral dislocation, acromioclavicular (AC) separation, clavicle fractures, proximal humerus fractures, and scapula fractures. Chronic injuries include rotator cuff tendinitis or tears, internal shoulder impingement, biceps tendon injuries, SLAP lesions, and scapular dyskinesis [S10, p.256].

Should injury rates are slightly higher during competition than practice. Conservative management is the mainstay of treatment for most shoulder injuries. Strength and conditioning programs — especially preseason conditioning — are effective in reducing shoulder injuries [S10, p.260].

## When to Use
- Any player who reports shoulder pain, instability, weakness, or mechanical symptoms (clicking, locking, snapping)
- Post-contact assessment after falls, collisions, or forced arm positions
- Pre-participation screening for overhead athletes
- Return-to-play decision-making after any shoulder event

## Key Principles

### History Taking
1. Document location and character of pain — top of shoulder suggests AC injury; posterior shoulder pain may indicate cervical etiology; nocturnal pain suggests rotator cuff pathology [S10, p.253]
2. Assess instability — direction of instability (adducted/internal rotation = posterior; abducted/external rotation = anterior), frequency, duration
3. Record previous injuries, treatments, injections, physical therapy, and surgeries — prior injury is a major risk factor for new injury [S10, p.253]
4. Note mechanical symptoms — catching, locking, snapping suggest labral pathology

### Physical Examination
1. Observe posture, shoulder position, and trunk alignment before focusing on the affected side [S10, p.253]
2. Remove all clothing to allow visualization of swelling, bruising, and muscular atrophy
3. Compare bilateral shoulder symmetry through active and passive ROM — forward flexion, abduction, adduction, internal and external rotation
4. Assess scapular movements carefully for synchronization with glenohumeral joint; scapular winging indicates serratus anterior or trapezius denervation [S10, p.254]
5. Palpate from painless to painful areas — humeral head, GH joint, AC joint, SC joint, bicipital groove, coracoid, scapula, clavicle [S10, p.254]

### Key Specific Tests
- **Jobe's (Empty Can) test**: Supraspinatus — shoulder at 90° abduction, 30° forward flexion, internal rotation, thumb down; resistance to downward force [S10, p.254]
- **Lift-off test**: Subscapularis — hand on back in maximal IR; fails to lift hand off against resistance [S10, p.254]
- **Belly-press / Bear-hug test**: Subscapularis — 90° elevation/IR, palm on opposite shoulder; weakness against external rotation force = positive [S10, p.254]
- **Hawkins-Kennedy test**: Impingement — 90° forward flexion, forced medial rotation; pain = subacromial impingement or rotator cuff tendinitis [S10, p.254]
- **O'Brien's (active compression) test**: SLAP and AC pathologies — 90° forward flexion, 10–15° adduction, full IR, pronated forearm; pain relieved with palm up = positive [S10, p.254]
- **Anterior slide test**: SLAP lesions — seated, hand on iliac crest, anterosuperior force on elbow; pain or clicking = positive [S10, p.254]
- **Yergason's test**: Biceps pathology — elbow 90°, slight pronation, resist supination; pain in bicipital groove = positive [S10, p.254]
- **Speed's test**: Biceps pathology — shoulder 90° forward flexion, extended elbow, supinated forearm; resist downward force; anterior shoulder pain = positive [S10, pp.254-255]
- **Neurologic evaluation**: Assess for radiculopathy, axillary nerve, suprascapular nerve, or long thoracic nerve involvement [S10, p.255]

## Common Shoulder Injuries in Basketball

### Glenohumeral Dislocation (Acute)
The shoulder is the most commonly dislocated joint in the human body during sports [S10, p.256]. Anterior-inferior dislocations predominate. The primary mechanism is capsulolabral separation from the anterior/inferior glenoid (classic Bankart's lesion) — usually from falling on an outstretched, externally rotated, abducted arm, or direct force [S10, p.256].
- Axillary neuropraxia seen in 5–35% of first-time dislocations [S10, p.256]
- **Conservative rehab**: ROM and strengthening ASAP; isokinetic exercises at 3–6 weeks; plyometric exercises at 6–8 weeks; biceps strengthening is crucial [S10, p.256]
- Return to sport when opposite shoulder ROM is matched and ≥90% strength is restored [S10, p.256]
- Recurrent instability is the major problem after anterior dislocation; surgical stabilization averages 4–6 months return-to-sport [S10, p.257]

### AC Joint Separation (Acute)
- From direct fall onto the acromion or adducted arm
- Most AC separations managed conservatively with a sling; far-displaced separations may require surgery [S10, p.257]

### Clavicle Fracture (Acute)
The clavicle is the **most commonly fractured bone around the shoulder in basketball** [S10, p.257]. Conservative via sling for minimally displaced; surgery for displaced/open fractures. Return to play after painless clavicle with radiographic union and full ROM/strength.

### Rotator Cuff Tears (Chronic)
- 40% of asymptomatic overhead throwing athletes have rotator cuff tears [S10, p.257]
- Traumatic tears uncommon in athletes under 35; in older athletes, combination of degeneration, instability, and impingement [S10, p.257]
- **Treatment by thickness**: <50% thickness = conservative; 50–75% = consider surgery; full thickness = poor prognosis even with surgery [S10, p.257]

### Internal Impingement (Chronic)
- Repetitive throwing causes anatomic adaptations; repeated excessive abduction and ER leads to impingement of posterosuperior glenoid and undersurface of rotator cuff [S10, p.258]
- Treatment usually nonoperative — reduce inflammation, rebalance scapula [S10, p.258]

### Biceps and SLAP Lesions (Chronic)
- GIRD (Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit) — increase in ER, decrease in IR at 90° abduction — is an adaptive change that can predispose to biceps, rotator cuff, and labral pathology [S10, p.258]
- 90% of throwers with symptomatic GIRD respond to posterior capsular stretching [S10, p.259]
- ~70% of athletes with SLAP lesions treated nonoperatively return to play at same level [S10, p.259]

### Scapular Dyskinesis (Chronic)
- Seen in 67–100% of athletes with shoulder injury; may also cause shoulder injury [S10, p.259]
- SICK scapula: scapular malposition and dyskinesis with inferomedial border prominence and coracoid pain [S10, p.259]
- Conservative treatment and prevention are primary objectives [S10, p.259]

## Imaging
1. **Plain radiographs** first — AP (multiple rotations), axial, scapular Y, transthoracic views (trauma series) [S10, p.255]
2. **Special views**: Stryker notch (Hill-Sachs), West Point (glenoid rim) [S10, p.255]
3. **Ultrasound**: Effective for biceps and rotator cuff; highly user-dependent [S10, p.255]
4. **MRI**: Most comprehensive for soft tissue — SLAP, capsular, and rotator cuff pathologies; MRI arthrogram has good accuracy/sensitivity [S10, p.255]
5. **CT**: Preferred for Hill-Sachs lesions, greater tuberosity, and glenoid rim fractures; CT arthrogram adds soft tissue detail [S10, p.255]

## Treatment Framework
1. **Acute phase**: Reduce inflammation and allow tissue healing — passive ROM, stabilization exercises, NSAIDs [S10, p.255]
2. **Post-acute phase**: Begin stretching and strengthening customized to weak muscles [S10, p.255]
3. **Late phase**: When pain, ROM deficit, and weakness are near-resolved — intensive strengthening [S10, p.255]
4. **Return to sport**: Team training first, then competitions consecutively [S10, p.255]
5. **Surgical consideration**: When conservative management fails to resolve pain and performance [S10, p.255–256]

## On-Field Evaluation Algorithm
1. Rule out life-threatening emergencies first — maintain basic life support [S10, p.259]
2. Identify red flag symptoms: intense pain, deformity, pulselessness, decreased sensation, discoloration [S10, p.259]
3. Assess ROM once red flags are ruled out [S10, p.260]
4. Decide: can the player remain in the game or must be removed? [S10, p.260]
5. In a secure environment (training room), perform thorough examination and determine imaging needs [S10, p.260]

**On-Field Key Points** [S10, p.260]:
- Proximal humerus or clavicle fractures: support with sling; no immediate return-to-play
- Glenohumeral dislocation: remove from field; reduction only if appropriate (X-ray first); apply sling after reduction; no immediate return-to-play
- AC separations: difficult to reduce on-field; remove from competition; sling; no immediate return-to-play

## Common Mistakes
1. **Ignoring posterior shoulder pain as instability** → consider posterior instability whenever posterior shoulder pain loads the joint
2. **Attempting field reduction without prior X-ray** → risk enlarging Hill-Sachs or Bankart lesions; always X-ray first unless life-threatening delay [S10, p.256]
3. **Skipping neurologic evaluation** → axillary neuropraxia in 5–35% of first-time dislocations; must be documented [S10, p.256]
4. **Under-appreciating GIRD** → a ≥25° difference in IR between shoulders may predispose to SLAP tears; posterior capsular stretching should be prescribed [S10, p.258]
5. **Neglecting preseason conditioning** → preseason conditioning programs are effective in reducing shoulder injuries [S10, p.260]
