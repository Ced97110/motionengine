# Back Screen Reads — the source coach

## Summary
The back screen (also called a blind screen) comes from behind the defender so the defender cannot see its arrival. Because of this blind-side approach, the back screen creates a lob opportunity when the defender runs into it — making it one of the most dangerous actions in basketball. the source coach outlines five specific reads based on the defender's reaction. [S7, pp.54-55]

## When to Use
- When a teammate's defender has turned to watch the ball or lost sight of the screener
- In transition from weak-side actions where a post player can set a blind screen for a cutting guard
- In designed plays where the screener's position is hidden before the cutter initiates

## Formation
Three players involved: ball handler (1), screener (5) coming from behind the defender, screened player/cutter (2) whose defender will be blindsided.

## Phases

### Phase 1: Setting the Screen
- Screener (5) approaches from behind the defender of (2) at 90° angle.
- Wide, low stance. Set screen so the defender cannot see it arriving.

### Phase 2: The Five Reads

**Read 1 — Defender Runs Into Screen (Lob)** [S7, p.54]
- Defender fails to avoid the screen and runs into it.
- **Cutter (2)**: Receive a lob pass from ball handler (1), cutting to the basket.
- **Screener (5)**: Rolls to the ball after setting the screen.

**Read 2 — Defender Slides High** [S7, p.54]
- Defender anticipates the cut direction and goes high over the screen to stay with the cutter.
- **Cutter (2)**: Cuts **low** under the screen to the basket.
- **Screener (5)**: Pops out (opposite direction) as a safety release.

**Read 3 — Defender Slides Low** [S7, pp.54-55]
- Defender anticipates and slides low to prevent the cut to the basket.
- **Cutter (2)**: Cuts **over the top** of the screen to the basket.
- **Screener (5)**: Pops out.

**Read 4 — Defender Anticipates the Cut** [S7, p.55]
- Defender reads the whole play early enough to avoid the screen entirely.
- **Cutter (2)**: Bumps and pops out **away** from the screen.
- **Screener (5)**: Rolls to the basket as the primary receiver.

**Read 5 — Ball Handler Cannot Pass Directly to Cutter** [S7, p.55]
- The defense takes away the direct pass to the cutting (2).
- **Screener (5)**: Pops out, receives the ball from (1).
- **Screener (5)**: Passes to the cutter (2) who has posted up after bumping and popping.

## Key Coaching Points
- The timing is critical — the cutter (2) must set up the defender and not move before the screener (5) is in position.
- The cutter cuts as close to the screener as possible — brushing the screener's shoulder — to prevent the defender from slipping through.
- The screener and cutter must read each other's movement and both read the defenders simultaneously.
- The screener's default is to **go opposite** the cutter's direction (unless a slip to the basket is open).
- Always remember the lob — it is the primary option when the back screen is executed correctly, because defenders running into blind screens cannot avoid it. [S7, p.54]

## Counters
- If the defense consistently anticipates (Read 4): set up the back screen with a fakes or dribble hand-off to re-draw the defender's attention before the screen.
- If the defense switches: look for the screener (5) posting up a smaller guard on a mismatch.
