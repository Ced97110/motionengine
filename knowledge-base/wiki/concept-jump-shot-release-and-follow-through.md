# Jump Shot Release and Follow-Through

## Summary
The release point and follow-through are the final, critical links in the jump shot chain. Releasing the ball at the correct moment — just before the top of the jump — maximizes power and arc. Freezing the follow-through until the ball passes through the net locks in focus, reinforces balance, and builds consistent mechanics that carry directly from practice into games.

Every detail matters: where the elbow finishes, what the balance hand does, and whether the shooter "shoots and stays" all affect shot quality, shot arc, and offensive rebounding position.

## When to Use
- Applies to every jump shot: midrange catch-and-shoot, pull-up, and three-point shots
- Especially important to emphasize in early-stage skill development
- Revisit when a player becomes streaky or shoots flat-trajectory line drives

## Key Principles
1. **Release just before the top of the jump.** Waiting until the peak causes downward momentum and the ball falls short. "Release right before the top — don't wait."
2. **Elbow finishes above the eyebrow, in front of the face.** Not beside the ear (too much arc), and not below the eyebrow (flat shot, rattled makes). "Elbow above the eyebrow, hand in the hoop."
3. **Shooting hand finishes in a straight line to the target.** The hand should point directly at the center of the basket — "hand in the hoop."
4. **Freeze the follow-through until the ball hits the floor (practice) or passes through the net (game).** Dropping hands early in practice equals dropping hands faster in games. "If your hands are moving, your feet are moving."
5. **Balance hand stays still.** Fingertips point toward the ceiling at roughly 45 degrees on the non-shooting side. No twist, no drop. Dropping the balance hand causes misses short and pulls the shot line off.
6. **Shoot and stay on jump shots from 15 feet and beyond.** The longer the shot, the longer the rebound. Staying in place allows tracking long rebounds or acting as an outlet; it also puts the player in proper defensive transition position. "Shoot, stay, then react."
7. **Body does not turn or twist in the air.** Any rotation pulls the shot line off alignment from the start.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: These mechanics apply universally. Every player who shoots jump shots must own these fundamentals.

## Variations
### Midrange (15 feet and in)
Shoot and stay; freeze follow-through until ball hits the floor during practice reps.

### Long-Range / Three-Point
Freeze follow-through until the ball passes through the net. The longer shot demands the same frozen follow-through — dropping it signals the shooter wasn't fully committed to the swish. "Think swish — shoot and stay."

## Common Mistakes
1. **Shooting hand finishes by the ear** → Too much arc; the shooter must finish with the elbow in front of the face, above the eyebrow.
2. **Elbow finishes below the eyebrow** → Flat, line-drive shot; shots clang off the front or back of the rim. Even makes rattle in. Cue: "Lift, don't push or sling."
3. **Dropping the balance hand** → Causes misses short and disrupts shot line via negative motion; balance hand fingertips stay pointed up.
4. **Releasing at the very top of the jump** → Downward momentum causes short shots. Release just before the peak.
5. **Following your own shot on a mid-range or long jumper** → Misses rebound long; stay in place and react after the ball leaves your hand.
