# Knee Tendinopathies and Tendon Tears in Basketball

## Summary
Tendinopathies are among the most common overuse injuries in basketball, primarily affecting the extensor mechanism (patellar and quadriceps tendons). Patellar tendinopathy — historically called "jumper's knee" — is prevalent in up to 31.9% of elite basketball players. These injuries cause significant time away from sport and require early identification and treatment. Tendon ruptures (patellar or quadriceps) represent the most severe end of this spectrum and require mandatory surgical repair with RTS rarely before 6 months [S10, pp.346-348].

## When to Use
- Evaluating any basketball player with anterior knee pain, particularly around the patellar tendon
- Identifying at-risk youth athletes (adolescent Osgood–Schlatter)
- Making surgical vs. conservative decisions for tendinopathy and tendon rupture

## Key Principles
1. Patellar tendinopathy is prevalent in up to 31.9% of elite basketball players — the second highest prevalence among jumping sports after volleyball (44.6%) [S10, p.347]
2. Tendinopathies involve excessive tensile loading and subsequent tendon breakdown — some may paradoxically be "underuse" lesions [S10, p.346]
3. Complete tendon rupture requires mandatory surgical repair — return to sport rarely before 6 months [S10, p.348]
4. Osgood–Schlatter syndrome in adolescents is self-limited — conservative treatment appropriate in the vast majority [S10, p.347]
5. Early diagnosis and treatment are critical — overuse injuries result in significant time away from sport [S10, p.348]

## Injury Types

### Patellar Tendinopathy (Jumper's Knee)
- **Prevalence**: up to 31.9% of elite basketball players; 11.8% in non-elite basketball players; 7% of high school players (11% men, 2% women) [S10, pp.346-347]
- **Mechanism**: repetitive high-stress loading of the patellar tendon — jumping, landing, acceleration, deceleration
- **Symptoms**: anterior knee pain at inferior patellar pole, worse with loading activities
- **Management**: conservative rehabilitation (eccentric loading program); see [[jumpers-knee-rehabilitation]]

### Quadriceps Tendinopathy
- Often grouped with patellar tendinopathy as "jumper's knee"
- More common in older players than patellar tendinopathy
- Similar mechanism: excessive tensile loading of the quadriceps tendon at proximal patellar insertion

### Osgood–Schlatter Syndrome (Adolescents)
- **Population**: boys aged 12–15 years; girls aged 8–12 years
- **Mechanism**: traction apophysitis of tibial tubercle — repetitive strain on secondary ossification center of tibial tuberosity [S10, p.347]
- **Prognosis**: self-limited; symptoms resolve with closure of physeal plate; residual deformity may remain [S10, p.347]
- **Management**: conservative in vast majority of cases

### Patellar Tendon Rupture
- **Mechanism**: rapid loading in an already-tensed tendon; may be ultimate consequence of chronic patellar tendinopathy
- **Clinical signs**: complete loss of function, severe effusion and ecchymosis, palpable gap between patella and tibial tuberosity, proximal patella position, inability to actively extend knee [S10, p.347]
- **Diagnosis**: clinical exam + history; ultrasound or MRI pre-surgery to confirm and plan
- Ask about prior corticosteroid injections
- **X-ray**: shows high-riding patella (patella alta)
- **Treatment**: mandatory surgical repair (suture + transosseous fixation); immobilize in full extension 6 weeks postoperatively; RTS rarely before 6 months [S10, p.348]

### Quadriceps Tendon Rupture
- More common in older basketball players
- Similar clinical presentation as patellar tendon rupture but palpable gap is proximal to the patella
- Caution: partial tears may present with intact extensor mechanism but reduced power — do not miss partial tears
- **X-ray**: shows patella baja (low-riding patella)
- **Imaging**: ultrasound or MRI to confirm and plan
- **Treatment**: mandatory surgical repair for complete tears; immobilize in full extension 6 weeks; RTS rarely before 6 months [S10, p.348]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: monitor anterior knee pain during pre-practice screening — early reporting prevents progression from tendinopathy to rupture
- **Coaches**: adjust training load when players report patellar region pain; integrate tendinopathy prevention in warm-up programs

## Common Mistakes
1. Missing partial quadriceps tendon tears — extensor mechanism still functions but with reduced power → assess strength carefully [S10, p.347]
2. Not inquiring about prior corticosteroid injections when diagnosing patellar tendon rupture — injections weaken tendon tissue [S10, p.347]
3. Returning players too early after tendon rupture — RTS before 6 months is rarely safe [S10, p.348]
4. Not distinguishing Osgood–Schlatter from patellar tendinopathy in adolescents — Osgood–Schlatter is at the tibial tuberosity, not the patellar tendon itself [S10, p.347]
