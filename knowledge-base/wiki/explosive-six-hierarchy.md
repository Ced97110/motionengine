# The Explosive Six Hierarchy

## Summary
The "Explosive Six" is the core movement system in *Explosive Calisthenics* — six bodyweight movement chains organized into two tiers: **power foundations** and **skill-agility expressions**. Not all six movements are equal; they must be developed in the correct order.

The two foundational power moves — **jumps** and **power push-ups** — must be trained first and consistently maintained throughout an athlete's career. Only after building adequate power through these basics can an athlete safely and effectively develop the three skill-agility movements: **kip-ups**, **front flips**, and **back flips**. The **muscle-up** sits in the middle of the spectrum [S9, pp.1-2, p.11].

## When to Use
- Athletes beginning explosive training should focus exclusively on jumps and power push-ups until they have a solid power base
- Skill movements (kip-ups, flips) should only be attempted when the athlete has adequate leg spring, midsection snap, and shoulder/arm power from the foundation work
- Basketball players can use the hierarchy to prioritize: vertical jump development before complex agility footwork patterns [S9, p.3]

## Key Principles
1. **Power first, skill second** — "You build it before you attempt the skill-agility work, by hard, consistent training on jumps and power pushups." [S9, p.3]
2. **Raw power requirements for skill moves**: leg spring to launch the body, midsection snap to tuck, shoulder/arm power for momentum and floor pushes [S9, p.3]
3. **Linear improvement beats progression-racing** — Don't rush to the "Master Step" and declare victory. Keep building power on the same exercises for your entire career. [S9, p.3]
4. **Power moves = fuel tank** — "Think of the basic power moves as your fuel tank. The more consistently you train with 'em, the bigger and more turbocharged your vehicle." [S9, p.3]
5. **Injury bulletproofing requires the foundation** — Athletes who skip the power foundation and jump straight to acrobatics suffer twisted knees, bad hips, sprained ankles. Progressive power training conditions bones and soft tissues to handle explosive forces. [S9, p.4]
6. **Muscle-ups occupy a middle position** — They are partly power, partly skill, suitable for athletes after the basic power foundation is established. [S9, p.11]

## Player Responsibilities
The hierarchy translates to basketball training priorities:
- **All positions**: Build vertical jump (power) before adding complex directional agility (skill)
- **All positions**: Build explosive push-up strength before adding complex upper-body skill movements
- **Coaches**: Do not introduce complex agility ladder patterns to athletes who lack the base power to execute them at speed

## Variations
### Basketball-Specific Translation
| Explosive Six | Basketball Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Jumps (power) | Vertical jump, reactive power, second-jump ability |
| Power Push-Ups (power) | Post contact strength, defensive hand-fighting, passing power |
| Kip-Ups (skill) | Complex floor-recovery movements |
| Flips (skill) | Advanced directional agility, acrobatic finishing |
| Muscle-Ups (middle) | Bar and ring pull-to-push transitions |

## Common Mistakes
1. **Attempting skill movements without the power base** → injury risk; the bones and soft tissues are not conditioned to handle forces [S9, p.4]
2. **"Beating" a chain by reaching the Master Step once** → mastery requires years of linear improvement, not one successful attempt [S9, p.3]
3. **Neglecting power maintenance once skill work begins** → power base must be continually fueled or skill performance degrades [S9, p.3]
