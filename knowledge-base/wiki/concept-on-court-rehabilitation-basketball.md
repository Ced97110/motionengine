# On-Court Rehabilitation — From Treatment Table to Return to Performance

## Summary
Rehabilitation for basketball players is not complete when an athlete returns to play — it is complete when the athlete returns to their **pre-injury level of performance**. This framework, developed by NBA/WNBA physical therapists (Brooklyn Nets, LA Clippers, Philadelphia 76ers), integrates a basketball and basketball-specific movements into every phase of rehabilitation, from the treatment table through return to full game performance. It emphasizes key performance indicators (KPIs), interdepartmental collaboration, progressive on-court loading, and motor learning principles to bridge the gap between playing again and performing at an elite level [S10, Ch.60].

## When to Use
- Following any lower extremity injury (ankle sprain, ACL, knee, hip, hamstring)
- Whenever a basketball player begins rehabilitation — even in the acute phase
- When planning return-to-participation and return-to-performance timelines
- When designing practice-based return-to-sport progressions

## Key Principles
1. **Return to performance ≠ return to play** — A point guard averaging 25 pts pre-injury but 10 pts post-return has returned to play but not to performance [S10, p.766]
2. **KPIs must be defined upfront** — Both physical (peak speed, accelerations, jumps) and basketball (points, rebounds, assists, blocks) KPIs should be established at the start of rehabilitation [S10, p.766]
3. **KPI deficits require interdisciplinary analysis** — A deficit in a basketball KPI may have a rehabilitation cause (fear, weakness) or a basketball cause (tactical) — both must be evaluated [S10, p.766]
4. **Early basketball integration** — A basketball can be incorporated even when non-weight-bearing (seated shooting, ball handling, core work with ball) [S10, p.767]
5. **Progressive complexity** — Variables progressed sequentially: stationary → moving, linear → lateral → rotational, bilateral → unilateral, simple → cognitively loaded [S10, pp.767–769]
6. **Working backwards** — Start with the end skill and deconstruct it into trainable components, then build back up [S10, p.769]
7. **Scripting bridges practice and games** — Go through offensive/defensive plays at controlled intensity before live scrimmage [S10, p.771]
8. **Psychological integration** — Involve injured athletes in team activities (film, scouting reports, rebounding at practice) to maintain motivation and cortical motor mapping [S10, p.766]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG**: KPIs = points, assists; key movement = driving both left and right equally; high cognitive load during return
- **SG/SF**: KPIs = points, defensive steals; key movements = cutting, contested finishing, defensive closeouts
- **PF/C**: KPIs = rebounds, blocks; key movements = jumping, post footwork, contact stability

## Rehabilitation Planning Framework

### Three Plan Components
1. **Objective milestones** — clinical criteria demarcating phase transitions
2. **Return-to-sport criteria** — athlete-tailored functional standards
3. **Return-to-performance KPIs** — quantifiable physical and basketball metrics defining pre-injury levels [S10, p.765]

### Position-Specific KPI Examples
- PG: points, assists, equal driving left and right
- Center: rebounds, blocks, post movements
- Physical: peak speed, acceleration/deceleration counts, total distance, jump count [S10, p.766]

## Phase 1: Acute Phase of Rehabilitation

### Goals
- Address impairments, restore basic ADL function
- Maintain general strength and conditioning
- Integrate basketball early for psychological well-being [S10, p.766]

### Basketball Integration (Non/Partial Weight-Bearing)
- Shoot from a chair or scooter — focus on form and short distances
- Seated ball handling, static passing drills
- Core exercises with ball (Russian twists, overhead throws)
- Hip strengthening (isometric adduction, ball-as-prop perturbations)
- Free throw form shooting while icing or elevating [S10, p.767]

### Balance and Proprioception Progression
- Single-leg balance on stable surface → foam pad → compliant surface
- Add basketball: hold → throw/catch → bounce → dribble → dribble patterns
- Dribbling patterns: in-and-out, forward/backward, pounding, alternating rhythms [S10, p.767]

### Stationary Dribbling (Once Weight-Bearing)
- One hand, pounding, crossover
- Pattern progressions: crossover→crossover; crossover→between legs; crossover→behind back
- Cycle starting move: crossover first, then between legs first, then behind back first [S10, p.767]

### Shooting Progression (Partial to Full Weight-Bearing)
1. Form shooting, feet flat
2. Rising on toes (feet stay on ground)
3. Small stationary jump shots (1–2 cm), progressing height and distance
4. Stepping into shots — linear first, then multidirectional based on injury type
   - Ankle sprain: forward/backward before lateral
   - Hamstring: lateral first, then forward/backward [S10, p.768]

### Monitoring
- Soreness criterion, stiffness, pain, swelling
- Significant increases require activity adjustment [S10, p.769]

## Phase 2: Subacute Phase — Return to Participation

### Exercise Progression
- Strength focus shifts: stability → endurance → power
- Neuromuscular control: integrate sport-specific movement patterns, single-leg and multiplanar tasks [S10, p.769]

### Working Backwards Method (Table 60.1)
| Skill | Baseline Components | Methods to Progress | High-Level Adaptations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cutting | Multidirectional stepping | Submaximal 45/90/180° cutting; curling | Increase speed and intensity; Add defender |
| Defensive slides | Controlled side stepping | Resistance with band/cables; anticipated COD | Unanticipated COD; Add offensive player |
| Jumping | Double and single-leg hop | Hopping patterns (multiplanar, over obstacles) | Perturbations; Cognitive load (catch ball) |

[S10, p.770]

### Cutting and Pivoting Progressions
- Train variety of cutting angles: V-cut, L-cut, flare, curl
- V-cut: from 3PT line → run toward basket → acute angle cut back out
- L-cut: from paint → 90° cut toward 3PT line
- Flare: cut around screen away from basket toward perimeter
- Curl: cut around screen toward basket [S10, p.770]

### Jumping Progression
1. Double-leg takeoff / double-leg landing
2. Double-leg takeoff / single-leg landing
3. Hop-scotch (alternating double and single leg)
4. Single-leg takeoff / single-leg landing [S10, p.770]

### Ball Handling Progression (Table 60.2)
1. Stationary ball handling
2. Ball handling with walking
3. Ball handling with jogging
4. Ball handling with defensive player [S10, p.771]

### Shooting Progression (Table 60.3)
1. Close range form shooting (feet flat, remain on ground)
2. Close range form shooting (rising on toes, feet remain on ground)
3. Close range spot shooting (small jump)
4. Long range spot shooting
5. Stepping into shot (linear)
6. Stepping into shot (multidirectional)
7. Movement into shot (shake up, drift, dribble hand-off, etc.)
8. Layups / finishing uncontested
9. Finishing with pads or blocks in path to basket
10. Finishing with light defense [S10, p.771]

### Defensive Progression (Table 60.4)
1. Side step at slow speed (control, weight shift, squat form)
2. Diagonal reverse side step at slow speed
3. Diagonal reverse side step at slightly faster speed
4. Diagonal reverse side step as if defending (with/without directional cues)
5. Defending an offensive player at slow speed
6. Defending an offensive player at faster speeds
7. Accelerate to position of offensive player, then defend [S10, p.772]

### Cardiovascular Fitness
- Off-court: biking, swimming, arm-biking, rowing, battle ropes, boxing, VersaClimber
- On-court: full court transitions, acceleration/deceleration, cutting while running, curved line running
- Conditioning drill examples:
  - Run diagonally at speed across court → shoot 3PT → repeat 4×; stop when 3 of 4 made
  - Accelerate from under basket to corner → defend coach → sprint to opposite corner → defend [S10, p.770]

### Return to Practice: Scripting Progression (Fig. 60.4)
1. Scripting with coaches at jogging pace (½ court)
2. Scripting with coaches at running pace (½ court)
3. Scripting with coaches at running pace (full court)
4. Scripting with team (½ court)
5. Scripting with team (full court)
6. 1v1 against coaches (gradually increasing intensity and contact)
7. 1v1 against player
8. 3v3 with coaches (½ court → full court)
9. 3v3 with players
10. 5v5 with players (½ court → full court)
11. Return to game play with lower-level team
12. Return to game play with team [S10, p.773]

**Scrimmage workload notes:** Full court 3v3 and 5v5 = highest external workload; Half-court 2v2 and 5v5 = highest accelerations per minute [S10, p.771]

## Phase 3: Return to Play → Return to Performance

### Three Methods to Add Pressure

**1. Decision-Making**
- Visual or verbal cues → react to opponent movements
- Start: cue given early (free throw line) → progress to late cue (charge circle)
- With a player: offensive = read to receive pass or roll off pick; defensive = react to opponent movements [S10, p.774]

**2. Cognitive Load**
- Simple: counting, reading while exercising
- Complex: commentating a video, I spy, 20 questions
- Mental fatigue training: rehab after tests, studying film, end of school day [S10, p.775]

**3. Perturbations**
- Auditory: music, unexpected noises, crowd sounds
- Visual: occlude eyesight, limit visual field, peripheral visual information, hands passed in athlete's field
- Kinesthetic: foam pads, BOSU balls, manual resistance, opposition contact (pads → player contact) [S10, p.775]

### Pressure Application Examples (Table 60.5)
| Exercise | Decision-Making | Cognitive Load | Perturbation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single-leg squat | Visual cue to squat; anticipate when watched player shoots | Tell a story; Play I spy | Manual perturbation; Peripheral objects |
| Free throw | Tennis ball dual-task; Scenario-based shooting | Watch/commentate another game; Tongue-twisters | Background noise; Blurred glasses |
| Layup | Verbal cue for side; React to defender | Solve math; Recite scouting report | Gently touching → full contact; Change surface |
| Defensive closeout | Eyes-closed start; Read shoot-or-drive | Third-person commentating; Count backwards | Push off with opponent; Peripheral blinders |

[S10, p.774]

## Motor Learning Principles for Secondary Prevention

Four principles (Gokeler et al.) to change high-risk movement patterns [S10, pp.775–776]:

1. **External focus of attention** — Use environment-focused cues rather than body-focused cues. Example: "Push the ground behind you" (external) vs. "Fire your glutes" (internal)

2. **Implicit learning** — Use analogies so athletes internalize the movement unconsciously. Example: "Build a stable column with your shoulder, hip, knee, and foot"

3. **Differential learning** — Experiment with multiple movement patterns to achieve the same goal. Example: pick up ball 10 different ways; single-leg squat on ground, foam pad, with perturbations, with/without basketball

4. **Self-controlled learning and contextual interference** — Give athlete control over exercise selection, duration, or feedback timing; use variable practice (high contextual interference) for better retention and transfer even if initial acquisition is slower [S10, pp.775–776]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Declaring "returned" at first game back** → Rehabilitation continues until KPIs reach pre-injury baseline [S10, p.766]
2. **Isolating the athlete from team activities** → Involve them in film, scouting, practice observation — maintains cortical motor mapping and motivation [S10, p.766]
3. **Linear progression only** → Introduce lateral and rotational planes before return to sport [S10, p.769]
4. **Using only internal coaching cues** → External cues promote better motor learning and automaticity [S10, p.775]
5. **Jumping straight to 5v5** → Use scripting progression — jogging pace halfcourt through full-court live play [S10, p.771]
6. **Ignoring cognitive and perturbation loading** → Game speed requires automatic movement; explicit attention-demanding rehab does not transfer well [S10, p.775]
