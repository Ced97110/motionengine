# Rebounding Areas: Primary and Secondary

## Summary
Effective rebounding requires players to understand that missed shots follow predictable carom patterns. The source distinguishes two rebounding zones — primary and secondary — and teaches players to position themselves in one of these areas before every shot attempt. A complete team rebounding system assigns two players to each zone on every shot. [S15, pp.177-179]

## When to Use
- On every shot attempt, both offensive and defensive players must immediately identify and move to their assigned rebounding area
- Teach as part of any shooting drill so rebounding becomes automatic
- Critical in transition: identify rebounding responsibility before the shot goes up

## Key Principles
1. **Primary rebounding area**: the area directly around the basket (paint area) — where the majority of missed shots carom; higher arc = higher ricochet but not longer carom
2. **Secondary rebounding area**: the area back toward the shooter — where shots that come up short most often land; the lesser the arc, the longer the carom
3. **Arc rule**: higher arc → ball bounces higher (more vertical) but not farther; lower arc → ball caroms longer (more horizontal) and farther from the basket
4. **Two-and-two coverage**: on every shot, two players attack the primary area and two players cover the secondary area
5. **No lazy rebounding**: every player must move to their assigned area immediately on every shot — never watch the ball passively

## Player Responsibilities
- **PF/C**: primary responsibility for the primary rebounding area — position closest to the basket
- **SF/SG**: secondary responsibility for the secondary area — cut off the long carom
- **PG**: defensive balance OR secondary rebounding area depending on transition risk

## The Blast-Out Technique
After securing a missed shot, the rebounder executes the following sequence to transition immediately to offense:
1. Bring the ball low in both hands, as near the floor as possible
2. Simultaneously swing the ball out in front and execute a long crossover step
3. Immediately toss the ball slightly out in front, go get it, and dribble down the floor until passing off

The blast-out protects the ball from defenders reaching in while simultaneously initiating transition offense in one fluid movement. [S15, p.177]

## Common Mistakes
1. **All players crashing the paint** → leave two players in the secondary area to handle short misses and long caroms
2. **Standing and watching the arc** → move immediately on the shot; position before the ball lands
3. **Catching the ball high** → secure the rebound at the highest point, then bring it LOW and swing out; do not hold it up for defenders to strip
4. **Forgetting to blast out** → players who stand after a rebound give the defense time to set up; blast out immediately
