# Basketball Court Types: Surface Science and Injury Implications

## Summary
The playing surface is one of the most important environmental factors affecting basketball player performance and injury risk. Since Dr. James Naismith invented the game in 1892, hard rock maple hardwood has emerged as the gold standard court surface. Research on alternative surfaces (polyurethane, outdoor materials) reveals trade-offs in shock absorption, joint impact, and ball bounce consistency. Court maintenance is a legal and medical obligation — poorly maintained surfaces directly increase injury risk. [S10, pp.947-950]

## When to Use
- Evaluating facility equipment decisions at high school, college, or professional level
- Advising players with lower extremity stress injuries about surface-related risk
- Assessing injury risk in recreational or outdoor environments using non-standard surfaces

## Key Principles

### Maple Hardwood (The Gold Standard)
1. Hard rock maple (Acer saccharum) has been the dominant NBA and NCAA surface since basketball's invention — 29 of 30 NBA teams play on maple [S10, p.949]
2. The Janka Hardness Scale ranks maple (1450 lbs pressure to mar) above white oak (1360), red oak (1290), black walnut (1010), and cherry (995) [S10, p.948]
3. Maple's tight grain makes it less susceptible to expansion/contraction with humidity and temperature changes, and resistant to splintering [S10, p.949]
4. Maple provides shock-absorbing qualities that minimize fatigue, joint damage, and potential injury — though research studies confirming these benefits are limited [S10, p.949]
5. Light color creates high contrast with the orange basketball, aiding visibility [S10, p.949]
6. The NBA requires teams to replace their floor every 10 years [S10, p.950]
7. Standard NBA/NCAA court dimensions: 94 × 50 feet; most courts use a 140 × 70 ft perimeter [S10, p.948]
8. A single hardwood court requires 80–100 trees and maple planks thicker than ¾ inch [S10, p.948]

### Polyurethane (Synthetic Alternative)
1. Rubberized polyurethane flooring was popular in the 1970s NCAA and used by the Houston Rockets from 1971–1975 [S10, p.948]
2. Harder surface is associated with higher bone mineral density in growing girls playing basketball [S10, p.948]
3. However, polyurethane is more unforgiving on knees and elbows, contributing to its decline in elite-level use [S10, p.948]
4. **No synthetic polyurethane is currently used in the NCAA or NBA** — but many high school and recreational gymnasiums still use it, which affects injury patterns at those levels [S10, p.948]

### Court Maintenance Requirements
1. Floors should be wet-mopped every evening and dry-mopped between each activity [S10, p.950]
2. Basketball courts should be buffed, screened, and recoated yearly [S10, p.950]
3. Courts used for non-sport activities may require more frequent maintenance [S10, p.950]
4. Poorly maintained or uneven surfaces directly increase athlete injury risk [S10, p.950]

### Mobility of Modular Courts
1. Modern maple courts can be delivered in sections and assembled quickly, enabling unusual venues (aircraft carriers, stadiums, outdoor arenas) [S10, p.949]
2. Outdoor play introduces risks: debris, obstructions, and dampness are dangerous to athletes — the WNBA's Arthur Ashe Stadium game (2008) was disrupted by weather [S10, p.949]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Neglecting maintenance schedules** → Follow annual buffing/screening/recoating schedule; daily mop protocol must be enforced [S10, p.950]
2. **Ignoring high school surface differences** → Many high school courts use polyurethane, which increases knee and elbow injury risk compared to maple [S10, p.948]
3. **Assuming outdoor courts are safe alternatives** → Outdoor polyurethane surfaces introduce debris and dampness risks not present indoors [S10, p.949]
