# WNBA Individual Offensive Efficiency

## Summary
The same Individual Offensive Rating framework used for NBA analysis applies directly to the WNBA. Applying it to the league's first five seasons (1997-2002) reveals that the players marketed as stars (Leslie, Swoopes, Lobo) were not necessarily the most efficient, and that the true offensive dominants were initially less-promoted players — most notably Cynthia Cooper.

Cooper's dominance of the early WNBA is statistically extraordinary: she generated **27 points per 100 possessions more than the league average** in 1997 — a margin that "dwarfs anything Jordan ever did in the NBA" [S16, p.177]. This reflects both Cooper's excellence and the early WNBA's wide talent distribution, but the analytical lesson is transferable: marketing prominence ≠ measurable offensive impact.

## WNBA League-Wide Efficiency Trends (1997-2001)
The WNBA has shown a clear trend toward lower pace and higher efficiency over time:

| Season | Poss/Game | Floor% | Off. Rtg. |
|--------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| 1997 | 75.0 | 0.439 | 92.3 |
| 1998 | 74.2 | 0.451 | 94.8 |
| 1999 | 71.4 | 0.456 | 97.1 |
| 2000 | 70.3 | 0.462 | 98.2 |
| 2001 | 68.4 | 0.449 | 95.9 |

The 2001 decline in efficiency likely reflects Cooper's retirement — parallel to the NBA efficiency decline when Jordan left [S16, p.177].

## Key Player Profiles

### Cynthia Cooper (Houston Comets)
- Off. Rtg. **119** in 1997, 1998, and 1999 — consistently 27 points above the 1997 league average of 92.3
- Floor%: 0.50-0.53; % team possessions: 26-30%
- "The Michael Jordan of the WNBA" — the biggest reason the Houston Comets were unbeatable in their dynasty years
- The gap between her rating and league average exceeds anything Jordan achieved relative to the NBA [S16, pp.176-177]

### Lisa Leslie (Los Angeles Sparks)
- Did not live up to early hype; improved steadily to Off. Rtg. **108** (12 points over 2001 league average of 95.9) in her MVP season
- Floor%: 0.46-0.52; % team possessions: 26-29%
- The Sparks' best offensive rating (107.1) came in her MVP 2001 season [S16, p.177]

### Sheryl Swoopes (Houston Comets)
- Returned from pregnancy to become a star; best season 2000 (Off. Rtg. 115, rightful MVP)
- 2002 season reflects efficiency decline when asked to carry more offensive load (30% usage, 108 rating) and/or age (34) [S16, p.178]

### Yolanda Griffith (Sacramento Monarchs)
- Off. Rtg. 115-117 across all four seasons measured; Floor% 0.56-0.60 — the most consistent floor % in the sample
- Won 1999 WNBA MVP as a rookie; offensive numbers "approach Cooper's" but don't quite match [S16, p.178]

### Crystal Robinson (New York Liberty)
- Elite role player; career-high Off. Rtg. **122** (2001) — "as high as you will find among players who played regularly"
- Classic three-point specialist role: 16-19% usage, high efficiency [S16, p.179]

### Latasha Byears (Sacramento/LA)
- Demonstrates the usage-efficiency tradeoff: early career high usage + low ratings (97-95); later career reduced to 16-18% usage → ratings improved to 118-125
- "By cutting down how often she tried to score — taking more smart shots and not trying to create as much — she improved her scoring efficiency" [S16, p.179]

### Chamique Holdsclaw (Washington)
- High usage (27-32%), below-average efficiency (91-110) — the archetypal "bad shooter firing too much" pattern
- Started career more like Stackhouse than Jordan; efficiency improving by 2002 (110 rating) but at very high usage (32%) [S16, p.179]

## Key Principles
1. **Marketing ≠ Measurable Value.** The WNBA's most promoted players (Leslie, Swoopes, Lobo) were not its best offensive contributors in Year 1.
2. **The best player dominates relative to league average.** Cooper's +27 gap over league average is a stronger dominance signal than Jordan's analogous gap in the NBA.
3. **Role clarity improves efficiency.** Byears improved dramatically by accepting a smaller role with smart shot selection.
4. **As leagues mature, they become harder to dominate.** The WNBA efficiency gap between stars and the league narrowed as the league developed.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Evaluating WNBA players by name recognition** → Cooper was underpromoted; the numbers identified her as the true star early.
2. **Giving high-usage players excessive credit** → Holdsclaw's 16+ points/game masked below-average offensive efficiency for most of her early career.
