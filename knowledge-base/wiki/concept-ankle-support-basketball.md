# Ankle Support in Basketball (Bracing & Taping)

## Summary
Over 50% of basketball players wear ankle joint support during play. Players who wear external ankle support sustain ankle sprains at a significantly lower rate than those who do not, with the protective effect especially pronounced in athletes with a prior history of inversion ankle injury. External support systems work through several biomechanical mechanisms: reducing inversion range of motion (both non-weight-bearing and weight-bearing), increasing muscle activation and excitability, and decreasing joint velocity during movement.

## When to Use
- For all athletes with a prior history of lateral ankle sprain (highest-priority group)
- As a preventive measure for all positions, particularly backcourt players who perform more cutting and directional change
- During return-to-play progression after ankle sprain rehabilitation
- Throughout an entire season once an athlete has sustained a first ankle sprain

## Key Principles
1. **>50% adoption rate** — the majority of basketball players already wear ankle support; those who don't are at measurably higher risk [S10, p.42]
2. **Reduce inversion ROM** — both braces and tape reduce the range of motion that leads to lateral ankle ligament failure [S10, p.42]
3. **Increase peroneal excitability** — ankle support increases muscle activation, providing a more rapid protective neuromuscular response to sudden inversion events [S10, p.42]
4. **Decrease joint velocity** — support slows joint motion during rapid direction changes, reducing peak force at the ATFL, CFL, and PTFL [S10, p.42]
5. **Prior injury = highest priority** — athletes with a history of inversion ankle injury receive the greatest risk reduction from external support [S10, p.42]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Apply brace or tape before every practice and game — do not skip on low-intensity days
- **PG/SG**: Highest dribbling and direction-change load; ankle support is especially important for backcourt players
- **PF/C**: Elevated ground contact forces from boxing out and rebounding make ankle support important for frontcourt players too

## Variations
### Lace-Up Brace
Stiff shell with lace fastening; most commonly used for maximum inversion restriction. Can be reapplied by athlete between periods.

### Semi-Rigid Brace
Plastic stirrup design; provides good medial/lateral stability with less restriction to plantar/dorsiflexion compared to lace-up.

### Athletic Taping
Applied by athletic trainer; effective but requires professional application and loses effectiveness as game progresses due to sweat and movement. 

## Common Mistakes
1. **Stopping support after symptoms resolve** → Continue wearing brace/tape through the full season after any ankle sprain — recurrence risk remains elevated for months
2. **Using support only for games, not practice** → Ankle sprains occur in practice too; consistent use across all court activities is required
3. **Fit issues** — An improperly fitted brace may restrict plantar/dorsiflexion excessively, altering running mechanics and potentially shifting stress to the Achilles tendon [S10, p.42]
