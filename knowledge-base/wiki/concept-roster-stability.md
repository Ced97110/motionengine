# Roster Stability — Impact on Team Performance

## Summary
Roster stability is a measure of how much a team's playing-time composition is preserved from one season to the next. It is calculated by comparing the **average number of minutes played by the same people** across two consecutive seasons, weighted by floor time [S16, pp.57-59].

A roster stability of **80%** implies that one full floor position (20% of playing time) was completely replaced. The worst offensive and defensive teams in NBA history almost universally had roster stability **under 75%** — meaning 2–3 floor positions were completely replaced — plus new head coaches in their first or second season [S16, p.57].

This pattern holds for bad defensive teams as well, with the worst defensive teams tending to be short and having high turnover at both the player and coaching levels [S16, p.59].

## When to Use
- Evaluating whether a new roster will underperform in year one
- Projecting team improvement/decline based on off-season personnel changes
- Understanding why historically bad teams were bad (not necessarily talent, but chemistry and familiarity)
- Coaching: managing expectations in rebuilding years vs. contending years

## Key Principles
1. **Below 75% stability = high risk of poor performance** — most historically bad teams fall in the 40–65% range [S16, p.57]
2. **New coach + new roster = compounding disadvantage** — many bad teams had both simultaneously [S16, p.57]
3. **Two to three replaced positions is very disruptive** — at 40–60% stability, the team is essentially rebuilding chemistry every year [S16, p.57]
4. **Colleges deal with this annually** — collegiate programs manage high turnover routinely, but they compete against programs with similar instability; NBA teams face opponents with far greater continuity [S16, p.59]
5. **Height as a secondary factor** — worst offensive teams also tended to be shorter than average, but typically by less than 1 inch; stability may be more important than height [S16, p.59]

## Roster Stability Thresholds
| Stability | Meaning | Risk Level |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| > 85% | Strong continuity, minimal turnover | Low |
| 75–85% | Normal year-to-year change (1 starter) | Moderate |
| 60–75% | Significant disruption (1.5–2 positions) | High |
| 40–60% | Rebuilding (2–3 positions replaced) | Very High |
| < 40% | Near-complete roster overhaul | Extreme |

## Notable Exception: 1986 New York Knicks
The 1986 Knicks had high roster stability under fourth-year coach Hubie Brown but still produced a poor offense (99.5 rating vs. league avg. 107.2). The cause: Bernard King (elite offensive player, limited minutes due to injury in 1985) was gone, replaced by Patrick Ewing (excellent defender, limited in his first year). The team's best player *changed character* dramatically despite roster stability — illustrating that **who** changes matters as much as **how much** changes [S16, p.59].

## Practical Implications for Coaches
- **Prioritize continuity at key positions** — even one major replacement at PG or center can disrupt team-wide communication and chemistry
- **Lower expectations in year one** of a new coaching job or major roster overhaul
- **Build installation periods** — new systems take longer to execute efficiently when most players are new to the scheme
- **Track your own roster stability** — if you lose 2+ starters in an off-season, plan for a development-focused season

## Common Mistakes
1. **Assuming talent upgrades immediately improve performance** → new high-talent players still require integration time
2. **Changing everything at once** → new coach + new roster + new system = compounded instability
3. **Not accounting for key player injury as a stability disruption** → losing a key player to injury is functionally similar to roster instability even if the headcount stays constant
