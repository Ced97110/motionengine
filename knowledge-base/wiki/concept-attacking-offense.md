# the head coach's Attacking Offense

## Summary
the head coach's offensive system is built on one mandate: **attack**. Players cannot be passive or hold the ball. The primary offensive goal is to find what Johnson calls an "80 percent efficiency shot" — a direct drive to the basket resulting in a layup. If that shot is not available, the offense must swing the ball to the other side of the floor via any available mechanism (post entry, pick-and-roll, isolation, or zipper action). Passing ahead of the defense is always preferred over dribbling, and maintaining proper floor balance against traps is essential.

The offense demands that every player — not just the point guard — be a capable ball handler and decision-maker. When the ball is swung to the weak side, the receiver must immediately act ("catch and shoot, catch and drive, catch and pass") and never hold the ball. [S7, pp.77-87]

## When to Use
- Any half-court possession when an 80% efficiency layup is not immediately available
- When the defense collapses or traps on the ball handler
- When transitioning from the full court into the half-court breaking area
- When the defense applies full-court or sideline traps after a made basket or turnover

## Key Principles
1. **Attack first.** Always look for the layup ("80 percent efficiency shot") before settling for anything else.
2. **Swing the ball.** If no layup is available, reverse the ball to the other side of the floor. The defense must rotate and adjust, opening up driving and shooting lanes.
3. **A fast pass beats the fastest dribbler.** Move the ball via passing, not dribbling, especially to the weak side.
4. **Square the floor** whenever a trap is present — four teammates form a square around the ball handler to provide multiple passing outlets.
5. **The dunker position.** Set a player directly under the rim with hands up whenever a baseline drive is in progress — this keeps the baseline passing lane free and provides an interior target.
6. **Big men on a string.** Keep 4 and 5 coordinated: one at the dunker spot, one at the three-point arc or wing as a pressure-release player.
7. **Catch and go.** The receiver of a swung pass must catch and shoot, catch and drive, or catch and pass — never hold the ball and let the defense recover.
8. **Screener separation.** On any pick-and-roll, the screener must separate from the pick — going low and away from the trap — to put pressure on the rotating defender.
9. **Adapt to personnel.** Always suit your philosophy to your players' strengths, adjusting spacing and roles to exploit individual skills.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (1):** Primary ball handler; initiates action; when trapped, looks for step-through pass, ear pass, pivot pass, or keeps dribble alive and pops away. On hot-player situations, becomes the pressure-release player.
- **SG (2):** Weak-side pressure-release player; must position himself higher than the ball handler to provide a better passing angle and floor stretch.
- **SF (3):** Fills wing or corner; spots up to stretch the defense; must be ready to receive the swing pass and attack immediately.
- **PF (4):** Part of the "big men on a string" — positioned either at the dunker spot or outside the three-point arc depending on the situation and 5's movement.
- **C (5):** Rolls to the dunker position after pick-and-rolls (if not a good shooter); or separates low and away from the trap if a good ball handler. Flashes to high post when needed as a passing hub.

## Variations
### Zipper Action (Ball Reversal)
2 cuts around 4, receives from 1, and passes to 5, who has screened for 3 and popped back. 3 curls around a double pick set by 1 and 4. This is a structured way to swing the ball when no direct reversal pass is available. [S7, pp.78-79]

### Squaring the Floor Against a Trap
When the ball handler is trapped, three teammates form a square around him to provide four passing options. If no direct pass is available out of the trap, one corner of the square collapses toward the middle to create a closer outlet. [S7, pp.79-80]

### Hot Player Isolation
When a player is "hot" (unstoppable in a game), set 4 and 5 at the low posts and 2 and 3 on the wings to isolate the hot player. When the defense traps him: 2 becomes the pressure-release player, 5 flashes to the high-post area at the free-throw circle, and 4 runs to the corner. The hot player dribbles back to stretch the trap and passes out. [S7, p.86]

### Attacking Post-Up Doubles
When the ball goes to the low post and a double-team comes, the low post must stay strong and decide: split the trap (if it comes from the strong side — "Angles!" call), dribble out, or kick to a squared teammate. It is easier to split a strong-side trap due to favorable angles. [S7, pp.83-85]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Ball holder freezes on the swing** → Receiver must immediately act: catch-and-shoot, catch-and-drive, or catch-and-pass.
2. **Screener stays near the trap** → Big man must separate low and away from the pick after the screen to pressure the rotating defender.
3. **Driver "jumps on the trampoline"** → Ball handler must not jump into a shot on a baseline drive, especially against charge-taking defenses.
4. **Surrender position when trapped** → Trapped player must stay strong, step through or pivot to pass; never raise ball high overhead in surrender position.
5. **2 sets himself at the same level as 1** → The pressure-release player (2) must be higher than the ball handler to stretch the floor and provide a better passing angle.
