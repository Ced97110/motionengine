# One-Two Step Footwork for Jump Shots

## Summary
The one-two step is the preferred method for receiving the ball before shooting a jump shot. The non-shooting foot lands first ("one"), followed immediately by the shooting foot ("two"), creating forward momentum and loading the legs for the jump. The result is a shot with power, balance, and rhythm — and a shooter who remains the aggressor rather than giving the defender time to close out.

This footwork pattern applies from midrange all the way to the three-point line and is the foundation for both catch-and-shoot and pull-up jump shots.

## When to Use
- Catching any pass for a jump shot (midrange or three-point)
- Pull-up jump shot off one or two dribbles
- Transition jump shot (deceleration into the shot)
- On-the-move three-point shots (with slight adjustments for direction of movement)

## Key Principles
1. **Non-shooting foot lands first ("one"), shooting foot follows ("two").** For right-handed shooters: left foot lands first, right foot follows. This order is essential for generating momentum into the basket.
2. **Do not catch and pause.** The "two" step should already be in motion when the ball arrives. Pausing kills momentum and turns the shooter into a stationary target.
3. **Step from heel to toe.** This transfers weight forward into the shot. Flat-footed landing disrupts power transfer and pivot ability.
4. **Long stride with the lead foot.** The longer and more aggressive the stride, the more power generated and the lower the body stays, eliminating negative motion.
5. **Shooting foot remains in a straight line to the target.** Misalignment of the shooting foot sends the shot line offline.
6. **Stay low until elevation.** Catching low and rising continuously (low to high) is faster and more efficient than a dipping motion.
7. **Be the aggressor.** The one-two step keeps forward momentum, forcing the defender to backpedal rather than close out on a stationary target.

## Variations
### Standard Catch-and-Shoot (Straight On)
Non-shooting foot steps first toward the ball, shooting foot completes the base. Rise directly into the jump shot. [S5, p.61]

### Three-Point Straight On
Same left-to-right (right-handed) or right-to-left (left-handed) sequence, but the stride is slightly more pronounced to generate additional power for the longer distance. Begin 3–5 feet behind the arc to time the step into the shot. [S5, pp.72-73]

### On-the-Move (Moving Left to Right, Right-Handed)
Step aggressively with the left (non-shooting) foot, opening it toward the target in a heel-to-toe step. Pivot into the shot and bring the right (shooting) foot around. Do not overstride or the shot line goes offline. [S5, pp.75-76]

### On-the-Move (Moving Right to Left, Right-Handed)
The right foot becomes the first step, opening toward the target. The left foot must then be "stubbed" — brought in just enough to create a balanced base. Without the stub, the left side becomes the lead side and forces a body twist during the shot. [S5, pp.76-77]

### Pull-Up Off the Dribble
Push the ball out in front with the live (non-pivot) foot leading into a long stride that mirrors the standard one-two step. Pick up the ball going to the strong hand; load the gun going to the weak hand. [S5, pp.65-66]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Jump stop instead of one-two step** → Kills all power; reload required; defender becomes the aggressor. Prefer one-two step for all midrange-and-out jump shots.
2. **Stepping with the shooting foot first** → No momentum generated; power and rhythm are lost. Always lead with the non-shooting foot.
3. **Flat-footed step** → Weight doesn't transfer forward; pivot ability is compromised, especially on the on-the-move shot. Step heel to toe.
4. **Overstriding on the on-the-move shot** → Off-balance, shot line misaligned. Control the stride length to maintain a proper base.
5. **Failing to stub the foot (moving against dominant direction)** → Body twists during the shot, pushing the shot offline left or right.
