# The Plyometric Edge: Shock Training Applied to Calisthenics

## Summary
Paul Wade makes an important distinction that is often misunderstood: **explosive training and plyometric training are not the same thing**. Jumping and power pushups are explosive movements, but they are only *plyometric* when the stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) is actively engaged — meaning the muscles are rapidly pre-stretched under load before the concentric contraction fires back. [S9, p.19]

The concept originates with Soviet sports scientist **Yuri Verkhoshansky**, who developed "shock training" in the late 1960s. The term *plyometrics* was coined by American athlete Fred Wilt observing Soviet athletes. The physiological mechanism involves the **myotatic (stretch) reflex**, muscle spindles, golgi tendon organs, and the [[stretch-shortening-cycle-plyometrics]] — where an eccentric pre-stretch amplifies the subsequent concentric contraction. [S9, p.19]

## The Two Methods for Adding the Plyometric Edge

### Method 1: Depth Jump / Drop-and-Jump
The classic Verkhoshansky method:
- Step or jump off a box or elevated surface
- Hit the ground — rapid eccentric loading shocks the muscles
- Immediately explode back up (onto another box, or into the air)
- The fall creates the "shock" that amplifies the jump power

**Pushup variation:** Start with hands on two boxes; drop off the boxes; land on hands, immediately explode back up onto the boxes. [S9, p.19-20]

**Advanced variation:** From standing, drop directly into the pushup position — only for athletes with robust joints and very high relative strength.

### Method 2: Continuous Reps Without Pause (Simpler)
This method does not require a box:
- The first rep of any set is NOT plyometric (no preceding drop/shock)
- The second rep and every subsequent rep CAN BE plyometric — if executed immediately upon landing with no pause
- Upon landing from the first jump, the landing itself creates eccentric loading; immediately bouncing back up uses that stored elastic energy
- "Knock them out machine gun-style. Hit your first rep hard as possible, then the second you land, use the rebound and elastic forces in your muscles to immediately bounce back up." [S9, p.20]
- **Same principle applies to explosive pushups** — use the fall from the first rep to charge the second

## When NOT to Apply the Rebound Principle
For skill-based explosive movements (kip-ups, flips), it is appropriate to **pause between reps** to mentally reset and adjust. The no-pause rebound method is specifically for:
- Jump chain work
- Power pushup chain work

[S9, p.20]

## Key Principles
1. **Plyometrics ≠ explosiveness** — understand the difference; only use the term when the SSC is truly being engaged
2. **The myotatic stretch reflex is the mechanism** — the nervous system interprets the rapid eccentric loading as a threat and fires back with amplified concentric force
3. **Shock training works** — Verkhoshansky's method has decades of experimental and practical validation; use it intentionally in jump and pushup training
4. **Keep reps continuous** — any pause between reps dissipates the stored elastic energy and breaks the plyometric stimulus
5. **Build up gradually** — depth jumps and box pushups place significant demands on joints; progress from continuous ground-level reps before adding height

## Basketball Applications
- **Second-jump rebounding** — the rebound principle is exactly what makes elite rebounders: they do not pause upon landing; they use the landing itself to immediately reload for the next jump
- **Repeated sprint/cut patterns** — elastic energy from deceleration fuels the next acceleration when there is no pause
- **Defensive lateral steps** — keeping tension in the hips and ankles between direction changes utilizes the SSC for faster cuts

## Common Mistakes
1. **Pausing between every rep** → Eliminates the plyometric stimulus; reduces the training effect to simple strength work
2. **Using box drops before building baseline explosive strength** → Depth jump landing forces are extreme; build base with continuous ground reps first
3. **Applying the rebound principle to skill movements (flips, kip-ups)** → These require mental reset between reps; forced continuous reps compromise safety and technique
