# Youth and Adolescent Basketball Injuries

## Summary
Youth and adolescent basketball players are a unique population because their musculoskeletal systems are skeletally immature. The ankle and foot are the most commonly injured body regions across high school and youth basketball, with ligament and muscle/tendon strains most frequent. However, young players also face injuries unique to their developmental stage: growth plate (epiphyseal) fractures, apophyseal injuries, osteochondral lesions, and growth-plate-specific ACL equivalents (tibial eminence fractures). Understanding these distinctions is essential for coaches, athletic trainers, and medical staff working with youth teams [S10, Ch.67].

In high school basketball, approximately 970,983 high school athletes and 4,225,321 children aged 6–12 participate in basketball annually in the United States [S10, Ch.67].

## When to Use
- Evaluating any youth player who presents with bone pain, joint swelling, or inability to bear weight
- Designing injury prevention programs for youth basketball programs
- Advising parents, coaches, and athletic trainers on youth-specific injury patterns
- Making return-to-play decisions for youth players after injury

## Key Principles

### 1. Epiphyseal Plate (Salter-Harris) Fractures
- The epiphyseal plate is **2–5× weaker** than surrounding fibrous structures (ligaments, tendons, joint capsule) in children compared to adults [S10, Ch.67].
- A force that causes a ligament tear in an adult is more likely to cause a growth plate fracture in a child.
- **Salter-Harris Classification**: Type I (through growth plate) → Type II (most common, 75%; growth plate + metaphysis) → Type III (growth plate + epiphysis) → Type IV (growth plate + epiphysis + metaphysis) → Type V (crush/compression, very rare but high growth arrest risk).
- Types I & II: Managed nonoperatively with closed reduction and casting/splinting.
- Types III & IV: Usually require ORIF.
- **Type V is easily missed** — involves the germinal matrix and has high growth arrest potential [S10, Ch.67].
- Symptoms may mimic ligamentous injury, leading to misdiagnosis. Radiographs first; CT for detail; MRI for soft tissue, chronic injuries, avascular necrosis.

### 2. Apophyseal Injuries (Basketball-Specific Examples)
Apophyses are normal bony outgrowths at tendon/ligament insertion sites that arise from separate ossification centers and fuse with bone over time. The growth plate is weaker than surrounding fibrous structures — making apophyses vulnerable in growing athletes.

**Common apophyseal injuries in basketball:**

| Condition | Location | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Osgood-Schlatter's Disease** | Tibial tuberosity | Incidence up to 9.8% in ages 12–15; atraumatic anterior knee pain with jumping; self-limiting; ice, NSAIDs, patella strap |
| **Sever's Disease** | Calcaneal apophysis | Heel pain; difficulty jumping/sprinting; 3.7 per 1,000 ages 6–17; activity modification, stretching, heel cushioning |
| **Iselin's Disease** | 5th metatarsal tuberosity | Lateral foot pain; can arise from overuse OR inversion injury; oblique X-ray most useful; conservative management |
| **Sinding Larsen-Johansson** | Inferior pole of patella | Similar to Osgood-Schlatter but at patellar inferior pole |

- Acute avulsion fractures: Due to forceful eccentric contractions across an unfused growth plate.
- Chronic apophysitis: Due to long periods of intensive training.
- Most apophyseal injuries are self-limiting and managed conservatively with rest, activity modification, physical therapy, and pain control. Avulsion fractures with significant displacement may require surgical fixation [S10, Ch.67].

### 3. Juvenile Osteochondritis Dissecans (JOCD)
- Incidence: 11–29 per 100,000 under age 19.
- **Most common location**: Medial femoral condyle (63.6%), lateral femoral condyle (32.5%), patella (1.5%).
- Higher incidence in males ages 12–19.
- **Etiology**: Multifactorial — focal repetitive trauma at sites with unique vascular architecture triggers ischemic events causing subchondral bone separation.
- **Presentation**: Early stages: nonspecific knee pain, stiffness, occasional effusion. Late stages: mechanical symptoms (grinding, locking, catching) when loose/detached lesions are present.
- **Imaging**: Plain films show circumscribed area of subchondral bone with crescent-shaped sclerotic radiolucent outline. MRI is highly sensitive and assesses cartilage and subchondral bone; however, MRI characteristics do not reliably predict instability in skeletally immature patients.
- **Treatment**: 50–67% of JOCD lesions heal nonoperatively in 6–12 months with immobilization, limited weight-bearing, and/or activity restriction for 3–6 months. Surgical intervention for stable lesions refractory to nonoperative management, unstable lesions, mechanical symptoms, or closing physis [S10, Ch.67].

### 4. Stress Fractures in Youth Basketball
- Girls' basketball is a "high-risk sport" (2.71 per 100,000 AEs in high school).
- Most common locations: Lower leg (tibia) and foot in both boys' and girls' basketball; specifically tibial shaft, medial malleolus, and metatarsal bones.
- Female sex is a known risk factor — menstrual disturbances → lower estrogen → lower BMD → higher fracture risk.

### 5. Ankle Sprains
- Ankle is the most commonly injured body region (48% in U18 players; 23.8% of all ankle sprains in US high school sports).
- Incidence: 7.74 per 10,000 AEs in high school basketball.
- **Mechanism**: Sudden inversion with or without internal rotation, low plantar flexion — most often during landing after jumping (rebounding or shooting).
- **Risk factors**: Higher BMI, slower eccentric inversion strength, fast concentric plantar flexion strength, passive inversion joint position sense, peroneus brevis reaction time.
- **Grading**: Grade I (stretch only), Grade II (partial tear, laxity with firm endpoint), Grade III (complete tear, laxity without firm endpoint).
- **Ottawa Ankle Rules**: Validated in children — take X-rays if fracture suspicion is high.
- **Treatment**: RICE → early ROM → controlled stress on ligament through motion/stretching/strengthening → functional bracing or taping → return to play when heel-to-toe gait is pain-free and sport-specific functional tests are passed.
- Surgery: For severe functional and mechanical instability with recurrent sprains refractory to nonoperative management [S10, Ch.67].

### 6. ACL Injuries in Youth Basketball
- Girls' basketball: Third highest ACL injury rate in US high school sports (10.3 per 10,000 AEs), behind football and girls' soccer.
- **Girls are at 3.80× relative risk** for ACL injury compared to boys in high school basketball.
- **Mechanism**: Mostly noncontact (boys 50.8%, girls 46.0%) — typically during landing, often preceded by opponent affecting movement patterns.
- **Biomechanical conditions**: Anterior tibial shear force, knee abduction moment, tibial internal rotation moment.
- **Important youth-specific note**: Skeletally immature athletes may fracture their **tibial eminence** (at the ACL insertion) rather than rupturing the ACL itself.
  - Meyers-McKeever Classification: Type I (non-displaced) → conservative; Type II (anteriorly displaced, intact posterior hinge) → often operative; Type III (completely displaced) → operative.
- **ACL reconstruction in open physis**: Multiple options — extraphyseal, all-epiphyseal, partial transphyseal, transphyseal [S10, Ch.67].

### 7. Sport-Related Concussion (SRC)
- Incidence in youth basketball: 0.13 per 1,000 AEs (6th highest across 12 sports under age 18).
- **Mechanism**: Player-player or player-surface contact while defending, chasing a loose ball, rebounding, or during general play.
- **Diagnosis is clinical**: Headaches, mood changes, dizziness, visual changes — nonspecific symptoms require careful clinical evaluation. SCAT5 and Child SCAT5 offer standardized sideline assessment.
- **Recovery**: Most youth athletes recover within 2–4 weeks. A small subset develops Post-Concussive Syndrome (PCS).
- **PCS risk factors**: Previous concussion history, pre-injury mood disorders, family history of mood disorders, delayed symptom onset.
- **Return to play**: Graduated protocol — rest → symptom-limited activity → light aerobic exercise → sport-specific exercise → noncontact drill → full contact → normal gameplay. Advance only if symptoms do not recur.
- **Return to learn**: Academic accommodations may be needed — shortened school days, reduced workload, extra time, quiet rooms. Academic baseline must be restored before returning to sport [S10, Ch.67].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Treating a youth player's growth plate injury as a ligament sprain** → The growth plate is the weak link; tenderness over a physis warrants imaging before a sprain diagnosis.
2. **Demanding early return to play from apophyseal injuries** → These are self-limiting but require adequate rest; pressure to return can worsen or convert to avulsion.
3. **Using "6 weeks" as the postpartum or post-injury magic timeline** → Recovery is individual; imaging and functional testing should guide RTP, not arbitrary timelines.
4. **Clearing a concussed player based on symptom resolution alone** → A graduated return-to-play protocol is mandatory; academic baseline must also be restored.
5. **Ignoring the psychosocial dimension** → Coaches can cause perfectionist concerns; all forms of abuse have been reported in youth sport settings. Health practitioners have a duty to recognize and prevent abuse.
