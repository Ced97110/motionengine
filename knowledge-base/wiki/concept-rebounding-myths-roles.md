# Rebounding Myths and Roles

## Summary
Dean Oliver uses play-by-play data from hundreds of NBA games (1999–2002) to debunk common rebounding myths and establish a clear conceptual framework. The biggest myth: three-point shots are easier for the offense to rebound than two-point shots. The data shows the opposite — 33% of missed two-pointers were offensively rebounded vs. 31% of missed three-pointers. The difference is trivially small and arguably goes against the myth [S16, pp.86-87].

More broadly, Oliver argues that rebounding has no business being treated as a third category separate from offense and defense. Offensive rebounding is an offensive skill that improves a team's offensive rating. Defensive rebounding is a defensive skill that improves a team's defensive rating. Mixing them together obscures what both actually do [S16, p.88].

## When to Use
- Teaching rebounding philosophy to players and assistants
- Evaluating whether a team's rebounding strength/weakness is on the offensive or defensive end
- Countering overconfidence in rebounding-centric systems
- Designing practice drills that treat offensive and defensive rebounding separately

## Key Principles
1. **Three-point shots are NOT easier to offensively rebound** — 33% OREB rate on 2PT misses vs. 31% on 3PT misses in 1999–2002 NBA data [S16, p.87]
2. **Offensive rebounding is an offensive skill** — it preserves possessions AND leads to better scoring opportunities; WNBA data shows FG% improving from 41% to 48% and points per play from 0.80 to 0.94 off offensive rebounds [S16, p.88]
3. **Defensive rebounding is a defensive skill** — the goal is to maintain position advantage and prevent the offense from getting close-range second chances [S16, p.88]
4. **Offensive rebounding improves offensive ratings; defensive rebounding improves defensive ratings** — the simplest, most important framing [S16, p.88]
5. **Close two-point shots generate significant offensive rebounds** — the shooter often has inside position; defenders may double-team, leaving another offensive player open [S16, p.87]
6. **Offensive rebound conversion matters more than count** — the 2002 Lakers scored 1.1 pts/play off offensive rebounds vs. 0.95 on other plays (+20%); they held opponents to 0.96 off ORs vs. 0.88 elsewhere [S16, p.89]

## Why the 3PT Rebound Myth Persists
Long rebounds do occur more often from three-point shots (going past box-out position), but:
- Mid-range jumpers and bank shots can also go long
- Close two-point misses generate abundant offensive rebounds because the shooter has inside position
- The two effects roughly cancel each other out [S16, p.87]

## The True Value of Rebounding
Win-correlation data (1998–2002 NBA) shows:
- **Offensive rebounds (raw)**: teams with more win only 46% of games — nearly useless as a predictor
- **Offensive rebounding percentage**: slightly better at 56%
- **When FG% is equalized**: offensive rebounding rises to 63% win correlation — more meaningful than it appears overall
- **In the NBA**: rebounding is less valuable than shooting, free throws, and turnovers; in high school/college it may be more important [S16, pp.90-92]

## Teaching Implications
- Teach offensive and defensive rebounding separately with different mentalities
- **Offensive rebounding mentality**: find space around defenders, get near the basket, convert at high efficiency — treat it as a second chance to score
- **Defensive rebounding mentality**: maintain position advantage, keep offensive player away from the basket, block out as "staying with your man"
- All players (including guards) should be taught defensive box-out as it emphasizes the defensive principle of staying with your assignment [S16, p.88]

## Common Mistakes
1. **"Rebounding wins championships"** → Lakers 2002 and Rockets 1994-95 won without being elite rebounding teams [S16, p.87]
2. **"Go after every ball" vs. "block out first"** → Blocking out maintains position which is at least as important as pure ball-pursuit
3. **Treating OREB and DREB as the same skill** → They require different positioning, timing, and mentality
4. **Using raw rebound totals** → Total is confounded by opponent FG%; use rebound percentage instead
5. **Prioritizing OREB over free throws and turnovers when shooting is equal** → The data shows free throws and turnovers are more decisive in close games [S16, p.91]
