# Face-Guard Press

## Summary
The Face-Guard Press is an aggressive full-court man-to-man press in which every defender physically face-guards their opponent — body-to-body — making it nearly impossible for receivers to get open for an entry pass. Used primarily after made free throws or dead balls on the baseline, the press pressures the inbounds passer, immediately traps the first entry pass recipient, and forces the offense to attempt long passes that interceptors can pick off. [S1, pp.186]

The philosophical premise: even if this press occasionally gives up a basket, the long-term disruption — forced turnovers, tempo changes, psychological pressure — is worth it. "If we give up one basket to get two or three, in the long run our aggressiveness will work in our favor." [S1, p.186]

## When to Use
- After made free throws and dead balls (baseline inbounds)
- When you need an immediate turnover (late-game deficit)
- Against a team with a weak inbounds passer
- As a surprise element to change the tempo mid-game
- When your team has the athleticism and conditioning to body up full-court

## Key Principles
1. **Big man pressures the inbounds passer** — nearest big man tries to influence the entry pass to one side of the court.
2. **Immediate trap on the first entry** — once the ball is entered, the big man and the closest defender trap immediately; the inbounds passer is now a threat who must be denied quickly.
3. **Deny the outlets nearest the ball** — the two nearest pass receivers are denied body-to-body; force the dribbler to attempt a long pass.
4. **Force long passes** — receivers are pushed up the court with body-to-body pressure, making short passes difficult; long passes are what the defense wants — interceptors can pick them off.
5. **"Ball!" call on every pass** — the on-ball defender yells "Ball!" immediately when a pass is made, alerting retreating teammates to sprint back and get level with the ball, arms raised in passing lanes.
6. **Retreating defenders look over their shoulders** — as defenders sprint back, they must look over their shoulders to locate the ball and intercept or deflect passes.
7. **Rover variation** — at times, do NOT pressure the inbounds passer; instead use a rover in the middle of the court to anticipate and steal the inbounds pass while other four defenders face-guard their opponents. [S1, p.186]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PF/C (inbounds pressure)**: Pressure the inbounds passer; body presence and hand activity; influence entry pass to one side.
- **PG/SG (nearest outlet defenders)**: Deny the nearest receivers body-to-body; be the second trapper once the ball is entered.
- **SF/Rover**: Either face-guard their opponent OR play rover in the middle to anticipate the inbounds steal.
- **All five**: Sprint back and look over the shoulder when the ball is passed over the defense; arms up in passing lanes; be ready to deflect or intercept.

## Variation: Rover Face-Guard
Instead of pressuring the inbounds passer, one player (usually the quickest defender) plays rover in the middle of the court, anticipating and looking to steal the inbounds pass. The other four defenders face-guard their individual opponents body-to-body. This prevents the easy relief pass that often beats standard face-guard coverage. [S1, p.186]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not getting body-to-body** → Correct: face-guard means chest-to-chest presence; giving a receiver even a half-step of space defeats the press.
2. **Trapping passively** → Correct: the trap must arrive before the dribbler can read and pass — active, decisive approach, not reactive.
3. **Chasing passes instead of intercepting** → Correct: when the ball goes over the press, retreating defenders look over their shoulders and try to intercept, NOT simply chase.
4. **Not communicating "Ball!"** → Correct: the on-ball defender calls it immediately on every pass — this cues retreating defenders to locate the ball.
