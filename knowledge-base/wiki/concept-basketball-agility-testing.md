# Basketball Speed and Agility Testing

## Summary
Efficient direction changes are a key determinant of basketball performance. During a game, unanticipated (reactive) direction changes occur more often than preplanned ones — meaning both types of agility must be tested separately. Three standard tests cover this spectrum: the **Reactive Lane Shuttle** (reactive agility from an external cue), the **Lane Agility Test** (preplanned multidirectional COD), and the **Three-Quarter Court Sprint** (linear acceleration and speed). All three are used at the NBA Combine, and normative data exist at all competitive levels [S11].

Athletes require a combination of perceptual-cognitive factors and strength characteristics to maintain fast directional changes. Testing under game-like conditions (reactive stimulus) provides more ecologically valid data than preplanned tests alone.

## Reactive Lane Shuttle Test (RLS)

### Purpose
Assesses reactive agility capability from a static start using an external stimulus. Unlike the 5-10-5 shuttle, the RLS challenges decision-making and perception alongside physical COD ability. Used in the NBA Combine [S11].

### Setup
- NBA-sized lane (16 ft / 4.9 m wide)
- Three electronic timing gates 8 ft (2.4 m) apart: one at each end, one at center of key
- Synchronize all gates for total time + splits

### Protocol
1. Athlete straddles the center gate
2. External cue signals direction (right or left)
3. Athlete moves laterally to break the first gate
4. 180° turn, sprint across to break the far gate
5. 180° turn, return through the starting position
6. Collect 2 trials each direction (4 total); report lowest scores
7. Repeat if athlete moves before the cue or in the wrong direction

## Lane Agility Test

### Purpose
Examines multidirectional, **preplanned** change-of-direction capability. The pattern includes forward sprint, lateral shuffle, backpedal, and reverse lateral shuffle — covering all four primary basketball movement planes. Reliable assessment of COD ability in basketball athletes. Note: elite basketball players shuffle only 6–7 ft on average in games vs. 16 ft in this test, but the test remains reliable [S11].

### Setup
- Four cones at corners of the key (NBA lane = 16 ft wide; college/HS = adjust cone placement outside lane)
- Timing gate at start/finish; optional additional gates at each corner

### Protocol (from figure 3.3)
1. Start: stationary two-point stance at left side of key, facing basket
2. Explode forward to first turn point
3. Cut right → maximum-effort left-to-right side shuffle
4. Turn → maximum-effort backpedal
5. Turn → right-to-left side shuffle back to start
6. Repeat entire rectangle in reverse order without stopping
7. Total time = both complete trips around the key
8. Collect 4 trials (2 each starting side); report lowest

## Three-Quarter Court Sprint Test

### Purpose
Assesses linear acceleration and speed over 75 feet (22.9 m) — baseline to opposite free-throw line. Basketball athletes perform multiple short sprints throughout every game in transition between offense and defense. The three-quarter court distance captures the relevant acceleration zone without a true max-velocity phase [S11].

### Setup
- Two cones at baseline (start)
- Two cones at opposite free-throw line (finish)
- Distance: 75 feet (22.9 m)
- Timing gates recommended; stopwatch acceptable

### Protocol
1. Athlete starts with front foot behind baseline cones
2. Sprint in a straight line through the finish cones at maximum effort
3. Continue sprinting through the cones (don't decelerate early)
4. Two attempts; record fastest to nearest 0.01 seconds

## Key Principles
1. **Test reactive AND preplanned agility separately** — each measures a different quality; both matter in basketball
2. **Report the best trial** — fastest time; not an average
3. **Standardize starting position** — foot placement and stance must be identical across athletes and trials
4. **Enforce the reactive cue protocol** — invalidate trials where the athlete anticipates the direction on the RLS
5. **Use timing gates when available** — hand-timing introduces unacceptable variability on short-duration tests

## Common Mistakes
1. Confusing the RLS (reactive) with the lane agility test (preplanned) — they measure different things; do not substitute one for the other
2. Using a college-sized lane for the lane agility test without adjusting cone placement — must place cones outside the smaller lane to maintain the correct 16 ft distance
3. Allowing deceleration before the finish line on the sprint test — instructs athletes to run through the cones
4. Testing on a cold floor without warm-up → injury risk and artificially slow times
