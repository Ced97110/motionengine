# Shooting and Scoring Off Screens

## Summary
Catching and shooting on the move off a screen is one of the most valuable offensive skills a perimeter scorer can develop. It is the culmination of catch-and-shoot technique applied in a game-realistic screening context. The chapter emphasizes that a skilled shooter must earn screens from teammates by being dangerous coming off them — using proper technique, hard cuts, and immediate shooting readiness.

The key technical elements are: staying low through the screen (body-to-body contact), setting up the defender with a V-cut or body bump before the screen, planting the inside pivot foot on the catch, reading the defender via peripheral vision, and using a heel-to-toe landing to stay in rhythm and on balance. [S5, pp.113-120]

## When to Use
- Any time a teammate sets a down screen, flare screen, or cross screen for a perimeter shooter
- When the offense runs catch-and-shoot actions off pin-downs, elevator screens, or horns sets
- Against man-to-man coverage where screen usage can create clean looks
- When the shooter has been scouted as a threat and the defense cheats over screens

## Key Principles

### 1. Stay Low — The Low-to-High Principle
- Assume a balanced, athletic stance with knees bent and bend at the midsection coming off the screen [S5, p.113]
- Stay low until you get into your shooting motion
- Low catch = ready to play; high catch = slow to shoot, defender recovers
- **"Always keep in mind the low-to-high principle. It makes you quicker and more efficient as an offensive player."** [S5, p.113]

### 2. Body-to-Body, Not Shoulder-to-Shoulder
- Come off the screen with your **shoulders meeting the waist or hips of the screener** (body to body) [S5, p.113]
- Coming off shoulder-to-shoulder puts the shooter too high, causing a high center of gravity and balance drift in the direction of travel
- Body-to-body keeps you low, balanced, and prepared to shoot or make a move immediately [S5, pp.113-114]

### 3. Set Up the Defender Before the Screen
- **Option A**: Get into the defender's body before cutting — forces them to play tight, making it more likely they will trail and get caught on the screen [S5, p.114]
- **Option B**: V-cut — take the defender toward the basket first, then reverse direction hard toward the screen, rubbing the defender off the screener [S5, p.114]
- Always make **hard cuts** to free yourself from your defender; treat every screen as a scoring opportunity

### 4. Plant the Inside Pivot Foot on the Catch
Advantages of inside pivot foot over jump stop:
- **Better body control and balance**
- **Better floor vision** — opening up toward the basket with the hip and shoulder allows use of peripheral vision to see the defender
- **Can read the defender** — if defender fought through the screen and is close, go to a scoring move instead of rising to shoot; if defender is trailing, rise and shoot [S5, p.115]
- **Can retrieve bad passes** — if the pass is thrown behind or wide, push off the planted inside foot to adjust [S5, p.116]
- With a jump stop, feet are in the air when the ball is in the air — cannot adjust direction or see the defender [S5, pp.115-116]

### 5. Heel-to-Toe Landing
- When stepping into the shot off the inside pivot, open the foot by **planting the heel first**, then shift weight to the toe [S5, p.117]
- Landing flat-footed breaks the rhythm — turns a one-two-step into a three- or four-step catch requiring a dribble to regain balance
- Heel-to-toe naturally squares the inside pivot foot to the basket as the body comes around
- **"Stepping heel to toe ensures that you will always be balanced."** [S5, p.117]

### 6. Hands Ready, Shot Pocket Formed
- Have hands up and shot pocket formed *before* catching the ball
- The passer looks for the hands as a target — ready hands signal where the ball should be delivered [S5, p.114]
- **"Possession is more important than position."** [S5, p.116]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG/SG/SF**: Primary users; must master all footwork details, V-cut setup, and body-to-body technique
- **PF (as screener)**: Set screens at the correct height (waist/hip level) so the shooter can rub off body-to-body; hold the screen long enough

## Common Mistakes
1. **Coming off screens shoulder-to-shoulder** → too high, center of gravity causes drift, off-balance shot → come off body-to-body at screener's waist/hip height [S5, p.113]
2. **Using a jump stop off a screen** → lose sight of defender, feet in air when ball is in air, cannot adjust to bad passes, momentum stops → use inside pivot foot [S5, pp.115-116]
3. **Landing flat-footed** → breaks one-two-step rhythm, forces extra dribble, fails to square to basket automatically → plant heel first, roll to toe [S5, p.117]
4. **Leaving space between yourself and the screener** → defender slips through the gap; screen wasted → stay tight, make contact with screener [S5, p.114]
5. **Not setting up the defender** → defender anticipates the screen and goes over easily → use body bump or V-cut to occupy the defender first [S5, p.114]
6. **Coming off screens lazily or slowly** → defender recovers in time; teammate has no reason to screen for you → come off screens hard and aggressively every time [S5, p.113]
7. **Hands not ready on the catch** → slower shot release; passer unsure where to throw → form shot pocket early, hands up as a target [S5, p.114]

## Variations
### Reading the Defender Off the Inside Pivot
- Defender trailing → open look; rise and shoot immediately
- Defender fought through the screen and is close → go to a scoring move (jab, shot fake, drive) [S5, p.115]

### V-Cut Setup
Take defender toward the basket → push off hard → reverse and sprint toward the screen → rub defender off the screener's hip → catch on inside pivot foot → shoot [S5, p.114]
