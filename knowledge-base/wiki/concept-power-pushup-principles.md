# Power Pushup Principles: Stance, Depth, and Myotatic Rebound

## Summary
Explosive (power) pushups operate on fundamentally different principles than slow, strength-focused pushups. Three variables — stance width, depth of descent, and use of the myotatic rebound — must be understood and applied correctly before attempting any step in the power pushup chain. These principles underpin all nine steps from the incline pop-up through the Superman pushup. [S9, pp.81-82]

## When to Use
- As the theoretical foundation before introducing any explosive pushup variation
- When athletes are transitioning from strength-based pushups to plyometric/power pushups
- During upper-body power development phases in a basketball conditioning program
- As a coaching framework for correcting common errors in explosive pushing movements

## Key Principles

### 1. Stance Width
- For slow, strength-based pushups, a **close stance** is ideal (prevents cheating by twisting, especially in one-arm work)
- For explosive pushups, a **wider base is beneficial** — somewhere between just beyond shoulder-width and twice shoulder-width is optimal for most athletes
- Advanced athletes with superior balance can use a closer stance
- Asymmetrical exercises are rare in explosive pushup work, so twisting/cheating is less of a concern [S9, p.81]

### 2. Depth of Descent
- Full ROM (sternum to fist's-width from floor) is ideal for strength pushups
- For power pushups, only **dip approximately one-third of the way down**
- Goal is maximum strength-speed, not full stretch — too deep slows the movement and takes the tendon's elastic force out of its optimal zone
- Think "dipping" rather than "lowering" [S9, p.81]
- Experiment: too shallow = not enough spring; too deep = tendons lose elastic advantage

### 3. Myotatic Rebound
- For strength pushups: **pause at the bottom** (builds muscle and strength)
- For power pushups: **do NOT pause at the bottom**
- Quickly dip under gravity and **immediately blast back up as fast as possible**
- This utilizes the **myotatic reflex** (stretch reflex) and the **elastic force** stored in the tendons
- Think of this as "applying rebound" — not bouncing, but using stored elastic energy [S9, p.82]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: upper-body pushing power is relevant for boxing out, passing, screening, and physical play. These principles apply regardless of position.

## Variations
### Strength-Focused Pushup
Full ROM, close stance, pause at bottom — builds maximum muscle and strength but does NOT develop power.

### Power Pushup
Wider stance, 1/3 ROM descent, no pause, immediate rebound — targets rate of force development and elastic strength.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Going too deep** → limits elastic rebound and reduces speed; stay at approximately 1/3 depth
2. **Pausing at the bottom** → eliminates the myotatic reflex benefit; transition immediately
3. **Using too narrow a stance for explosive work** → reduces stability; widen base to shoulder-width or beyond
4. **Thinking of it as "bouncing"** → cue athletes to "apply rebound" — the intent is maximal effort upward, not passive bouncing
5. **Jumping straight to clap pushups without building base** → use the full 9-step progression chain
