# Late-Game Defensive Strategy

## Summary
Late-game defensive decisions — when to foul, how to protect a lead against the three-point shot, and how to handle switching — must be planned well in advance, not improvised under pressure. Del Harris provides specific pro-tested thresholds and principles developed during his time coaching the pro team, including data-backed foul-timing rules and the three plays that most commonly beat a defending team in crunch time. [S7, pp.266-268]

## When to Use
- Final 3 minutes trailing by 10+ points (start fouling)
- Final 90 seconds trailing by 7-9 points
- Final 30 seconds or less in a two-possession (3-5 point) game
- Final 6 seconds with a 3-point lead (foul to prevent a tying 3)
- When the opponent needs a 3-pointer to tie with any seconds remaining

## Key Principles
1. **Plan it before the game**: "You'll generally want to plan your late-game strategies well in advance to avoid coming up with something desperate and ill-advised when you're under pressure." [S7, p.266]
2. **Drill it in practice**: Every late-game scenario must be practiced — two-minute games, seven/nine-possession games, out-of-bounds series.
3. **Try to steal the inbounds pass before fouling**: In every trailing situation, attempt the steal first before committing the foul.
4. **Know opponent's FT% by player**: Always know who the best and worst free-throw shooters are; intentional fouling of poor FT shooters ("Hack-a-a dominant center") can be a legitimate strategy at appropriate deficits.

## Fouling Rules by Deficit

| Deficit | When to Start Fouling (pro) |
|---|---|
| 10+ points | 3:00 remaining |
| 7-9 points | 1:30 remaining |
| 1-6 points (2 possessions) | 0:30 or less |

- The larger the deficit, the earlier fouling must start to stop the clock and create possessions
- Lower-level competition → start fouling sooner (lower FT% means more benefit from fouling)
- Always try to steal the inbounds pass first before fouling [S7, p.266]

## Fouling With a 3-Point Lead
- Data from the pro team' analytics team: **the odds favor fouling with 6 or fewer seconds remaining**, even against a 90% free-throw shooter
- Previous practice threshold (5 seconds or less) was also effective
- **Critical caveat**: The foul must come before the player can initiate a shooting motion — no 3 free throws, no 4-point play possibility
- Make this decision in advance and practice it [S7, p.267]

## Protecting Against the Three-Point Tying Shot

### The Three Plays That Beat You Late
1. **The three-pointer** — quickest route for a trailing opponent
2. **The quick two-point score** — takes little time off the clock; opponent often uses it first if down 5 with two possessions
3. **The offensive rebound put-back** — "the single play that decides most 1- or 2-point games" [S7, p.267]

### Defensive Rules When Opponent Needs a Three
- **All five defenders play on the top side of their assignments** — deny the ball from above the 3-point line on every receiver
- Prevent passes to the strong-side corner (most common catch-and-shoot spot)
- **Do not drop back to help on penetration** — the natural help instinct creates catch-and-shoot 3 opportunities
- **Do not trap in the low post** — same problem; opens kick-out 3
- **Block out on every missed shot** — crashing the board with no defenders remaining is how trailing teams win

### Five-Man Arc Formation
- When all possessions are 3-point attempts, line all five defenders in an arc above the three-point line
- Players switch out on all picks but must communicate loudly
- Put in your best-switching lineup; remove a bigger, slower player if needed

## Drills for Late-Game Situations

### Out-of-Bounds Game Series
Play a game to 3 baskets from all OOB positions (side, end, full court, three-quarter, corner). Change clock: 20 → 10 → 5 → 1 second. Every possession change = new OOB play. [S7, p.268]

### Three-Possession O-D-O Game
Coach sets score and time. One team starts from any position (full court, side/end OOB, rebound/steal). Offense scores = 1 point; defense gets a stop = 1 point. Ball goes to other team on the other end in the same situation. Original team gets a third possession. Winner is 2-1 or 3-0; winner gets ball to start next game. [S7, p.269]

### Point-Deficit Situation Series
- Practice 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5-point deficits with specific clock settings
- Practice with lead AND deficit for each point spread
- Use card-draw situations: 100 game cards, player draws a card and coaches their team [S7, p.269]

### Two-Minute Game
- Score stays fixed for the entire 2 minutes (e.g., defense always 2 points behind)
- Only the clock decreases — both teams practice their lead-protecting AND comeback habits simultaneously
- Vary the spread: tied, 1 up/behind, 3 up/behind, 5 up/behind [S7, p.268]

### Seven/Nine-Possession Game
Each team keeps the ball for 7 or 9 possessions cycling through: full-court inbounds (man press), full-court inbounds (zone press), three-quarter court inbounds, front-court SLOB, end BLOB, set play vs. man, set play vs. zone. [S7, p.268]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Improvising under pressure** → all late-game scenarios must be pre-planned and rehearsed in practice
2. **Dropping to help on penetration when protecting a 3-point lead** → allows open catch-and-shoot 3s off kick-outs
3. **Neglecting offensive rebounding prevention** → the put-back decides most 1-2 point games; box out on every shot
4. **Fouling the wrong player** → target poor FT shooters; never foul a shooter in a shooting motion with 3-point lead
5. **Not drilling it** → "Jim Calhoun... drills nine of them per practice, and his teams are always prepared." [S7, p.269]
