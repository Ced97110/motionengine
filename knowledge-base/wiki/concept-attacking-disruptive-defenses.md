# Attacking Disruptive Half-Court Defenses

## Summary
Most coaches prepare their offense against standard 2-3 and 3-2 zones but fail to prepare adequately for disruptive zone variations — the 1-3-1, box-and-one, and triangle-and-two. These defenses are specifically designed to disrupt the normal zone attack through traps, denial of the ball to the best player, and tactical gimmicks. The offense must be prepared through deliberate practice, not last-minute game-time adjustments.

The core offensive principles against all disruptive zones are identical: **be patient, align in an overload, maintain proper floor balance and spacing, use player and ball movement, and attack the offensive boards**.

## When to Use
- Pre-game preparation for any opponent that may use gimmick defenses
- Tournament situations where underdog teams use disruptive zones to pull upsets
- When your opponent's 2-3 or 3-2 sets are not producing quality shots
- Whenever a 1-3-1, box-and-one, or triangle-and-two defense is deployed

## Key Principles
1. **Preparation is mandatory** — disruptive zones require special practice attention; they cannot be figured out in-game
2. **Align in an overload** — create 3-on-2 advantages by overloading one side with your best shooters
3. **Patient ball movement** — quick shots against these defenses play into the defense's hands; move the ball to find openings
4. **Floor balance and spacing** — all five players must maintain proper positions to prevent zone collapse
5. **Attack the offensive boards** — ball movement combined with offensive rebounding creates second-chance points
6. **Put best shooters in open zones** — identify which zones are unguarded and fill them with shooters
7. **Identify the weakest defender** — disruptive zones often have an Achilles heel; attack the weakest link

## Against the 1-3-1

### What It Does
Keeps the offense on the perimeter, eliminates layups, and traps corners and wings.

### Offensive Alignment
Use the **LA guard-to-forward set** (misdirection — ball starts one side, reverses to the other for the attack).

### Key Actions
- **O2 passes to O4** and cuts through to the opposite corner
- **O5 screens away for O3**, relocates to the low block (out of low defender's vision)
- **O3 cuts from wing to ball-side elbow** looking for a shot
- **O1 uses a V-cut** to get open at the top of the key
- **O4 looks at O3** coming behind the middle defender; if no pass, O3 continues to ball-side baseline
- **O1 looks to lob to O5** as O5 steps into the lane behind the defense
- If lob unavailable, O1 dribbles and **passes to O2** who may shoot or pass inside to O5
- **O4 relocates to the elbow** forming a triangle with O2 and O5

### Critical Points
- O5 must be positioned out of the low defender's vision before flashing into the lane
- The misdirection (ball starting left, attacking right) is the core of this set
- Always have someone on the offensive boards

## Against the Box-and-One

### What It Does
One defender plays man-to-man on the best player/ball handler; four play zone. Designed to shut down one star or disrupt the playmaker.

### Offensive Alignment
Modified LA offense. X2 is assigned man-to-man to O2.

### Key Actions
- **O1 passes to O3**, gets return pass; passes to O2; O2 passes to O4
- **O2 dives to strong-side block** and **screens X4** (the zone's baseline defender)
- **O3 uses the screen on X4** and clears to the strong-side corner
- **O4 passes to O3** and clears to the weak-side block
- **Realignment**: O1 at wing (ball), O3 in ball-side corner, O5 at high-post elbow ball-side, O4 on weak-side block, O2 on ball-side block screening X4 (occupying two defenders)
- **O1, O3, and O5 establish a passing triangle** — each looks for an open shot
- If O1 reverses on the dribble, **O2 uses O4's screen for a turnout**; O5 crosses the lane to form a new triangle with O4 and O2

### Critical Points
- O2 screening X4 while being guarded man-to-man creates a 2-on-2 situation that stresses the defense
- The passing triangle (O1-O3-O5) finds the open holes and gets best shooters to those spots
- The objective is not to isolate the star — it's to find the open zones in the defense

## Against the Triangle-and-Two

### What It Does
X4 and X5 cover the baseline in tandem (one slides to cover jump shooters, the other covers the low block); X3 covers the middle and top; X1 and X2 play man-to-man on guards. Invites non-shooters (O3, O4, O5) to shoot from outside while denying guards (O1, O2) the ball.

### Offensive Alignment
From half court — triangle overload with shooters and rebounders.

### Key Actions
- **O1 passes to O5** (high post or wing)
- **O1 and O2 dive to their respective same-side low blocks** — creating congestion and screening baseline defenders X4 and X5 or using cross screens
- **Open areas**: baseline corners, wings, and top of key — occupied by O3, O4, and O5 (the unguarded three)
- **O3 with the ball creates 2-on-1 advantages** — either with the corner (O5) against X4, or the top (O4) against X3
- **Ball movement between O3, O4, and O5** with good passes and "taking up slack toward the basket" establishes shooting range
- **O1 and O2 at the blocks** can screen the baseline defenders or cross-screen to get open

### Critical Points
- The three non-guarded players (typically O3, O4, O5) must occupy the open zones, not crowd them
- X4 and X5 are in tandem — when one slides out, the other covers the block; attack the sliding defender
- With patience and discipline, this defense *will* give up open shots in the corners, wings, and top of key
- **Rebounders must attack the glass** — O1 and O2 at the blocks are prime rebounding position
- The shooter must be the *right* player — put your actual perimeter shooters in the open zones

## Common Mistakes
1. Rushing against disruptive defenses → play into the defense's disruptive intent; be patient
2. Best player forcing shots against the man-to-man defender in a box-and-one → reduce their involvement and find the open zone players
3. Failing to maintain floor balance → allows zone defenders to overload one side
4. Not preparing for disruptive defenses in practice → having nothing to run at game time
5. Not attacking the offensive boards → missing second-chance points that disruptive defenses create
