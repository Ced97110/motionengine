# Possession Usage vs. Efficiency Tradeoff

## Summary
One of the most important and counterintuitive insights in *Basketball on Paper* is that **a player's individual offensive efficiency rating tends to decline as their offensive usage (% team possessions) increases**. A player who is highly efficient at 15% usage will almost certainly be less efficient at 30% usage, because they are being forced to take harder shots, create more often against set defenses, and exhaust their most efficient scoring opportunities.

This tradeoff has direct implications for roster construction, lineup decisions, and player development. You cannot simply "turn on" a role player's efficiency at a higher volume, and you should not interpret a star's efficiency as portable to any context.

## When to Use
- Roster construction: don't expect a 125 Off. Rtg. role player to maintain that rating if promoted to primary scorer
- Lineup decisions: understand that asking a player to do more will change their numbers
- Evaluating whether to increase a player's usage in late-game situations
- Interpreting a scorer's efficiency drop when their usage rises mid-season

## Key Principles
1. **Efficiency and usage exist in tension.** The easiest shots get taken first; the more possessions a player uses, the harder their average shot becomes [S16, p.176].
2. **Role players' efficiency is context-dependent.** "That doesn't mean that you can turn efficient Horace Grant into a leading scorer at the same efficiency" [S16, p.176].
3. **The exception proves the rule.** Michael Jordan's ability to maintain elite efficiency (117-126) at 30-36% team possession usage is historically unique — it defines why he is the greatest offensive player in the metric's history [S16, p.163].
4. **Latasha Byears proves the inverse.** By reducing her usage from 27% to 16-18%, her Off. Rtg. improved from 95-97 to 118-125 — a dramatic demonstration that less can be more [S16, p.179].
5. **The tradeoff is non-linear.** Small increases in usage (e.g., 20% to 23%) may not significantly affect efficiency; large jumps (15% to 30%) almost always do.

## Evidence from Career Data

| Player | Low Usage Season | Rtg | High Usage Season | Rtg |
|--------|-----------------|-----|------------------|-----|
| Reggie Miller | 18% (1988-89) | 118 | 24% (1996-97) | 121 |
| Steve Kerr | 12% (1996 Bulls) | 141 | 15% (1992 CLE) | 124 |
| Dennis Scott | 17% (1997 ORL) | 107 | 24% (1992) | 104 |
| Latasha Byears | 16% (2002 LA) | 125 | 27% (1998 SAC) | 95 |
| Chamique Holdsclaw | 27% (2000) | 99 | 32% (2002) | 110 |

Note: Holdsclaw's 2002 improvement at high usage (32% → 110) suggests she was developing as a scorer, but she was still below average despite the higher volume.

## Implications for Roster Construction
- **Balance usage across efficient players.** The best offenses distribute possessions among three or more efficient players (all ≥110 rating, all ≥20% usage) rather than concentrating on one superstar.
- **Don't pay role-player efficiency for star-player responsibility.** A player posting a 125 rating at 13% usage is not as valuable as that number seems if you need them to use 28%.
- **The ideal offensive lineup has multiple credible threats.** This forces defenses to make choices and keeps each player in their efficiency sweet spot.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Promoting efficient role players to primary scorer roles** → Off. Rtg. almost always drops significantly.
2. **Blaming efficiency decline on "effort"** → Often the real cause is increased defensive attention from higher usage.
3. **Ignoring usage when comparing ratings** → A 115 rating at 13% usage is very different from 115 at 28% usage.
