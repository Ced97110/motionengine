# Single-Leg Stance Balance

## Objective
Restore ankle stability, balance, and proprioception after injury; develop single-leg balance required for cutting, layup landings, and running — and reduce risk of re-injury.

## Setup
- 1 player
- Flat stable floor surface (beginner) → foam pad, Dynadisc, or BOSU balance board (advanced)
- No equipment required for basic version

## Execution
1. Stand erect with full body weight distributed through the **affected lower extremity**.
2. Shift all body weight onto the affected leg, keeping a **slight bend in the knee** — do not lock out the knee.
3. Maintain a **fixed, still posture** throughout the balance hold. Focus on a fixed point on the wall for visual stability reference.
4. Perform for a prescribed hold duration (start with 20–30 seconds per leg, build to 60+ seconds).
5. **Progressions:** Introduce an unstable surface (foam pad, Dynadisc, BOSU) or **close the eyes** to remove visual stabilization input and challenge proprioceptors more directly.

> Eyes-closed single-leg balance is significantly more challenging and represents an advanced proprioception demand — introduce only after comfortable on the stable surface. [S2, p.130]

## Coaching Points
- **"Still as a statue"** — the goal is controlled stillness, not wobbling through it
- Slight knee bend is mandatory — straight-leg standing bypasses ankle stabilizers
- Grip the floor with the toes for additional intrinsic foot muscle activation
- Progress to unstable surfaces only when stable-surface balance is mastered
- Monitor for excessive trunk sway — cue the player to use ankle adjustments, not trunk compensation

## Primary Muscles
- **Primary:** Gastrocnemius, soleus, tibialis anterior, extensor hallucis longus, extensor digitorum longus, flexor hallucis longus, flexor digitorum longus, tibialis posterior, peroneals, interossei, lumbricals
- **Secondary:** Gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, hamstrings, quadriceps, erector spinae, rectus abdominis [S2, p.130]

## Basketball Application
Single-leg balance is required during every running stride, every layup landing, and every cutting motion. Players frequently sprain ankles landing on an opponent's foot — the ability to maintain stability on an unstable surface can prevent this from causing a serious sprain. [S2, p.130]

## Progressions
1. **Beginner:** Stable flat floor, eyes open, 20-second holds
2. **Intermediate:** Foam pad or Dynadisc, eyes open; increase hold duration to 45–60 seconds
3. **Advanced:** BOSU or rocker board, eyes closed; add contralateral arm/leg movements while maintaining single-leg balance
