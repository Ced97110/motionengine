# Lower-Body Plyometrics for Basketball

## Summary
Plyometric training uses the stretch-shortening cycle of muscles — a rapid eccentric (landing/loading) phase followed by an immediate concentric (takeoff) phase — to develop explosive power. For basketball players, lower-body plyometrics directly improve the physical attributes most critical to performance: vertical jump height for rebounds and contested shots, reactive floor quickness for beats-opponent first steps, lateral explosiveness for cuts and defensive slides, and landing mechanics that reduce injury risk.

The key to plyometric transfer is **specificity**: exercises must closely mirror the demands of basketball movements. Vertical jumps transfer to rebounding and shot contesting; single-leg jumps transfer to layup push-off; lateral hops transfer to jab steps and defensive cuts; split jumps transfer to fast-break acceleration after rebounds. [S2, pp.111-119]

## When to Use
- **Pre-season conditioning**: Build the explosive base before full-contact practice begins
- **In-season maintenance**: Lower volume, high specificity to maintain power without accumulated fatigue
- **Post-injury return**: Progress from balance and proprioception work through plyometrics back to sport-specific explosive demands
- **Individual skill training**: Pair plyometric sessions with technical skill work for combined physical and technical gains

## Key Principles
1. **Minimize ground contact time**: The plyometric stimulus comes from a rapid eccentric-to-concentric transition — "the floor is hot, get off it fast."
2. **Arm drive matters**: Simultaneous upward arm throw on every takeoff significantly increases jump height.
3. **Soft landing mechanics are non-negotiable**: Land with slight flexion at the ankle, knee, hip, and trunk — stiff-legged landings risk ACL and patellar injury.
4. **Progress box height and distance conservatively**: Start low, master technique, then increase load — ego-driven box height selection is a leading cause of plyometric injuries.
5. **Alternate bilateral and unilateral work**: Basketball requires both two-foot (double-leg) and one-foot (single-leg) explosive actions — train both.
6. **Lateral plyometrics are as important as vertical**: Cutting, defensive slides, and jab steps demand lateral hip explosiveness, not just vertical power.
7. **Volume and intensity are inverse**: High-intensity plyometrics (depth jumps, maximum-height box jumps) require full recovery between sets — do not circuit with other fatiguing exercises.

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Lower-body plyometrics are position-agnostic — every basketball player benefits from explosive leg power, quicker cuts, and better landing mechanics.
- **PF/C**: Emphasis on bilateral vertical jump (rebounding), depth jumps (contested shot contests), and soft landing mechanics (high-contact landing scenarios).
- **PG/SG/SF**: Emphasis on single-leg takeoff (layups), lateral hops (defensive agility, jab steps), and split jumps (fast-break first step).

## Core Basketball Plyometric Exercises

### Single-Leg Hurdle Jump
Jump over a row of hurdles off one leg, landing and immediately taking off again on the same leg. Builds layup push-off power and reactive single-leg quickness. [S2, p.111]

### Depth Jump Into Jump Shot
Step off a box (12–30 in.), land on both feet simultaneously, immediately jump to maximum height while simulating a jump shot. Trains the stretch-shortening cycle under game-specific conditions. [S2, pp.112-113]

### Multiple-Box Jump
Consecutive jump-on, jump-off sequence across 2–4 boxes. Develops reactive floor quickness — the speed of the takeoff after landing is the key training variable. [S2, pp.114-115]

### Skater Hop
Lateral single-leg hops with trailing foot touch between each hop. Builds lateral hip power (gluteus medius, tensor fasciae latae) essential for cuts, jab steps, and defensive slides. [S2, pp.116-117]

### Split Jump Into Run
Split squat jump from half-kneeling position, landing in split stance, immediately sprinting. Trains the fast-break first step out of a rebound landing. [S2, pp.118-119]

## Primary Muscles Trained
- **Takeoff**: Gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, quadriceps (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus intermedius, vastus medius)
- **Landing**: All of the above + biceps femoris, semimembranosus, semitendinosus (hamstrings)
- **Lateral movements**: Add tensor fasciae latae

## Common Mistakes
1. **Jumping off the box instead of stepping** (depth jump) → Stepping creates the correct landing impact; jumping off reduces the plyometric stimulus
2. **Stiff-legged landings** → Cue "absorb with the knee, hip, and trunk" — all three joints must flex
3. **Knee cave on single-leg landing** → Indicates lateral hip weakness; regress to bodyweight single-leg balance work before progressing
4. **Too much rest on the floor between boxes** → Defeats the reactive purpose; cue "floor is hot"
5. **Box height too high too soon** → Progress height only after technique is mastered at lower heights
6. **Neglecting lateral plyometrics** → Coaches who only program vertical jumps leave lateral quickness underdeveloped
