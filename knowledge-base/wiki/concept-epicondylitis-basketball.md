# Lateral and Medial Epicondylitis in Basketball

## Summary
Lateral and medial epicondylitis are overuse injuries resulting from repetitive wrist extension and flexion respectively. Despite being called "epicondylitis," the pathology is actually microtrauma and tendon degeneration — not true inflammation. The extensor carpi radialis brevis (ECRB) is most commonly involved in lateral epicondylitis; the flexor-pronator origin in medial epicondylitis. Ninety percent of athletes resolve with nonoperative management. Corticosteroid injections are controversial and not evidence-based over less invasive methods. [S10, pp.287-288]

## When to Suspect
- Gradual onset of achy or burning pain over the lateral (tennis elbow) or medial elbow
- Pain worsened by resisted wrist extension (lateral) or flexion (medial)
- Grip weakness is a common complaint
- History of repetitive overhead or wrist-intensive activities
- Risk factors: obesity, tobacco use [S10, p.287]

## Pathoanatomy
- **Lateral epicondylitis**: ECRB tendon (most commonly), EDC may also be involved [S10, p.287]
- **Medial epicondylitis**: flexor-pronator origin [S10, p.287]
- **Key point**: pathology is **degeneration and microtrauma**, NOT inflammation despite the naming [S10, p.287]

## Key Principles

### Diagnosis
1. Primarily clinical diagnosis
2. Tenderness along lateral or medial epicondyle
3. Lateral: pain reproduced with resisted long finger and wrist extension
4. Further imaging generally not required; if uncertain:
   - X-rays: usually normal
   - Ultrasound: hypoechoic swelling of tendon origin [S10, p.287]
   - MRI: intratendinous signal change [S10, p.287]

### Treatment Hierarchy
1. **Activity modification** — avoid inciting activities [S10, p.287]
2. **Forearm support band** (tennis elbow strap)
3. **Physical therapy** — eccentric exercises and stretching; avoid lifting with palm down (reverse curls) [S10, p.287]
4. **Deep massage** with or without ultrasound
5. **Corticosteroid injection**: controversial; no evidence of improved outcomes vs. less invasive methods; one RCT: 69% success with injection vs. **91% success with therapy** [S10, p.287]
6. **Surgery** (after failed nonoperative): excision of the affected portion of the tendon; one RCT showed no additional benefit of surgery over sham procedure [S10, p.288]

### Post-Surgical Rehabilitation
- Wrist splint for 2 weeks
- Physical therapy starts at 4 weeks post-surgery
- Return to weight lifting or sports: 3 months post-surgery [S10, p.288]

## Key Facts for Coaches and Medical Staff
- 90% of athletes resolve symptoms with nonoperative management [S10, p.287]
- No established best-practice nonoperative protocol [S10, p.287]
- Corticosteroid injections are controversial — physical therapy superior in RCT evidence [S10, p.287]
- Surgery is rarely needed and may offer no advantage over sham procedure [S10, p.288]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Rushing to cortisone injection** → Physical therapy has superior evidence; injection should not be first-line [S10, p.287]
2. **Ignoring eccentric exercise component** → Eccentric strengthening is essential to tendon remodeling [S10, p.287]
3. **Confusing radial tunnel syndrome with lateral epicondylitis** → Radial tunnel syndrome presents with lateral elbow pain but NO motor weakness; epicondylitis involves more specific tendon tenderness [S10, p.289]
