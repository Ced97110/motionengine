# Back-Drop Flip (Front Flip Chain — Step 8)

## Objective
Attempt the first true airborne front flip (no hands on the floor) and land safely in a back-drop/semi-squat position — the critical "missing link" step between the flyspring and the running front flip.

## Setup
- **Players**: 1 (solo)
- **Equipment**: MAXIMUM PADDING IS REQUIRED — thick mattress, gymnastics foam pit, or equivalent. This is a mandatory safety requirement.
- **Space**: Open area with thick padding covering the landing zone

## Execution
1. Take a run-up of several steps to build momentum.
2. Perform a *block jump* (see the blocking technique) with both feet simultaneously, throwing the hands downward (this time without contacting the floor).
3. Jump up hard, simultaneously throwing the hands down and pulling the curled upper body down at the hips — this generates forward rotation.
4. Immediately pull the knees up to the chest in a tight *tuck* — this is what drives the rotation speed.
5. As rotation carries the body over, begin extending the legs. (This prevents landing flat on the back — feet should contact first to protect the spine.)
6. The bottoms of the feet should be the first body part to touch the ground. Feet act as shock absorbers; then glutes and back contact the ground. Spreading hands can also absorb extra force. [S9, pp.160]

**Key X-ray**: Almost every athlete attempting the front flip will go through a period of landing on their glutes. This is expected and is "a big part of the process." Embrace the back-drop flip as the missing link — it is not a failure, it is a training stage. [S9, p.160]

## Coaching Points
- **"Never land flat on your back."** — Always extend the legs so the feet contact first, even in the back-drop landing. This protects the spine regardless of the surface.
- Tight tuck = faster rotation. Loose tuck = under-rotation and back landing.
- Block with both feet equally — asymmetrical take-off causes sideways rotation.
- Padding is non-negotiable at this stage. A thick mattress is explicitly recommended.
- Track the landing point: glutes → semi-squat fall-back → semi-squat without falling back → Step 9.

## Progressions
1. **Beginner (Regression)**: Begin with absolute maximum padding (thick mattress). Land wherever you land — the goal is to feel the rotation and develop timing.
2. **Intermediate**: Weight begins to shift over the feet at landing (semi-squat position, but falling back).
3. **Advanced**: Consistent semi-squat landing without falling back → remove padding and move to Step 9 (Running Front Flip).

**Safety Warning**: This is the one step in the chain where minimal-equipment philosophy is explicitly suspended. Always use maximum padding. Bare floor or thin mat attempts are dangerous and should never be made before the semi-squat landing is consistent. [S9, p.160]
