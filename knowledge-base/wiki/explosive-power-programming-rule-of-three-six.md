# Power Programming: The Rule of Three and the Rule of Six

## Summary
Programming explosive power training requires a radically different approach than muscle-building or endurance training. Two simple rules govern all power training: the **Rule of Three** (maximum reps per set) and the **Rule of Six** (total reps per exercise per session). Together, they create a complete, flexible programming system that maximizes speed-strength while preventing fatigue-induced slowness and injury [S9, pp.4-8].

The core insight: the first rep is the fastest and most powerful rep. By the 10th rep of a squat jump, fatigue toxins have built up and the movement is the *slowest* it will be in the set. Training high reps on explosive exercises literally teaches the body to move slowly. Low reps ensure every repetition is done at peak power output [S9, p.5].

## When to Use
- Any time an athlete is performing power-based explosive exercises: jumps, power push-ups, Olympic lift variations, plyometric drills
- When the goal is speed-strength development, first-step quickness, or vertical jump improvement
- *Not* applicable to skill-based movements (which use Time Surfing or Consolidation Training) or endurance/hypertrophy work [S9, p.6]

## Key Principles

### The Rule of Three
1. **Never exceed 3 reps per set** for any power/explosive exercise [S9, p.4]
2. Valid set structures: 1-rep sets ("singles"), 2-rep sets ("doubles"), 3-rep sets ("triples") [S9, p.4]
3. **Why low reps?** "By the tenth rep, fatigue toxins have built up in your muscles, energy has been depleted, and that jump is the slowest jump." [S9, p.5]
4. Safety: Higher reps on explosive movements increase error risk as fatigue accumulates; errors on high-velocity moves cause injury [S9, p.5]
5. Even elite powerlifters and weightlifters use very low rep sets to become the strongest humans — low reps drive neuromuscular adaptation for speed-strength [S9, p.5]

### The Rule of Six
6. **Total reps per exercise = a multiple of six** [S9, p.6]
   - Beginners: **6 total working reps** per exercise
   - Intermediates: **12 total working reps** per exercise
   - Advanced: **18 total working reps** per exercise
7. Working reps = all reps performed after the warm-up; warm-up reps don't count [S9, p.7]
8. The rules mesh together to give multiple valid set/rep combinations (see below)

### Rest Between Sets
9. Rest **10-30 seconds** between sets — long enough to get your breath back and be ready [S9, p.10]
10. Maximum rest: **3 minutes** — beyond this, neurological benefits of the previous set begin to fade [S9, p.10]
11. **Never sit or lie down** between explosive sets — this tells the nervous system to downshift through the gears. Stay on your feet. [S9, p.10]

### Level Definitions
12. **Beginner**: new to explosive training; returning from layoff/injury; finds the exercise extremely difficult even once [S9, p.9]
13. **Advanced**: can easily handle at least step 7 of the chain being worked [S9, p.9]
14. **Intermediate**: everything between beginner and advanced [S9, p.9]

## Set/Rep Combination Examples (for 6 total reps)
| Combination | Description | Pros |
|---|---|---|
| 6 × 1 | Six singles | Maximum focus per rep; exploits full CNS concentration |
| 3 × 2 | Three doubles | Exploits myotatic rebound; good focus |
| 2 × 3 | Two triples | "Bread-and-butter" for many coaches; efficient |
| 3×1, 1×3 | Mixed hybrid | Variety; keeps the nervous system engaged |
| 1×3, 1×2, 1×1 | Descending hybrid | Pyramid down; another valid hybrid approach |

"There are plenty of roads to Mecca, my friend. There are lots of different options as long as you follow the Rule of Three and the Rule of Six." [S9, p.8]

## Player Responsibilities
Applicable to all positions performing explosive training:
- **All positions**: Apply Rule of Three to all jump training, plyometric work, and explosive push-up variations
- **Coaches**: Use Rule of Six to create simple, scalable session volumes for players at different levels — don't over-program
- **Athletes**: Rotate between singles, doubles, triples, and hybrids from session to session for variety while maintaining the rules

## Variations
### Conditioning vs. Power Goals
If an athlete wants to build endurance or demonstrate high-rep clap push-ups, ignore the Rule of Three — those are endurance goals, not speed-power goals. The rules only apply when the objective is explosiveness and speed-strength [S9, p.6].

### Multiple Exercises in a Session
Athletes may use multiple exercises within their set/rep structure. Distribute the 6/12/18 total rep budget across chosen exercises. All power movements have value and can generate linear improvement [S9, p.10].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Doing 10+ reps on squat jumps** → training slow movement; stop after 1-3 reps to preserve maximum power output [S9, p.5]
2. **Racing through progressions** → reach the Master Step and declare victory; instead, keep building linearly higher/more powerful on the same exercise [S9, p.3]
3. **Sitting or lying between sets** → signals the CNS to downshift; stay standing [S9, p.10]
4. **Treating 6 reps as "not enough"** → more is not better for explosives; excessive volume increases injury risk from joint/soft tissue overload [S9, p.9]
5. **Using these rules for endurance or hypertrophy goals** → these rules are specifically for speed-power; different goals require different protocols [S9, p.6]
