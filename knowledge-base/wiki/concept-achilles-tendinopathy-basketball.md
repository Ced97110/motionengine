# Achilles Tendinopathy in Basketball

## Summary
Achilles tendinopathy (AT) is a leading cause of disability in basketball players, enabled by the Achilles tendon's critical role in storing and releasing elastic energy during explosive movements like jumping, sprinting, and cutting. Unlike acute Achilles rupture, tendinopathy is a chronic overuse condition defined by pain, swelling, and impaired performance. Players with Achilles tendinopathy have a better chance of return if they are **younger in age and early in their professional career** [S10, p.498]. Nonoperative management should be attempted for a minimum of 3–6 months before surgical consideration [S10, p.499].

## When to Use
- Any basketball player with posterior lower-leg/heel pain aggravated by activity
- Morning stiffness or stiffness after rest that improves with warm-up — classic AT symptom
- Players with lateral ankle instability (pronation "whipping action" on the AT) or forefoot varus [S10, p.498]

## Key Principles

### Etiology and Risk Factors
1. **Excessive tendon loading** during training — exacerbated by imbalance between muscle power and tendon elasticity [S10, p.498]
2. **Ankle plantar flexor strength** and **dorsiflexion excursion** are the two primary predictors of Achilles overuse injury [S10, p.498]
3. **Lateral ankle instability** → excessive hindfoot motion → compensatory pronation → "whipping action" on AT → tendinopathy [S10, p.498]
4. **Plantaris tendon** may contribute to midportion tendinopathy via adhesions with the paratenon [S10, p.498]
5. **Extrinsic factors**: Changes in training pattern, poor technique, previous injuries, footwear, hard/slippery/slanting surfaces [S10, p.498]
6. **Gastrocnemius contracture** is a critical intrinsic contributor — jumping athletes are predisposed; must be assessed (Silfverskiöld test) and addressed [S10, pp.500-501]

### Two Distinct Clinical Entities
| Type | Location | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Non-insertional (midportion) | 2–7 cm from distal insertion | Tenderness/thickening moves with ankle ROM |
| Insertional (enthesopathy) | Posterior calcaneus | Fixed tenderness; calcifications; aggravated by dorsiflexion |

[S10, p.499]

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Features
- Activity-limiting pain and chronic swelling of the AT
- **Morning stiffness** or stiffness after inactivity — often the first symptom
- Pain at beginning AND end of training sessions with a period of diminished discomfort during activity ("warm-up" effect)
- Pain is often a **late symptom** of tendinopathy
- **Midportion tendinopathy**: Tenderness, thickening/nodularity, swelling that DECREASES when tendon is under tension
- **Paratendinitis**: Swelling and tenderness in central 1/3; swelling/tenderness remain FIXED relative to malleoli through ROM
- **Insertional tendinopathy**: Pain/swelling/tenderness at posterior calcaneus; aggravated by active to passive motion [S10, p.499]

### Imaging
| Modality | Key Findings | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| X-ray | Soft tissue contour deviation; insertional calcifications/enthesophytes | Baseline only |
| Ultrasonography (US) | Enlarged tendon; fibrillation; hypoechoid areas; power Doppler detects neovascularization | Best for diagnosis and follow-up |
| MRI | Internal morphology, paratendinopathy vs. main body; hyperintensity | Use when US inconclusive; high sensitivity — correlate with symptoms carefully |

[S10, p.499]

## Management — Nonoperative

### Core Principles
- Same principles as patellar tendinopathy (see [[concept-patellar-tendinopathy-basketball]])
- Minimum **3–6 months** nonoperative management before surgical consideration [S10, p.499]
- 29% of patients required surgery during an 8-year follow-up in one prospective study [S10, p.499]

### Exercise Therapy
- **Rest + eccentric exercises** are the mainstay — 60–80% of patients can safely continue preferred activity [S10, p.500]
- Essential: Stretching of the gastrocnemius/soleus complex (jumping athletes are predisposed to gastrocnemius contracture) [S10, p.500]
- **Eccentric + ESWT combined** produces higher success rate than either modality alone [S10, p.500]
- Anti-inflammatory medications: Modest short-term improvement; facilitate eccentric exercise; caution — analgesic effect may allow players to ignore early symptoms and cause further damage [S10, p.500]

### Injection Therapy
- **Corticosteroids**: Not recommended — limited benefit with potential tendon rupture risk [S10, p.500]
- **PRP**: Multiple injections preferred over single; however, recent literature suggests equivocal efficacy in chronic AT vs. saline — further RCT evidence needed [S10, p.500]
- **High-volume ultrasound-guided injections** (10 mL 0.5% bupivacaine + 25 mg hydrocortisone acetate + up to 40 mL normal saline): Short- and long-term pain relief in 70% of patients with resistant AT; comparable to ESWT [S10, p.500]

### Orthotics
- **Heel pads**: Alleviate insertional AT; clinical evidence limited [S10, p.500]
- **AirHeel brace** (intermittent compression): Alternative to eccentric training for patients with activity-limiting pain; no clinical difference vs. eccentric training in chronic AT [S10, p.500]

## Management — Operative

Approximately one-third of patients fail 3–6 months of conservative management [S10, p.500].

**First assess for gastrocnemius contracture** (Silfverskiöld test) — gastrocnemius recession alone may resolve refractory AT [S10, p.501]

| Condition | Surgical Option |
|---|---|
| Isolated paratendinopathy | Minimally invasive stripping; endoscopic; or open paratenon debridement |
| Midportion tendinopathy | Percutaneous longitudinal tenotomy; open debridement + tubularization |
| >50% tendon pathologic | Flexor hallucis longus (FHL) tendon transfer |
| Insertional tendinopathy | Debridement + reattachment; calcaneal bone resection |
| Nonviable tendon | Hamstring autograft reconstruction |
| Refractory with contracture | Gastrocnemius recession |

Surgery for insertional AT: 71% satisfactory return to sports when surgical care is tailored to degree of tendon involvement [S10, p.503]

### Minimally Invasive Stripping Technique
- Prone position, tourniquet to 250 mmHg
- Two 0.5 cm incisions at proximal AT origin (medial and lateral); two at distal end
- Small hemostat frees AT of adhesions; nonabsorbable suture passed proximal → retrieved distal → used to strip posterior tendon periphery from proximal to distal [S10, p.501]

### Percutaneous Longitudinal Tenotomy
- #11 scalpel blade inserted parallel to tendon fiber long axis at point of maximal tenderness
- Cutting edge directed caudally through full tendon thickness
- Blade angled at 45° medially and laterally; then reversed 180° and process repeated [S10, p.502]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not stretching gastrocnemius/soleus** → Contracture is common in jumping athletes and is a primary extrinsic risk factor; always address flexibility alongside loading [S10, p.500]
2. **Inadequate nonoperative trial** → Minimum 3–6 months required before surgical consideration; most patients respond if given sufficient time [S10, p.499]
3. **Ignoring insertional vs. non-insertional distinction** → Different treatment protocols, imaging findings, and surgical approaches for each [S10, p.499]
4. **Using corticosteroids** → Risk of tendon rupture without long-term benefit [S10, p.500]
