# Post Defense — Fronting and Three-Quartering

## Summary
Fronting the post is a primary tactic for taking away the offensive team's inside game and forcing them into uncomfortable situations. The core principle is that the defender's position relative to the post player must be dictated by where the ball is on the perimeter. Used correctly with active perimeter pressure on passers and mandatory weak-side help, fronting the post prevents easy entry passes and eliminates the low post as a scoring option. [S1, pp.50, 58-59]

## When to Use
- When guarding a dominant low-post scorer who receives the ball deep and scores at high efficiency
- When the perimeter defenders can apply enough pressure to make the entry pass risky
- In combination with weak-side help to protect against the lob over the top
- When you want to force the offense to go to options they don't regularly practice [S1, p.49]

## Key Principles
1. **Ball dictates position** — general rule from Figure 5.17 [S1, p.58]:
   - Ball in the **corner** or **on top**: play **three-quarter** position (defender on the high side between post and ball)
   - Ball on the **wing**: play **full front** (defender completely between passer and post player)
   - Ball at the **top**: three-quarter or full front depending on scheme
2. **Step across with the inside foot** to establish the front position as the ball moves to the wing.
3. **Sit on the legs** of the post player — this prevents the offensive player from jumping to receive a lob pass over the front. [S1, p.58]
4. **Perimeter pressure is mandatory.** Fronting the post is only effective when guards put pressure on passers, making it difficult to enter the ball. [S1, p.50]
5. **Always provide weak-side help** when fronting the post — without it, a skip pass and lob will result in an easy basket. [S1, p.50]
6. **Meet post players early in transition.** Stand up offensive big men before they can seal and get comfortable low-post position. [S1, p.48]

## Defending the Post Feeder's Cut (2-on-2 Application)
When the post entry pass is made from the wing, the **passer's defender** must:
- Drop level with the ball with butt to the **baseline**
- Force the cutter to go **up the middle** toward defensive help, not toward the baseline
- This takes away the cutter's choice of direction entirely [S1, pp.58-59, Figure 5.19]

Alternative option: sag toward the middle to force the cutter baseline if that suits your defensive scheme.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PF/C (Post Defender)**: Three-quarter or front based on ball position; sit on legs; step with inside foot to establish front
- **PG/SG/SF (Perimeter Defenders)**: Pressure the passers so entry is difficult; jump to the ball on passes; provide weak-side help on lob coverage
- **Weak-Side Forward**: Drop to help-side position; ready to protect against lob over the front or skip pass

## Common Mistakes
1. Fronting without perimeter pressure on passers → the entry pass is easy and the front is beaten by a lob
2. Not sitting on the post player's legs → offensive player jumps to receive the lob pass
3. Not providing weak-side help when fronting → skip pass or lob results in an uncontested basket
4. Post defender getting beat to position → meet post players early before they establish low-block seal
5. Giving the cutter a choice of direction after the entry pass → force cutter one way by positioning butt to baseline

## Variations
### Three-Quarter Only
Always three-quarter regardless of ball position — simpler to teach, easier to recover from, but gives up more of the front-side flash.

### Full-Front Only
Always front — most aggressive, requires the best perimeter pressure and strongest weak-side help.
