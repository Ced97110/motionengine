# Whole-Part-Whole Teaching Method

## Summary
The whole-part-whole teaching method is one of the most effective frameworks for developing basketball skills. A skill or technique is first introduced to the entire team as a complete concept (the first "whole"), then isolated into breakdown drills that target specific components (the "part"), and finally reassembled into team settings at game speed (the second "whole"). The method progresses from walk-through to game speed systematically, with defensive resistance increasing as offensive players develop confidence. [S6, pp.265-267]

Footwork and pivoting skills require this patient, systematic approach because they are foundational to every offensive and defensive action. Shortcuts in teaching footwork always show up in games.

## When to Use
- When introducing any new offensive or defensive skill or concept to a team
- When players are executing a skill at partial proficiency but breaking down under defensive pressure
- When adding complexity to a motion (e.g., moving from no defender to dummy defender to live defender)
- At the start of a season, and as a review mechanism mid-season whenever errors appear in games

## Key Principles
1. **Introduce the whole concept first**: Demonstrate the complete action to the entire team, clarifying every player's role and responsibility before any practice begins.
2. **Walk-through phase**: Every player walks through the technique under close coaching observation. "Players learn best by doing." Allow questions and corrections before increasing pace. [S6, p.265]
3. **Speed up incrementally**: After the walk-through, increase pace progressively through breakdown drills — slow → moderate → game speed. Never rush to game speed before technique is ingrained.
4. **Breakdown drills isolate specific techniques**: Use focused drills (e.g., dribbler + screener + cutter + dummy defender on ball handler) to isolate and repetition the exact movements needed.
5. **Progressive defensive resistance**: Dummy defenders start passive. As offensive proficiency grows, defenders increase intensity toward live defense. Add a defender on the screener only after the basic action is solid.
6. **Return to the whole**: Bring individual players back together in a full team setting to run through the skill. The difficulty the defense places on the offense should reflect the team's current proficiency level.
7. **Rotate all players through all roles**: In breakdown drills, every player must play both the offensive and defensive side of every skill. No player should experience only one role.
8. **Short drill windows, multiple repetitions**: Breakdown drills should be short (e.g., 55 seconds of work per role) to maximize mental alertness and productivity. Return to the drill later in practice for further reinforcement.
9. **Competition is the final validator**: Every drill's final phase must include competition (individuals vs. individuals, or groups vs. groups) at game speed with game-realistic conditions.

## Mass Footwork Drill Application
Diagram 6-31 illustrates the whole-part-whole method applied to a mass footwork drill:
- Three 4-player groups (full 12-player squad) work simultaneously across the court
- Each group has defenders on perimeter ball handlers and an offensive post player
- Coaches can limit dribbles available and what post players can do after receiving
- Players rotate to the next position every 55 seconds (5-second transition window)
- In 4 minutes: all four roles covered by each player in a group
- Repeated 2+ times with different starting locations = 12 minutes total, all players proficient in both perimeter passer/feeder and interior post receiving/attacking roles

## Common Mistakes
1. **Skipping the walk-through** → Players execute the motion at the wrong speed before technique is established, embedding incorrect habits.
2. **Keeping defenders passive too long** → Players practice correctly only without resistance, then break down in games. Increase defensive pressure as quickly as players can handle it.
3. **Not rotating players through all roles** → Some players never practice the defensive or post aspects, creating "weakest link" breakdowns in games.
4. **Drilling too long in one phase** → Mental fatigue and diminishing returns. Short, focused windows with planned returns later in practice are more effective than marathon sessions.
5. **Skipping the return to the whole** → Individual proficiency must be validated in the team setting before it transfers to games.
