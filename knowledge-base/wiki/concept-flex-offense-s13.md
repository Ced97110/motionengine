# Flex Offense

## Summary
The Flex Offense is a disciplined continuity half-court pattern originating from the early-1970s shuffle-cut that was refined by adding baseline screens and a pick-the-picker (screen-the-screener) option. It is a three-player system involving two screens on every cycle: one baseline screen for the cutter and one pin-down (screen-the-screener) for the first screener. Player positions are interchangeable, with all five players cycling through all five roles over a complete possession.

The flex targets layups and open 15-foot jumpers from the elbow area. It is best suited for patient, disciplined teams with mobile big men who can pass, make decisions, post up, and hit the mid-range jumper. Gary Williams (2002 NCAA champion, Maryland) and Al Skinner (Boston College) were notable proponents [S13, pp.138-141]. See also [[concept-flex-offense]] for the version from S7.

## When to Use
- When the team has patient, disciplined players who will execute precise screens and timing
- When the opponent struggles to fight through multiple screens
- When the coach wants to control tempo and force a zone response from defenders who can't stop the layup action
- **Not recommended** when the team is trailing significantly (slow scoring pace)

## Key Principles
1. **Precision passing** — timing the pass to the cutter is everything; a late pass kills the layup opportunity.
2. **Proper screening technique** — screens must be set at the correct angle and held until the cutter clears.
3. **Shot selection** — the flex produces layups or 15-foot (4.5 m) jumpers; no bad shots come from a properly run flex.
4. **Patience** — the offense requires multiple cycles; players must trust the pattern and not freelance.
5. **Decision making** — cutters read high or baseline; screeners decide between down screen and screen-the-screener timing [S13, p.142].

## Player Responsibilities
All five positions are interchangeable and each player cycles through every role:
- **Baseline cutter**: Uses the low-block screen; chooses high (middle) or baseline cut looking for a layup or post-up.
- **Low-block screener**: Sets the baseline screen, then immediately becomes the receiver of the pin-down (screen-the-screener).
- **Elbow receiver**: Catches at the free-throw lane elbow area for a potential 15-foot shot; if not open, continues the pattern by setting a down screen or screen-the-screener.
- **Perimeter ball handler**: Makes the pass to the elbow and sets the down screen or screen-the-screener for the baseline screener.
- **Corner safety**: Holds the deep corner as a spacing and quick-outlet option.

## The Five Cycles (Complete Pattern)

**Cycle 1 (Setup):** O1 and O2 in two-guard front; O3 and O4 in opposite deep corners; O5 on the strong-side block. O1 passes to O2. O3 makes a timed shuffle cut (high middle or baseline) off O5's screen for a layup or post-up. O1 sets a down screen (screen-the-screener) for O5, who relocates to the elbow. O2 looks first for O3, then for O5 at the elbow. O2 passes to O5; if O5 is not open, play continues.

**Cycle 2:** O2 sets a down screen (screen-the-screener) for O3 and relocates to the left deep corner. O3 sets a screen for O4, then uses O2's screen to the elbow. O5 looks for O4 using O3's screen (baseline or middle), then for O3 at the elbow.

**Cycle 3:** O5 passes to O3 (optional up-the-lane jump shot for O3). O5 sets a screen-the-screener for O4 and relocates to the deep right corner. O3 looks for O1 running the baseline off a screen for a layup or post-up. If no pass to O1, O4 relocates to the elbow for a pass from O3.

**Cycle 4:** After O3's pass to O4, O2 runs the baseline off a screen from O1. O3 sets a down screen on O1, who relocates to the elbow for a pass from O4.

**Cycle 5:** O4 passes to O1. O5 runs off O2's baseline screen for a layup or post-up. O4 down screens for O2 and slides to the deep right corner. O2 relocates to the right elbow (free-throw line extended). O1 surveys all options.

At this point, **all five players have run a complete cycle through all five flex positions** [S13, pp.139-141].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Cutter choosing the wrong lane** (high or baseline) without reading the defense → teach cutters to read the defender's position before committing.
2. **Guards getting stuck in the corners or under the basket** on turnovers/long rebounds → opponents push the ball in transition for easy fast-break points; designate one player to read when to abort and get back.
3. **Imprecise screen angles** → the screener must set at the exact angle that forces the defender to go the long way around.
4. **Impatience** — teams fall into freelancing when the pattern doesn't produce a quick shot → coaches must reinforce that the flex requires multiple cycles.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths
1. Effective for disciplined teams with a patient coach
2. Players learn screening, timing, passing, decision-making, and shot selection simultaneously
3. Dictates tempo — methodical when properly executed

### Weaknesses
1. **Not a catch-up offense** — points come at a slow pace; unsuitable when trailing
2. Requires total buy-in from coaches and all players; one non-compliant player breaks the pattern
3. Guards screening on the low blocks or in the corners are dangerously out of position for defensive transition on long rebounds or turnovers [S13, p.141]
