# Orthobiologics for ACL Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) tears are among the most common severe sports-related injuries. Basketball's demands of jumping, cutting, and deceleration place players at high risk. Orthobiologics — platelet-rich plasma (PRP), bone marrow aspirate concentrate (BMAC), and stem-cell/cell-based therapies — have been investigated as adjuncts to both conservative management and ACL reconstruction (ACLR) surgery, aiming to improve graft healing, shorten return-to-play, and reduce re-injury rates.

The overall clinical evidence is mixed: PRP likely improves ACL graft maturation (up to 50% by MRI) but has not consistently shown superior clinical outcomes. Leukocyte-rich PRP (LR-PRP) may be detrimental, while leukocyte-poor PRP (LP-PRP) and BMAC show more promise in specific applications [S10, pp.588-593].

## When to Use
- Partial ACL tears where surgery is being deferred — consider intra-ligamentous PRP/PRGF injection
- ACL reconstruction (ACLR) — orthobiologics may be added to the graft at time of surgery to accelerate ligamentization
- Graft harvest site (BTB autograft) — PRP at donor site may reduce anterior knee pain
- Young athletes (≤21 years) — biologic augmentation of hamstrings autograft ACLR showed reduced re-injury rates in one cohort study

## Key Principles

### Basic Science
1. **Growth factors drive ACL healing**: TGF-β1, FGF-2, PDGF, VEGF, and insulin-like GF all have receptors on ACL fibroblasts and promote cell proliferation, migration, ECM production, and collagen synthesis [S10, p.588].
2. **PRP mechanism**: Platelet activation releases concentrated growth factors locally at the injury site. Fibrin clot instability in the intra-articular environment is a major limitation — PRP dissolves prematurely in synovial fluid containing plasmin [S10, p.589].
3. **Scaffold solutions**: Combining PRP with a collagen hydrogel scaffold (collagen-PRP hydrogel) protects PRP from degradation and significantly improves ACL repair outcomes in preclinical porcine models [S10, p.589].
4. **Stem cells**: BMSCs, ACL-derived vascular stem cells, TDSCs, and hUCB-MSCs all enhance ACL healing during early phase in animal models. Clinical evidence remains limited [S10, pp.589-590].
5. **LR-PRP caution**: Leukocyte-rich PRP increases local tissue inflammation and may delay or alter healing — nearly all clinical ACLR studies used LR-PRP, which may explain mixed results [S10, p.592].

### Clinical Applications

#### Partial ACL Tears (Without Repair)
1. Intra-ligamentous injection of PRGF (platelet-rich growth factors) during arthroscopy: 18/19 professional soccer players returned to sport in one study, average 16.2 weeks (Tegner 9 level) [S10, pp.590-591].
2. Autologous conditioned plasma (ACP) injection: 71.1% return-to-sport at mean 5.8 months (85.8% subjective return to pre-injury level) at 33-month follow-up [S10, p.591].
3. **Limitation**: Absence of control groups makes it difficult to attribute outcomes specifically to PRP.

#### Partial ACL Tears (With Repair)
4. ACL suture repair + intercondylar notch microfracture + PRP: 78% return to sports at 5-year follow-up, significant reduction in anterior translation (4.1 mm → 1.4 mm) [S10, p.591].

#### ACL Reconstruction
5. PRP improves graft maturation on MRI by up to 50% based on a systematic review of 11 controlled trials [S10, p.591].
6. Radice et al.: Time to complete graft homogeneity — 177 days (PRP) vs. 369 days (control); BTB subgroup: 109 days vs. 363 days [S10, p.592].
7. **Clinical outcomes (functional scores)**: Most studies show NO significant difference in IKDC, KT-1000/KT-2000, KOOS, or Tegner scores between PRP-augmented and control ACLR groups [S10, p.592].
8. Young athletes (≤21): PRP + porous collagen carrier with hamstrings autograft showed decreased second ACL injury rate, reduced revision rates, higher return to pre-injury competition (132/143 patients, avg 22 weeks) [S10, p.592].

#### Harvest Site (Donor Site Healing)
9. PRP at patellar tendon harvest site: Better immediate postoperative pain, less tendon gapping on MRI at 6 months [S10, p.593].
10. PRGF at BTB harvest site: Decreased anterior knee pain vs. controls in one double-blind RCT [S10, p.593].
11. **Conflicting evidence**: One double-blind RCT found no difference in kneeling pain or patellar defect size [S10, p.593].

## Future Directions
- **BEAR Technique** (Bridge-Enhanced ACL Repair): Collagen scaffold soaked with whole blood (delivers platelets) + suture stent for primary ACL repair. First human trial (10 patients) showed outcomes similar to hamstrings autograft ACLR at 12-24 months, with higher hamstring strength indices in the BEAR group. Also prevented cartilage lesion development seen after untreated ACL transection [S10, p.593].
- Adipose-derived regenerative stem cells (ADRC) added to BTB autograft ACLR: No significant difference in outcomes vs. controls at 12 months [S10, p.593].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using LR-PRP for ACLR** → switch to LP-PRP; leukocyte-rich preparations increase inflammation and may impair healing [S10, p.592].
2. **Expecting PRP to improve functional scores** → current evidence does not support this; PRP primarily affects MRI-assessed graft maturation, not clinical outcomes [S10, p.592].
3. **Ignoring scaffold stability** → injecting PRP without a carrier scaffold in the intra-articular space leads to premature dissolution; collagen hydrogel scaffold improves delivery [S10, p.589].

## Take-Home Messages (from Source)
- The best available clinical evidence does NOT demonstrate efficacy of PRP injections for ACL reconstruction on functional/clinical outcomes [S10, p.598].
- Insufficient high-quality evidence to recommend or reject PRP for partial ACL tears [S10, p.598].
- Lack of sufficient studies on cell-based therapies; no concrete evidence-based recommendations possible yet [S10, p.598].
