# Upper Extremity Injuries in Basketball

## Summary
Upper extremity injuries account for 10–15% of all basketball-related injuries and are more frequent during games than practices. The hand and wrist are the most commonly injured regions (8–9%), followed by the shoulder and elbow. Upper extremity injuries may be more common in male than female basketball players overall, though specific injury types vary by sex. The majority of upper extremity injuries result from contact — with the ball, another player, the floor, or playing apparatus — making primary prevention challenging. The key to management is understanding each injury's mechanism, optimal immobilization period, and progressive rehabilitation pathway back to sport-specific skill.

## When to Use
- When evaluating and managing upper extremity injuries in basketball players
- When designing return-to-play criteria and rehabilitation progressions
- When explaining injury risk and mechanisms to players and parents

## Key Principles
1. **10–15% of all injuries** are to the upper extremity; more common in games than practice [S10, p.55]
2. **Contact is the primary cause** of upper extremity injuries — ball contact, player contact, floor contact, and apparatus contact are the four mechanisms [S10, p.57]
3. **Hand/wrist most common** (8–9%), followed by shoulder and elbow [S10, p.55]
4. **Injury prevention is difficult** because contact cannot be eliminated; strengthening the active restraints (muscles) around vulnerable joints is the best available strategy [S10, p.57]
5. **One RCT showed 22-28% shoulder injury risk reduction** by improving glenohumeral internal rotation ROM, external rotator strength, and scapular control [S10, p.57]
6. **Cardiovascular cross-training is essential** — with wrist and hand injuries, cross-training can be initiated early in recovery to maintain conditioning [S10, p.62]

## Injury-Specific Information

### Finger Fracture
- **Rate**: 0.09 per 1000 AEs during games; more common in women's basketball [S10, p.55]
- **Mechanism**: Forced flexion of PIP joint (central slip + dorsal avulsion fracture) or forced flexion at DIP joint (mallet finger — terminal extensor tendon tear or bony avulsion) [S10, p.56]
- **Most affected digits**: Fourth and fifth digits most often involved; most fractures are non-displaced or minimally displaced
- **Time loss**: Average 20–33 days [S10, p.56]
- **Rehabilitation**: Reduce pain/swelling → motion exercises → strengthening → dexterity training (speed of movement for fast-twitch hand muscles) [S10, p.57]
- **Return**: Splinting or buddy taping; surgery if required allows return 3–4 weeks after non-displaced metacarpal fracture [S10, p.57]

### Thumb Sprain (UCL)
- **Rate**: 0.09 per 1000 AEs; equal in men and women [S10, p.56]
- **Mechanism**: Radial deviation/abduction force to MCP joint — falling on outstretched hand with thumb abducted, catching jersey, having shot blocked via sweeping motion [S10, p.56]
- **Consequence**: Acute UCL tear causes pain, swelling, instability; long-term risk of thumb osteoarthritis
- **NBA data**: Thumb ligament tears were least frequent hand injury but required the longest return (average 67.5 days); all required surgery [S10, p.56]
- **Rehabilitation by grade**:
  - Grade I/II: Thumb spica splint 4 weeks → strengthening + ROM
  - Grade III (complete tear): Surgery → cast 2–4 weeks → removable splint 2 weeks → ROM at 4 weeks → strengthening at 6 weeks → return to sport by 12 weeks [S10, pp.57-59]

### Wrist Sprain / Scapholunate / TFCC
- **Rate**: 0.15 per 1000 AEs during games; more common in men [S10, p.56]
- **Mechanism**: Forced wrist hyperextension — tripping, awkward landing from jump, drawing a charge, diving for a loose ball [S10, p.56]
- **Key injuries**: Scapholunate ligament tear (causes wrist instability, limits wrist cock or follow-through during shooting); scaphoid fracture; TFCC tear [S10, p.56]
- **Dart throwers motion**: In scapholunate rehabilitation, minimal scapholunate joint movement with activation of stabilizing muscles (ECRL, ECRB, FCU) — preferred for early rehab [S10, p.59]
- **Rehabilitation**: Immobilization → active motion → passive motion → proprioceptive exercises → strengthening → functional activities + early cardiovascular cross-training [S10, p.62]
- **Scaphoid fracture timelines**: Middle-third fracture: 8–12 weeks casting; proximal-third fracture: 12–24 weeks casting; after surgery, ROM begins at 2–4 weeks [S10, p.61]
- **TFCC tears**: Peripheral injuries often require surgery with 4–6 week immobilization post-op [S10, p.61]

### Shoulder Injury
- **High school rates**: Equal between boys and girls; boys more often diagnosed with sprain/strain, girls with dislocation/separation [S10, p.56]
- **Mechanism (girls)**: Contact with another player (67%) or the floor (25%)
- **Mechanism (boys)**: Contact with playing apparatus (30%) is the primary cause
- **Most return within 1 week** for high school sprain/strain or subluxation [S10, p.62]
- **Women's pro**: Shoulder subluxation is the 8th most frequent game injury (0.11 per 1000 AEs) [S10, p.56]
- **AC joint**: High inferior/posterior forces from falls, players, or apparatus to the acromion → acromioclavicular ligament tearing [S10, p.56]
- **Instability rehabilitation**: Joint mobilization + strengthening + endurance training + neuromuscular retraining → 78% return to sport within 1 year vs. 92% with surgery; directed at entire shoulder complex (glenohumeral AND scapulothoracic) [S10, p.62]

## Upper Extremity Rehabilitation Return-to-Play Tests
- **Closed Kinetic Chain Upper Extremity Stability Test (CKCUEST)**: Side-to-side alternating hand touches across a fixed-distance gap in push-up position — measures upper extremity stability
- **Unilateral Shot Put Test**: Seated single-arm throw for distance — measures explosive upper extremity power and asymmetry [S10, p.61]

## Upper Extremity Rehabilitation Progressions
### Closed-Chain Progression (for wrist/shoulder)
1. Wall push-up with neutral wrist
2. Prone push-up with neutral wrist
3. Uneven push-up in extreme wrist extension

### Open-Chain Dribbling Progression
1. Wall dribble
2. Upright dribble
3. Small ball dribble
4. Forward/backward dribble
5. One-hand cross dribble
6. Figure-eight dribble

### Open-Chain Passing Progression
1. One-hand chest pass
2. Two-hand chest pass
3. Wrap-around pass
4. Overhead pass [S10, pp.58-60]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not initiating cardiovascular cross-training early** → Upper extremity injuries should trigger immediate lower-body cardiovascular maintenance; conditioning should not be lost [S10, p.62]
2. **Ignoring scapular control in shoulder rehab** → Scapulothoracic joint must be addressed alongside glenohumeral joint for successful return to sport
3. **Returning from thumb UCL tear without adequate healing** → Grade III tears require surgery and 12-week return protocol; short-circuiting this leads to chronic instability and osteoarthritis
4. **Rushing wrist stability after scapholunate injury** → The SLIL is critical for shooting mechanics; returning before adequate healing causes re-injury and chronic instability limiting follow-through
