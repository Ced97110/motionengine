# Jump Shot Biomechanics

## Summary
The basketball jump shot is the most recognizable and arguably most important skill in the sport, with shots averaging approximately 4 meters from the basket over the course of a game. Biomechanics has identified five distinct phases of the jump shot and has quantified the key kinematic factors that determine shot success. Expert shooters are distinguished from novice shooters primarily by lower shot-to-shot variability in mechanics, not by any single "ideal" kinematic pattern. Ball trajectory — particularly the angle of entry into the basket — is the primary determinant of success or failure.

## When to Use
- When analyzing and correcting shooting mechanics in players at any level
- When designing rehabilitation progressions for upper extremity injuries that affect shooting
- When making evidence-based recommendations for shooting instruction

## Key Principles
1. **Five-phase structure** of the jump shot: Preparation → Ball Elevation → Stability → Release → Inertia [S10, p.54]
2. **Expert advantage = less variability**: Extensive analyses have not identified a single ideal kinematic pattern; experts simply have less shot-to-shot variability than novices [S10, p.55]
3. **Release angle determines trajectory**: Success is higher with a release angle of 49–60° from parallel — this increases vertical displacement and maximizes the effective width of the rim upon entry [S10, p.55]
4. **Lower release velocity = greater accuracy**: Lower ball release velocity leads to increased success; this is typically accomplished through large wrist flexion angles producing backspin (~2–3 revolutions on a foul shot) [S10, p.55]
5. **Release height matters but is individualized**: Higher release height is generally advantageous but depends heavily on shooter size and jumping ability [S10, p.55]
6. **Trajectory factors are multifactorial**: Ball trajectory is influenced by release mechanics, distance from basket, defender presence, body posture, pre-shot movement, field of view, and shooter expertise/physical characteristics [S10, p.55]

## Five Phases of the Jump Shot
### Phase 1: Preparation
- Ball positioned close to body at waist level
- Shooting hand behind the ball; non-shooting hand held laterally for support
- Athlete begins to lower into jump position

### Phase 2: Ball Elevation
- Shoulder flexes 90–135°
- Elbow in a position of flexion to position the ball for release
- Lower extremities beginning explosive extension

### Phase 3: Stability
- Lower extremities actively extending (hip, knee, ankle extension)
- Upper extremities characterized by **wrist hyperextension** (ball cocked back)
- Maximum energy storage before release

### Phase 4: Release
- Rapid, coupled **shoulder flexion** and **elbow extension** with **wrist flexion**
- Expert shooters add some radioulnar pronation after full wrist flexion [S10, p.55]
- This is the most critical phase for accuracy

### Phase 5: Inertia
- Sustained wrist flexion (parallel to the ground) during descending flight and landing
- "Freeze the follow-through" — hold until ball hits the rim
- Balance hand lifts away; shooting hand stays in follow-through

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: Understand their role in the shot chain — lower body generates energy, upper body channels it through a consistent kinematic sequence
- **PG/SG**: Highest shot frequency; shot variability must be minimized through volume repetition
- **PF/C**: Post-up shots often involve hook mechanics, but jump shot mechanics apply to face-up and mid-range post offense

## Common Mistakes
1. **High release velocity** → Softer touch (lower velocity + more backspin) produces higher success rates; "shoot softly" is a valid cue [S10, p.55]
2. **Low release angle** → A flat shot (release angle <45°) reduces the effective target window; coach a higher arc
3. **Variable mechanics** → Novice shooters have high shot-to-shot variability; consistent repetition of the same mechanics (not a perfect pattern) is the goal
4. **Non-shooting hand interference during release** → The non-shooting hand should be "along for the ride" in Phase 4; pressure from the balance hand creates lateral deviation
