# Individual Defensive Ratings — Project Defensive Score Sheet

## Summary
Official basketball statistics measure defense very poorly. The only official defensive statistics collected (defensive rebounds, steals, blocks, and fouls) are a "meager collection that does no justice to the value of defense in basketball" [S16, p.199]. A basketball equivalent of baseball's ERA — showing points allowed per 100 possessions for individual defenders — requires additional data collection.

Dean Oliver's Project Defensive Score Sheet, conducted in cooperation with WNBA teams, collects five additional individual defensive statistics that measure what a player actually *did* on defense, not just what the official scorer happened to record. These statistics are essentially the inverses of the offensive statistics already tracked: if an offensive player scores, the defender allowed it; if the offensive player missed, the defender forced it [S16, pp.199-200].

## When to Use
- **Film sessions**: Assign a scorekeeper to track these stats while watching game film to evaluate individual defender performance
- **Live scouting**: Volunteers at games track these in real time during play
- **Identifying who really won the defensive battle**: The Four Factors analysis tells you *what* the defense did well; the defensive score sheet tells you *who* did it
- **Post-game coaching feedback**: Provide specific, objective defensive feedback to individual players beyond "you played good/bad defense"

## Key Principles

### The Five Project Defensive Score Sheet Statistics
1. **Forced Miss (FM)**: When a defender forces an offensive player to miss a field goal attempt. If a help defender contributes to the miss, both defenders share credit (e.g., each gets 0.5 FM) [S16, p.199].
2. **Forced Turnover (FTO)**: When a defender directly forces an offensive player to commit a turnover. Different from steals — includes charges drawn, deflections that lead to turnovers, etc. [S16, p.199].
3. **Forced Free Throw Miss (FFTA)**: When a defender fouls an offensive player who then *misses* the free throws. The defender fouled, but the defense still "won" that possession [S16, p.199].
4. **Allowed Field Goal (DFGM)**: When a defender allows an offensive player to score a field goal over them [S16, p.199].
5. **Allowed Free Throw (DFTM)**: When a defender commits a foul that leads to made free throws [S16, p.199].

### Relationship to Official Statistics
- Official FGM by offense = unofficial DFGM for the defender
- Official FGA without FGM = unofficial FM for the defender
- Official blocks = a subset of FM (blocks are always forced misses)
- Official steals = a subset of FTO (steals are always forced turnovers)
- Team FM/FTO covers situations where no individual defender is responsible [S16, pp.199-200]

### How to Use with the Four Factors
Start with the team's Four Factors analysis to identify *which* defensive category the team excelled or failed in:
- **If field goal % was held low**: Find players with high FM and FM+BLK totals — they forced the missed shots
- **If turnovers were forced**: Find players with high FTO and FTO+STL totals
- **If offensive rebounds were limited**: Look for defensive rebounders (official stat suffices)
- **If opponent free throw rate was low**: Check that fouls were distributed (no single player drawing excessive fouls) [S16, pp.202]

### Coaching Application
- After a game, the coaching staff reviews the score sheet to identify who "did the job" defensively [S16, p.202]
- When a player (like Delisha Milton guarding Lauren Jackson in the Sparks-Storm example) gives up many DFGM, the staff can decide whether to provide help defense or change the matchup next time
- Team DFGM (shots made with no individual defender credited) reveal rotation failures or transition breakdowns

## Player Responsibilities
- **On-ball defender**: Primary owner of FM, DFGM, FFTA, DFTM for their assigned player
- **Help defender**: Shares FM credit when help defense forces a miss (each gets partial credit)
- **All defenders**: Can contribute FTO through pressure, traps, or strategic positioning
- **Post defenders**: Typically have the highest individual DFGM counts (guarding the highest-usage offensive players)

## Variations
### Total Forced Misses (FM + BLK)
Combining unofficial forced misses with official blocks gives a complete picture of a defender's ability to force missed shots. This is the best single measure of interior shot-contesting ability [S16, p.202].

### Total Forced Turnovers (FTO + STL)
Combining unofficial forced turnovers with official steals gives a complete picture of a player's ability to take possessions away from the offense [S16, p.202].

## Common Mistakes
1. **Relying only on official stats for defensive evaluation** → Blocks and steals capture only a fraction of defensive contribution. A player like Simone Edwards (Storm), who forced 5 misses in 19 minutes with zero blocks, is completely invisible in the official stat line [S16, p.202].
2. **Crediting defense for what offense did** → Sometimes an offensive player misses a wide-open shot with no defender; that is a *team* forced miss, not an individual achievement. Don't over-credit individual defenders for opponent's uncontested misses [S16, p.199].
3. **Ignoring context when evaluating DFGM** → A defender who allows many field goals while guarding the opponent's best scorer (e.g., a Milton guarding Jackson) should be evaluated differently than one who allows many goals on catch-and-shoot opportunities [S16, p.202].
4. **Neglecting the Four Factors first step** → Don't skip directly to individual numbers. First establish what *kind* of defense was important in the game — if turnovers mattered, FTO leaders are the heroes; if field goal defense mattered, FM leaders are [S16, p.202].

## Case Study: Seattle Storm vs. LA Sparks, July 11, 2002
- **Four Factors analysis**: Sparks shot only 33% (vs. 42% average), had 27% turnover rate (vs. 22% average) — Storm won by forcing misses and turnovers, not by limiting offensive rebounds [S16, p.201]
- **Key defenders for Seattle**:
  - Kamila Vodichkova: 7.0 total forced misses (3.0 FM + 4 BLK) in 25 minutes — best field goal stopper
  - Simone Edwards: 5.0 FM in 19 minutes, zero blocks — effort *only* visible via the unofficial score sheet
  - Adia Barnes: 4.5 total FTO (2.5 FTO + 2 STL) in 28 minutes — best turnover forcer
  - Felicia Ragland: 2.0 total FTO in 21 minutes
- **LA problem**: Delisha Milton gave up 9 DFGM guarding Lauren Jackson — staff decision: provide help next game [S16, p.202]
