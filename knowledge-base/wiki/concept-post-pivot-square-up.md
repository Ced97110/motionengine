# Post Pivot Square-Up Moves (Front Outside Pivot and Sikma Move)

## Summary
When a post player catches the ball and cannot determine the post defender's position — because the defender is neither half-fronting nor making physical contact — the post player cannot use the "show and go opposite" read. Instead, two pivot techniques allow the post player to turn and face the basket while simultaneously locating the defender by sight.

The **Front Outside Pivot (and Square-Up) Move** and the **Sikma Inside Pivot (and Square-Up) Move** (named for pro Hall-of-Famer an elite post player, who originated the footwork at Seattle) both achieve the same result — squaring up to face the basket and the defender — but differ only in the *direction of the initial pivot*. Once squared up, the post player attacks with any of the same moves a perimeter player uses from a live-ball position. [S6, pp.196-201]

## When to Use
- When the post defender is playing straight-up (no fronting, no contact) and cannot be read by feel
- When the post player needs to face the basket to locate the defender by sight before attacking
- As an alternative to the drop-step or Olajuwon move for post players who prefer a face-up game
- For perimeter players who need to create separation from a ball defender (reverse-pivot post move also used by guards) [S6, p.199]

## Key Principles

### Shared Mechanics (Both Moves)
1. **Same universal catch mechanics** — two-foot hop simultaneous with the catch, chin the ball, elbows out, snap head over the high shoulder [S6, p.196]
2. **Dribble not used for the pivot** — the pivot and square-up are executed before the dribble is used. Once squared up, the dribble is available for any attack. [S6, p.198]
3. **Result is the same** — the post player ends up squared up, facing the basket and the defender [S6, p.199]
4. **Attack options after squaring up** — if X5 plays up tight: use the **front crossover pivot** (opposite direction of the pivot foot) or the **blast move** (same direction as the pivot foot). If the defender plays softly: rise up for a jump shot or use the dribble to attack the basket. [S6, p.198]

### Front Outside Pivot (and Square-Up) Move
5. **Front pivot direction** — the front pivot is *away from the basket* (which is also away from the defender, since the defender is between the post player and the basket). The free foot swings 180 degrees back toward the basket. [S6, pp.197-198]
6. **Either foot can be the pivot foot** — the post player selects whichever foot is most appropriate. Diagram 4-18 shows the right foot as pivot (swinging left foot to the baseline side); Diagram 4-19 shows the left foot as pivot (swinging right foot to the middle). [S6, pp.199-200]

### Sikma Inside Pivot (and Square-Up) Move
7. **Reverse pivot direction** — the reverse-pivot post move uses a *reverse pivot toward the basket and toward the defender*. The free foot steps toward the basket and the defender in a 180-degree reverse pivot. [S6, pp.199, 201]
8. **Creates space** — because the free foot reverse-pivots toward the defender, the reverse-pivot post move actually pushes the defender back slightly, creating separation. *This is also used by perimeter players to create separation from a ball defender.* [S6, p.199]
9. **Same result, different feel** — both moves land the post player square-up to the basket and defender, but the Sikma generates slight separation while the front outside pivot does not [S6, p.199]
10. **Starting position matters for the Sikma** — if the lower (baseline) foot is used as the pivot foot, the post player may end up squaring up *below* the block, reducing scoring and driving angles. If this is a frequently used move, the initial post-up position must be higher on the lane line. [S6, p.201]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PF/C (post player):** After the two-foot hop and chin, if the defender cannot be read — use the Front Outside Pivot (front pivot away from basket, free foot swings 180° back toward basket) or reverse-pivot post move (reverse pivot toward basket and defender). Land squared up. Read the defender's proximity. Attack with perimeter-equivalent moves (crossover drive, blast drive, jump shot) depending on defensive pressure. [S6, pp.197-201]

## Variations
### Front Outside Pivot — Baseline Direction (Diagram 4-18)
Post player on the offense's left side uses the right foot as pivot foot. Makes a front pivot away from the basket toward the baseline by swinging the free (left) foot around the right pivot foot. Ends squared up facing the basket with the left (baseline) foot now as the free foot. [S6, p.199]

### Front Outside Pivot — Middle Direction (Diagram 4-19)
Same post player uses the left foot as the pivot foot. Steps toward the middle with the free right foot. Ends squared up facing the basket with the right foot closer to the middle. [S6, p.200]

### Sikma Move — Top Foot as Pivot (Diagram 4-20)
Post player on the left side of the lane uses the top (left) foot as pivot foot. Free right foot makes a 180° reverse pivot toward the basket and the defender. Post player ends squared up, facing the basket, with the left foot now as the baseline foot / pivot foot. [S6, p.201]

### Sikma Move — Lower Foot as Pivot (Diagram 4-21)
Post player uses the lower (right) foot as pivot. Free left foot makes 180° reverse pivot toward the basket. Post player may end up below the block — must start higher on the lane to compensate. [S6, p.201]

### Sikma Move for Perimeter Players
The Sikma reverse-pivot mechanic also applies to guards and wings who need to create separation from a ball defender when in a live-ball situation — the same footwork creates the same space on the perimeter. [S6, p.199]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Using the dribble before the pivot** → wastes the dribble and limits attack options after squaring up. Fix: complete the pivot to face-up position before the dribble is ever used.
2. **reverse-pivot post move with lower foot as pivot without adjusting starting position** → post player ends up below the block, losing scoring angles. Fix: if the lower-foot Sikma is a preferred move, start the post-up higher on the lane line.
3. **Not reading the defender after squaring up** → attacks in the wrong direction. Fix: after squaring up, re-read the defender's proximity and spacing before initiating any attack move.
4. **Using the Front Outside Pivot when the defender IS making contact** → unnecessary — if contact is present, the post player can read by feel and use the drop-step or Olajuwon instead. Fix: use these pivot-and-face-up moves only when the defender's position is ambiguous.
