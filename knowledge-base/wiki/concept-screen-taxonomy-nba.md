# Screen Taxonomy — pro Coaches Playbook (the source coach)

## Summary
the source coach (Motion Offense assistant) provides a comprehensive taxonomy of every screen type used in pro half-court offenses. Each screen type is defined by its location, the roles of the screened player and screener, and — critically — the reads both players must execute based on exactly how the defense reacts. The underlying principle across all screen types is the same: the offense must read the defender first, then choose the correct cut. [S7, pp.57–65]

Screens are only as good as the reads that follow them. If players decide in advance what they'll do instead of reacting to the defender, the screen becomes predictable and the defense wins.

## When to Use
- Half-court sets when the primary ball handler cannot create off the dribble alone
- When personnel mismatches (small on big, or shooter on help-slower defender) can be exploited
- Late-shot-clock situations requiring a guaranteed look
- Continuity offenses requiring multiple actions per possession

## Key Principles
1. **Read first, cut second** — the screened player must watch the defender before committing to a cut direction
2. **Screener always reacts opposite** — when the cutter goes one way, the screener opens to the ball in the opposite direction (pop or roll)
3. **Location determines name** — the same reads and options apply to back screens whether run on the baseline (flex), at the high-post corner (High-Post), or diagonally (hawk)
4. **Screener is always a threat** — if the screener's defender hedges or switches, the screener slips to the basket immediately
5. **Spacing protects reads** — all non-involved players must spot up to force defenders to account for them during rotations

## Off-Ball Screen Types

### Back Screen
Set behind a cutter, perpendicular to their direction of travel.
- **Defender anticipates cut** → cutter cuts directly to basket; screener pops out
- **Defender slides over top** → cutter goes away from screen, then cuts into lane; screener pops out
- **Defender slides under** → cutter cuts high over the screen; screener opens to ball in opposite direction
- **Defender slides over screen** → cutter comes out low along baseline; screener opens and goes high
- **Defender anticipates (lob read)** → cutter stops near basket, receives lob pass; screener goes opposite
[S7, pp.57–58]

### Cross Screen
Two players on opposite blocks. One player screens across the lane for the other.
- **Defender anticipates** → screened player stops near basket for lob; screener opens opposite
- **Defender slides over top** → screened player goes away then cuts into lane; screener pops out
- **Defender slides under** → screened player cuts high over screen; screener opens to ball opposite
- **Defender slides over screen** → screened player comes out low baseline; screener opens and goes high opposite
[S7, pp.57–58]

### Flex Screen
A back screen run along the baseline. Reads and options are identical to back screen reads. This is the initiating action of the flex offense. [S7, p.58]

### Corner Pin Down Screen
Set by a midpost player for a teammate in the deep corner. Reads and options are identical to standard pin down reads but run at this specific half-court location. [S7, p.58]

## On-Ball Screen Types

### Side Screen
Screener fronts or has back to sideline.
- **Defender trails** → ball handler drives over top; screener rolls opposite
- **Defender slides under** → ball handler pulls up for jump shot; screener rolls opposite
- **Defender anticipates drive** → ball handler drives baseline; screener rolls opposite
- **Screener's defender shows late** → ball handler splits between two defenders and drives; screener rolls
- **Double team** → ball handler takes two dribbles away, passes to rolling screener or to teammate whose defender helped
- **Defender forces baseline** → three options: drive to middle for pull-up; pass to rolling screener; pass to flash-cut teammate at free-throw area elbow
[S7, pp.58–60]

### High Screen (Midscreen)
Set in the middle of the court outside the three-second lane.
- **Defender trails** → ball handler drives around screen; screener rolls opposite
- **Defender slides under** → ball handler pulls up for jump shot; screener rolls
- **Defender pushes outside** → ball handler passes to rolling screener
[S7, pp.60–61]

### Horns Screen
A high screen with a second high screener, giving the ball handler a screen on either side of the free-throw lane. Reads are the same as the high screen. The non-screener reacts opposite to the screener: if screener rolls, non-screener pops; if screener pops, non-screener rolls. [S7, p.61]

### High-Post Screen (High-Post Rub Cut)
A vertical back screen at the corner of the free-throw area from the High-Post offense (John Wooden). The ball handler passes to a wing and rubs off the screen. After the screen, the screener rolls to basket or pops. All back-screen reads apply at this location. Involves three players: screened player, screener, and wing passer. [S7, p.61]

### Hawk Screen
A diagonal screen at the free-throw line area, used when the ball is at the wing. Created by the head coach with the pro team. The ball handler (1) drives to the wing while the high post (5) back-screens for 2; 5 then rolls to the basket opposite the ball handler's drive. All standard reads apply. [S7, p.62]

## Special Screens (4+ Players)

### Screen the Screener
Combines a baseline screen and a down screen simultaneously, requiring the defense to guard two screen types at once.
- 1 makes a crosscourt pass to 2 and then sets a down screen
- 3 comes off 4's baseline screen; 4 pops out high off 1's down screen
- 2 first looks to pass to 3 coming off the baseline screen or posting up
- 2 can also pass to 5 for a post feed; if 3 is not open, pass to 4 popping out or to 1 rolling
- Continuity: 2 passes to 4 and sets a down screen for 3; 5 comes off 3's baseline screen
[S7, pp.62–63]

### Double Screens
Two players screen shoulder to shoulder.

**Parallel Double Screen** — 4 and 5 screen parallel to the free-throw line; 2 breaks up off it.
- Defender trails → 2 curls and cuts to basket
- Defender cheats over → 2 flares for jump shot
- 5's defender hedges/switches → 5 slips to basket
- 4's defender helps on 5 slip → 4 rolls, 1 lobs
- 5 can also screen for 4, who cuts to basket
[S7, pp.63–64]

**Perpendicular Double Screen** — 4 and 5 screen perpendicular to baseline; 1 at top of lane.
- 2 cuts along baseline off double screen
- Defender trails → 2 curls to basket
- Defense cheats over → 2 fades for shot
- Same slip/roll counters as parallel double screen
[S7, p.64]

**Stagger Double Screen** — Series of down screens. Bigs out, shooters inside to start.
- 3 sets cross baseline screen for 2; 2 continues off 5's down screen
- 1 looks to pass to 2 coming off down screen or to 5 rolling if defenders switch
- If 2 and 5 not open → pass to 3 popping off 4's down screen on opposite wing
[S7, pp.64–65]

**Single/Double Screen** — Shooter starts under basket; 3 and 5 set perpendicular double on one side; 4 sets single down on other side. 2 chooses which way to go.
- If 2 goes off double (3+5): 3 then goes off 4's single; 1 can pass to 2, 5 (roll), 3 (off 4), or 4 (roll)
- If 2 goes off single (4): 3 pops off 5's down; 1 can pass to 2, 3, 4 (inside), or 5 (inside if switch)
[S7, pp.65–66]

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (1)**: Primary ball handler; reads defense and chooses the correct pass; keeps dribble alive when going away from screens
- **SG (2)**: Primary cutter/shooter; reads defender position before committing to cut direction (curl, fade, straight)
- **SF (3)**: Secondary cutter/shooter; mirrors actions on weak side; spots up for kick-out
- **PF (4)**: Screener in most actions; reads whether to roll, pop, or slip based on how defense reacts
- **C (5)**: Primary screener (post feeds, double screens); slips immediately when defender hedges or switches; rolls opposite the ball handler

## Variations
### Hawk Screen
The unique diagonal screen from the wing (5 back-screens for 2 while 1 drives wing-side) — especially useful when the ball is already at the wing and the high-post defender is slow to help. Named after the pro team under the head coach. [S7, p.62]

### Horns Double High
Two post players at the elbows give the ball handler a screen choice to either side of the key. The non-screener's reaction (opposite the screener) provides an automatic second scoring option. [S7, p.61]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Deciding before reading** → players must see the defender's path before cutting; rehearse reads in walk-throughs
2. **Screener not reacting** → the screener must immediately open to the ball opposite the cutter's direction; "roll or pop — never stand"
3. **Cutter too early** → set up the screen with a V-cut before using it; give the screener time to establish position
4. **Non-involved players flat-footed** → all players not in the screen must spot up and stay ready; this forces the defense to account for them
5. **Screener's roll ignored** → when a screener slips or rolls and is open, passing to the original cutter instead wastes the primary scoring opportunity

## Reactions to the Defense (PnR Specific)

**Hedge and Over**: X4 hedges, X1 goes over → ball handler dribbles away from hedge, passes to rolling screener (4) or splits between X4 and X1 if hedge is delayed; teammates spot up for kick-out

**Hedge and Under**: X4 hedges, X1 goes under (screened player not a shooter) → ball handler drives inside the lane to create shooting options for self or teammates

**Trap and Rotate**: X1 and X4 trap → all four teammates spread out; ball handler dribbles out; screener (4) slips early for outlet; if 4 catches and X5 helps, 4 passes to 5 under basket

**Push to Baseline**: Defenders force baseline → screener (4) rolls to basket; nearest teammate flash-cuts to free-throw elbow; ball handler can pass to either
[S7, pp.66–67]

## Practice Progression
1. **No defender** — run all options in walk-through to establish reads
2. **50% defense** — defenders contest but don't go full effort
3. **Game-speed defense** — full competition; players must execute correct reads under pressure
[S7, p.71]
