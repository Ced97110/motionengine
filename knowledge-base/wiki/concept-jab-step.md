# Jab Step

## Summary
The jab step is a short, quick movement at the defender with the nonpivot foot. Its primary purpose is to get the defender off balance — to get them back on their heels or retreating — so the offensive player can attack with a jump shot or drive. The jab must be short (only 1-2 inches / 5 cm forward), precise, and directed *at* the defender rather than to the side. A well-developed jab step series is an essential tool for any perimeter scorer and an indispensable skill for three-point shooters.

The jab step was mastered to an elite level by players like an elite scorer (a pro team), a legendary scorer, Reggie Miller, and Ray Allen. The book's author notes personally learning the non-dominant foot jab from watching Toney, demonstrating that the skill can be developed with deliberate practice at any level. [S5, p.103]

## When to Use
- When holding the ball with a live dribble in triple-threat position
- To test a defender's positioning before committing to a shot or drive
- Against tight, aggressive defenders who do not want to give up the drive
- As a setup for the jab-and-go when the defender becomes conditioned to retreating
- Especially effective for three-point shooters who use the jab-rise-and-shoot

## Key Principles
1. **Jab short and at the defender** — only 1-2 inches forward; jab to the side or too long leaves you out of stance and off balance [S5, p.103]
2. **Stay in athletic stance** — from the jab position you must still be able to rise and shoot, pull back and jab again, or drive by
3. **Jab with shooting foot first, then progress to nonshooting foot** — develop the more natural foot before advancing to the an elite scorer–style opposite-foot jab [S5, p.103]
4. **Be aware of help defense** — if you jab too many times without making a move, help defenders get set; make concise, precise decisions [S5, p.103]
5. **Multiple jabs compound pressure** — the second and third jab build on the first; a defender who survived the first jab may freeze on the second, opening the shot or the drive [S5, p.105]

## Footwork Details

### Standard Jab (Shooting Foot as Jab Foot)
In a straight-on catch with the shooting foot as the nonpivot foot: jab with the shooting foot forward at the defender, remain low and balanced, then choose your action — shoot, jab again, or go.

### an elite scorer–Style Jab (Nonshooting Foot as Jab Foot)
When catching on the move and pivoting on the shooting foot (less common but essential to master): jab with the nonshooting foot. The defender will typically play the drive, not expecting a jump shot from this position. A skilled shooter can still rise and knock down the shot from this stance. [S5, p.103]

## Jab Step Variations

### Jab-Rise-and-Shoot
Jab at the defender → defender retreats → rise straight up into a balanced jump shot. Every great three-point shooter (a legendary scorer, Reggie Miller, Ray Allen) relies heavily on this move. [S5, p.104]

### Jab-and-Go (Drive)
Jab at the defender → defender stays → drive past the defender. On the last step, take a long step past the defender, attack along the defender's hip, and use an arm bar to protect the ball. Push the ball out past where you step so the dribble covers maximum distance. Decide at the rim: pull-up jump shot or layup. [S5, p.107]

### Jab, Jab, Jumper
Jab once (defender stays) → jab again (defender retreats) → open jump shot. Built on the principle that even elite lockdown defenders will bite on multiple fakes. Keep both jabs short and quick to maintain a balanced shooting position. Some players can jab 3-4 times quickly and still shoot on balance. Work jabbing with both feet. [S5, p.105]

### Jab, Jab, and Go
Jab once (defender stays) → jab again (defender freezes, conditioned to expect the shot) → drive past the frozen defender. Make the last step a long step past the defender's hip. Use the arm bar to protect the ball and stay tight to the defender to keep them off balance. [S5, p.107]

### Jab-and-Go into Pull-Up
The jab threatens the drive, the defender backs off for the shot, and the offensive player reads this and drives past the now-retreating defender into a pull-up jump shot. Similar footwork to the show-and-go. [S5, p.104]

### Jab and Shot Fake (Combination)
Jab-step hard at the defender while simultaneously raising the ball as on a shot fake. Forces the defender to defend both the drive threat and the shot threat simultaneously, causing them to close out soft. Optional: add a slight **head fake** (tilt head back while raising ball) to further freeze the defender. Best used by midrange post players (e.g., Kevin Garnett's signature move). [S5, p.109]

## Drilling Progression
1. **Stationary**: Jab with shooting foot, rise and shoot from seven spots. Use "go for makes," "shooting strings," or "three-strikes" formats
2. **Straight-on catch**: One-two step, pause, then jab into jump shot. The pause is critical — it creates time to read the defender
3. **On the move (wing)**: Spin out toward baseline or middle from either wing, pivot on inside foot, jab, and shoot
4. **Both pivot feet**: Drill from both wings, spinning out to baseline and middle (four combinations per wing)
5. **Live reads with defender**: Defender closes out; offensive player reads direction and responds with correct jab action
6. **Competitive scoring**: Keep score; deduct points for incorrect reads (e.g., defender closes right, offensive player jabs right → charge) [S5, pp.103-104]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Jabbing to the side** — not at the defender → defender doesn't react; player loses their athletic stance → always jab directly *at* the defender
2. **Overstriding on the jab** — too long a step → out of balanced shooting position; defender can recover → keep it 1-2 inches
3. **One-two-stepping directly into the shot without pausing** — mistaking the footwork for a jab → always pause after the one-two step catch to allow for a genuine jab read
4. **Taking too long** — help defense gets set → make decisive moves quickly
5. **Only jabbing with the dominant foot** — limits effectiveness when catching on the move → develop the nonshooting-foot jab [S5, p.103]
