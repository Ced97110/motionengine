# Squaring the Floor

## Summary
"Squaring the floor" is the head coach's core team principle for defeating a trap at any point on the court — in the backcourt, at the sideline, at the low post, or anywhere the ball handler faces a double-team. When a trap is set, the other four offensive players immediately form a square around the ball handler so he has a passing outlet in every direction. If it is impossible to pass out of the trap, one corner of the square collapses toward the middle to create a shorter outlet. [S7, pp.79-83]

## When to Use
- Any time the ball handler is double-teamed (backcourt, sideline, baseline drive, low post, middle pick-and-roll)
- After an inbounds pass when the receiver is trapped in transition
- When the defense traps a "hot player" to take the ball out of his hands

## Key Principles
1. **React immediately.** The moment a trap is set, all four off-ball players find their square corners.
2. **Spread as wide as possible.** Players position themselves to stretch the defense and maximize passing angles. "Good passing and squaring the floor are key against trapping."
3. **Pressure-release player is higher.** The nearest outlet teammate (e.g., 2) must set himself higher than the ball handler — not at the same level — to provide a better passing angle and stretch the floor.
4. **One corner collapses if needed.** If no direct pass is available, one corner player moves toward the middle of the square to create a closer, safer outlet.
5. **Ball handler stays strong.** Never assume a "surrender position" with the ball high overhead. Step through the trap, pivot away, or keep the dribble alive.
6. **Catch and go.** The receiver of the outlet pass must immediately act — catch and shoot, catch and drive, or catch and pass — not hold the ball.

## Player Responsibilities
- **PG (1):** Ball handler; maintains strong position; reads the square for the best outlet.
- **SG (2):** Nearest pressure-release player; positions himself higher than 1 to create a better passing angle.
- **SF (3):** Fills the far corner of the square to stretch the defense.
- **PF (4):** Takes another corner; ready to continue the ball reversal.
- **C (5):** Takes the remaining corner or flashes to the high post as an interior outlet option.

## Variations
### Squaring vs. a Baseline Trap
When 1 drives the baseline and is trapped by X5 and X1 who deny the corner: 5 slides to the dunker spot under the rim with hands up (keeps baseline passing lane free AND provides an interior target), and 3 drifts to the baseline to spot up in front of 1. [S7, p.81]

### Squaring vs. a Low-Post Double
When the ball goes to the low post and the defense doubles, the other four players square the floor immediately. The low post reads the "Angles!" call — if the trap comes from the strong side, there may be a chance to split it and score inside. If the trap comes from the weak side, splitting is harder due to the angle and the charge risk. [S7, pp.83-84]

### Passing Out of a Baseline Trap
Four options for the baseline-trapped ball handler:
1. Step-through pass
2. Ear pass (near the ear of a defender when their hands are down)
3. Pivot pass
4. Keep the dribble alive and pop away from the trap ("better to overdribble than lose the ball on a turnover") [S7, p.82]

## Common Mistakes
1. **Two players at the same depth** → Always stagger heights so the defense cannot cover two outlets with one defender.
2. **Holding the ball after receiving the outlet** → The receiver must immediately attack, not allow the defense to recover.
3. **Big man stays near the trap** → The screener/big man must separate low and away to create a new threat and pull the rotating defender.
