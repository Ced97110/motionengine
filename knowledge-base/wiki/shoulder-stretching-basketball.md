# Shoulder Stretching for Basketball

## Summary
The shoulder is one of the two most critical joints for basketball performance, involved in all shooting, blocking, and rebounding motions. Overuse injuries of the shoulder are common due to frequent overhead movements. A consistent shoulder stretching program restores and maintains the flexibility required for full range of motion during shooting and defending, and forms the foundation of basketball injury prevention (prehabilitation). [S2, pp.151-155]

## When to Use
- Daily maintenance — 3 to 5 times per day for most static stretches
- Pre-practice warm-up (dynamic mobility like thoracic rotation)
- Post-practice/game cool-down (static stretches held 30 seconds)
- During rehabilitation from shoulder overuse injuries
- As prehabilitation for players with prior shoulder complaints

## Key Principles
1. Hold static stretches for the prescribed duration (30 seconds for most; 10 seconds × 10 reps for sleeper stretch) — do not rush [S2, pp.151-154]
2. "Feel the stretch, don't force it" — advance only when flexibility allows
3. The posterior shoulder capsule is the most commonly restricted area in overhead athletes; prioritize the posterior capsule and sleeper stretches [S2, p.151]
4. Thoracic mobility (rotation) supports shoulder range of motion — the spine and shoulder work together [S2, p.155]
5. Address both the anterior chest (pectoralis) and posterior back (latissimus, rotator cuff) to maintain balanced shoulder mechanics

## Stretch Inventory

### Posterior Capsule Stretch
- **Target muscles**: Rotator cuff (infraspinatus, supraspinatus, subscapularis, teres minor), posterior deltoid; secondary — serratus anterior, latissimus dorsi
- **Execution**: Stand erect. Pull one elbow across the body until a stretch is felt in the back of the shoulder. Hold 30 seconds. 3-5 times per day.
- **Advancement**: Hold a pole or stationary object with the non-stretching hand and lean toward the arm.
- **Basketball focus**: Restores flexibility in the back of the shoulder to allow increased range of motion during shooting and defending. [S2, p.151]

### Latissimus Dorsi Stretch
- **Target muscles**: Latissimus dorsi (primary); serratus anterior (secondary)
- **Execution**: Stand and hold a pole or stationary object with one hand. Lean back until a stretch is felt along the border of the latissimus muscle. Hold 30 seconds. 3-5 times per day.
- **Basketball focus**: Restores back and shoulder flexibility to increase shoulder range of motion during shooting and defending. [S2, p.152]

### Pectoralis Stretch
- **Target muscles**: Pectoralis major, pectoralis minor (primary)
- **Execution**: Stand in a corner or doorway with elbows at shoulder height, forearms on the walls. Lean into the corner until a stretch is felt in the upper chest. Hold 30 seconds. 3-5 times per day.
- **Basketball focus**: Opens the anterior chest to improve shoulder range of motion for shooting and defending. [S2, p.153]

### Sleeper Stretch
- **Target muscles**: Rotator cuff (infraspinatus, supraspinatus, subscapularis, teres minor), posterior deltoid (primary)
- **Execution**: Lie on the affected shoulder as if sleeping on your side. Raise the lower arm 90° in front; bend elbow 90° so hand is near upper shoulder. Use the upper hand to gently press the lower hand toward the floor. Hold 10 seconds. 10 repetitions.
- **Basketball focus**: Restores flexibility in the back and shoulder to increase range of motion during shooting and defending. [S2, p.154]

### Thoracic Spine Mobility
- **Target muscles**: Spinalis, longissimus, external oblique, internal oblique (primary)
- **Execution**: Assume quadruped position (all fours). Lift one arm and place hand behind head. Rotate toward that arm, feeling the stretch in the upper back. Hold 3-5 seconds. 30 repetitions each side.
- **Advancement**: Stand with a stick held behind the back. Bend knees slightly and flex hips ~75°. Rotate side to side.
- **Basketball focus**: Restores thoracic mobility to support shoulder range of motion during shooting and defending. [S2, p.155]

## Player Responsibilities
- **All positions**: All five stretches are relevant to every basketball player regardless of position. Overhead athletes (shooters, post players who rebound) especially benefit from daily execution.

## Common Mistakes
1. Rushing through stretches → hold for the full prescribed duration; rushing does not produce lasting tissue change
2. Forcing the posterior capsule stretch too aggressively → ease into the stretch; the capsule is delicate
3. Skipping thoracic mobility work → the thoracic spine and shoulder are linked; tight thoracic rotation limits overhead function
4. Only stretching before practice and not after → post-activity static stretching while muscles are warm produces greater flexibility gains
5. Performing the sleeper stretch with only one or two reps → the 10-second × 10-repetition protocol is evidence-based; do all 10
