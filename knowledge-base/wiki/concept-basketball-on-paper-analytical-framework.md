# Basketball on Paper: Core Analytical Framework

## Summary
"Basketball on Paper" (S16) by Dean Oliver establishes a possession-based framework for evaluating individual and team performance. The core idea is that all basketball statistics should be expressed relative to possessions (per 100 possessions), not per game, to account for pace differences across eras and teams. The primary metrics are **Offensive Rating** (points produced per 100 possessions), **Defensive Rating** (points allowed per 100 possessions), **Floor%** (how often a player "scores" on a possession — i.e., makes a field goal or gets to the line), **Stop%** (how often a defensive player ends the opponent's possession), and a derived **Win-Loss record per 82 games**.

These metrics allow apples-to-apples comparisons across eras, positions, and roles — enabling coaches, front offices, and analysts to evaluate players who don't show up prominently in traditional box scores.

## When to Use
- Evaluating role players whose box-score stats understate their contribution
- Comparing players across different eras (adjust for league average and pace)
- Determining whether a defensive specialist's value is worth the offensive cost
- Identifying which players raise or lower team efficiency when on the court

## Key Principles
1. **Pace adjustment is essential**: A 50-point-per-game season in 1962 (126 possessions per game) is equivalent to ~35 points per game in 2002 (91 possessions per game) [S16, pp.284-285]
2. **Team context matters**: A player's individual defensive rating partly reflects the quality of the team defense around him — man defenders on bad defenses look worse than they are [S16, p.292]
3. **Offensive rating measures efficiency, not volume**: A high floor% and offensive rating means a player produces points efficiently, regardless of his scoring average [S16, p.287]
4. **Win-loss record is the ultimate summary**: The analytical framework converts all metrics into expected wins and losses per 82 games, making player value directly comparable [S16, pp.290-296]
5. **Stop% captures defensive contribution**: Unlike steals and blocks, stop% measures how often a defender actually ends a possession — including man-to-man coverage with no stats [S16, p.292]
6. **With/without analysis reveals true impact**: The clearest evidence of a player's value is how the team changes when he enters and leaves the lineup [S16, pp.283-284]

## Metric Definitions
- **Offensive Rating**: Points produced per 100 possessions used by that player
- **Defensive Rating**: Points allowed per 100 possessions while that player defends
- **Floor%**: Fraction of a player's possessions that end in a "score" (FG made or trip to line)
- **Stop%**: Fraction of opponent possessions that the defender ends
- **PtsProd/G**: Estimated points produced per game (a volume statistic)
- **%TmPoss**: Percentage of team possessions used by that player while on the court
- **W-L(%)**: Expected wins and losses per 82 games based on the above metrics

## Common Mistakes
1. **Confusing scoring average with offensive value** → A player scoring 30 ppg in a fast-paced era may be less efficient than a player scoring 20 ppg in a slower era; always adjust for pace and league average [S16, p.285]
2. **Overvaluing defensive stats** → Steals and blocks alone don't capture great man defense; stop% and team defensive ratings with/without the player are better indicators [S16, p.292]
3. **Ignoring league average** → An offensive rating of 105 means something very different if the league average is 95 vs. 104 [S16, p.287]
